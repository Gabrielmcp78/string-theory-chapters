III. THE DISCOVERY
Cornell Physics Building—David’s Lecture Hall

Ishikawa’s footsteps echoed through empty corridors as he ran across the quad, mind racing. Every “crazy” theory David had been developing, every impossible mathematical framework he’d dismissed as brilliant but unmoored from reality—what if it had all been breakthrough disguised as breakdown?

The phone was already ringing when he reached David’s building, the sound echoing down the hallway like an emergency alarm.

Ring. Ring. Ring.

Ishikawa fumbled with his keys, hands shaking as he unlocked the physics wing.

The ringing grew louder as he approached David’s lecture hall.

Ring. Ring. Ring.

“Hold on, Marcus!” he hollered, not realizing, of course, that the phone hadn’t been answered yet and he couldn’t be heard. As he approached the corner and turned into the corridor of David’s main lecture hall, he began to fumble through a massive ring of keys that served the entire building. A mild panic overtook him—he had no idea which it was, and they weren’t labeled the way they should have been, or if they were, the labels had long since worn away. He cursed himself for the oversight, but as he reached the threshold, he realized it was ajar.

Someone was already inside.

He pushed through the doors, the ringing of Marcus’s call bifurcating his focus.

Ring. Ring. Ring.

Rune moved to pick up the handset on the desk when he saw her.

It was Celeste—standing before the massive boards in a sort of trance; unmoved, unreachable it seemed; completely overtaken by the sight before her.

“Celeste?” he uttered, not wanting to frighten her.

Nothing. He moved slowly closer to her and, reaching out his hand to touch her shoulder, whispered her name once more. “Celeste?”

Still no reply, but her stare forward seemed to beckon him to look—really look. He could not help but square his body and gaze forward to match Celeste’s focus. Suddenly everything she was entranced by seemed to extend its power over him as well. The sight, now fully seen, flooded over him, stopping him dead in his tracks as his brain struggled to process—or even believe—what was before him.

Every surface David had worked on pulsed with gentle radiance. Klein–Gordon operators flowering into glissandi of sixteenth notes, Ricci tensors dissolving into harmonic progressions, the complete unified field equations cascading across chalkboards in David’s precise handwriting. It all seemed somehow spring-loaded—coiled—ready to fly off the wall at any moment.

Ring. Ring. Ring. Ring…

The fourth couplet snapped Rune back. He lunged at the phone and snatched up the receiver.

“Marcus? Yes, I… I’m here, Marcus. I can hear you. I’m in David’s classroom… well, I don’t believe what I’m—everything he was working on seems to have been set aglow and I quite literally… well, Marcus, if it wasn’t right in front of me, I’d never believe it.”

Instantly, upon the last utterance of Dr. McGrath’s name, Celeste snapped out of her trance. Her head jerked toward Rune and the handset he held to his ear.

“Marcus? Dr.—did you say Marcus McGrath?”

“Yes, dear. Marcus McGrath is on the line. From UCLA.”

“Put it on speakerphone,” Celeste demanded. And without waiting for Rune to move, she grabbed the phone from his hand, ran to the desk unit, hit the speaker button, and slammed the handset back into its cradle. “Marcus? It’s Celeste Armitage.”

“Celeste! Of course. Thank God you’re there. Celeste, where’s Da—”

“Marcus…” Tears streamed down her face as she looked around the space. “Oh God, Marcus, you have to see this. It’s everywhere. The same equations from the recording, but they’re—they’re alive.”

“Marcus, it’s Rune. I’m looking at a room covered in glowing mathematics. The same theories I called ‘crazy’ just days ago—they’re all here. Everything he was working on.”

“The equations are moving,” Celeste reported, watching symbols shift and complete themselves. “And there’s something else, Marcus. They seem to be entwining with… treble clefs and arpeggios and…”

“I suspended a genius,” Rune whispered, horror and recognition washing over him as he shuffled backward and collapsed into the first row of student benches.

“It’s like they’re alive,” Celeste murmured. “Just waiting for David to come back and finish the work.”

“But where is he?” Marcus’s voice carried through the speaker, urgent and afraid. “If he achieved this breakthrough—if these theories are correct—where the hell is David?”
