11.1  Walk C     |    for David, Roger, towel, and crowbar


They didn’t speak much at first. There was nothing to organize. The morning had already taken what it wanted, and David’s body, wrapped tight at the ribs, did not reward conversation.

A few blocks in, Linn said, almost casually, “You don’t have a meeting.”

It wasn’t phrased as concern. It wasn’t even phrased as a question.

David shook his head. “No.  Just needed some… buffer zone.”

“Good,” Linn said, and after a beat: “We’ve got time.”

They kept going.

A minute later, long enough that it shouldn’t have belonged to the same exchange, Linn made a small, involuntary sound. A laugh that escaped him before he could decide whether it was appropriate.

David felt it tug at his ribs and stopped, one hand braced at his side, waiting for the pain to pass.

Linn tried to swallow the laugh and failed, the attempt making it worse. He bent forward slightly, hand on his thigh, shaking his head like he couldn’t believe what had returned to him.

“Jesus,” David said, through his teeth. “What—”

Linn wiped at his eyes. “The locker room,” he said. “Remember? Seventh grade gym.”

David closed his eyes.

“The stalls,” Linn added. “Those low ones that always got stuck.”

A laugh tore out of David before he could stop it. It hurt immediately; he folded forward and let it happen anyway, the sound coming in short, broken bursts that cost him breath.

“Yeah, but not like this. I mean… that door was done!”

“Oh God, I tried everything. It was not gonna budge.”

“And then I’m changing on the other side of the room. I hear this terrified, whispering, ‘Psst…Dave!’”

“I remember I was trying to channel you. I thought maybe if I focused, I could get your attention without anyone else noticing.”

“Oh man, did that fail. Every guy in the room was glued to that stall door, watching me try to break it down.”

“Yeah, and then the worst idea in the world.”

“Hey, it was your idea.” David started to spit giggle now. “I just did what I was told and pulled your legs when you got stuck.”

The two were out of the realm of controllable now.

“You said, ‘Okay, big pull!’” Linn’s voice cracked as another round of laughter took over.

“And I grabbed the towel,” David said.

Linn covered his face. “And it was the only thing that came with you.”

They stood there, laughing like idiots, like boys, like people who once had bodies that betrayed them publicly and survived it anyway.

“You went from boy in towel locked in bathroom stall… to coach standing over your legs… “

“All ass, no towel,” Linn jumped in, “just wedged up under the door with coach right over the top of me, trying to crowbar the door open.”

“I mean, you were just kicking and screaming, and I was just standing there cheering you on like…”

“Oh you were not. You were laughing your ass off with the rest of ‘em,” Linn protested.

“You’re absolutely right,” David said. “As much of an ass hole as the next 12-year-old.” Then the name returned to David, and it took him 5 tries just to get the next word out clean between outbursts. “We… we called you Crowbar for the rest of middle school!”

“You did not!” Linn protested, then burst into laughter again.

David nodded vehemently, “Oh, we sure as hell did! God, when I told my dad about it later… full-on spit take with his favorite lager.”

“Come on. You’re just making that up now.”

And the two of them both fell out of funny as Arthur’s presence floated over the memory.

“No. No, I swear he laughed for an hour straight. Also came up with the better nickname too. Linn-bar.”

They both slammed back into another round of rolling, thunderous laughter.

Linn pulled up deadpan serious, “I just assumed that was because I’d always show up for my lessons with a couple of brews for us to share after. You know? Linn… Bar.”

“Not sure he ever called you anything else the rest of his life.” David’s laughter turned into a hiss as it pulled against his ribs. He wrapped an arm tight around his side and waited for the pain to settle. It did, slowly, the way pain always does when it’s been acknowledged.

When they finally straightened, David’s breathing had changed. It was slower now, even deeper.

Linn’s face flattened as he turned to David. “You know, your dad would’ve had a few things to say about that rehearsal today.”

David straightened, “Yeah. Would have never let me live it down.”

“Disagree. He would’ve been proud of you today. Would’ve seen his son, playing through his pain, not his skill. He’d have seen an artist, letting his instrument share in his grief. That’s what I saw.”

Silence sat sober between them for a moment. “Can’t teach that.”

David made a quarter turn away and looked up, the way you do when there are tears to control and stupid dignity to cover for. “Wish I’d learned more from him.”

“Yeah,” Roger smirked, “Don’t get too hard on yourself, Dave. He gave away most of his best tricks to me, anyway.”

David put his hand on Linn’s shoulder and just squeezed tightly, then nodded. “You needed all the help you could get.”

“Linn-bar,” Roger shook his head. “Can’t believe that.” He looked at his watch. “We’ve got a couple still,” Roger said.

David wiped his eyes with the back of his hand. “I’m sure she’s in there already.”

“Oh, I guarantee you she is,” Roger smirked. “Better deliver you for the interrogation.”

“Yeah… okay. Hey,” he paused to make sure the proper point was made. “I don’t know why the hell you put up with me all these years, but… you’re a good man, Roger. A good friend.”

“Yeah. You’re kinda a shithead, but I’m loyal to a fault, so… you’re pretty much stuck with me.”

“Thanks… Crowbar.”

Roger shook his head, then broke the moment into a feigned, death-filtered warning.

“Don’t look now, we’ve been spotted.”

David jumped into better posture and, with a deadpan face and straight arms, slowly swirled ‘round to face the restaurant and a waving, wondering Celeste in the far-off corner.




11.2  THE CAFÉ


Afternoon thinned across the café windows, laying amber strips over the stone floor. Chairs held their half-slouched guests like phrases waiting for resolution. Celeste had claimed a corner table, two cups already steaming on its surface when the two men decided it was best not to keep he waiting any longer.

Linn guided David to the seat with better back support, then stepped away. “I’ll be at my office,” he said, addressing both of them. “Text if you need anything.”

David nodded. Linn’s hand rested briefly on David’s shoulder.  The pressure enough to register, was gone before it could become a statement. Then he left, the café door settling closed behind him with barely a sound.

Celeste pushed one of the cups toward David. “I ordered for you. Black, no sugar.”

“Thanks, as long as you didn’t make it yourself.”

She gave David a glance that stood as a pure acknowledgment of fact as she protested the insinuation with mocked indignation.

As their laughter settled, she reached out, quickly at first and then more carefully, slipping the sunglasses from David’s face and setting them on the table. He allowed it with a look cast toward the floor, as though the injuries themselves had become something to apologize for. The damage was worse without the dark lenses to conceal it: the swollen eye socket, the split above his eyebrow stitched together with butterfly bandages, the bruising that spread across his cheekbone in mottled purples and fading yellows.

“I’m only gonna ask this once. Now. Are you in trouble?”

He nodded sheepishly that he was not.

“Pain?”

“Nothing I can’t handle.”

She smiled. Professor tough guy, huh.”

“You look… different,” she said.

He managed half a smile, though it pulled at the split lip. “You mean aside from the obvious?”

“No. Because of it.” She leaned forward slightly. “You let Linn help. You came to rehearsal. You’re here.”

David’s hand found the coffee cup, fingers wrapping around its warmth. “I didn’t have much choice about Linn. He found the door open.”

“You had a choice about staying. About not sending him away.”She paused. “About the tuning experiment.”

“That wasn’t about the—“ He stopped, recalibrated. “The math doesn’t care what shape I’m in.”

“Maybe not. But you do.” Her gaze held steady. “You could have rested. Linn gave you permission to leave. You stayed. You pushed.”

David stared into his coffee, watching steam rise in slow spirals. “I can’t tell if I’ve broken through or come apart. There’s no edge.”

“I know,” she said.

The conversation settled into silence, but not emptiness. Around them, the café continued its afternoon routines: espresso machine hissing, silverware meeting porcelain. Their stillness existed within it, a rest held inside the larger composition.

Celeste’s hand moved across the table, palm up. An offering more than a demand. David looked at it for a long moment, the calluses from viola strings, the slight tremor that came from holding tension for hours at a time. Then his hand met hers.

The contact was neither tentative nor assured. Just present. Her thumb traced the edge of his palm, finding the faint cerulean stain that still marked the skin there. She didn’t ask about it. Her touch simply acknowledged its presence, just as she’d acknowledged the bruising, just as she’d acknowledged his decision to keep playing despite the cost.

“The tuning,” David said finally. “It’s not just an idea. There’s structure underneath. Something that holds.”

“I felt it.” Her fingers tightened slightly. “We all did. Even Linn.”

“Linn was holding me up.”

“Linn was playing the music. You gave him something worth holding.” She paused. “There’s a difference.”

David’s ribs protested as he shifted in his chair, seeking a position that didn’t pull quite so hard. Celeste noticed, her expression flickering with something that might have been concern but settled into patience instead.

“I should probably go home,” he said. “Sleep. Actually sleep.”

“Probably.” She didn’t release his hand. “But you don’t have to go alone.”

The words hung in the air between them, neither question nor statement. Just possibility, offered in the same tone she’d used to suggest the coffee, the same way she’d followed his experimental tuning without demanding explanation first.

David looked at her fully then, not through pain or exhaustion or the protective distance he’d maintained for months. Just looked. Saw the way she’d positioned herself at the table to minimize the light hitting his injured eye. Saw the careful neutrality in her posture that made space for whatever he might need. Saw the decision she’d already made, waiting only for him to meet it.

“Okay,” he said.

She stood first, gathering her bag. David rose more slowly, the elastic wrap reminding him of its presence with each breath. She waited without hovering, then fell into step beside him as they moved toward the door.




11.2  walk  D - Part 1    |    For Celeste and David


Outside, the afternoon had begun its slide toward evening. The walk wasn’t long, less than ten minutes on a good day, fifteen at the pace David’s body now required. Celeste matched his stride without comment, the way Linn had that morning. But where Linn’s accommodation had been fraternal, hers carried different weight.

They walked in silence, their footsteps finding an unintentional rhythm, a slow 3/4 that neither rushed nor dragged. Students passed them, absorbed in their own trajectories. The physics building rose to their left, its windows catching the low sun. David didn’t look up at his office.

“Every day, you know,” Celeste said, as if picking up a conversation already in progress. She stared straight ahead toward the crest. Towards the bench.

David frowned, hands burrowed in the pockets of his jacket. “What?”

“Every day, I think of it.”

He squinted at her, waiting for the punchline, but she only watched the ground ahead.

“Celeste, I don’t know what you mean.”

She pointed with her chin. “It’s right there. I pass it every day. So do you. And every time, another flash of what might have been.”

He stopped, awkwardly, as if the sidewalk had ended without warning. He did not want to burden her with the buzz and the menace of it. He wouldn’t know how to explain it, even then.

“Well, it’s silly, I know,” she said, as if trying to float it away.

“It is silly.” His lips pressed into a line, sharp as a string under tension. “You shouldn’t.”

“You mean you don’t ever dream of—.”

David exhaled hard through his nose. “No. I don’t. Ever!”

A pause. Then, quieter:

“I’ve bled that day out long ago. Drained it. Whatever that thing is in the ground, it doesn’t belong to me anymore. It’s not my problem.”

But he knew, it was absolutely his problem.

He gestured toward the crest. “Those two up there? They’re squatting on ghosts, not dreams.”

High on the hill, two of their own students sat on the bench, oblivious to the coming encounter. Their voices drifted down through the sharp air, lazy and amused.




11.3  The bench


“I’m just saying,” Roman said, smoke leaking from both corners of his mouth, “if the multiverse is real, then somewhere I’m already sober. Perfectly sober. That’s just quantum balance, babe.”

Greene, flat on her back beneath the skeletal sycamore, didn’t open her eyes. “You’re failing quantum mechanics in this universe.”

“Maybe in this one,” Roman said. “But in another, I’m a tenured professor with a full head of hair.”

“You have a full head of hair now, idiot.”

“Yeah, but it’s scared. It knows what’s coming.”

Green groaned, laughing. “This is what tenure does to people. They go all cursed prophet. You’ve been breathing too much library dust.”

Roman passed the joint with exaggerated precision. “I breathe ideas. My body is a finely tuned antenna for cosmic nonsense.”

“That’s why you failed the midterm.”

“No, I failed because I challenged the grading rubric. With passion.”

Green snorted, then froze. Her gaze flicked toward the path.

“Oh. Shit.”

Roman turned. “What—?”

“Lang. And Armitage. Like, right there.”

Roman sat bolt upright, foot catching in Green’s bag. The joint snapped from his fingers. He made a strangled sound, scrabbling to crush it against the underside of the bench. It hissed faintly, a curl of smoke spiraling up.

The instant the ember met wood, a low-frequency tremor rippled through the air, almost inaudible, but enough to make David gasp from twenty feet away.

The world dropped out from under him. His knees buckled. He collapsed to the ground.

“David!” Celeste lunged, catching him by the arm. For a moment he hung there, suspended by her grip, breath coming in short, sharp bursts. He couldn’t breathe. He couldn’t… He couldn’t actually breathe.

The students leapt to their feet, music major first. “Professor Lang! Are you okay?” Greene hollered.

“Green? Roman? You two. Help me. Help me get him to the bench.”

They scrambled to his side, one under each shoulder, and together they half-dragged, half-carried him to the bench. David collapsed onto the slats, head bowed, eyes wild and unfocused. Roman hovered nearby, hands fluttering helplessly.

Celeste knelt in front of him, her voice low. “Breathe. In and out. Just listen to me.”

The sound came again, clearer this time. A dissonant hum, like a cello string arco bowed underwater.

Only David seemed to hear it.

He pressed his palms to his temples, riding the wave of sensation until it passed.

“Are you all right, Professor?” Green asked, her voice trembling.

David straightened slowly. Color returning to his face in increments. He nodded. “Yes. Yes, I think so.”

Celeste stood, brushing off her knees. “Thank you, both of you.”

The students looked unsure. Should they linger or flee?

“No problem, Professor Armitage,” Greene offered. “Hope you feel better soon, Professor Lang.”

Celeste nodded, “See you in seminar next week, Greene.”

They acknowledged as they melted away in the dusk.

As David began to recover, he called after his quantum mechanic student, “Mr. Roman, don’t get any ideas about this improving your abysmal marks on the midterm. But, between us, you’ve got a better shot at a re-take than you did 5 minutes ago.”

“Yes, sir. Thank you, sir! Feel better, David. I mean…PROFESSOR.” And with that, he turned and scampered off.

David stared at the bench, then down at the smoldering joint stub mashed into its wood. “I’m okay. It was just… my ribs caught.”

“If you’re waiting for me to interrogate you, keep waiting.  Who you get in fistfights with and why is no longer any of my business, thank God.”

She figured that would be enough rope for him to hang himself on. She was sure he’d out with it in seconds flat. She was wrong. He spoke not a word.

Together, they sat for a long moment, listening to the silence that had settled impossibly over the quad.

Then something stirred in her. Something with pain attached. She knew instantly it would have to out. Celeste stood, reluctantly, and walked slowly forward, looking out over the ridge, her hair snagged by the wind, her gaze fixed on the distant Finger Lake. The water caught the last light, a bruised blue. David slowly rose and moved down beside her, as if being closer could help him see what he knew he could not. She didn’t look at David, but he watched her anyway, his hands jammed in his jacket pockets, trying not to fidget, trying not to speak first.

Another, patient silence. A breeze whipped down from the leafless trees above, the air filling Celeste’s lungs, almost forcing the truth from her head and heart to her throat and tongue and then…

“I miss being a scientist, David,” she said, voice clean and simple, as if announcing the weather.

He let the silence hang. A crow called somewhere behind them, and Celeste’s eyes shone, glassy with the threat of tears she refused to blink away.

“I know you do, Cele,” David said. “I tried to shield you from being around my work weekend after weekend. I didn’t want my science getting pushed in your face.”

She turned, mouth open to answer, but nothing came. Her throat worked. She looked away, out over the trembling water.

He shifted his weight, scuffed the dirt.

The silence between them served as confirmation. “You deserved to be treated like the brilliant woman that you are. I should have trusted you.”

She turned to look him in the eye. She needed to see past his words in this moment.

He met her turning gaze, hoping for something… he didn’t know, anything. Absolution, if he was being honest, but consolation would be a huge win, would be enough.”

The weight of dissonance traced back over time, now pressed down upon them both: renewed clarity and cruelty in equal measure. David, working all weekend, secluded behind closed doors, desperate to shelter her from the work he knew she missed so much. Celeste, trying not to disturb the brilliant professor, thinking his work was the only thing he cared for anymore. Both of them wounding in the exact moment they meant to give care. It was more than the moment could hold.

The wind flew up from the canyons beyond, filling the silence between them with the scent of glacial water, fresh dirt, and winter white. Its interference of natural comfort, of truth prompting them both to whisper the other’s name at once.

“David…”

“Celeste… I see it now. I tried to protect you like you were a twelve-year-old girl, didn’t I? God… I’m sorry, Cele.”

She heard him. She believed him. But it was not enough to close the canyon, nor bridge its peril nor absolve its pain. But she knew the chord must resolve. And so she let the dominant forces do their work. She let the tonic in. Begrudgingly…. Resolvedly so.

“Yes, David. You did. And yes… I know you are.”

A thin breeze rose, picking at the hem of her coat. She stepped forward, closer to the edge of the cliff, closer to the lake, as if she might walk the distance or measure it. She breathed in deeply and let it out in a slow, even stream. The sun, as if in response to the silent reckoning through respirate, slipped behind a bank of clouds, causing the blue on the water darkened, deepening toward something like promise.

“Come on,” she said, as a slight smile cracking her lips. “Can’t linger here much longer without this becoming a funeral dirge.”




11.4  WALK D - part 2   |   For Celeste, David, bridge, and porch light


They descended from the quad in silence, Celeste matching David’s careful pace without comment. The evening had begun its slow compression of light: amber fading to violet, shadows pooling in doorways and beneath the skeletal branches overhead.

David’s breathing had settled into a rhythm dictated by the constraint on his ribs. Each inhalation measured, each exhalation controlled. Celeste noticed but said nothing. Her presence was answer enough.

They passed the physics building. David didn’t look up at his office window, didn’t acknowledge the tower of unfinished work waiting in the darkness above. Celeste felt the weight of his deliberate avoidance, the way someone might pass a former home, knowing every detail of its interior but refusing the backward glance.

They crossed the canyon bridge and closed the final few blocks of the trip to the house on the hill. Somewhere between “I’m sorry” and the porch steps, Celeste had put out her hand and David found he had taken it in his.

When they reached the porch steps, Celeste climbed the first two, then turned to kiss David on the cheek. From this angle, their eyes were level despite the height difference. She looked deeply into his, as if making a determination of authenticity on a rare diamond. The porch light illuminated half her face, leaving the other half in shadow.

He couldn’t read her. She knew he couldn’t read her. When she moved again to go inside, David found she had not released his hand. The slight tremor from hours of maintained tension disappeared.




11.5  The house on the hill (return)


She led him up the remaining steps, across the porch where a swing moved slightly in the evening breeze, and opened the door.

Warm light spilled out into the dusk. The smell of old wood and lavender. Inside, the hallway was lined with framed concert programs and photographs of ensemble performances spanning decades.

“You want tea?” she asked, already moving toward the kitchen. “Or something stronger?”

“Tea’s fine. I like what you did with all the programs. God, so much musical history in these,” he said out loud.  So much of their history—he only thought.

David stood, taking in the place that had been his lost cause project for so many years.

“Hello, old friend.” Then he saw the broken bannister finial sitting on the table. He picked it up.

Looks stripped.

“Hey, Cele, this finial, it looks stripped. Want me to—“

She was already running back to the foyer to stop him.

“No!” She said, grabbing the globe from his hands and setting it back down on the side table. “That’s my problem now. Not yours! Hands off!” And she skillfully glanced at him, then turned and went back into the kitchen. “In fact, resist the urge to fix anything this evening. You’ve got enough repair on your hands as it is, I think.”

She was right, of course. So David stood there just letting the house’s different acoustics settle around him.

Celeste returned with two mugs, steam rising in delicate spirals. She handed him one, then gestured toward the stairs.

“Come on,” she said. “You can’t work on the draft standing in my hallway.”

He followed her up to the second floor, past the bathroom that took him seven months to gut and rebuild to completion… well, almost completion. They passed the guest room, which, if he were honest, was in his day the throw-all-the-tools room. Nothing more. Then into the bedroom. The large windows facing the street seemed to see David when he walked into the room. It might have just been a small conceit of his, but he thought he clocked a wink of light from their classic single panes. The desk, the dresser, the side tables and lamps, the bed, they were all the same. David couldn’t decide if that was a good, comforting thing, or awkward as hell.

She set her mug down on the desk, turned to face him.

“This isn’t—” David started.

“I know what it isn’t,” she said. “And I know what it is. You need rest. You need to not be alone. We can figure out the rest as we go.”

The simplicity of her statement, the lack of drama or complication, disarmed him completely.

“Okay,” he said. Then silence between them, before, “Celeste, you are—“

“A goddess, a saint, a genius? Yes, these things I know.”

She smiled then, genuine and unguarded. “Now sit down before you fall down. You’re pale. You look horrible.”

David lowered himself onto the edge of the bed. The mattress was firmer than his, the pillows arranged with care rather than thrown haphazardly. Celeste moved to close the curtains, then paused.

“Do you want them open or closed?”

He thought about it. About the evening light still clinging to the rooflines across the street. About the fact that he’d spent too many hours in deliberate darkness lately.

“Open,” he said.

She left them that way. Turned to face him. The space between them held all the accumulated history of their relationship, the relationship that had ended, the collaboration that had continued, the care that had persisted through every transformation.

She crossed the distance. Sat beside him. Her hand found his.

“Thank you,” David said quietly.

“For what?”

“For not asking questions I can’t answer yet.”

“They’ll keep,” she said. “The questions always keep.”

Outside, the evening completed its transition into night. Inside, two people sat on a bed in a room filled with ordinary light, connected by nothing more complex than presence and the choice to stay.

After a moment, Celeste stood. “I’ll be right back.”

She left him there, returning minutes later with the tea refreshed and a blanket from somewhere deeper in the house. David had removed his jacket and his shoes. The tuning fork sat on the nightstand, catching the lamplight.

Celeste noticed it but didn’t comment. She just settled beside him, close enough to matter, far enough to let him choose.

He chose closer.




11.6  The draft


David woke to the disorientation of an unfamiliar ceiling, then remembered. The house. The bed. The tuning fork was a cool weight against his sternum, tucked beneath his shirt.

She wasn’t beside him. He turned his head carefully, finding her silhouette at the desk by the window. She’d pulled on an oversized shirt and was writing something, her posture suggesting absorption.

"What time is it?" His voice was rough.

She turned. "Almost six. You slept three hours." She stood, crossing to the bed. "How do you feel?"

"Like I got mugged and then had sex." He paused. "In that order."

The corner of her mouth lifted. "Accurate." She sat on the edge of the bed. "I made tea. And Linn texted. Twice."

David reached for his phone on the nightstand. Two messages waited. Linn: Office until 6 if you need anything. A second one, fifteen minutes later: Heading home. Call if you need me to come back.

Celeste watched him read them. "I texted him back. Told him you were sleeping, that I’d make sure you got home okay."

"You didn’t have to—"

“I know.” She handed him his glasses. “But he was going to keep checking otherwise, and you needed the sleep more than he needed immediate confirmation.”

David pulled himself to sitting, a process that required strategic breathing. The elastic wrap had shifted slightly; he tugged it back into alignment. “I should go. I have work—“

“You have work you can do here, if you need to.” She gestured toward the desk. “Or you can go home. But you shouldn’t be alone if you’re going to push yourself.”

He looked at her, at the matter-of-fact way she’d rearranged her evening to accommodate his presence. “The draft,” he said. “The paper. I need to finish the section on harmonic decomposition.”

“So finish it.”

She stood, moving to clear space at the desk. Her own work went into a folder without ceremony. She pulled over a second chair, angling it so he could sit without the window’s glare hitting his injured eye.

“Tea’s in the kitchen,” she said. “I’ll bring it.”

David made his way to the desk slowly. By the time he settled into the chair, she’d returned with a mug and his jacket. His laptop was in the inner pocket. He opened it. The draft document appeared, pages of equations and prose that had seemed impossibly tangled yesterday now resolving into clarity.

Celeste returned to her own chair, pulling out a score and a pencil. She worked in parallel silence while David traced the relationships between harmonic decomposition and quantum superposition.

Evening settled in gradual increments. The lamp by the desk cast a small perimeter of clarity. David sat without perfect posture. He couldn’t manage it with the ribs, but the desk’s edge shaped itself around his forearms, providing support.

He did not categorize. He traced.

Notes on the staff aligned with curvature in the string diagrams. Not synthesis. Just parity. The blue stain on his palm had faded to a ghost, but he felt its resonance along the ulnar path. He wrote slowly. Symbols nested within gestures.

Once, from the attic rafters, a faint thump sounded, as if something had shifted in the empty space above. Celeste glanced up, met his eyes, returned to her score without comment. The house held its memories close.

The final equation did not resolve into brilliance. The final cadence did not transcend. But the draft held together. No exhilaration. No disappointment.

Only structure. Only the shape of an idea sustained without collapse.

David closed the laptop. Celeste looked up.

"Done?" she asked.

"For now."

She set down her pencil. "There’s leftover pasta. Or I can walk you home."

He considered. His apartment would be cold, empty, still holding the morning’s violence in its walls. Here, there was warmth. Company that required nothing but allowed everything.

But he needed to return. Not to flee, but to test the new equilibrium.

"I should go home," he said. "But thank you. For the offer."

She nodded, understanding. "Let me walk you at least."

They gathered his things. At the door, she helped him with his jacket. His hand went to his breast pocket, checking for the tuning fork. It was there, cool and certain.

"Thank you," he said again.

"For what?"

“For texting Linn. For the tea. For—“ He gestured at the desk, the bed, the space she’d made without fanfare. “All of it.”

“You would have done the same.”

Would he? A year ago, probably not. He’d have retreated, isolated, insisted on handling everything alone. But she was right about what he’d become, or hoped he was becoming. Someone who let Linn find him. Who came to rehearsal injured. Who accepted help and company.

“Yeah," he said. "I would.”

She smiled, just a small, genuine gesture, and opened the door.




11.7  Walk  E   |   for David, Celeste, notebook, and boundaries


The walk to his apartment was quiet. The night was clear, the air sharp. At his building, she stopped.

"You’ll call if you need anything?" she asked.

"I will.”

David leaned to kiss her. Nothing forced. Nothing assumed. Celeste took the chance to restate at least one thing that the day’s events had… blurred. A slight turn of her head to give him a cheek was all. And then…

"Goodnight, David."

He smiled, acknowledging the boundary adjustment.

”Goodnight, Celeste."

And with that, she turned and walked back toward the hill, her figure growing smaller against the pallid haze of the streetlights.

David unlocked his door, pushed it open, and stepped halfway inside before turning to look back. For the next one-minute-forty-six seconds, David watched her walk away. Even after the last of her glinting hair in the moonlight had given up sending any more light the distance to David’s eyes, he watched. She had no idea, but he always watched her until the going was more than gone. Then a deep breath before he tucked inside, closed and locked the door with care, and climbed the spiral staircase to the loft. His apartment welcomed him with its familiar, resonant silence. The blackboard triptych stood closed. The cello case leaned against the sofa.

He sat at his desk, the tuning fork cool in his hand. He didn’t strike it. He simply held it, feeling its latent frequency humming against his skin, a companion to his own pulse.

Outside, night settled over Ithaca. Inside, the lamp held its small circle of light, and a man who had spent the day navigating pain and music and the spaces between them sat alone in his apartment, connected by nothing more complex than memory and choice.

He opened his notebook to a fresh page and began to write.

For now, it was more than enough.




11.8  10 Minutes, Maybe Twelve


Celeste was not, under any circumstances, going to give him the satisfaction of looking back as she walked away.

He was watching her go. Of course, he was. He’d been watching her go for twenty years. A man of habit, if nothing else, he wasn’t going to stop tonight.

Or at least, she hoped he wasn’t going to stop… not tonight.

She shook her head and smiled to herself.

If the day hadn’t already eaten up every last inch of her bandwidth, she might’ve spared a moment to examine this annoying, lingering truth of him. But as it was, it only took about two and a half blinks before the walk home became home. So much so that she nearly tripped on the first porch step.

It was as if someone had picked up her house and dropped it right in front of her while she’d been too busy checking both ways to cross the street. It was jarring. But the whole day had been.

Celeste walked up to the door, shoulders slumped, the shawl attacking her cheeks with woolen fuzz. As the door gave way, the house welcomed her squeaking, crackling. It was obvious to Celeste, however, that there was disappointment implied in these structural groaning. The house had spent an entire evening with its favorite tenant returned. The realization that this return was merely temporary hit the frame like foundation footings sinking into soft earth.

Celeste stepped inside and with only mild amusement slammed the door behind her in mock defiance. “Whatever house! You’ll see! You’ll see who really loves you best.”

Jesus. I’m taunting an empty house.

She looked up at the balcony rail where, that morning, she had teetered on the brink of something unforgivably stupid. She laughed, but only halfway.

Then, a decision. She went straight to the closet in the causeway and pulled out her toolkit. Not his. Hers. The kit was well-organized, clean, even logical in its layout. She crossed to the side table at the bottom of the stairs, picked up the skull of the finial from where it had been left, and plopped herself and the box down on the third step.

The screw was stripped. Of course it was. It had always been stripped. She had pointed that out years ago now, but it hardly mattered. She braced the rail with one hand, tried once, felt it slip, adjusted, tried again. The wood resisted before it caught. She swore under her breath, reset the angle, tightened slowly, deliberately, feeling the threads take. And then three full twists until there was no twist left to have. And then it was done. Just done.

Ten minutes. Maybe twelve.

“Who’s your favorite now, huh?”

Celeste stood. She tested it once with her hand. Then, because no one was watching, she leaned her full weight into the banister and felt it hold.

She gathered the tools, returning everything to its proper place, and as she put the toolbox squarely into the closet, she heard it. Nothing. Absolutely nothing. She peeked her head out from the closet, and gently, slowly, closed the door. A hundred-year-old house, especially this one, was rarely so sonically behaved. It was suspect. She tiptoed toward the kitchen with feather-like feet, then turned to head upstairs. Enjoy it, she thought. Trust it when you—

—Click!

And just like that, the electric kettle… clicked… off!
