a fantasia on Bach's Sarabande in four movements

Johann Sebastian Bach
Cello Suite No. 1 in G Major
BWV 1007, Sarabande
I. The SARABANDE
Largo

The opening chord settled into the room with harmonic gravity, an open fifth so stark and unadorned it felt poised to swallow light. David’s eyes closed, obeying the pull of that interval. The Sarabande stood and breathed, and inside its hollow architecture he found space, the kind of space denied him for years.

The geometry learned at nine years old rose in his hands, steady and familiar. His father’s hand was there too, bringing with it a sense of pipe tobacco, warmth, the weight of a body that had not yet learned it could leave.

Easy now. Let it breathe. Don’t choke it.

The memory folded itself into the present without warning, as if the fifth itself had summoned it.

The triple pulse moved gently under his bow. Three steps, breathe; three steps, breathe. It opened before him like a long reach of shoreline at low tide, the sea drawn back into something slower and more sovereign, a largo so deep it seemed not to move at all, only to remain. Each phrase arrived the way waves arrive—advancing, withdrawing, leaving something altered in their wake. He did not chase them. He only let them come, and when they did, he stepped aside. His arm moved. The music moved through him. And he watched from a place within himself he had not entered in years—not since before the proofs, before the grants, before he had learned that the world paid more readily for certainty than for truth.

The phrases leaned into their suspensions, aching, lengthening, ripening at their own impossible pace. The dissonance hung unresolved in the bare interval, the perfect fifth that held both emptiness and promise. The open strings carried their raw grain, their unsoftened honesty, and he let them. Their austerity a kind of truth, a brazen tone weaving through tiny stings of inevitability, possibility, of prayer.

Something long braced inside him, some filament stretched across the hollow of his ribs, began to unfasten grain by grain.

In the rests between phrases, the Sarabande opened small windows of silence, forming outlines of absence. The shape of a voice that should be there. His hands knew a second part not written in the score, a viola line built from years of Wednesday evenings and late rehearsals. Celeste’s intervals. Now, meeting nothing but the room’s bare patience, he played through the space where she would have replied and let it stand open. The restraint cost him something he felt in his sternum, a resonance of regret.

Behind his eyelids, blue gathered. His creative brain forged a new grammar of blue. He knew that this was a new word. This was the blue of things that have always been waiting for you to stop running long enough to see them. Light shifted along the wall. The tuning fork trembled, its narrow frequency widening into a field he did not consciously notice, though his breath and heart slipped toward it, into it, as if through an unseen doorway.

He played through the first phrase and the room fell away. Through the second and time fell away. Through the third and the David he had built—equations on the wall, tuning fork on velvet, careful distances—fell away too. What remained was the boy in the cramped studio, terrified and alive, discovering that music could say what he could not. The boy who still believed that playing was enough.

His mother’s voice from that morning drifted through the widening blue: He wasn’t playing for me. He was just playing.

David drew the bow and felt the note bloom fully before it began to fall, filling the empty fifth with the awareness of its emptiness. His father’s hand, his grandfather’s design, his mother’s heart, all lived in the shape between notes; the hollow architecture itself. And in the density of grip and claw, in the lineage pouring through his hands into the instrument, he felt the first, tentative, unbearable stirring of peace.

II. The Grid
Presto meccanico

Three blocks west, the substation that feeds the university district and the nearby homes groaned under a pressure no engineer had foreseen when the transformers were rated forty years ago. A tidal wave of coronal‑mass‑ejected particles—traveling ninety‑three million miles in seventeen hours—was now slamming into the delicate copper‑and‑silicon web we call infrastructure.

Above the atmosphere, a solar band spilled across the dark.

Wires held, taut and humming, until they thinned and separated in blue-white fractures that moved outward in sudden, branching vectors, and the failure, once it began, spoke to the next transformer in the language transformers understand—voltage—and the next transformer listened and agreed.

Inside the apartment, the music of the great master cradled David inside the arc of each phrase, his bow traveling steadily, shoulders loose, pulse aligned to the grave tread of the dance that had forgotten how to dance and chosen instead to endure. Three hundred and three years since the master had completed the work, and each note still obeyed his baton. Three minutes of notes, measured within those centuries between the two men, yet Bach had David entranced. Each note flowing from centuries of these same sixteen bars—a lineage of sound composition—the foundations of the Western musical world. Nothing could have been further from his attention than the happening of a power grid or industrial infrastructure. And he did not hear the first substation go, nor the second, nor the third. He did not, could not know that over the next three minutes, 744 transformers throughout greater upstate New York would follow suit, nor that 196,884 such marvels of electrical engineering worldwide would be rendered useless, and convince the next 196,884 of its colleagues to follow suit thereafter, and again just after, and again. It was order pretending to break apart—an overture to something vast and unseen.

The final descent came with the inward fold that Bach demanded. He let it settle exactly where it wished to land.

Resonance accrued—in the wood, in his ribs, in the air itself. As the Sarabande ended, something began to bloom, compelling David forward into open pages free of written notes. He dared not stop. He could not.

The vibration remaining in the strings climbed through his wrist and into his spine, spreading heat along the column of his back. His sorrow did not subside with resolution. It widened—finding architecture in what he had just played the way water finds the shape of the vessel that holds it. Years of frustration rose through his chest: failed proofs, abandoned models, the quiet humiliations he had folded into competence. And beneath all of it, underpinning all—the deeply entrenched buzz of the last weeks and months.

He could feel the shift in his own skin. The sweat gathering along his collarbone. The rosin dust on his fingertips growing tacky with the heat his body was producing.

He was, in every real sense, exuding his own field. Heart, mind, sorrow, and the last tattered edge of sanity—braided into a single low hum.

His grief had found its carrier.

The storm outside had found its mark. Frequency inscribed itself into every frame the camera caught—each pulse of hand and heart slipping into alignment with the rising swell of the heliosphere. The vessel had been tempered and, in playing, was being tuned. David had never thought to question the direction of the performance. Never stopped to ask which between them was instrument and which was player.

He could not know what next would come—he had no way to see it nor understand from whence it came. But beneath the cello’s grain pressed against his chest, a second pulse began to insist itself. Density turning volatile. Shame stirring into momentum. His grief was nearing a threshold, ready to break loose. All of it—the weight of his father’s death, the woman he couldn’t hold, the slow unraveling of every human thread he’d ignored—had gathered into something altogether and newly palpable.

III. Invitations of ill
Agitato con abbandono

David gripped the bow, his fist clenched tight around the frog as if it were a weapon, and wrenched it across the strings. He didn't bother to find a note, to settle his fingers on any particular spot along the fingerboard. His left hand was a fist, his wrist collapsed, his vibrato—if it could even be called that—was a frantic, compulsive tremor born of tension, not expression. He didn't care if the horsehair scraped or bit into the gut, if the resulting crunch was pure scratch tone, or if his bow arm abandoned all pretense of a controlled stroke. He only knew, in that moment, that whatever still lingered inside him had to get out. Now.

The sound that erupted was thunder and lightning commingled, inevitability interlocked with astonishment—all of it the aftershock of the Sarabande's weight. He was caught on the boundary where absence sharpens desire, a resonance of everything he could not lay claim to and everything he craved regardless, cycling through him with shattering clarity.

The notes erupted free of all plan and care: trumpeted peril-blasts of unadorned dread—fist for knuckles in this vibrant wake. If only he… had… a flourishing shade beam… fading. Pressing now. Forward… faster, knotting phrases together with precision such that each pitch hammered clean to splinter, to chard and then into the next. A manic lucidity he had never dreamed to permit himself was now on him, through him.

Willow-flows and... Lightning. Someone laughing… fragile in tense leaves… Gone.

The instrument pressed against his chest, feral and awakening, its body shuddering and alive—vibrating a fraction from his sternum, as if his heart had split and doubled, each chamber filling with sound.

From the ground about his feet the chalk dust stirred, drawn into minute vortices that tracked the lowest frequencies and collapsed again before he could name them.

The bass line rose from somewhere older than intention, a harmonic ground he had denied for years but whose intervals still lived in tendon and ligature, in the musculature of forearm and inclination of wrist. Fifths shaped for him on an evening he could still taste if he allowed himself. Tuned by hands coursing through air, sculpting ratios into objects his body could inhabit rather than calculate.

Above such grounding, a voice entirely his own rose out at last—into the charged air between his ribs and the wood. A cry carrying the rough grain of years of silence and self-betrayal. The fierce, uneven honesty of himself laid bare, cutting clean through the stale clamor of old evasions and careful lies.

Joy and fury braided together. He could feel both in his forearms—heat and hunger wiring through tendon and muscle. They rose beneath the skin, the horsehair biting into the string. Discovery had burned clear through failure and left behind something cleaner than ambition.

Motion no longer belonged to him alone. The room tilted, its walls breathing in time with his pulse, the blue light pressing harder against his closed lids until the darkness behind them became a shifting field of indigo. Sweat traced from his temple to the hinge of his jaw as the boundary between himself and the instrument dissolved into a question that only the body—shaking, resolute—could ever answer.

With reckless clarity, the line streamed forward—exacting and ecstatic at once—and when the last stroke burned through bow hair and string and out into the air, it refused extinction. His hands trembled; his chest heaved. Silence arrived: charged, unstable, dense with what had passed through him.

David lifted the bow from the string, and in that moment of intended return to stillness, a second, rogue wave of magnetic shear struck his fixed point on Earth.

His world tumbled out of the heavens.

IV. The Reckoning
Fortissimo subito - Poi morendo

It was, indeed, an unfortunate hour to be speaking of coherence.

The flash struck—a beam of photons rifting into slivers that split the sequence of now into a before and after never again to be reconciled.

David was flare-shocked into this new now by the sequenced explosion of every transformer within five hundred miles. The blinding body blow hit him blunt and bright—a photon-flashing fist to the head. His weight shifted backward, legs kicking forward trying to counter the hit.

As gravity rewrote its grammar, the chair beneath him misread the new syntax and buckled. His flailing legs caught the cello—a cascading terror that ripped the instrument from the grip of his hand and the floor both. It rotated upright as though refusing surrender, hanging for one impossible beat in the air as pure vector, spinning like a top—rotation after rotation—before descent resumed and carried it forward, barreling toward the spiral staircase.

The phone burst from its mount with a sound like a small bone breaking, screen spalling, circuitry blown outward across the glass treads.

David struck the tile. Five fingertips pried open and alive with current, the evidence of transmission written in blood across his palm.

For a long moment, he lay there unable to fully process what had just happened. Had he done this? Had this been nothing but a strange fall off the back of a stool? Surely not. And then his finger screaming at the violated bundles of nerves—pulsing. Pounding. This was more than a clumsiness.

David… had just been observed.

The sirens brought him back.

Not all at once. First the sound—a distant braided wail threading through the walls. Then the light, gone wrong, tinged in emergency amber. David opened his eyes. The instant taste of carbonate and gypsum on his lips. The ceiling the wrong distance. The walls closer, ill-formed.

He tried to sit. The room tilted, steadied, tilted again. Then, using the kitchen island as brace and surety, he pulled himself vertical—knees negotiating a temporary ceasefire with the floor.

Through the window, the sky pulsed in staggered orange. A city discovering how much of its life ran on sixty hertz.

David processed none of this.

His scattered gaze fell to the floor—the center of the room—and what he found there stopped him the way brutality stops you in the dark, before comprehension, before language. His throat released a categorically unknowable sound. Shock and pain and impending, unwanted solitude made audible.

There before him lay the cello, splayed out on its side. Bridge shattered, neck torqued at an impossible angle, the body split along its seam near the lower bout. He was looking at the gutted insides of his mother's hope, his father's instruction, and source of pride, the loving details of shape and curve, of the science and artistry of his grandfather’s design—the instrument of all their love translated into maple and spruce, now opened to the room like a body on a table.

He could not bear to look at it for long.

The phone lay near the staircase, screen a spiderweb of fractures. Beneath the damage, the display burned with single-minded purpose. A progress bar creeping forward in increments so small they might have been imagined.

PROCESSING…

The device was running hot—the warmth radiating with the feverish insistence of a body fighting to complete a task it was never built to perform.

David crossed the room in a controlled stumble. He knelt. His good hand closed around the phone, and the heat surprised him—like holding a small animal with a racing heart. He tucked it against his chest and made for the couch. Eight steps. He fell into it all at once, cushions receiving him with a sound like a held breath released.

The sirens thinned, or he did.

Sleep approached from all sides, poised to reclaim this body of work and injury. The distance between David and the surface of the world was widening—layer by layer, with the authority of deep water. His right arm hung limp off the edge of the couch, the phone balanced in his loosening grip, screen still pulsing against his palm.

PROCESSING…

The progress bar inched forward.

Willows. The particular quality of afternoon light through leaves. A laugh he had not heard in thirty years and cannot reconstruct except as warmth—as safety—as the feeling of a world that had not yet asked anything of him. His grandfather's hands. The equations those hands had carried. What those equations had become. What he had spent his life trying to answer.

PROCESSING…

Then into the solemn quiet of one desperate and lonely room… a sound intruded. The first, barely audible. The counterweight cable, frayed through years of chalk dust and impatience, finding its frequency at last. A single filament parting. Then another. The high, reedy complaint of metal at its uttermost limit.

Tick-tick-tick-tick… TINK.

The cable gave.

The freed end whipsawed through the dark—a metallic shriek crossing the full width of the room—and caught the glass spiral staircase along its arc. Treads exploding outward in a cascade of prismatic detonation, each pane shattering into a spectrum. Light fracturing across every surface of the apartment in a single savage bloom—violet to red, red to violet, the full signature of the sun rendered in broken glass and emergency amber and the last pulse of the blue field, all of it crossing the room in the fraction of a second before the real weight arrived.

The phone, the shattered screen in David’s hand, unaware of the danger above… changed.

UPLOAD?

CONFIRM | DENY

His thumb rested on the glass. There was no decision. Not really. Only the reflex, tuned by a decade's repetition—swipe, tap, yes, accept. The faint shift of light caught in his skin and translated upward, past cognition.

UPLOAD CONFIRMED…

The upload climbed through the fractured network, packet by packet, finding seams in the overloaded infrastructure, slipping through gaps like water through a broken wall. Forty-seven minutes, compressed and encrypted, sent outward into a grid that was, even now, failing in every direction.

The resonance of all that had transpired in this fated night now took up the baton and stood maestro ready. A signal of air, of momentum’s gate, and then: The Downbeat.

The blackboard wall, now free of the restraints of cable and counterweight, uprooted from its channels.

One nanosecond of impossible suspension—the entire two-story mass hanging, the equations still legible, years of labor luminous in the fractured light, every proof and hypothesis and derived ratio blazing in the instant before—

Impact.

The tsunami rolled outward. Forty years of chalk released at once—a white detonation expanding in concentric rings through the prismatic haze, refracting the light further, folding the spectrum into the particulate, until the entire apartment became something that had no name in physics or music or any language David had ever studied. An iridescent fog. A room made of suspended knowledge, of light broken into every frequency it contained. Forty years of a man's life, of his searching, now breaking against all the room could offer—identity, knowledge, ego even—held briefly, expanding, morphing in mid-collapse as oxygen battled chalk and gypsum into submission and slowly, gently, let them settle upon the surfaces below.

The chalk and the spectrum hung in the air together—equations still faintly tracing themselves in the suspended particulate, dissolving as they descended, the terms separating, the relationships coming apart. The Bekenstein bound—the limit of the knowable—sank back through the layers toward the floor, toward the threshold below which nothing could be said.

Gone.

His arm hung over at the edge of the couch. Blood on the forearm, already tracked to the wrist. The hand open. The fingertips extended. The phone resting in their cradle—screen dark, upload complete, the heat of it faded. The small animal stilled.

Chalk settled over everything. Over the blood and the broken glass and the fine shimmer the event had left behind. Over the room. Over the man.

The phone then slid towards the last centimeter’s grasp, reaching the cradle of his fingertips—the same fingertips that had drawn the Sarabande out of silence—and for a fraction of an earth-bound second, the device did not fall. No…

The device… passed… through.

His hand, for an instant so brief it would never register on any conscious record, fell out of phase. The phone occupied the space where his fingers were and encountered no resistance.

Then the moment collapsed. The phone struck the floor with a sharp, decisive crack—the sound of something finished.

David lay there, still. Silent. Air pulled laboriously to his lungs. Less now. And less, even still. His face, where the chalk had found it, was peaceful upon the cushioned couch beneath. The lines had let go. One hand curled softly around the residue of music. The other palm open, upturned—blood and chalk and light.

The sirens stopped.

Chalk and dust and air now resting. The room was done.

Darkened.

Black.

Outside, in every direction, an electricity-starved and huddling planet, newly unburdened, scattered, and data-free, was about to open its eyes and see.
