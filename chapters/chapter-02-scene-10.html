Allegro dissonante
| Breakdown |

As David shut the door to the Feynman Hall, he stopped, turned back, and quietly flipped the lock behind him. At precisely the same moment that the last student took their seat, the hall’s fluorescent tubes began to pulse flicker at 119.8 Hz. It was a frequency just fast enough to render thought unstable. He scanned the room’s faces. Student eyes oscillated like fluid in lab glass. Their attention jittered—quantized into meaningless spikes by incoming notifications and unspoken dread. He moved to the boards, the dreaded, accusatory boards.

The chalk struck rail with such force it disintegrated—releasing a C-sharp into the air’s particulate memory. David didn’t notice, but his sleeve wore the residue like an armband.

“Consider,” he rasped, his elbow grinding into the tray’s sediment of forgotten theorems, “the similarity between Feynman diagrams and Baroque ornamentation.”

Thirty-seven students blinked asynchronously.

From Row Five:

“But doesn’t Gödel’s incompleteness theorem contradict that?”

David spun mid-equation. “Only if you assume orthodoxy defines possibility.”

The chalk snapped before the pain arrived. Carbonate dust arced across the floor, followed by a sharp hiss from the radiator—as if the infrastructure had joined the act of disapproval.

Someone’s smartwatch chirped a minor third. Without thinking, he responded—adjusted the tertiary term of the harmonic projection on the board to compensate.

And the hum—the buzz saw. It was back, oscillating now. Octavating from 68.48 Hz. up to its immediate double and returning to the bass tone—low, wide, glacial. It drained the room of edge and stored David inside its atmosphere. Vents, fluorescents, electronics synced beneath his voice, a sub-harmonic thick enough to erode meaning.

He paused. The equations on the board weren’t wrong. They were just… rearranged. Terms inverted. Notation drifting.

He blinked, and could swear that the board blinked right back.

“You’re not teaching,” said a voice. Maybe out loud.

He looked down. His cuff was bleeding chalk.

The room slowed. Like time buffering—memory caching failure, clearing, reloading—over and over again.

Someone coughed in 3/4 time. The fluorescent lights pulsed to match it. Metered recursion. Resident order.

He knew this rhythm. He was this rhythm.
