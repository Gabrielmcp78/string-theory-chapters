I. Street Smarts | November 28, 2026
Allegretto irreale

The queue inside Genoa’s shimmered in odd little micro-pauses, as though it wanted to move but could remember only how to stop. David fell into place, relying on the old rhythm: left foot, right foot, a shift of weight. Cold leaked in along his spine.

Two customers ahead stood a young man in a brown Carhartt jacket. Broad shoulders. Chin tucked. Defensive spine. Thornden. David felt the faint, reflexive lift—teacher recognizing student in the wild, proof that years had accumulated somewhere, that time had a vector. The same severe haircut. The same left ear angled out just enough to project stubbornness.

Thornden was staring at a point in midair roughly three feet in front of his chest. Nothing occupied it. He didn’t blink.

David shifted forward half a step. “Mr. Thornden?”

No movement.

“Intro to Electrodynamics. Two years ago. You rewired the lab supply backward and nearly took out the building.”

His jaw stayed set, and in the cold, his breath should have fogged—but it didn’t. That registered. The fluorescent lights pulsed, and for a moment, Thornden’s outline wavered. Just slightly. David blinked hard, but the outline held.

When the cashier called “next,” the woman in front of Thornden moved forward. Thornden did not.

“Thornden?”

A hand touched his shoulder. Firm. Real.

“Hey.”

David turned. Hamid stood behind him—thick wool cap, half-smile permanently etched into the left side of his face, the beer case balanced against his hip.

“You good, man?” Hamid asked quietly. “You’re just talking to, like… nobody.”

When David turned back, the conveyor belt was already advancing a loaf of rye through the space where the young man had been.

The cashier slid a single clementine across the counter. It rolled once and stopped against the plastic divider. “Someone left that,” she said. “Yours?”

David looked down at his basket. The netted bag of clementines sat intact. He counted them again. Seven.

The single fruit on the counter gleamed. Its skin was darker than the others, bruised blue at the stem. He reached out. The cold bit his fingertips. He picked it up and for a moment had the disorienting sense that it pulsed faintly against his palm.

He closed his hand around it and stepped forward. As the register resumed its steady beep, the fluorescent light above him softened back to normal. He paid, nodded to the cashier, and headed toward the exit.

Outside, the cold was different. Less surgical. More honest. As he stepped onto the sidewalk. Something struck the toe of his shoe.

He stumbled and looked down. A clementine rolled past him along the curb, wobbling once before steadying toward the gutter. He looked back at the doors of Genoa’s Market. No one.

David turned in time to watch the fruit reach the lip of the storm drain and stop. It didn’t fall in. He approached it and nudged it with his foot. But the small orange did not respond in kind. It did not roll forward. In fact, it rolled in opposition, back toward him in a slow arc and came to rest against his shoe.

He bent, picked it up, and straightened. When his eyes refocused into the middle distance, everything that regained hard edges felt fractionally misplaced. A receipt fluttered near the entrance, then froze—pinned to the asphalt by nothing visible. Traffic lights at the intersection cycled obediently from green to yellow to red. Nothing moved.
