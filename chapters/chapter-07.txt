I.  Preliminary Observations

[Moderato con scoperta crescente]

If any sunlight survived the city’s particulate haze, it entered the study by way of refracted conspiracy, filtered through three competing windowpanes and a sash that hadn’t seen weatherstripping since the Eisenhower administration. The glass, occluded by yellowing NASA flux graphs and annotated calendars, cast a jaundiced pall across the tabletop and the man hunched above it.

His cheek, still marked by the fading glyph of the assault, had shifted from deep aubergine to a convalescent gray, the kind of battle scar no scientist expects to encounter in the mirror. Yet David had grown accustomed to its shadowed presence on his face. He saw it as a marker now, a talisman, a weapon he wasn’t afraid to wield.

The table was a topography of obsession. Manuscript folios, birth registers, death rolls mingled with solar ephemerides and a monograph on “The Waxing and Waning of Geniuses, 1640–1911.” At the edge, a row of index cards advanced, each marked with a birthdate and location in his compressed hand. Near the lamp, a dark, prism-edged shard sat beside a pen, swallowing the light that touched it.

He paused, pen mid-notation, and regarded the cards with skeptical intimacy. The line of names halted at a space cleared for one object: the tuning fork. Gifted, or inflicted, by the old woman of indeterminate provenance. Its metallic sheen looked clinical against the otherwise paper-soft room. He’d measured its mass, mapped its decay on a battered oscilloscope, even attempted to analyze its alloy by licking it and calibrating the aftertaste. It defied classification. What gnawed at him most was the phenomenon, observed more than once and subtle enough to question, where the fork vibrated in response to certain equations or dates.

He stared at the next card, Leibniz, 1646, Leipzig, and then at the solar chart pinned above the lamp. At first: nothing. As he moved the card rightward, aligning its date with a Schwabe cycle peak scrawled in Sharpie, the fork pulsed, an awareness that ran the length of his radius and ulna.

His breathing shortened. He glanced at the lower row: Mozart, Euler, Faraday, all realigned to match the solar maxima from 1610 onward. Their births clung to the cycle’s crests with an intentionality he could no longer dismiss.

Then Newton. That card he had set apart, half defiant, half reverent. Newton’s birth occurred in the trough of the Maunder Minimum, the long silence. A life bracketed by a quiet star.

The pattern surfaced and sharpened around the single exception. Every visionary in his visions, each ghostly instructor or hallucinated nemesis, clustered at solar flare except the one who had spoken from the hush. External and internal drive. Flare and famine. Two solutions to the same equation.

His fingers trembled. One card slipped, spun to the floor. He reached, missed, steadied himself on the fork. The tines touched his skin and resumed their vibration, calm, absolute.

He reassembled the sequence as a spiral. Newton he placed at the center trough, a stone in a drained riverbed. His breath came uneven, the kind that precedes either panic or discovery.

A memory flashed: the old woman, eyes like a clinical thermometer, saying that all language was wrong, that he was translating what couldn’t be said. Another voice, Midlands vowels scraped by sleeplessness: The laws are only the score. You must learn to hear the music.

He stood, limbs tingling with hypoxic delight, and crossed to the blackboard. The surface was a palimpsest: music staves without notes, tensor ghosts, abandoned proofs. He grabbed chalk with his steadier hand and began again.

First: the harmonic series, written with a calligrapher’s care. Then, below: the solar cycles, a step function, peaks and troughs. To the right, the birth years of his cohort, each plotted against flare. Newton’s lifespan stretched below as a quiet bar.

Patterns surfaced like watermark, just visible and unmistakable. He paused often to rest the fork against the board, as though it might draw power from lectures past or confer legitimacy.

When he finished, his hands were glazed with chalk and sweat, the fork warm as blood. Across the room, the cello waited.

He stepped back and surveyed the triptych: resonance math, solar epochs, human genius. The chalked arcs converged on flare and spanned silence. The sun occupied the center of it. Prime mover. Metronome of the mind’s great debut and its quiet intermissions.

He dropped into the battered chair. For a long moment he only stared, letting the pattern sink into cortex.

At the desk, the spiral held. The fork rested at the center. David watched as evening light, now crimson and low, threaded through the triple-paned glass.

It struck the fork’s tines at the critical angle. Light flared through the room and the dark shard beside it received the beam.

The light entered the shard and emerged altered, white sheared into spectral bloom that bled across the cards. Looking through it, David could imagine the city’s orange haze stripped away, revealing a structured magenta lattice stretching across the horizon. The geometry hung over rooftops and smokestacks with the quiet authority of a score waiting for its musicians. Familiar forms resolved into patterns that felt intimate and immense, descending from the star itself. It was as though the shard persuaded the world to disclose itself differently.

For David, the bruise, absorbed by the pattern, had become the first node of a biological alignment.





II.  Method and Resonance


Afternoon drew the room into focus by way of subtraction. The outside world receded, replaced by a stillness so complete the drift of chalk dust rasped against the glass.
 Lang stood in the room's geometrical center, shirt untucked, cuffs stippled white, and regarded the blackboard like a criminal about to confess. A latticework had emerged overnight: dates, cosmic angles, irrational numbers repeating in motif, musical notation woven between set theory and prime factorization.
 The alignment was too precise to dismiss. Solar cycle maxima coinciding with birth years of history's titans, a pattern carrying the familiar irritation of arriving late to something that had apparently been happening for centuries. Even the blank spaces participated. Places where chalk refused to cohere resolved into rhythm held lightly enough that the next breath might dissolve it.
 He paced with a quartet's inner pulse counting beneath the movement. Every fourth stride returned him to the cello in the corner.
 For days he had been content to let mathematics perform music's duties. The arrangement had seemed elegant enough until he looked at the instrument again and felt the deception collapse. A ventriloquist could be impressive right up until you remembered what the act actually required. Then all the mystery drained away and you were left staring at a grown man with his hand up a puppet's ass.
 The cello waited where he had left it.
 Enough of that.
 When he finally sat, the instrument settled against his sternum with the cold familiarity of something neglected but not abandoned. A slight adjustment at the spike, a turn at each peg, and the strings drifted back into agreement beneath his hand. Nearby, the tuning fork occupied its usual place on the desk, radiating the confidence of an object that had never once felt obliged to explain itself.
 The open A entered cleanly beneath the bow, its decay quivering against the harmonic before he shifted into third position and stopped the D string at F-sharp.
 The interval changed the room.
 Air gathered between f-holes and chalkboard, thickening toward a standing wave until the fork broke into a microscopic orbit and began singing a phantom overtone two octaves above the note.
 Another explanation quietly left the building.
 He leaned harder into the bow.
 Sound struck shelves, books, equations, and returned altered. For a moment the room locked into phase. Chalk dust hung suspended in amber, arranging itself into fleeting Chladni geometries before gravity reclaimed jurisdiction.
 The resonance climbed through neck and nut, entered his bones, and found the bruise in his jaw with enough certainty to suggest a  prior acquaintance.
 Wood. Flesh. Waveform; the distinctions became negotiable.
 Something in the frequency felt familiar. The bruise, apparently, had joined the experiment.
 For every three heartbeats, it answered twice, locking him into a fractioned three-over-two syntonic ratio with the room itself.
 When the bow left the string, silence arrived compressed rather than empty. The after-sound lingered in his skull until it ceased to resemble music and approached something older. Through the dark shard on the desk, the magenta lattice beyond the window seemed to tighten and clarify, its geometry gathering itself into relations he felt rather than understood.
 For just an instant, he knew how small he had once been.
 "It was always this," he whispered.
 The thought followed him back to the board.
 The formula lingered just beyond articulation. The notations crowded the margins with the infuriating composure of a solution that had mistaken his participation for a courtesy.
 He uncapped a blue grease pencil and began adding names: Mozart. Einstein. Others from the midnight symposium. Each plotted against solar maxima. Each anchored to flare. Then his pencil stopped. There remained one date he had somehow neglected to plot. The omission seemed almost noble until he realized it was his own.
 David stared at the spread for a moment. Days spent arranging the births of geniuses, visionaries, and assorted historical inconveniences across four centuries, and only now had it occurred to him that he, too, possessed a birthday.
 He grabbed an empty card. His eyes moved across the spread, making certain he preserved the same coding, the same notation, the same standards he had applied to everyone else. Satisfied that the procedure was at least technically defensible, he grudgingly wrote his name across the top.
 The card looked immediately less respectable.
 David Lang.
 There. The contamination had occurred. He copied the date, aligned it against the cycle, and sat back.
 SOLAR MAXIMUM
 For several moments he stared at it with mounting irritation. The result possessed a certain vulgarity. Ordinarily this would have been the point at which a responsible scientist reminded himself that human beings are remarkably gifted at manufacturing evidence in support of their preferred conclusions. David had delivered that lecture often enough to graduate students, colleagues, funding committees, and on one memorable occasion, a dean.
 The difficulty was that the argument cut both ways. How convenient to discover a passion for objectivity the moment the data started implicating him. And who, exactly, was he to dismiss it? The same institutions that had spent decades paying him, promoting him, publishing him, and handing him increasingly expensive opportunities to continue being himself had apparently concluded he belonged in the conversation. Was he now expected to develop a principled objection?
 The thought struck him as equal parts absurd and alarming.
 Because stripping himself from the dataset could be interpreted as rigor. It could also be interpreted as cowardice. Scientific caution and self-protection had become difficult to distinguish.
 David stared at the card.
 The universe, if it had an opinion on the matter, appeared to have filed it already.
 When he returned to the larger pattern, the dates resolved like a fugue's final cadence, an act of cosmic superiority that made clear one certifiably begrudging truth: the universe kept infinitely better notes than he did.
 Across the page the pattern gathered into a single arc. Peaks, intervals, eruptions of genius rising from background noise until the whole curve began to resemble a sentence written in a grammar older than language and every bit as indifferent to being understood.
 Chalk dust slipped from his fingertips as certainty arrived in the only form he trusted: incomplete, argumentative, and sufficient to proceed.
 When he raised the chalk, the phrase arrived with the uncomfortable authority of something discovered rather than invented.
 SOLAR MAXIMUS HYPOTHESIS
 He underlined it twice and stood watching the dust settle through the late afternoon light.














III.  Findings and Implications


Twilight bled into the study by slow degrees, its silence broken only by the scratch of graphite and the whisper of turning pages. Lang hadn’t bothered with the overheads. The only light came from the lamp clipped to the bookshelf, its bulb burning with a sickly yellow that cast his hand in monstrous silhouette across the scattered paper and cards.

He worked with a patience that bordered on ritual. Each card was reviewed, cross-checked, annotated, then slotted into sequence on the legal pad. Graphite and paper steadily erased distinctions he had once considered important. Physics, music, memory. Variables became voices. Patterns became phrases.

The tuning fork sat where he’d left it, at the center of the cards. Still warm, he thought. Or perhaps that was him. Occasionally his eyes drifted toward the cello in the corner and recalled the sensation of the open A, the fifth above it, the overtone that seemed to linger after the room itself had finished listening.

Next came something different. Not a person. An object.

THE MESSIAH. 1716. CREMONA, ITALY.

The violin to end all violins. His pencil hesitated. The thing occupied an awkward position in the theory. There was no birth to plot, no cortex to map, no human life for the sun to impress itself upon. Yet rumor clung to it heavier than names. Every description of its voice seemed to drift toward the language of revelation.

Antonio Stradivari. Born 1644, separated from Newton by only two years. The Messiah emerged in 1716, the final year of the Maunder Minimum, as the long solar famine began to loosen its grip. His pulse lurched. The answer lay deeper than craft. The wood itself carried the argument. Stradivari had shaped it, certainly. Yet the voice that survived three centuries seemed rooted in something older than the maker’s hand. Alpine spruce. Trees grown beneath a diminished sun. Ring laid against ring with a discipline later generations could never quite reproduce. Growth slowed by deprivation until the material itself became something unusual.

Newton had lived within the hush. Stradivari had lived there too. One carried something out of it in thought. The other carried something out in wood. The realization settled through him with unnerving ease. The sun did more than mark genius. It shaped conditions. It disciplined growth. Flesh, mind, timber. Entire systems drawn through the same long interval of deprivation. A transduction of hunger into voice. He remained with the thought long enough for it to stop feeling speculative. By the time he looked up, confidence had already taken root.

The room had darkened around him. Only a thin smear of horizon light remained beyond the window. He stretched, vertebrae popping one by one, and turned toward the glass above the radiator. A handful of stars had begun to emerge through the city’s haze. Beyond them, beyond sight itself, the cycle had already started its next ascent. Behind him the blackboard had faded into shadow, but the pattern remained. He could still see it ghosted across his vision. Curves. Peaks. Silences. Connections.

Then came the hum. Quiet enough he almost missed it. It gathered in the room the way warmth gathered near old pipes. Through the floor. Through the soles of his feet. Into his teeth. For a moment he mistook it for the building. Then recognition arrived. The rhythm was familiar because it was already in his mouth. He had been making it for years. Under his breath while working. While walking. During long stretches of concentration when thought accelerated beyond comfort and the world retreated to a manageable distance.

His grandfather used to do the same thing. David could see him clearly. Standing at the sink at dusk. Sleeves rolled. Fingers tapping a counter in time with something nobody else appeared capable of hearing. The melody followed the same path it always had. Dusk. Solitude. The mind outrunning itself. For years David had treated the habit as inheritance. Now the distinction between inheritance and transmission seemed less certain. The tune continued beneath his breath. Waiting there. As though it had been waiting there all along.

Outside, darkness gathered across the city. Inside, another boundary seemed to soften. The cards on the desk. The fork. The blackboard. The remembered melody. Newton beneath a silent star. Stradivari shaping wood born of the same silence. Threads that should have remained separate began drawing toward one another with the effortless confidence of things returning home. The hum persisted. David let it. And in the deepening dark he felt the larger field he had been circling for months. Close as breath. Patient as gravity. Vast enough to make every previous explanation feel provisional. For the first time, he stopped resisting the sensation. He let it take him in.







IV. Lossless Transfers


He had all three boards open. The hum still lived at the back of his teeth. The black panels stood floor to ceiling, chalked over, wiped down, chalked again. The center board was crowded with ratios and substitutions, the white marks driven hard enough into slate to bruise it. He had gone at the same transition from six directions. Each had collapsed at the identical seam.

The current line held nearly the right shape. Nearly. The body frequency averaged, doubled, borne upward into a relation that wanted to lock and refused.

He wrote through the last bracket. For one thin instant the structure gathered, held. Then at the threshold: collapse.

The joints of his hands pulled inward, cramping. The hands knew the man they were attached to must stop. Must break. Must breathe.

David dragged the side of his hand across the board and left a white wound. Chalk dust rose off the ruined line and hung there, then began to settle. Failed thought returning to the air as grit. The room thickened as it fell.

From above him, from the grated bridge crossing over the chalkboards, an arid voice joined the dust.

"If you are a scientist, you cannot stop such a thing."

The chalk dropped from his fingers and struck the floor.

He looked up. The catwalk's dark grid hung in higher darkness, a deeper shadow amongst shadows. The voice had come from directly above him, hit brick and glass and metal, and returned altered. By the time the sentence reached him it seemed to be behind him too.

"If you are a scientist," it went on, "you believe that it is good to find out how the world works… it is good to turn over to mankind at large the greatest possible power to control the world and to deal with it according to its lights and its values."

He knew the words, thin audio from an old reel Elliot kept like a relic, the syllables worn flat by rewinds. The terror lay in how well they fit the room. They landed as if the apartment had been waiting years to say them.

David stepped back from the boards and turned. Below the loft line the apartment held itself in strata: the low red of the fire, the kitchen dark under the sleeping loft, the long window holding a thin wash of city light, the spiral stair rising between levels like a black coil of wire.

Nothing moved.

"No," he said.

The voice came down through the grating again, patient and merciless. "It is not possible to be a scientist unless you believe that the knowledge of the world, and the power which this gives, is a thing which is of intrinsic value to humanity, and that you are using it to help in the spread of knowledge and are willing to take the consequences."

"No!" He screamed, his voice revving upward toward something guttural, as he hurtled toward the spiral staircase. One hand pressed the rail as he climbed.

Halfway up he looked across at the bridge over the boards. Empty. Yet David could hear from nowhere and everywhere a presence breathing down his neck, pulsing through his body and mind.

At the top landing he lunged right, then right again, reached the chalk wall and burst out onto the catwalk. The grating gave a hollow complaint under his weight. Directly beneath him the boards rose like a black altar slashed with white. The fireplace sat centered and true in the brick wall beyond.

"Where are you?" he screamed.

Instantly the energy of his anger resounded back at him from the room. The balcony door slammed wide open. Cold struck him so hard it emptied his lungs. He turned and bolted toward the open glass door, chasing what he could neither see nor name yet knew kept pace with him in the space.

As he pushed through into the late November night, the Ithaca sky opened overhead. He ran to the far railing, slamming into it to look down over the streets below, as if chasing a thief or something wild in retreat.

East Hill rose before him in tiers of scattered light. Rooflines fell off into dark. The ravine held its own deeper blackness.

David looked up, breath heavy. He stood there, eyes narrowing against the great moon's glare, willing the night sky to supply answers.

The moon hung silent before him, almost full, lucid and remote. So bright it seemed to have authored itself. Then the old fact came to him plain: all that light was borrowed. The moon served as emissary, stand-in, a filter for the great star. Her Majesty the Sun lay out of sight, hidden below the world, impossibly distant, and still its reach governed what could be seen.

David watched as a whispered cloud passed over the lunar glow. Gathered vapor, water in air. At once the whole visible city softened and lost its edges. Roofs dulled, brick dimmed. The deck rail under his hand altered its relation to the dark. Force continued. It had shifted, diffused, redistributed.

As the cloud passed, lunar white resumed its dominant cast over everything he could see.

David stayed in the cold a moment longer, breathing through the sting in his chest. Then, from somewhere deeper than the sting, he felt it: a pull to return. A new something to confront in this night of voices.

He stepped back inside onto the catwalk high above the main room and pulled the balcony door shut. As the door sealed behind him, he knew the room below had altered. The arrangement of things remained, but their charge had shifted. Perhaps the fire had lifted a little. Perhaps a page on the coffee table had moved. The apartment no longer felt unoccupied. It felt taken.

He moved slowly to his left and leaned over the railing atop the stairs. At once, even through distance and the haze of dark, he understood: the occupancy of the room had fully altered. Deep in the leathered core of the old seat by the hearth, a presence was newly seated. A density of shadow and shape that the eye could not quite close on. The fire had burned low enough that the form was more suggestion than fact, a dark that held when the rest of the room moved.

Then a figure emerged. The body made its mistake before the mind could intervene: the broadness through the chest, the angle of the head, one arm resting the way Arthur used to let it fall when he sat up too late with a part book open across his knees, pencil tucked behind his ear.

“Dad?"

The word came out of him before he knew he was speaking. Relief and dread and the raw, unthinking hope of a man who had been calling into absence for so long he no longer expected an answer. The word went out into the room and landed somewhere, on someone.

The figure lifted its head.

Firelight caught the face that turned up toward the catwalk and for one staggered beat David’s body insisted: Arthur. Then the details refused—too much time in the bone, too much history at the mouth—and the resemblance reformed in a different register, wrong in age but right in set: the same stubborn line in the neck, the same refusal in the eyes. Misfired chord.

"Son," the old man said. The word simply left him, the way air leaves a room when a door opens.

David's hand tightened on the rail. He did not correct it. Could not. The word had landed in his chest and was still ringing there, a note he had never been given and did not know how to hold.

The distance held them apart. Twelve feet of air between the catwalk and the chair. And in that distance Arthur was alive. As interval. The way certain frequencies exist only in the space between two sounding bodies and vanish when they meet. Arthur lived in the gap as a property of the distance itself: the son the old man had lost, the father David had never been able to reach, present now as the charged air between two men who had both been reaching for him in different directions for different reasons their whole lives.

David came down the stairs. The spiral turned him slightly away from the fire and then back toward it with each step, the iron rail cold under his palm. Each time the light found his face again, something in the angle changed. The descent became a kind of flash reset: a different Arthur flickering into being in the space between them with every step, a life the old man had not lived to see arriving in pieces.

At the fourth step: Arthur at twenty-two, the year Juilliard turned him into a blade.

At the seventh step: Arthur at thirty-one, the year he won second chair and learned what it meant to be brilliant without being first.

At the tenth step: Arthur at forty, the year the violin case stayed shut.

At the next turn: Arthur at fifty-five, the year David left for graduate school, the last time they had stood in the same room without the weight of everything unspoken pressing between them.

The old man in the chair watched each version arrive and dissolve, his mouth moving without sound, his hands gripping the arms of the chair as if the room itself were tilting. He was seeing his son at every age he had missed, every age he had imagined in the long timeless dark.

And David, descending, felt each version pass through him.

The last step brought him to the floor. The boards were cold through his socks. The fire was at his back now, and the light fell full on his face. He was forty-seven. He was only himself.

The old man looked at him. The old man's face, which had been open and reaching and full of a son who was not there, slowly stilled. The door that had opened closed again. Arthur dispersed entirely, half into the face looking up, half into the face looking down, leaving both of them standing in what remained: the complicated fact of each other. The interval collapsed. Arthur was gone from the air between them, completed, the way a standing wave vanishes when the two frequencies that created it finally meet.

"I didn't know if this would work," the old man said. His voice was rough, as if it had not been used in a long time, or as if the use it had known was not the kind that wore on vocal cords. "I'm not certain I understand what this is."

David took a breath. The 68.48 was still there, in the walls, in the floor, in the space behind his sternum. It had become something you could stand on.

"You've been here."

The old man's face stilled. The particular stillness of someone being described in a language without the right nouns. "There is no here from where I am," he said. "And no been. There is only this." He lifted one hand, palm up, at the space between them. "And now you are in it with me."

The room hummed. The boards held their equations. The chalk dust hung in the air. The cello leaned against the wall, untouched. In the silence between them something was tuning.

"You used to hum," David said. "In your office. I'd sit under the desk while you worked the chalk."

The old man said nothing.

"Why didn't you tell me?" The question came out the way Dad had come: unplanned, already in the air.

The old man was quiet. The fire settled. The hum of the room became audible. "Because Arthur left," he said finally. "And I thought if I stayed quiet, maybe you wouldn't have to carry it either. I was wrong."

"It found me anyway."

"Yes."

David stood there, his hands empty at his sides, forty-seven years old, the walls humming at 68.48. He could feel it as something he was finally tuned to receive. "I don't know what to do with it."

The old man looked at him. "You're already doing it," he said. "That's why I'm here."

The old man's gaze did not waver. He blinked once, slow, as if the lids had weight. His hands hung at his sides with the careful stillness of someone used to not touching what might change. In the hearth the coals shifted with a soft collapse, and he did not look at the sound. He looked only at David, holding him in place the way a clamp holds a piece of wood: not kind, not cruel, just sure.

David turned toward the boards. The chalk dust was still on his fingers from earlier. The failed equations ran in layers across the wall, each erasure leaving its ghost in the grain.

The old man rose, effort in the knees and hips, one hand briefly on the chair arm. The motion human enough to make the room close around him. He stood by the fire, the boards behind David still full of failed white weather.

"The trouble with secrecy," he said quietly, "is that it makes one body try to bear what should belong to many."

David's hand came up. His fingers touched the wall where the 68.48 was written, the numbers smudged but legible. "If I let it go," he said, "it could hurt them."

"It will hurt them.” The truth of it landed harder than comfort would have. "So will grief. So will music. So will love, if it is real enough." Eliot’s voice stayed level. "Nothing you do can make it safe."

David's hand dropped from the wall. He turned back to face the old man, his grandfather, present and impossible, standing by the fire. "Then what?”

“You need a form that cannot become a chamber," the old man said.

The sentence entered David before he understood it. The cello in the dark. The fork on the desk. Arthur trying all his life to save him by moving him toward music. Elliot trying all his late life to temper what he had passed by building apparatus around it. Frames, housings, protocols, they all kept power from turning personal. The family mistake that had never truly been about science and music at all, but about what could hold a thing without suffocating it to death.

"A song, David."

The apartment answered in minute distributed sympathies. A wire somewhere above. The steel lip in the kitchen. A frail acceptance in the window glass. Nothing theatrical. Only enough to let him feel that the room had heard.

Then the hum began. Low at first, almost lost under the base tone of the fork that had not yet begun to sound but was already present in the walls. Elliot hummed the lullaby, the same wandering line David had heard before he had words for it, before physics, before grief, before Arthur's arguments had put names to any of the factions in the house. David made one broken sound and then another. Elliot hummed on.

And then the melody broke. It snagged, thinned, lost one overtone and then another, as if something at the edge of the room put a hand into the web of it and tore a hole there. The apartment, which had been answering in small hidden places, went listening-still.

Elliot's face began to change. Age rushed in like ruin. Skin pulled tight across bone. The eyes widened with a fury so concentrated it seemed to burn the air between them. Warning and grief fell away. Rage surged forward, rage stripped of all social use. The indignation of a man who had died inside misunderstanding and now saw the humiliating geometry of it: his own, Arthur's, David's, a long line of men touching mere fragments and calling the contact insight.

An then, with a disembodies scream that threatened to rip ear drums and assault the air, a tortured barely human new face emerged, wiping the visage of this grandfather clean away.

Even through the horror of whatever this newly formed specter had endured, it was clear to David the identity of what stood before him. Dr. Kenji Tsukino.

The tuning fork began to wobble and screech. Un-struck. Sounding. From the table behind David the first tone rose clean and low: 68.48 Hz. He felt it in his sternum before he located it in air. Then came the octave. Then the next. Then again.

Each ascent went through the apartment like a knife finding every seam. The bridge over the boards took it. The loft rail took it. The kitchen steel answered. Window glass trembled. Hidden cavities in brick woke and rang. A spoon in the sink gave one thin, treacherous cry and fell still. The whole place became one resonant instrument and his body, caught inside it, became another chamber to be found and forced open.

David stumbled back, his hand hitting the stair post. The next doubling struck his jaw. The next went in behind his eyes. By the next he could no longer tell whether the sound lived in the room or in the fluid of his own body. His teeth sang. His ribs felt less like bone than tuning surfaces. The pressure climbed cleanly, terribly, no distortion, only more and more exact force entering him.

Tsukino watched him with a contempt so bright it seemed to cauterize the air. Everything David had arranged about himself went down under that look. Caution. Genius. Stewardship. All of it collapsing into a single image: a man with one shard in his hand admiring the cut of his own reflection and calling that admiration knowledge.

Another doubling. He dropped to one knee. Another. The room vanished. Fire, stair, boards, city, gone. Pattern remained. Pain remained.
