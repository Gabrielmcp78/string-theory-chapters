VII. CANTUS

McGrath paused, needing an assurance from his people that the temporal drift had indeed stabilized, then leaned toward the mic. The leaning was slower than time. He was searching, in this moment; reaching toward something to say—anything that might stand a chance at being enough. He knew the look of what was coming, and owed them yet another grim, clear glimpse into the future.

“All right, team. Whatever this is, it’s rewriting the protocols faster than any government can react. But, mark my words… they will most definitely react. Before they clamp down, we have a chance to decide what must survive.”

A brief discussion ensued to ground the criteria, the threshold of preservation.

At Cornell, Celeste wiped chalk dust from her hands and glanced at Rune, his face lit by the phosphorescent equations still crawling across the boards.

“Rune?

“He wanted the world to hear itself,” she said. “If they bury this, it dies in silence.”

“Celeste, we keep it alive—quietly,” McGrath answered. “A frequency this volatile needs a sanctuary, not a stage.”

“A cantus firmus,” Kovács said from Geneva, voice steady now. “The fixed melody that holds the counterpoint together.”

McGrath almost smiled. “A base line for reality.”

Across the room, his assistant called, voice sharp against the static. “It’s the White House again, sir. They want an update immediately. Chief of Staff scheduled an in-person at 07:30 tomorrow in the Oval.”

“Is the package ready?”

“Yes, sir. Waiting on your go.”

“Hold it a moment more.”

He came back to the line. “They’re going to lock this down,” he said quietly. “Every channel, every packet. The line will stay open only a few seconds once the order lands. Until then, send everything—raw data, local logs, uncompressed. When the blackout hits, this is gone.”

Acknowledgments rolled in—Rune from Cornell, Kovács at CERN, Celeste somewhere in the background, calm amid the static. Packets began to pour: neutrino flux, magnetometer harmonics, solar telemetry, code fragments, and the living waveform itself. Transfer meters crawled. Around the world, grids flickered; containment protocols stirred.

“I’ll collate and seal what we have. When the power drops, one node remains off-registry,” McGrath said. “Rune, that node is yours.”

“Marcus,” Rune asked, “what do we call this?”

Through the distortion, Celeste’s voice carried clean as a bell: “Cantus.”

“Project Cantus,” McGrath agreed. “We are tied to one another from this day forward. Few know what David Lang has discovered. It’s our job to hold it tight—to wait for a way to bring this to the world. Call me naïve, but I believe that day will come. Until it does, we are the keepers of this silent flame.”

He turned to his team. “Begin encryption. Off-registry node—Cornell physics, Ishikawa’s credentials. Package all harmonic data under a single tag.”

The name propagated instantly through the channels, appearing as filenames, packet headers, directory roots: CANTUS-01, CANTUS-SIGMA, CANTUS-ARCHIVE. Every scientist on the line felt a small, irrational relief—naming the impossible made it human again.

Kovács spoke one last time. “If they erase us, remember the frequency. Seven-point-eight-three hertz. That’s where the world began to sing.”

Rune added, almost a prayer, “Cantus perpetious.” The everlasting song.

Marcus paused, blinked, and a flash of fire and blood—of youth’s angst and rebellions fury flooded back. When he opened his eyes, his vision returned—blur field through tears.

In the Feynman Lecture hall, Rune Ishikawa had felt something, a flicker in his bones—a knowing, an understanding, a need to reach back—to acknowledge…

“Rune?” Marcus’ voice came over the line, his voice almost imperceptible—unrecognizable to all but Celeste and Rune, who heard in it’s utterance the tones of a young astrophysicist they once knew—long ago.

Rune had already moved to the speaker, seeming to know the moment was now. Celeste’s hand began to quiver. Rune reached out his own to pull her in close.

“Come, Ms. Armitage. Mr. McGrath… I’m here. We are both here.”

For one minute and forty-six seconds, the line sustained a certain silence.

The three held each other across distance in an impossible binding, a deep understanding that what had been said…done… had passed into the beyond. That this quartet, through 20 years of silence, had performed it’s task and lost it’s leader to the execution of it’s very instrumentation.

As the seconds ticked from :47 to :48 Marcus turned to his assistant, dropped his head and simply said, “Send it.”

The update sped toward Washington. He keyed the final command and routed the compressed archive to Cornell’s buried server.

“Sing quietly, my friends,” he said into the dying connection. “Sing until it’s safe.”

One by one, the channels went dark under the NSA seal. The aurora outside his window dimmed, leaving only the faint echo of harmonic vibration in the power lines—a planetary lullaby fading toward silence.

Somewhere deep in Cornell’s server racks, the archive settled into dormancy, wrapped in encryption no algorithm could quite keep from humming.

In the lab—Sub-level Two—a screen alive for fifty-four years, a forty-seven-second continuous cycle of glyph and creation, went dark. A menace of misunderstanding released—by promise, by coherence.

A heartbeat later, the clocks resumed their steady tick, perfectly in sync.

Reality, for now, had retuned itself.
