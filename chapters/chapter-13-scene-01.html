I. THE NETWORK      November 29, 2026
UCLA Keck Observatory | 9:48 A.M. Pacific

Dr. Marcus McGrath moved through his observatory like a conductor orchestrating survival. The electromagnetic pulse had silenced ninety percent of Earth’s scientific infrastructure at 2:47 A.M., but the Keck arrays hummed with impossible vitality—not merely operational, but inexplicably and increasingly enhanced. His standard radio telescopes were detecting signals in frequency ranges they had never been designed to process.

And then this damned video. Nothing like it had ever crossed McGrath’s desk. It had arrived just six minutes after global communications collapse: a holographic recording whose compression algorithms defied conventional understanding—forty-seven minutes of footage occupying impossibly small storage, yet containing mathematical information that was currently rewriting his equipment’s firmware and showed no sign of stopping.

“Tolliver! I need an update on the current operational-institutions list—now!” Marcus shouted, delighted. This was the kind of warpath he had been born for, and every underling in the building knew it, thrilled to be receiving marching orders from the world’s most beloved astrophysicist at this historic moment.

The needle-nosed face of James Tolliver, Marcus’s secondary research assistant, slid into view around the corner. “Got it here, sir.” The second-year grad student scrambled to pull up the current list—a cobbled-together Emergency Scientific Response Network that McGrath and his team had been assembling since dawn.

“CERN is back online,” Tolliver reported. “Fermilab is operational. Caltech’s arrays are functional but, according to Dr. Kilpatrick, they are acting ‘funky.’ ”

“What else is new,” Marcus snorted.

“We’ve also got JPL, Berkeley, MIT, Harvard, and… Cornell.”

“Sir!” another assistant called. “The spectral-analysis software looks like it’s rewriting its own code. In fact—Professor, I’m banking new data right now. Somehow the audio data is spanning eleven-dimensional frequency space!”

McGrath frowned at the implication. “Keep on it, everyone. Today is one of those days we get to throw everything we know out the window and smile all the way through!”

“Professor?” Tolliver pressed, dragging him back to the list.

“Yeah, Tol, what’ve you got?”

“Cornell, sir. What are they doing up and running? Kinda out of their league, aren’t they?”

“Are you kidding? That’s Rune Ishikawa’s department.” Marcus clicked his tongue, then froze. “Wait a minute—Cornell?”

He turned toward the bank of engineers crunching the metadata still pouring off the nodes of this otherworldly file format. “Kozlowski—where’s that metadata analysis? Do we have coordinates on the origin of that recording yet?”

“Coming in now, sir. Sending to your workstation.”

McGrath pulled up the newly unscrambled GPS coordinates and waited for the search algorithm to narrow into the precise location.

| Ithaca, NY. |

“Holy shit. Rune, what have you done to the Finger Lakes?” Marcus took two deep breaths, then barked, “Get me a priority line to Cornell Astrophysics—now!”
