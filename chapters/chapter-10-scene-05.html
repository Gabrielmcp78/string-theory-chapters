V. The Conduction
Presto - Poi adagio - Poi ripresa

Every experiment has an origin, a point where it must begin. David felt the weight of that point like a hand on his shoulder. He had finished every equation. Laid out every instrument. The storm was rising beyond the walls, whether he moved or not. If he lingered, the work would turn to hesitation, and hesitation would turn to decay. There was no further refinement left to hide inside.

He drew a slow breath, counting it the way he had counted the seconds in his charts. Each inhale measured and deliberate. Every angle rehearsed. Every resonant sequence mapped against the crest his data implied was building in the magnetosphere. Weeks of preparation had narrowed to this single alignment. He knew he was ready. The plan was airtight. The variables constrained, contingencies accounted for. In his mind, it could not fail.

At exactly 7:14 p.m., he set the bow to the A-string and drew it across—the calculated intersection of storm and downbeat.

Nothing opened. Nothing sounded. The note, when executed, never came. It just scraped—dry, thin, resistant. Friction without lift. Nothing happened. Nothing burned. Nothing spoke. Nothing. Full stop.

He checked his angle. Pressure. Placement. They were all perfect.

He tried again. The same thin collapse.

A third pull yielded a sharp, high squeal that died almost immediately, as if ashamed of itself.

Now David began to feel it. The window he had so carefully calculated—that sliver of time where the door he dreamed of might actually be vulnerable to breach—was slipping away. All of it.

His throat tightened. Months of structure dissolved in seconds. It felt as though he had thrown himself upon the sea in fluent theorem and been judged not wrong—but irrelevant.

And then he heard it once again.

  Symmetry attracts violence. Listen.

But in his desperation to prevail—to force the universe to acknowledge his work—the wounded part of him took control. Blinded by the sting of failure, the fool he thought he'd buried allowed the very violence he'd been warned about to seduce him.

His grip on the bow tightened, twisting from precision into a chokehold. Pride, flayed raw by repeated failure, pulsed through him, poisoning the philosophical currents he had once trusted for clarity. That disciplined energy, meant to steady his hands, now threatened to burn them outright. Stripped of any care for consequence, his fully bruised ego began planting charges of pure guilt and rage deep in his gut.

He lurched forward. A stream of combusted anger poured from his mouth—muttered chains of mangled words, curses tangled just behind his teeth. Something deeper and older, something not entirely human, was tearing its way out: charred, ruined pieces of a self he dared not claim, but was now perilously close to embracing.

“What do you want?!"

The words struck the walls and fell to the floor, dead weight. He waited, chest heaving.

The air did not answer. The tuning fork on the table continued its narrow, indifferent vibration. Outside, the distant chorus of car alarms kept sounding their mindless refrain. Nothing bent toward him. The silence that answered was not empty—it was a wall, solid and utterly unmoved.

“Why won’t you just—"

He seized the bow and cut it through the air. The gesture was absurd from the start—less music than reprisal, a useless but satisfying act of war against every false structure that had presumed to contain him. He slashed across the room in disgust, striking counters, tables, and couch alike. Papers burst upward at each pass, torn from their resting places and sent whirling into the air. Every surface presented itself as an accomplice—of data, of order, of the smug administrative fiction that information, properly arranged, could still amount to truth. The pages scattered across the floor in loose white brigades, floating briefly before collapse.

“Take it. I gave you everything I had!”

The cello pressed against his chest like a brace. No shift. No tremor. No concession.

“And for what? To sit here and prove something that nobody will ever—? To lose everyone I ever—"

The sentence broke apart before it finished.

Silence settled again. It did not rush in—it had never left. His voice had been the disturbance.

He stood there, breathing hard, the bow hanging useless from his hand. The storm outside did not pause for him. The room remained intact, unmoved. Just him. Breathless for nothing it seemed, waiting for no one.

"I don't know what you want.”

The words smaller now.

He pulled the cello close, holding it in his arms like nothing in the world could know him or protect him more than this pressured forge of wood and wire. Hunched over, every sob, every choke, and cough reverberated through the chambered wooden body. So much so that when he fell to his lowest whisper, his weakened voice resounded through the room as though there were fifty Davids at once.

“I don't know who I'm supposed to be,” they said.

The words left him and seemed, for one brief moment, to belong to something more than silence, more than self. An undeniable realignment settled into the cracks of the room. Something had shifted.

He lowered himself to the floor among scattered pages and rosin dust, the bow still loose in one hand, the cello leaning into him like a living weight. All around, the apartment settled into that peculiar stillness that follows grief when it has exhausted its first violence and become something heavier, more patient.

On the kitchen island just above him, the manila envelope that had carried Arthur Lang’s last will and testament lay open near the counter’s edge. The official papers, exhumed only the day before, had finally been signed and sent back to Boston, granting permission for the attorney to put in motion the tying up of all that remained after a long and exacting life had come to its close. The documents were ordinary, or so he had been told. Extensive, certainly, but ordinary in the way the machinery of death is—formal, competent, indifferent to the magnitude of what it arranges.

Now the envelope had been disturbed. In the wake of David’s collapse, things had shifted slightly from their places. A single sheet had worked itself loose and hung half-free, as though the room had quietly revised its own sense of what mattered.

The silence deepened. Beneath it, something opened.

His father’s voice came to him with the force of recognition, more immediate than memory. It had never left him entirely, only sunk beneath performance, pride, ambition, injury—the bright, useless clamor with which he had defended himself against love.

Some things are not meant for an audience.

David went still.

The voice carried no urgency. It was clear, level, unhurried—the calm of something that had been there all along. It had always lived there. He had simply spent too many years drowning it out.

A draft moved through the apartment. Its passage was strange enough that David glanced toward the front hall, half expecting to find the door ajar. The hallway stood empty. Only the air had shifted. When he turned back, the loosened page tipped free, slipped from the envelope, and drifted to the floor before him with quiet finality.

He reached for it, then stopped, his hand suspended above the page as though stillness itself might postpone whatever waited in the act of touching it.

For a moment, time loosened.

Christmas morning rose before him in broken brightness: Grandpa Elliot arriving with a present so large it seemed to dwarf him; the first glimpse of polished wood beneath torn wrapping paper; the faint tack of fresh oil beneath his fingertips. Then in the present moment, the slip of his actual left hand caused the cello beside him to answer with the gentlest accidental resonance.

The room returned all at once.

He bent and slid his fingers beneath the edge of the page. As soon as he lifted it, he caught the scent: citrus, quick and unmistakable. Faint, long-settled, yet present enough to stop him where he was.

His father.

Arthur had never worked without a bowl of clementines somewhere within reach. David remembered the lessons: peel folding back in his father’s hands, brightness entering the room with the released oils, the trace of it carried onto the strings when Arthur leaned over to adjust a wrist, correct a position, reshape a phrase. Even now, the back of the page seemed to hold the lightest ghosts of orange-tinted prints.

And in the wreckage of himself, David smiled.

It lasted only a moment, but it was real.

His father, and his father before him. The instrument. The hand. The scent. The lesson. All of them gathered in that small space with a force more intimate than explanation. Love had persisted in the things it touched and left behind. David could feel that now with a clarity more solid than thought.

Then he turned the page over.

On the other side was a single piece of music: Bach’s Cello Suite No. 1, Sarabande.

It occupied only half a page. Sixteen bars. Eight, then eight again in repeat. Thirty-two measures that looked almost austere in their simplicity and yet required, from anyone foolish enough to underestimate them, an honesty nearly impossible to give.

Beneath the music, in his father’s beautifully measured hand, was a note.

  David,

  Don’t judge it, my son. Just once, for me, just play.

  I always loved listening to you play.

  —Dad

He stared at the page until the ink blurred.

What he wanted then was not reasonable. Not possible. Not permitted by the architecture of the world. He wanted to pick up the phone and dial the number that still lived, uncorrected, in his hands. He wanted to hear his father answer on the second ring and say, in that unadorned voice: Hello, son. He wanted to hear in the background, the soundscape of the study coming through the line—the fire giving off its low and companionable crackle, the turntable in the corner, some mono recording of the old masters breathing softly into the room. He wanted, with all the useless force of love, to be returned to the ordinary facts of him.

But the dead do not answer. However fervently they are summoned, the other end remains silent. And yet…

David sat with the page in one hand and the cello beneath the other, and let that silence become what it had always been—not emptiness, but presence stripped of consolation. It had always belonged to the room. He had simply been too loud to hear it.

Under his palm, the strings gave a faint, involuntary tremor, and he closed his eyes in response.

There was no audience here. No judgment worth fearing. No standard left to satisfy except the one grief lays upon the living: that they continue, and that they do so truthfully.

He set the page before him. Reached for the bow. Drew in a breath that did not steady him, but was enough to start. Because there was nothing else to do. Because his father had asked. Because somewhere beneath the vanity, beneath the injury and the lifelong performance of being wounded, his body already understood what his mind had not yet agreed to.

Play.

Just play.
