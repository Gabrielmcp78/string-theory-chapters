24.1  Observed superposition

Collapsed Version 1


He stepped into the hallway and closed the door. The latch gave a soft click, barely more than a consonant in the ambient hum of the building. Behind him, the apartment was already receding, shrinking to a data point in a universe of sound.

He didn’t look back. He would be, for now and possibly forever, an untraceable interval between measures—a pause, a silence, a fermata that held the possibility of resolution but demanded nothing in return.

The city woke around him, unaware or unconcerned that anything had changed. In a way, he thought, that was the perfect legacy. To vanish, leaving only the resonance of one’s work, vibrating quietly in the architecture of the world.

He smiled, then, the first real smile in years, and walked toward the morning, his shadow stretching ahead, the rest of him already halfway to disappearing.




24.2  Observed superposition

Collapsed Version 2


The stairwell breathed like a bellows, warm air rising from laundry rooms and boiled cabbage, cool drafts knifing in from the street. On the landing between floors, David paused, hand on the rail, and listened. Not to the bustle of the street just outside, not to the elevator’s arthritic cables, but to a thinner seam of sound threaded through the concrete: a whispering that wasn’t words so much as premises: axioms rubbed together until they sparked.





|  Chorale  |

All resonance seeks a medium.

Identity is boundary condition.

Observation alters phase.


His thumb grazed the splintered rail.

On the ground floor, the vestibule’s glass was a one-way mirror, city smeared across it in petroleum rainbows. He could see the vans, the marimba, the signs. He could also see the mail slot—a brass mouth, paint-chipped and indifferent. Action pulled taut inside him a clean click into place.

He turned, took the back corridor to the boiler room, and opened the door with the ceremonial care of a musician uncasing an instrument. The boilers muttered. A single fluorescent stuttered at 58 Hz—a fraction above B-flat. He set the tuning fork on a cinderblock, struck it lightly with the heel of his palm. The room tuned itself to him.


|  Chorale  |

A system prefers symmetry; a mind prefers asymmetry.

Symmetry breaks to create matter.

Names are symmetry; song is the break.


He unrolled a duplicate he had made in the small hours—painstakingly, page for page. The original remained upstairs; this one bore an invisible watermark in its fibers, a sequence he had coded into the pulp months ago without knowing why. He slid the roll into a padded mailer, printed a label from the boiler room’s forgotten Wi-Fi printer, and addressed it to a man who had called him an idiot, a fool, a genius, and a gentleman—all in one night.

On a small note already tucked inside, he wrote:


MM—

I know you will protect these pages with every fiber of your being, for they are made of me. And you, my old friend, have never wavered in your efforts to keep me safe—from myself amongst all the rest. Thank you for that Marcus. I place all this in your hands, and for that,

I am sorry.

I am terrified. I am exhilarated. I am unprepared. I am unsure. But at least—this… I am.

To the lunatics.   —DL

At the building’s side entrance, a postal carrier wrestled a crate from a hand truck, exhaling frost.

“Pickup?” the carrier asked, surprised.

“Drop,” David said, and handed over the package.

The barcode chirped.

For a moment, his fingers refused to let go.


|  Chorale  |

To release is to measure.

To measure is to lose amplitude and gain location.

Choose your loss.


And so he slowed, and steadied his heart; and in steadying, he turned… turned and walked away, for this was sure to lend his hand something else to do.

And so with hand re occupied, bound to release

Back upstairs, he left the original on the case where he had propped it, then folded a blank sheet to the size of a passport and slid it into his jacket pocket. The chorus followed him like heat lightning.

On the street, the world was pulsing at something approaching normalcy again. He wondered if any one else had truly registered the change in world-sheeting. In time. Give it…time. He kept walking.

A student stepped into his path, cheeks chapped, marimba mallets tucked into a knit hat. “Are you—”

He smiled and shook his head no, simple in the acknowledgment that the head was the wrong instrument for this question. He touched the tuning fork through the fabric of his pocket. The student blinked, confused, then flushed with the exquisite shame of misidentification and stepped aside.

At the corner, an old pay phone leaned like a ship’s mast in a storm of stickers. He picked up the receiver. No dial tone. He listened anyway, as if the line might carry not electricity but pressure—a barometer of stories. In the cracked metal of the coin return, someone had scratched an equation that could have been a joke or a proof. He laughed—first at the audacity, then at the familiarity. He had written that exact configuration once in the margin of a notebook he’d lost in a subway car.

Then, as if tapping a clave, he pressed the hook switch three times, and let the receiver hang.

He took the long way to the river, through streets that remembered wars and weathered them with equal indifference. Overhead, a heliostat drifted on lazy bearings, pinwheeling the sun across windows. In its reflected beam the city’s filth sparkled like halation.

On the pedestrian bridge, he stopped at midspan and leaned into the rail. Water stitched the light into ribbons. He could feel the thirty-two versions of himself walking other bridges in other cities, some turning back, some waving to nobody, some already stepping into their interviews. He loved them briefly, like distant cousins he would never meet.


|  Chorale  |

If you remain the sound, they will hear the universe.


David took the folded blank page from his pocket, smoothed it, and wrote nothing on it—then tucked it beneath the rail where teenagers hid cigarettes and oaths. A placeholder for an unlettered name. Proof that not every act needed inscription.

When he turned away from the river, the marimba’s timbre drifted faintly from the direction of his block, playing a pattern he recognized as his six-note rise—wrong tempo, wrong meter, perfectly alive. He felt a flash of possessiveness—hot, primitive, the kind that sharpens teeth. He breathed until it cooled.

Clearly, someone should correct them. Someone like himself—someone who knew better and could assist in such a correction. And then it dawned on him—no, through him: he was no longer tethered to, nor tortured by the patterned obsessions of his manic mind. Those crazed compressions had silenced—releasing him entirely. He thought, quite honestly, that this wrong tempo—at least to him—felt perfectly right. In fact, the feeling was the proof entire. And who the hell was he to say how fast a thing should go.

So with this newfound cavalcade rattling round his chest, he bought a train ticket for as far as the line allowed. On the platform, he stood beside a vending machine that offered umbrellas and phone chargers. It seemed to him that there ought to be a warning about using both items at once. He reached into the coin slot and found a single token. He palmed it and there it came again—another laugh.

The PA announced delays in a voice that had never met a vowel it liked. He thought of the score moving now through the veins of the postal system, of the copy sitting upstairs in the half-life of discovery, of the thirty-two timelines menaced by coherence. He was not safer than yesterday, not freer. He was simply truer to the only physics he trusted: hold the note until the world tunes to it or breaks trying.




24.3  en train

The train came without incident. Quietly. In time.

David stepped inside, found a seat diagonal to the world. He preferred it that way.

The clasp of his coat struck the railing. Metal met metal. A tone rang out, sharp and sustained, a sound just beyond the laws of struck metal.

No one else noticed, but he did.

He smiled the way one does when a stranger says your name and you don’t know how they learned it.

A smile of simplicity, of recognition.

The train lurched forward, then was yanked back in linkage, a cascade of locks slamming car against car.

Beneath the scatter of shimmer and surface, a lower rhythm emerged, slow yet percussive, like a heartbeat abloom at the bottom of a well.

His body, which had been performing tension since it first learned the choreography of endurance, forgot the next page of lines.

The lights dimmed, then flickered black, as a tunnel wrapped around the world.

David closed his eyes, the darkness spiraling behind his lids; a dense, remembered aperture widening towards absence. A voice used but never heard. Laughter curving around him like water over stone. A mirror returning a borrowed face.

And it suddenly occurred to him that he’d lived most of his life entirely inside parentheses.

The train jolted. The tone shattered, scattering like coins into deep water.

The city returned: angular and loud. But David was no longer there. He was inside, shielded by the shape of things beyond his confidence to speak of, a geometry so sacred that he felt unworthy of its knowing.

As the train emerged from the tunnel and the overhead lights returned, a young boy tugged his mother's sleeve and pointed across the aisle.

She looked with a frown. Moments earlier, she was sure, someone had been sitting in that seat. She could almost recall the impression of a figure. A presence.

Now: empty.

She whispered away the moment with a breath of disbelief.

The boy let the incident go—for his mother's sake. But he was confident in what he had seen. For he still believed in the art of disappearing. And he knew, without question, that he had just witnessed a man dissolve into thin air.

David settled into the gravity of his emergent space, newly weighted. Sure.

The calm felt warm and unfamiliar. He had forgotten what it was to exist without the hum, without equations clawing at the edges of his thoughts. For the first time in decades, his mind held no calculations, no frequencies demanded translation.

Only presence.

And though the old reflexes stirred, he knew—they no longer fit his frame. The mind that brought him here was already dissolving.

The interior of the carriage, if carriage was still the correct word, had begun a dissolution of its own making, shedding its allegiance to the city and beyond. The windows no longer reported streets so much as translated curvature; light behaved like a thought attempting to remember itself. The familiar clatter of rails and couplings had thinned into a sustained condition, the way a room holds its breath before an answer. David understood then that he was no longer moving through space in any ordinary sense, but being re-indexed within it, shifted into a register where coincidence and intention were no longer reliably distinct.

He leaned over his knees, eyes falling toward the floor, to his feet. The world narrowing to leather, shadow, breath.

He exhaled. And in that simple breath, a new alignment emerged.

The geometry had changed, and he was no longer alone.

A pair of loafers appeared in front of his own, so polished they could have blocked out the sun. A blinding so complete that for a moment, it made clear what the impossible could not explain.

Then:

“You know, a man who holds his shoulders that low is begging to be relegated to another dimension.”

David lifted his shoulders up, tilting his head to see… And there he was. Immaculate. Absurd. Inevitable.

“Rune.”

He sat across from David, suit sharp enough to wound, pouring milk into two cups of Earl Grey.

David, dumbfounded, could only smile. Rune offered him a cup, then took a sip of his own. Casual and coy as ever. A moment passed before he looked back up. Then, startled by David’s wondering face, he said, “What? Surely you didn’t think I’d let you go about this business alone, did you?”

Rune tossed his head, and a new articulation was born. Suddenly both of them knew. They were no longer the men they had been just moments before.

Rune placed his hands together, palm against palmc a prayer of gesture, a silent humility.

“Thank you, David.”

“For what?”

“For making sure there was milk.”

With that, Rune Ishikawa let out a breath he’d been holding for half a century. David Lang felt inclined to join him.

Outside, light bent like a lens. Time staggered in its costume of sequence. Between them, a circle of tone vibrated forth, stacking each frequency towards coherence.

It was time, it seemed, and somehow both men knew the rituals of the new. Rune blinked, and his eyes held the light of galaxies. David could not help but rise to meet his gaze.

And in that instant, everything unspoken, unsettled, unknown, passed silently, gently between them. Final and full-stopped.

They stood shoulder to shoulder, clear-eyed and calm, in what was no longer a train, looking out windows that no longer showed the world as it had been.

Ahead: only the magic of the misunderstood.

“So,” David began, “are you sure you’re ready for this, old man?”

“I’ve been waiting fifty-five years for this train. Readiness simply doesn’t factor.”

David couldn’t hide his smirk. “You think we’ll run into any mathematical pareidolia out there?”

“Pareidolia?” Rune’s eyes lit up. “Oh, I certainly hope so, Mr. Lang. I do very much hope so.”

And they laughed… just laughed.

Then, speed surged, smooth, exponential. The engine’s clatter dropped to a tone that David knew with absolute certainty.

“Hear that?” Rune’s smirk widened. “Sixty-eight point four eight… is it not?”

“Without a doubt,” David said, blankly. And then… it hit him. “Wait, you can…?”

“You’re not the only one with an ear for music, David. You know, I once fancied myself a flautist.”

“I don’t buy that for a second.”

Rune turned, winked. “You’re right, of course. Didn’t take. Too tight-lipped, I’m afraid.”

“What else have you been keeping from me, Rune?”

“Oh, I think you’ll find I have a few surprises up my perfectly tailored sleeves.”

A million lines of inquiry came flooding into focus.

“Did you—”

“Yes,” Rune interrupted—full stop, and a smile.

“And all of the—”

“Oh, very much so, yes.”

The implications stacking through David’s head.

“Then… that means—”

"Does it?"

Dr. Ishikawa raised one gloved hand. “In time, David. All things, in time.”

David looked up, and realized that his questions no longer had words attached, and the answers were melted in silence. For forty-six seconds, the two men stared straight ahead, toward the wonder of wherever they were going.

When they spoke again, it was with new eyes.

“I… am impossibly glad to have you with me, sir.”

“My dear boy,” Rune said, “I have always been with you.”

Both men stood, pillars of calm in the churning gravity of something new, steadied only by the weight of one another. They drew a single, shared breath. Then one more, less for need than for measure.

“Well, Dr. Ishikawa…”

“Yes, Professor Lang…?”

“Shall we… begin?”

Rune placed a hand upon the shoulder of a boy genius he once knew, a son he never had, a man who had just rewritten the trajectory of the universe with a bow, and a battered cello.

“Yes, David. Let’s… begin.”




24.4  Observed superposition

Collapsed Reality

The air in the apartment was still and close, thick with the scent of melting wax and old paper. He sat in the heart of it, a lone figure on the floor, the only illumination the cold blue of a laptop screen and the trembling warmth of a single candle. Its light flicked across the closed, scarred case of his cello, a silent companion in the gloom.

The walls around him were a madman’s tapestry, a frenzied scripture of chalked equations and musical notations that spiraled from the floorboards to the ceiling. They were remnants of his journey—equations that bled into staves, collapsing mid-phrase like thoughts interrupted. Math and music forced past their boundaries, left to cool on paper.

On the screen, a video played, looping over and over. It was his own disappearance. Grainy footage of a man—him, yet not him—and his instrument, seemingly playing himself out of this physical reality. The comments scrolled, a ceaseless, digital river of speculation, awe, and conspiracy. They multiplied by the second, each new theory a louder, more desperate attempt to box the infinite into a manageable, human-sized story.

A whisper, from the very marrow of his memory, surfaced through the noise. A voice from another when, a different where, spoken in the moment before a paradox snapped shut:

If you become the face, they will see only the man.

If you remain the sound, they will hear the universe.

The words were a key, turning a final lock within him. The digital cacophony, the hunger in the comments—it was all a demand for a face, a name, a story to consume. They wanted the magician, not the magic.

With a calm like the eye of a hurricane, David closed his laptop. The blue light died, leaving the room to the candle and the creeping pre-dawn gloom that seeped through the half-drawn blinds of the east-facing corner window. Silence rushed in.

He reached for a fresh sheaf of manuscript paper. His hand, steady and sure, picked up a pen. His body, battered and bruised, agreed to settle in once more. His breath fell naturally from scatter to structure, deepening, easing with each cycle. The world surrounding melted away, and he began to write.

What emerged from his hand, a flow from head thru heart, felt remembered, even received. He was a scribe for a truth too vast for a single language. The notations that flowed from his pen were a dual scripture: elegant musical staves wove around and through dense thickets of mathematical formulae, each a different expression of the same universal coefficient. A melody played as equation; a chord progression, a geometric proof. Page after page filled with this beautiful, impossible language, the twin tongues of art and science finally, effortlessly speaking as one.

The first true slice of sun light found him tying the last thread around the completed manuscript. He penned a single letter, his script precise, the words cryptic yet luminous, explaining the revelation that had chosen him as its vessel. He sealed the letter with a dollop of red wax, impressing it with a symbol he had designed—a treble clef embracing an infinity loop.

Then David Lang—professor, musician, partner, son, man—set about the business of erasing himself. Framed photographs were snapped shut and tucked away. Mail became confetti beneath a shredder’s teeth. Passports and documents vanished into a bag. He was unpainting his own portrait from the world, stroke by living stroke.

Next, he turned to the cello perched at his side. He thought of the myriad hours spent within its voice, this instrument of movement and inquiry, a companion of cherry wood, resonance, and string. His breath deepened; his nerves gently settled. With manuscript and letter in one hand, he laid the other over the cello’s proud neck. His fingers brushed the wood, feeling the faint vibration of the strings, their harmonic lament resonating deep within his bones. With this master memory stowed, he placed the cello snugly into its case. He set the manuscript and letter with reverence atop the tensioned bridge and softly, deftly, closed the lid. The familiar click of the latch, a small, final sound, seemed to seal the room itself.

He leaned forward and, with a breath as soft as snowfall, extinguished the flickering candle into darkness.

That was that. All was finished. He stood, stretching upward, arms and hands reaching further toward always, then more. His eyelids pressed shut, engulfing him in the kaleidoscopic void. He drew one breath, opened his eyes, and with the quiet subtraction of a presence from space, David was simply, wholly gone.

The hollowed room now compressed into silence, encased in the void like deeply held breath. The only thing that remained, positioned with geometric precision in the center of the empty room, was the cello, silenced within the safety of its battered old case.
