II.3 THE HOTEL ROOM | Buffalo, NY - Present Day
Andante luminoso
| Revelation |

The room had the over-insulated density peculiar to conference hotels, a heat that did not warm so much as accumulate. Air moved through the vent in a flat mechanical rhythm. Papers sagged across the desk in loose drifts—frequencies, ratios, fragments of a model that had approached coherence only to recoil from it at the edge of naming.

David closed the door and stood for a moment without taking off his coat. The silence in the room was not silence. The hum had followed him up from the lobby, thinned now and distributed through vent-work, lamp ballast, refrigeration—the hidden workings of the building continuing beneath all appearances of rest.

He set the conference duffel on the chair. When he turned, the cello case was already drawing the eye.

He knelt and opened the latches.

The instrument lay inside with its usual gravity, varnish sunk to brown under the room’s yellow light. Familiar, yes. But after the shop, familiarity no longer felt simple. He touched the neck, then the shoulder, then lifted the body toward the lamp. A smell rose from the open case: rosin, old wood, horsehair—and beneath it, something that did not belong to any conference room. Cedar oil. Brass polish. The shop again, impossibly present in the instrument.

He drew the bow from the case and tightened it slowly.

The first open C entered the room and stayed there. Not louder than expected. Deeper. It passed beneath the vent hum and found it. The two frequencies did not merge. They approached. Hovered. The contact point shivered just outside coherence—enough to raise the hair along his forearms.

He played the note again, holding it longer.

A page on the desk shifted a quarter inch. A water glass on the nightstand drew a minute ring along its inner surface. Somewhere in the wall a cheap screw answered with a sympathetic rattle. The room had not come into tune. It had revealed the degree to which it wanted to.

He shifted to G, then back to C, then took the octave above. The cello answered with something that felt less like projection than recognition. His sternum vibrated. In the afterring he could almost hear some thinner structure pressing toward form and stopping just short of it.

He stopped playing. The room did not wholly let go.

On the desk, one of his conference pages had shifted enough to expose a penciled ratio he did not remember writing: 3:2. Below it, in his own hand: holds, but does not open. He stared at the line with immediate irritation—not because it was wrong, but because it was his, and yet it had reached the page without passing through memory.

The hum in the room thickened, or his hearing moved closer to its center. He raised the bow again and played a stopped fifth against the open string. For one held instant the instrument seemed to gather itself inward. The lamp filament thinned to a thread. His notes on the desk drew into rough order as if touched by a draft too subtle to feel and too precise to dismiss.

Then the alignment broke.

The note fell back into ordinary decay. The vent resumed its mechanical breathing. Whatever had nearly crossed into form withdrew at once, leaving behind only its pressure.

He lowered the bow and stood very still, listening to the residue.

Only then did he notice the business card on the desk, half exposed beneath the shifted page.

REPAIR SHOP FOR STRINGS. E. Guare, Proprietor.

Cream stock. Black ink. No ornament. In fact, no other markings of any kind. This card assumed you either knew where to go or never would.

Buffalo. Shop. Cello.

He lifted the instrument toward the lamp and angled the treble f-hole into the light. The maker’s label sat where it always had. Behind it, deeper in the cavity, something caught the yellow spill: a narrow repair strip glued to the inner rib. The graphite had faded at the edges, the hand softening where time had thinned it. What he could make out, holding the cello there under the lamp, was this:

BFL 11/85. C.G. for C.L.

He stared.

C.L.

Cassandra Lang.

The thought arrived only partially reasoned. What the hell were his mother’s initials doing inside his cello?

He lowered the instrument carefully onto the bed and stood over it.

Call Mom.

No. Jesus. She’d be dead asleep.

Still—C.L. In the cello. Buffalo, 1985. Nothing about it seemed right, but there it was.

He turned toward the chair and started digging through the conference duffel. There had been a penlight in the swag somewhere, buried under brochures and branded junk. If he could find the thing, he could take a proper look before he called and risked sounding insane.

His hand was still in the bag when the phone rang.

He froze.

For one unsettled instant the timing itself seemed to answer him. Then he lunged for the phone.

“Hey, Mom.”

“Oh good, I didn’t wake you.”

“Mother, I haven’t gone to sleep before 11pm since I was five years old. You didn’t wake me. I barely start breathing by this time of day.”

A slight giggle. “Well that settles it,” she said, apparently to no one at all.

He closed his eyes. “Mom, you are in the middle of someone else’s conversation again.”

“What? Oh—yes. Sorry, sweetheart.” A pause. “Well, I was trying to decide if I should just call and tell you about this…”

He sat down on the edge of the bed. “Funny. I was just standing here wondering if I should call you.”

“Well then,” she said, “I’ve saved you the trouble. So what’s the skinny. ”

He looked back at the open cello, the f-hole turned slightly toward the lamp like an opened mouth. Well, I’m not sure I just fell into this tonight but, mom did you ever have any work done on my cello?”

“Me? David, darling. This soprano has never touched a stringed instrument in her life! What makes you think…”

“Well there is a… a luthier's log slip pasted inside, I’ve never noticed it until now but it bears your initials C.L.”

“In the cello?”

“A repair mark. Buffalo, 1985. And initials.” He paused. “C.G. for C.L., I think.”

“My initials in your cello? Honey,” a small incredulous laugh popped through. “Why would my initials be in your cello? That was your father’s thing.”

“Of course. Right. Well, guess that’s a mystery for now. Hey, you said you had something?”

The line was quiet for moment.

“Mom?”

Then faintly, Cassie’s familiar self-murmur began to bleed through the silence. Well now, oh I can’t imagine… But it’s got to…

“No,” she said, quieter now. “That’s not right either.”

“What’s not?”

“The papers were your father’s. The scores, the folders, all that endless carrying on. But the cello…” A breath. “No, sweetheart. The cello was Elliot’s thing. I mean, I remember he bought it for you, worked out the whole plan with you father when you were barely 4… maybe 5. It was years before I allowed the two of them to actually give it to you. I was sure the thing would crush my baby whole.”

“I found something too,” she said. “I’ve been sitting with it for a few days. I didn’t know who to call.”

His hand tightened around the repair slip. “What is it?”

“Music. In your father’s handwriting. Inside his Bach.”

She did not need to say which Bach. Arthur had kept one score of the Suites always by his chair, its pages thick with years of pencil.

“I was looking for something else,” she said. “I opened it because I thought maybe there’d be a note, something he’d written to me in the margins I’d never noticed. And I found this folded paper tucked inside the Prelude. Staff paper. One line. Eight bars. In his hand, but not his usual hand—careful, like he was copying something. At the bottom he wrote: E.L. — heard this once.”

David’s breath stopped.

“Your grandfather,” she said. “Elliot.”

The room contracted.

“I’ve been playing it on the piano,” she said. “It’s strange, David. The intervals, they don’t…”

“Don’t what, Mom?”

“This melody doesn’t do what melodies do.”

“Can you read me the notes?”

She named each pitch with the precision of a woman trained to analyze score in the dark.

David listened.

Something in him went very still.

He knew this melody. He had known it his entire life—before he knew that knowing could take the form of memory. Elliot in the armchair by the window, David on his lap, the old man’s voice a low rumble in his chest, moving through a tune that wandered but never resolved. The line curving without closure. Carrying the child to sleep before words, before theory, before the partition between music and thought had hardened into names. The oldest sound in him. Older than the cello. Older than physics.

“Mom,” he said—his voice rough without his having chosen it. “That’s Grandpa’s song. The one he was always humming.”

A long silence.

“Oh, David, honey…” Her voice had changed. Thinner. Older. As if it were passing through a newly opened chamber. “That’s it. You’re right.” A pause. “Arthur used to say it was the only song Elliot ever knew.”

The only song he ever knew.

Not because he had written it. Because he had received it once and carried it for the rest of his life.

His grandfather had been carrying it too. Not as physics. As a lullaby.

“David?” His mother’s voice brought him back. “Are you still there?”

“I’m here.”

He set the repair slip down, crossed back to the bed, and took up the penlight at last.

“Hold on.”

He bent to the f-hole. Under the sharper beam the hand changed without changing. The first letters had thinned where the graphite failed—upper arms nearly gone, the old strokes faded just enough to close what should have opened. What he had taken for C’s were E’s with half their bodies worn away.

The mark read plainly enough:

BFL 11/85. E.G. for E.L.

At the bottom of the case, caught beneath an old shoulder rest, lay the folded repair receipt he had only half registered before. He worked it open and held the beam there.

REPAIR SHOP FOR STRINGS, Buffalo, N.Y.

client: E. Lang.

internal adjustment completed / see verbal instructions only.

Not Cassandra.

Elliot.

Near the saddle, where the grain tightened toward the endpin, the minute irregularity beneath the varnish returned to him with new force. Around the endpin collar, the ebony sat fractionally newer than the surrounding wear suggested it should. Someone had opened the body and closed it so carefully the fact survived only as hesitation in the surface.

“David?” she said again. “What on earth does it mean? I’ve been racking my head over it.”

He looked from the slip to the open cello case, and from the case to the card on the desk, and from the card to the pages that had shifted under the room’s partial answer.

“I think he heard something,” David said slowly. “And I think his body turned it into music before he had words for what it was.”

She did not ask what he meant.

“Well,” she said after a moment, “I can hear your mind whirring from here. That’s enough for me to rest easy with it. You’ll tell me what you figure out, right, Davey? Don’t forget.”

“I will. Hey. You alright?”

A deep-breathed pause hung over the line.

“Oh, sweetheart, you know me. I’m a mess, and I’m doing fine. Don’t worry about me. What about you, honey?”

He smiled despite himself. “You know me, Mom. I’m fine. And I’m a total mess.”

A soft laugh moved between them and was gone.

“It’s so very… quiet… without him,” she said at last.

“Oh, Mom.”

“I hate the quiet.”

Another silence.

“Anyway,” she said gently. “I love you, sweetheart.”

“Love you too. Goodnight.”

The line clicked quiet.

He sat on the edge of the bed, the slip in one hand, the phone warm in the other. The melody moved through his mind the way it always had—wandering, unresolved, never quite landing. He had fallen asleep to it a thousand times. He had never once thought to ask where it entered the world.

He wrote what he could before the thought thinned:

Buffalo. November 1985. E.G. for E.L. internal adjustment — responsive body / incomplete trigger?

He stopped there.

The cello lay open at the foot of the bed. Something had moved from atmosphere into fact, and fact—however partial—carried its own narcotic authority.

David lay back, the slip pressed against his chest, and let the room take him.

And then: firelight, ash lifting on wind, a yard bright with ruin.
