VI. Two months later   May 24, 2005
Moderato con tensione crescente

"David, you are not doing this. I will not allow it."

"Oh, okay—so you go from 'I don't understand a word you said' to—"

"I did not say I didn't understand. I said normal people like me would never wrap their heads around it. There's a difference. So what is the actual point of such a science-drenched piece of data if it can't speak to anyone outside your little priesthood?"

"Dad, you're just—"

"No. Now, son, don't—that's not what we're discussing." The scrape of a match, then the familiar soft exhalation. Even after forty years in the concert hall, he'd never quite surrendered the habit. "You're telling me that Doctor…"

"Ishikawa."

"Doctor Ishikawa, yes. You're going up to Ithaca this afternoon. To do what, exactly? Bury the research in a damned time capsule? What is this—a Hardy Boys novel?"

David snorted, too loudly. "It's not a joke."

"Doesn't sound like one." His father's laugh fizzed through the line, sharp and brittle. "Sounds like I raised a man about to hand his life's work to a university bureaucrat with a God complex. Tell me you're at least making a copy. Or booby-trapping it."

"Dad, Jesus."

“No, let me finish.” The line hissed with another exhalation. “You're talking about locking yourself in with another lunatic running the show. Controlling your life. Just like your grandfather. Have you forgotten? Just like my dad. Jesus, son. It ruined him—that work. I just don’t… ” He stopped. Another sizzle came from the other end of the line, followed by a long, tired exhale that carried resignation, regret—and, more clearly now, resentment.

David's free hand pressed into the worn corduroy of his thigh, leaving parallel dents. “Ishikawa isn’t… Dad it’s not like that. He’s trying to protect us.”

“Why? Because you've got something hot enough to fry the Ivy League?” His father's tone had gone flat, dangerous as black ice. “Son, don't you see they'll eat you alive?”

“Celeste trusts him. Marcus too.” The words hung there, a desperate counterweight thrown against the tide.

“Celeste trusts you, David. Your research. Not the system. Not Ishikawa. They are both doing this for you. Don’t be naive, son.” A pause. “Or is that part of your experiment now—throwing yourself to the wolves just to watch them fight over the scraps? Isn't that how it works? Your Quantum Observation principle?”

He closed his eyes. The phone felt impossibly heavy in his palm. “You're missing the point. If we don't show Ishikawa—“

“He’ll what? Huh?” An exhale on the line. Longer this time. Then, almost gently: “Son… some things are not meant for an audience.”

The words landed somewhere in his sternum and stayed there.

Silence stretched between them—the kind that carries the weight of everything unsaid across twenty years and three hundred miles and all the conversations they'd never quite managed to have.

“I just don't want you to make another mistake you can't take back.”

“Another, Dad? Really?”

“Son, you know what I mean.” The weariness was back now, older than the anger, deeper than the fear.

“Do I? No I mean really, Dad. What the hell is that supposed to—”

“Just promise me you'll be careful. Okay?”

He nodded, even with no one watching. “I'll be careful.”

Silence fell over the line and over the years. The October dusk snapped through the kitchen window and ran the world cold and pink and strange. David. stood there for a long moment, receiver still pressed to his ear, listening to nothing. Then, finally:

“I love you, son. You do what you have to do.”

Click. The line went dead before David had a chance to even respond.

He stood there, staring at the receiver in his hand and whispered to the dead air, “Love you too.”

He set the phone down slowly, picked up the bag he'd packed the night before, and walked out the door.

“In the grand theater of exploration and discovery, there are not turning points, but tuning points—subtle shifts, almost imperceptible, where history hums against the grain of consequence. These are not moments of triumph, but of calibration: the quiet betrayal of what could have been. For in progress we find the ultimate two headed monster; at once a beast that does not ask permission—and a tyrant incapable of absolution.”

  -Dr. Kenji Tsukino, Ithaca, NY 1970
