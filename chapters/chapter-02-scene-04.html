| Phone Call |

Celeste’s voice hit the line like a rupture.

“You did what?”

“Exactly what I just told you.” David shot back, barreling through the NYU student union, phone nearly slapped from his hand as he pushed into the chaos of Gramercy.

“I did what had to be done,” he snapped. “I showed him.”

“Showed who?”

“Dr. Kline.”

A pause. Then her breath hitched—a soft, involuntary intake, as if the floor beneath her had shifted.

“David… you didn’t. Not the data?”

“I did. I showed him everything,” David said, voice thinning now. “The plots. The resonance curves. I thought—God, I thought he could take it.”

“You thought?”

In the background, a rustle—footsteps on tile, a muffled exhale. Then Marcus’s voice tore through the line:

“What the fuck, David?!”

“Marcus, just hold on now. The moment Kline saw the data—he froze. Went white.”

“David, man! We talked about this. We agreed. No acoustic harmonic data. Not at NYU.”

“You weren’t there. It was a judgment call. I told you, I had to know—Had to see if he… I thought I had him.” 

“Fuck!” Marcus’s scream detonated into the phone. Then, to Celeste: confirmation by panic.

“I’m telling you, Marcus, something hit him—hit him deep. He staggered like the numbers had gut-punched him or something.”

“You are unbelievable. Do you have any idea what this means?”

David knew exactly what it meant. The stupidity—so constant, so thick—it made him dizzy. He stepped off the curb and almost vanished beneath a blur of screaming cabs slicing through the roundabout.

“Julian Kline? And you gave him resonance?” Marcus was past fury now—his voice curdled into mockery. “Congratulations, genius. It’s not theory anymore. You just took it live—you opened the goddamn door.”

“It’ll hold,” David said. “It’s not—”

“You don’t know that! Jesus, David, you’re so naïve. The whole damn world is going to come flooding in! You’ve fucked us—all of us!”

David could hear him pacing, the voice jerking sideways as Marcus turned on Celeste.

“Your boy just dragged us off a cliff.”

“Marcus—listen to me. It’s going to be okay. You have to trust—”

“Trust you?” Marcus snapped. “After this? No. No more. I’m not doing this over the phone. Get your ass home. Now. Before you wipe out another wing of science and take our careers with you.”

“Come on, Marcus. You know I would never—”

“No, David. The only thing I know is that you’re an idiot.”

Celeste took the phone before he even felt her move.

“Idiot?” David was spiraling now. “Who’s the idiot here, huh? Me, or—”

She didn’t yell. She never did. Not when her father vanished. Not when David cracked the ceiling. Not even now.

“David,” she said—soft, precise.

And that was all it took.

He froze. Exhaled. Silence pressed in like gravity.

“I’m on my way.”
