II. Dream Lecture
Andante sostenuto - Andante luminoso

The walk to campus was conducted under a dome of sound. The 68.48 Hz frequency had woven itself into the fabric of the morning, a pressure that vibrated behind his eyes and made the autumn leaves tremble on their branches with a sympathy that felt unnatural. It was in the grind of the city bus’s engine, the distant whine of construction equipment, the very hum of the power lines overhead. The world had become a resonator for a single, insistent note.

He turned the corner toward Feynman Hall, and the pressure intensified, solidifying into a wall of noise that seemed to emanate from the building itself. The entrance was clogged with bodies. Students stood three deep in the doorway, craning their necks. This was for his 9 a.m. elective, “Advanced Topics in Quantum Foundations,” a class that typically drew eighteen dedicated souls and a handful of the hopelessly lost.

He pushed through the periphery of the crowd, a few muttering “’Scuse me, Professor,” their eyes not quite meeting his. The interior of the hall was a shock. Every seat was taken. Students sat cross-legged in the aisles, leaned against the side walls, perched on the steps between rows. The air was thick with the heat of too many bodies and a tense, electric anticipation.

And in the back row, lined up like skeptical crows on a wire, sat the department’s old guard. Professors who had dismissed his work in faculty meetings, who had written letters questioning his tenure viability, whose own research was a monument to cautious incrementalism. They watched him, faces unreadable.

On the projection screen at the front of the hall was the title page of his newly published—impossibly published—paper from Physical Review Letters. Someone had already put it up. He hadn’t sent it to anyone.

As he moved down the center aisle, the hum focused into a spearpoint of pressure aimed directly at his sternum. His vision greyed at the edges. The weight of all those eyes, the physical press of the sound, the sheer improbability of the scene—it was a dissonance too great to sustain. His hand went to his breast pocket, fingers closing around the cool metal of the tuning fork. He didn’t take it out; he just held it through the fabric of his shirt, pressing it against his pounding heart.

The unbearable pressure of the hum shifted, sliding from a threat towards something that seemed foundational. He was calibrating to it—no, he was being tuned just like everyone in the room—twisted and tamed in this peculiar harmony. The noise in his head burned of, clearing like early cloud cover as he reached the podium. Then taking a moment to place his notes, he took stock: breathe even, limbs calm, no fidget, no concerns, no flop sweat, no errant thoughts. Actually no thoughts at all. He had no idea what was bout lecture on, but in having no idea, he knew every word was ready and waiting for him.

And as he looked out at the sea of faces it was clear, they were waiting for him too. There was none of the usual pre-lecture chatter of phones and whispered conversations. The room was simply filled with that brand of reverent silence that was itself a form of attention.

His voice as he began, emerged clear and measured. He didn’t read from his notes. Each slide on the screen, each concept introduced simply unspooled from him with a fluency that felt both like his and not his. He was a conduit. The traversal and explanation of the complex connections between harmonic oscillation and quantum decoherence, between vibrational modes and information transfer, he managed it all with clarity and rigor. Even some humor was shining through here and there, and this time it was not deployed as cover for some idiotic lapse of his own making. The words were there, perfect and ready, as if he were reading from a script written on the inside of his skull.

He saw understanding dawn on faces that were usually blank with boredom. A sophomore who had struggled with basic wave mechanics was nodding, her pencil poised over her notebook as she captured the flow of ideas. The skeptics in the back row had lost their skeptical slouches. They were leaning forward, one with his glasses pushed up on his forehead, squinting at the screen as if trying to decipher a magic trick.

The scheduled hour ended, and to his delight, no one moved. Hands went up all across the room. The Q&A began, and the questions were not the usual requests for clarification. They were probes, deep and intelligent, coming from undergraduates who yesterday couldn’t solve a simple eigenvalue problem. A physics major asked about the implications for quantum gravity using terminology usually reserved for graduate seminars. A music composition student, whom David had never noticed before, asked a startlingly precise question about the mapping of dissonance onto probability fields.

He answered them all. The answers came from somewhere beyond his conscious mind, articulated with a confidence that was entirely new to him. He was playing the room like his cello, and the room was resonating back at exactly the right frequency. The tuning fork in his pocket was a cool, silent witness to it all.

Finally, after ninety minutes, the spell broke. The department chair in the back row stood, giving a curt nod that seemed to release everyone else from their trance. The room erupted into genuine applause before floating down into the low buzz of conversation. Students gathered their things slowly, as if waking from a deep sleep.

David stood at the podium, his skin buzzing, his mind eerily quiet. The triumph was there, a bright, hot coal in his gut. He had delivered the lecture of his life. But as the hall emptied, the hum returned to its baseline drone, and he was left with the cold, metallic taste of a question: What, exactly, had just happened?
