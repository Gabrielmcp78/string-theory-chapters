Morning came brittle.

Light threaded through the blinds with caution—perhaps suspicion. The air smelled of old paper kept too long in heat. Dust drifted in slow eddies. Nothing stirred without consequence. Nothing dared disturb.

David sat at the edge of the bed, surrounded by notes he barely recognized. Ink had bled sideways overnight—each page now a blurred artifact of thought, requiring triple-readings just to confirm the handwriting was indeed his own.

Even then, intent remained just beyond his reach. He gathered them anyway, then faced his own presentability.

The shower ran hot but failed to bite. Sensation collapsed inward; touch into taste, sound into silence. He couldn’t tell if he was tired. The mirror answered for him: tired. No, exhausted. The verdict came from elsewhere. He trusted that more than himself.

Steam fogged the glass. He wiped it clear and met a face he didn’t recognize. The surface shivered, as if his breath had passed through the glass instead of dispersing into the air.

Outside, the world felt over‑calibrated. Light struck at wrong angles, shadows arriving before their sources. Above, the very sky, it seemed to David, carried the wrong temperature.

Then, as he arrived at the gates of lower campus, something beyond his ability to explain away began. All around him, colors grew teach, slipping out of the natural alignment of the mind’s eye. Hadn’t that building been white last Wednesday? Now the student union screamed bright green. David stepped onto the lawn and, from the corner of his eye, thought the grass was magenta. Magenta. The color doesn’t even exist. He recoiled, hopping back onto the stone path.

His footsteps echoed immediately—no delay, no space to breathe.

Leaves carpeted the ground, all the expected warm browns and oranges of late autumn. The wind lashed his face as he neared the physics wing, yet the trees stood nearly bare, branches stripped clean, as if the barrage of falling leaves had exhausted itself weeks ago.

Something in his mind began to fold. Sentences unraveled into symbols, then reassembled themselves in an unfamiliar grammar.

He couldn’t tell if he was thinking or being thought. He felt arranged. Assembled by something else. Still, he pressed on. The morphing world attacking him from all sides was not going to win. Not today.

He stopped and focused his faculties. Tuned out everything except what he knew he truly knew.

Numbers. Good.

Walking became math. The hallway tilted a degree too far from Euclidean; he corrected his stride vector. The floor resisted certainty. Time didn’t flow—it staggered.

Something in him had slipped. He was listing now, quietly, toward the scattered edges of his own shape, reoriented only by contact.

He carried that shift with him—down the corridor, across the threshold. Quiet as a bruise.
