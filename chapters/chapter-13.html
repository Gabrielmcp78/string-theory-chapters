I. THE NETWORK      November 29, 2026
UCLA Keck Observatory | 9:48 A.M. Pacific

Dr. Marcus McGrath moved through his observatory like a conductor orchestrating survival. The electromagnetic pulse had silenced ninety percent of Earth’s scientific infrastructure at 2:47 A.M., but the Keck arrays hummed with impossible vitality—not merely operational, but inexplicably and increasingly enhanced. His standard radio telescopes were detecting signals in frequency ranges they had never been designed to process.

And then this damned video. Nothing like it had ever crossed McGrath’s desk. It had arrived just six minutes after global communications collapse: a holographic recording whose compression algorithms defied conventional understanding—forty-seven minutes of footage occupying impossibly small storage, yet containing mathematical information that was currently rewriting his equipment’s firmware and showed no sign of stopping.

“Tolliver! I need an update on the current operational-institutions list—now!” Marcus shouted, delighted. This was the kind of warpath he had been born for, and every underling in the building knew it, thrilled to be receiving marching orders from the world’s most beloved astrophysicist at this historic moment.

The needle-nosed face of James Tolliver, Marcus’s secondary research assistant, slid into view around the corner. “Got it here, sir.” The second-year grad student scrambled to pull up the current list—a cobbled-together Emergency Scientific Response Network that McGrath and his team had been assembling since dawn.

“CERN is back online,” Tolliver reported. “Fermilab is operational. Caltech’s arrays are functional but, according to Dr. Kilpatrick, they are acting ‘funky.’ ”

“What else is new,” Marcus snorted.

“We’ve also got JPL, Berkeley, MIT, Harvard, and… Cornell.”

“Sir!” another assistant called. “The spectral-analysis software looks like it’s rewriting its own code. In fact—Professor, I’m banking new data right now. Somehow the audio data is spanning eleven-dimensional frequency space!”

McGrath frowned at the implication. “Keep on it, everyone. Today is one of those days we get to throw everything we know out the window and smile all the way through!”

“Professor?” Tolliver pressed, dragging him back to the list.

“Yeah, Tol, what’ve you got?”

“Cornell, sir. What are they doing up and running? Kinda out of their league, aren’t they?”

“Are you kidding? That’s Rune Ishikawa’s department.” Marcus clicked his tongue, then froze. “Wait a minute—Cornell?”

He turned toward the bank of engineers crunching the metadata still pouring off the nodes of this otherworldly file format. “Kozlowski—where’s that metadata analysis? Do we have coordinates on the origin of that recording yet?”

“Coming in now, sir. Sending to your workstation.”

McGrath pulled up the newly unscrambled GPS coordinates and waited for the search algorithm to narrow into the precise location.

| Ithaca, NY. |

“Holy shit. Rune, what have you done to the Finger Lakes?” Marcus took two deep breaths, then barked, “Get me a priority line to Cornell Astrophysics—now!”

II. THE CALL
Cornell Physics Department | 11:47 A.M. Eastern

Dr. Rune Ishikawa found himself in an unfamiliar—and potentially compromising—state of affairs as the most inexplicable morning of his career bled into afternoon. He had spent the preceding hours monitoring solar-flare data that defied every model he’d developed over thirty years of astrophysics and cosmological research. The electromagnetic storm should have destroyed Cornell’s detection equipment, but somehow their arrays were not only functional—they were operating beyond theoretical specifications.

The final hour of the morning was slipping away as the venerable doctor floated in a sort of befuddled daze of mental inquiry. Only as he began the dance of the doze-off, head-drop, jerk-awake routine did he realize he hadn’t eaten in fourteen hours. Abandoning his monitors, he set out on a mission for sustenance. Three mini-fridges into reconnaissance, all he’d found was mold and a catalogue of unsanctioned “experiments” he mentally earmarked for inclusion in the next faculty newsletter.

Just when despair had begun to settle in, he unearthed a jar of crunchy peanut butter lurking behind a curry disaster—and, in a cupboard above, a single sleeve of saltines made an offering of itself to the weary professor. He was attempting the impossible—scraping peanut butter with a saltine—when he heard it.

Ring-ring.

The faint, unmistakable double cadence of the old rotary phone in the lab.

Ring-ring.

Installed in 1964, it had stubbornly survived the digital revolutions of the eighties and nineties. Amused it still worked, Rune chuckled, and reengaged the battle of his make shift lunch—the peanut butter clearly had the upper hand.

By the third messy bite, clarity struck.

  Shit. That might be the only phone within fifty miles that still works.

He bolted for the lab, the peanut butter jar ricocheting from his grip and bouncing down the hallway in a bizarre kick-the-can relay. The absurdity only spurred him faster.

The phone was still ringing when he burst into the control center. He tossed aside binders, a cork board of faded index cards, and a regulations posting from 1991, finally uncovering the mid-brown relic.

He snatched it up and shouted, “I’m here! Hello?”

Dead line.

“Damn it.”

Frustrated, he crammed crackers and peanut butter into his mouth in a frantic avalanche, only to glue the roof of his mouth shut with paste. He scrambled for water, even stale coffee dregs—

Ring-ring.

The battle axe rang again.

“Hello—” Rune tried to articulate through the peanut spread.

“Dr. Ishikawa?” squawked a voice on the other end.

“Speaking.” Rune mercifully found a bottle of water under the desk, swigging until his mouth was clear enough for educated discourse.

“Sir, this is the International Emergency Satellite Service. I have a secure priority call for you from UCLA Astrophysics. Please hold for Dr. Marcus McGrath.”

“Marcus?! Yes—yes, put him through. Marcus, are you there? Can you—”

“Rune! I’ve got you. You sound like a transistor radio. Can you hear me?”

“I hear you. I’m here.”

“Well, well, Dr. Ishikawa—the great Guardian of East Hill. Seen anything interesting lately, old friend?”

“Dr. McGrath! I didn’t think celebrities of your stature were allowed satellite calls before 2 P.M.! Marcus, what the hell is going on? What do you know?”

“Rune, it’s beyond. The presidents put me at point for coordinating global scientific response to this morning’s electromagnetic event. I was glad to see Cornell on our list of surviving facilities. How did your equipment hold up?”

“I honestly don’t know,” Ishikawa admitted, staring at data streams that shouldn’t exist. “I was collecting solar-flare measurements when the pulse hit. Expected total failure. Instead, our arrays began detecting cosmic background patterns I’ve never seen before.”

“Are you processing a holographic recording? Anonymous cellist—about forty-seven minutes of footage?”

“Yes. It arrived through the emergency network. Strangest file I’ve ever seen. Our computers are exhibiting behaviors that…” Rune hesitated, studying equations blooming across his screens.

“Rune? Did I lose you?”

“I’m here, Marcus. Sorry. These interlaced frameworks… the glyphs… they look…”

“Familiar?” Marcus cut in. “Yeah, I had the same thought. Like twenty-years-ago familiar.”

“My dear boy, I know what you’re implying, but this… this is not that. What I’m seeing… this is operating at a scale we’ve never touched. This is a completely different universe of complexity.”

“You’re right,” Marcus said quietly. “I just can’t believe it.”

“No, look at these segments. The algorithmic landmarks… they’re… David’s.” The implications now began to flood and codify Rune’s mind simultaneously. “Marcus, there’s something else. It’s about David. Before we go any further, you need to know. A few months ago, he began pushing these theories; obsessing about some kind of consciousness-integrated mathematics, an eleven-dimensional cognition model. It stopped being harmless speculation. I was genuinely worried for his sanity.” Rune lowered his voice. “Lang… what have you done?”

“What do you mean? Rune, what’s wrong with David? Is he there? Can I talk to him?”

“Oh God, Marcus. No. He’s… not himself. I suspended him two weeks ago—for his own safety.”

“Wait, wait.” McGrath’s voice spiked with urgency. “Consciousness mathematics? Eleven dimensions? Rune, don’t you see? He was onto something! Everything he was ‘mumbling about’—this is it!”

Understanding crashed over Ishikawa. “Marcus, I’m looking at the footage now. Those equations—Jesus Christ, they’re covering David’s chalkboards. His office, his lecture hall— they’re both plastered with it.”

“His boards?” Marcus’s voice sharpened. “They’re still there?”

“Yes. I left everything exactly as he—” Rune stopped, blood running cold. “Marcus, write this down.” He lunged for an extension sheet on the adjacent wall, the tug of the short phone cord pulling the handset away from his mouth as he yelled, “607-836-9939. That’s David’s classroom extension. I’m on my way now. Call me there in two minutes.”

He slammed the phone down and sprinted for the door.

III. THE DISCOVERY
Cornell Physics Building—David’s Lecture Hall

Ishikawa’s footsteps echoed through empty corridors as he ran across the quad, mind racing. Every “crazy” theory David had been developing, every impossible mathematical framework he’d dismissed as brilliant but unmoored from reality—what if it had all been breakthrough disguised as breakdown?

The phone was already ringing when he reached David’s building, the sound echoing down the hallway like an emergency alarm.

Ring. Ring. Ring.

Ishikawa fumbled with his keys, hands shaking as he unlocked the physics wing.

The ringing grew louder as he approached David’s lecture hall.

Ring. Ring. Ring.

“Hold on, Marcus!” he hollered, not realizing, of course, that the phone hadn’t been answered yet and he couldn’t be heard. As he approached the corner and turned into the corridor of David’s main lecture hall, he began to fumble through a massive ring of keys that served the entire building. A mild panic overtook him—he had no idea which it was, and they weren’t labeled the way they should have been, or if they were, the labels had long since worn away. He cursed himself for the oversight, but as he reached the threshold, he realized it was ajar.

Someone was already inside.

He pushed through the doors, the ringing of Marcus’s call bifurcating his focus.

Ring. Ring. Ring.

Rune moved to pick up the handset on the desk when he saw her.

It was Celeste—standing before the massive boards in a sort of trance; unmoved, unreachable it seemed; completely overtaken by the sight before her.

“Celeste?” he uttered, not wanting to frighten her.

Nothing. He moved slowly closer to her and, reaching out his hand to touch her shoulder, whispered her name once more. “Celeste?”

Still no reply, but her stare forward seemed to beckon him to look—really look. He could not help but square his body and gaze forward to match Celeste’s focus. Suddenly everything she was entranced by seemed to extend its power over him as well. The sight, now fully seen, flooded over him, stopping him dead in his tracks as his brain struggled to process—or even believe—what was before him.

Every surface David had worked on pulsed with gentle radiance. Klein–Gordon operators flowering into glissandi of sixteenth notes, Ricci tensors dissolving into harmonic progressions, the complete unified field equations cascading across chalkboards in David’s precise handwriting. It all seemed somehow spring-loaded—coiled—ready to fly off the wall at any moment.

Ring. Ring. Ring. Ring…

The fourth couplet snapped Rune back. He lunged at the phone and snatched up the receiver.

“Marcus? Yes, I… I’m here, Marcus. I can hear you. I’m in David’s classroom… well, I don’t believe what I’m—everything he was working on seems to have been set aglow and I quite literally… well, Marcus, if it wasn’t right in front of me, I’d never believe it.”

Instantly, upon the last utterance of Dr. McGrath’s name, Celeste snapped out of her trance. Her head jerked toward Rune and the handset he held to his ear.

“Marcus? Dr.—did you say Marcus McGrath?”

“Yes, dear. Marcus McGrath is on the line. From UCLA.”

“Put it on speakerphone,” Celeste demanded. And without waiting for Rune to move, she grabbed the phone from his hand, ran to the desk unit, hit the speaker button, and slammed the handset back into its cradle. “Marcus? It’s Celeste Armitage.”

“Celeste! Of course. Thank God you’re there. Celeste, where’s Da—”

“Marcus…” Tears streamed down her face as she looked around the space. “Oh God, Marcus, you have to see this. It’s everywhere. The same equations from the recording, but they’re—they’re alive.”

“Marcus, it’s Rune. I’m looking at a room covered in glowing mathematics. The same theories I called ‘crazy’ just days ago—they’re all here. Everything he was working on.”

“The equations are moving,” Celeste reported, watching symbols shift and complete themselves. “And there’s something else, Marcus. They seem to be entwining with… treble clefs and arpeggios and…”

“I suspended a genius,” Rune whispered, horror and recognition washing over him as he shuffled backward and collapsed into the first row of student benches.

“It’s like they’re alive,” Celeste murmured. “Just waiting for David to come back and finish the work.”

“But where is he?” Marcus’s voice carried through the speaker, urgent and afraid. “If he achieved this breakthrough—if these theories are correct—where the hell is David?”

IV. Around The Horn
Geneva, CERN Control Room | 5:48 P.M. CET

The Large Hadron Collider had been dark since the pulse. Officially, the beams were cold, the cryogenics still recovering, the superconducting magnets inert. No power, no collisions, no reason for the detectors to be awake at all.

And yet ATLAS wouldn’t stop talking.

Lines of data poured into Dr. Mira Kovács’s terminal, a steady stream of readouts as though the collider were still smashing protons. Her colleagues had long since staggered home—or were stuck in traffic on Geneva’s crippled grid—but Kovács stayed, pacing the empty control room, eyes locked on the impossible flow of numbers.

“Residual noise,” she muttered in Hungarian. “Ghost currents.” Even as she said it, the excuse dissolved. The “noise” wasn’t random.

Her intern, Meyer, hovered at a safe distance until the symmetry became undeniable. “Doctor… I ran a Fourier transform. Just to be sure, and… this is harmonic, Doctor.”

On his screen, the jagged scatter smoothed into a crystalline lattice of harmonics. The frequencies were stacked, not like the hiss of static, but like notes in a chord.

“It’s… singing,” he whispered.

Kovács froze. “It’s harmonizing…” The lattice folded in on itself, bloomed into nested tori, then stretched into a sprawling 196,884-point constellation of symmetry. For a beat she didn’t understand. Then her stomach turned.

The Monster group. The monstrous symmetry that had haunted her graduate years—the absurd bridge between modular forms and string theory. And here it was—staring back at her from the very core of her famed collider.

“This isn’t decay,” she murmured. “These are instructions.”

Meyer looked at her, pale. "Instructions?"

But she was already moving, hands trembling as she keyed the secure line. "This is Dr. Mira Kovács, CERN. Priority one. Connect me to UCLA. Dr. Marcus McGrath."

Static hissed across the relay, the emergency network struggling against broken satellites. She waited, heartbeat drumming.

While the line clicked, the screens shifted again.

Event displays from the silent collider stitched themselves into images—curves and nodes aligning with staves of notation. Data wasn't just plotting; it was drawing. The detectors were trying to notate something.

The relay cracked open, a tinny operator's voice confirming: "Dr. McGrath, patching through."

Kovács didn't look away from the impossible constellation unfolding across her wall of monitors. Modular forms shimmering into scales, lattices resonating with harmonic ratios no experiment should ever produce.

When McGrath's line went live, her voice was tight, almost breaking, but she forced the words out:

"Dr. McGrath, this is Kovács at CERN."

"Dr. Kovács. Hold on, I'm patching you in." Marcus signaled to his assistant to complete the patch. Within seconds he had the okay to continue. "Dr. Kovács, you are on with Dr. Rune Ishikawa and Celeste Armitage at Cornell."

"Mira, it's Rune. How are you, my friend?"

"Rune, my dear. Wonderful to hear your voice, but under such incredible circumstances. Tell me what is going on."

Marcus jumped in to focus the endeavor. "Dr. Kovács, we believe Cornell is the epicenter of this event. More specifically, the work of Professor…"

And as the name left his lips, Dr. Kovács said it simultaneously: "David Lang."

"I should have known," Kovács whispered. "Lang has been on my call sheet for weeks. I must have 47 requests for cycle reservation on CERN and ATLAS. I didn't return his calls after the first 10 or so—just ranting voicemails about…" as the reality rushed over her, "Oh God… about ALL OF THIS."

"Yes, Mira, we are all realizing the same thing. Tell us what you have there," Marcus encouraged.

“We're seeing structured resonance inside the ATLAS detector. Independent of there being any power, let alone activity, to the LHC. I'm looking at eleven-dimensional signatures, harmonic, recursive. They match your file exactly. I don't care what anyone says—this isn't noise.”Ishikawa studied the final chalkboard, where David's work seemed to reach its culmination. He looked to Celeste, but she wasn't looking at him. She was already moving.

Celeste stepped into the center of the room with a focused, surgical intensity that silenced Rune. She’d been away from the boards like this for twenty years—but as she grabbed a piece of chalk, the last two decades seemed to evaporate. She moved with a practiced economy that even Rune couldn't match.

“The unified field?” Celeste scoffed, her hand a blur as she circled a specific operator on the board. “No. That was just David’s vanity. He wanted to feel like he and Einstein were having a fireside chat. This isn't a Grand Theory, guys. It’s a map.”

Rune leaned in, peering over his spectacles as Celeste began scribbling a complex lineage of functions.

“This... this variable here, Celeste. Do you see the recursion?” Rune asked.

Rune’s eyes widened. He watched her hand move, the realization beginning now to press in. He stepped back, his shoulders dropping.

“Rune?” Celeste asked, pausing mid-stroke. “You still with me?”

Rune gave a soft, breathless laugh. “I always told David you were the only real scientist in that house, Celeste. I just forgot how terrifying you are when you’re correct.”

“Earth to the nerds in Lang-land!” Marcus’s voice sparked from the speakerphone in LA. “I’m hearing static and chalk dust. Listen I was just thinking, we might be looking at a modified field equation."

Rune looked at Celeste, then at the board. “Go on, Dr. Armitage. Tell the Hollywood celebrity what you’ve found.”

Rune jumped in, unable to contain his excitement. “Exactly! If I may build on what Dr. Armitage has uncovered... David hasn't just disappeared. He has octavated. He’s moved to the same note, just a higher register of reality.”

“Kovács here,” Mira’s voice jumped back in, frantic. “I’m pulling up David’s last ten voicemails. If he’s octavated, then these rants aren't gibberish—they're coordinates!”

McGrath grimaced. “I think all of you sound quantifiably insane! And I’m inclined to agree with every word of it. Hold on.” Marcus clanked back at his techs for confirmation. “Yes… okay, we’re pulling everyone back on the line.”

“Exactly!” Someone from MIT. “We need different vocabulary. Different…”

“We need a different language,” Rune said, barely above a breath.

Then Celeste: “Enough. All of you.” And the miraculous superpower of Celeste Armitage… Held. She had barely spoken in the speaker phone loud enough for Rune to hear her less ten feet away. But instantly, the line went still. The storm broke.

“It’s music,” she said. Plainly.

Silence still, across five continents. Her voice trembled, but each word struck with the clarity of a tuning fork:

“You’re all so desperate to label what you don’t understand that you’ve missed what’s right in front of you. This began with a cello—a man and a bow and a string. Nothing. Processing…

At Fermilab, a physicist stared at neutrino flux resolving into scales.

At CERN, Kovács watched coefficients fold into harmonic lattices.

At JPL, an engineer muted his mic and listened to the heliosphere echo back a chord.

At MIT, their AI printed one word over and over: CANTUS.

Even McGrath, who lived to command, could only whisper into the silence:

“…Music.”

Rune’s hand shook on the desk, palm pressed against the glowing chalk. His voice came out raw, half prayer, half confession:

“My God… she’s right.”

And for the first time since the flares struck Earth, the world’s greatest minds fell quiet—because they finally understood what they were hearing.

V. THE WEIGHT OF NAMES    September 1971
Three days after the ARGO incident

A man—young, slight, and moving with the precise care of someone trespassing inside memory—creaked open the oak doors of the newly renovated music chapel on East Hill.

Inside: silence. Dust. Cold breath in the air.

He slipped between pews and climbed behind the towering organ pipes. There, behind a false panel only he knew to unlatch, lay the entrance—an access tunnel few at the university knew existed, and fewer still could navigate.

It had never been meant for routine maintenance. It had been built for ARGO. A silent artery connecting the chapel to the most forbidden space on campus.

He stepped inside. The tunnel closed behind him with a quiet click. The air grew colder as he descended. The route was narrow, ribbed in iron and stone. No light but the one he carried. Symbols carved along the junctions—some chalk, some etched into steel—guided his way. Glyphs his uncle had taught him, back when he was still permitted inside the old language.

Twenty minutes forward, belly of the hill. Past abandoned ducts and purpose-built lies. Until he arrived—through the vent.

He emerged not at the lab, but within it.

The vent grating shifted silently behind him, sealing him inside the most quarantined space Cornell had ever erased.

The lab was humming. Run 28 had never stopped. The terminal glowed green in the dark. A glyph unfurled on-screen every 47 seconds—subtle variations, micro-differences, iterations approaching something unnamable.

He did not look. His uncle had warned him: Some structures only become dangerous when observed.

The thermal printer sat inert, its spool empty. The whiteboard bore Kenji's last thought—cut of, just stopped, mid-equation—as if his mind had simply… stepped out.

The young man photographed it. Then, pulling a perfectly starched, precisely folded handkerchief from his trousers, he wiped it clean.

He moved methodically, knowingly through the space. Every action ticking off one more item on the disciplined and absolute list of must-do’s streaming in his head. Seventeen notebooks went into his canvas bag. He would burn them. Not from fear. From duty.

The equipment breathed around him. The tubes were warm, oscillators idling at rest frequencies. He replaced one of them; a vacuum tube already blown due to the oils of ill-placed and uncovered fingers during installation. He cleared two clogged vents. Reattached a coaxial strand knocked half-loose.

This was not a shutdown procedure he was executing. This was maintenance. This was respect.

And then—before leaving—he moved to the main door. The primary seal. A steel slab the width of a vault, still bolted from the outside stood sentinel before him. No one had entered here since Dr. Tsukino vanished.

From the interior, he slid the iron bar into place. Massive. Groaning. The weight of finality. Now the door was sealed from both sides.

As he made his way back to the to the ventilation shaft, a chill of deep, cavernous air whipped through his chest. He coughed, cleared his throat—the echo of it spiraling out through the passage tunnels for an eternity. Just as the percussive echo died away, he swore he heard footsteps shuffle behind him in the room. He turned slowly, his mind screaming at him that it was nothing, his body, his joints completely disbelieving of such a narrative. Crawled back through the tunnel that joined sound to science.

Structure to pattern. Resonance to intent.

Twenty minutes later, he emerged again behind the chapel organ, dust on his coat, breath shallow but steady.

"Who's there?"

A voice from the nave. A flashlight beam scanned the darkness.

He stepped out.

“Sorry, Father. It's just me…”

The priest lowered the beam. “Mr. Ishikawa? At this hour?”

“I needed a place to think.” He gestured vaguely toward the pews.

The priest studied him. His expression softened. “I am heartily sorry Rune, for the pain and turmoil your family has endured these last weeks.”

“That is most kind of you, Father.”

“I'll leave you to it.”

“Thank you, Father.”

As the priest retreated, a young, relieved Rune Ishikawa stood alone in the chapel.

Beneath him, locked and sealed from within, the lab was still running. Still tuning. Still evolving.

Some experiments do not end. They wait…

VI. TEMPORALity
Present Day

The speaker grid crackled. The last harmonics of Celeste’s declaration still hovered in the line, a shimmering after-tone no compression algorithm could erase.

Then a smaller sound intruded—ticks, like an old wall clock bleeding through the feed.

“What is that? Who’s patching analog noise?” McGrath barked.

No one answered. The ticks accelerated, outpacing any human rhythm. Screens in the Keck control room drifted out of sync; one showed 09:56 A.M., another 10:04, another a timestamp from two minutes in the future.

“Tolliver,” Marcus said softly, “verify system time.”

“Already did, sir. It keeps… sliding. The NTP server says we’re negative thirty-four seconds from standard.”

“Negative—?” McGrath stopped. “That’s not lag. That’s prediction.”

At CERN, Kovács leaned into her terminal. “Marcus, are you getting duplicate packets? I’m receiving your questions before you speak them.”

“What?”

“I just logged your last sentence—word for word—thirteen seconds ago.”

Rune Ishikawa watched the chalk glow intensify, symbols advancing across the boards like text from an unseen typist. “It’s echoing us,” he murmured. “The system’s anticipating dialogue. It’s building harmonic expectation—the way a melody knows its own resolution.”

Celeste stared at the blackboard equation that had begun to pulse:

Each pulse a heartbeat. Each heartbeat slightly ahead of her own.

She whispered, “The future’s bleeding backward.”

Monitors across the network replied. A new data stream overlaid the existing chatter—frames of video appearing in reverse order, showing their conversation rewinding, then branching. Every few seconds the feed corrected itself.

“This is quantum phase slippage,” McGrath said, voice tight. “Temporal coherence collapse across the entire mesh. We’re inside the waveform.”

The realization rippled through the call: they were no longer observing David Lang’s resonance—they were participating in it. Every response folded back into the signal and returned ahead of its source. The experiment had annexed causality.

For thirty-two seconds, global science stood inside a chord that included its own future.

Then the harmonics converged, compressing to silence.

McGrath exhaled, half-laughing. “Hey, I bet I can tell you all what’s gonna happen in about twenty-eight seconds.”

Laughter trickled in, forced at first, but needed—everyone knew that much. Until the twenty-eight seconds passed and with the clockwork of just-before-this, the sound mist of temporal static flanged back at them—current. It was zero minutes : zero second in time.

VII. CANTUS

McGrath paused, needing an assurance from his people that the temporal drift had indeed stabilized, then leaned toward the mic. The leaning was slower than time. He was searching, in this moment; reaching toward something to say—anything that might stand a chance at being enough. He knew the look of what was coming, and owed them yet another grim, clear glimpse into the future.

“All right, team. Whatever this is, it’s rewriting the protocols faster than any government can react. But, mark my words… they will most definitely react. Before they clamp down, we have a chance to decide what must survive.”

A brief discussion ensued to ground the criteria, the threshold of preservation.

At Cornell, Celeste wiped chalk dust from her hands and glanced at Rune, his face lit by the phosphorescent equations still crawling across the boards.
