EPISODE I


6.1   THE INSTRUMENT SHOP  |  Buffalo, NY           Present Day

[Adagio religioso]

[|  Detour  |]

He found the shop by accident, though it felt intentional from the start. It was cleverly wedged between a boarded-up travel agency and a florist whose blooms looked more resigned than alive.

A woodcut sign in the window read:


INSTRUMENT REPAIRS. CONSULTATIONS.

QUIET. YOU CAN HEAR IT, YES?


He stared through the window like a child before a toy shop but did not enter. Just stood, mid-gaze, mid-thought, perhaps no thought at all.

The proprietor, an older woman with kind, piercing eyes, saw him lingering and shuffled towards the door.

“You will freeze if you stay like this,” she warned, as she gently ushered him inside with deft, warm hands.

The air in the shop smelled of cedar oil, brass polish, and long-kept vows.

A viola lay open-bodied on the counter, its belly split like a confession. Nearby, a cracked cello rib rested on linen, patient and poised for repair.

She gestured for him to look around, then returned to restringing the viola.

David moved among the shelves, touching nothing. Instruments in varying states of injury lined the walls. Their wounds were small: honest and human.

A narrow drawer: BRIDGES—FRAGILE.

Another: SOUND POSTS—DON’T LOSE.

The warnings felt like liturgy.

At the register, he watched as the old woman flipped something between gnarled fingers. An object. He felt drawn to it, as if history had been forged into its surface.

She stopped abruptly and pocketed the object. Her eyes met his. He could not tell whose impulse triggered first. They both felt the flood of recognition. Body having stepped here before. Mind having thought this moment already.

“Do I know you?” David asked.

She paused. “Do you… have a cello in for repair?”

“How did you know I played the… cello?”

She smiled but made no answer. Whatever lay in her pocket, however, answered with a faint, low shimmer, rooting the same thin interval he’d heard since the shuttle. He moved toward the sound, but as he neared, it stilled. Silenced.

Something in him contracted, then softened, like memory returning in a language he no longer spoke.

He laid his palm on the cracked neck of a cello. Its surface was warm to the touch, and carried with it the vague sense of humility.

They stood close, shoulders nearly touching, but did not turn to face one another. Understanding passed between them without words.

Moving to leave, he stopped at the threshold.

“Will I see you again?”

“You already have,” she replied.

He lingered longer than he meant. Saw less than he wanted. He left quieter than he arrived.






6.2   THE WORKSHOP   |   Buffalo, NY

[Largo irreale]

[|  Form A(1)b–Revised  |]

The signage ended at Room 6D.

Room 7B wasn’t listed. David found it by accidental inattention, half a floor past logic, behind a door that stuck before yielding with a sound like paperwork being forcibly approved.

Inside: twelve chairs, arranged in a hesitant oval. No one looked like they had chosen their seat. No one seemed as if they remembered how they’d gotten there.

At the center, a woman in charcoal held a stack of gray forms. The near-match in tone produced a subtle visual failure as she moved among the chairs; at moments, the pages seemed to vanish into her clothing, only to reappear one by one in her hand.

“This is Form A(1)b–Revised,” she instructed. Her voice had the shape of announcement but no clear origin. “Complete it as though it were A(1)a–Original. Ignore pre-filled fields unless they’re blank.”

She moved in slow orbit, placing a copy in each lap.

David glanced at his: checkboxes nested inside contradictions, diagrams that defied perspective. A discreet look at the forms to either side confirmed they were not standardized. He could not tell whether the differences were what mattered, or whether what mattered had itself been altered. He looked to the form for answers. A foolish errand.

A three-dimensional lattice was labeled:

YOUR ROLE

It seemed to shift when viewed head-on.

One section read:

SIGN HERE IF YOU PARTICIPATE

Diagonally overlaid with:

DO NOT SIGN THIS FORM.

Someone raised a hand.

“Do we… participate?”

The facilitator smiled the way statues smile when misunderstood.

“You’ll know.”

No one spoke again.

The fluorescent lights flickered with a pulse less random than rhythmic. The same unresolved interval he’d heard in the hotel vents, in the elevator buttons that lit without input. He’d counted it twice already. Or sixty-eight times. The repetitions blurred so close together that certainty felt like a category error.

The flicker emitted a tone: visual rhythm made audible.

He blinked. Squinted. The facilitator was still speaking, but her lips seemed barely to move.

“…emergent disciplinary spaces…”

The phrase arrived in fragments. Echoes with no origin.

He looked down. The diagram was changing—not morphing, but rotating emphasis. As if being read from another perspective.

He hadn’t moved.

The ink shimmered at certain angles. Notation became structure, became symbol, became nothing.

His limbs felt heavy, over-defined. Present in a way he couldn’t fully accept. The kind of weight you feel in a dream just before you fall through it.

The woman collected the forms without looking at them. When she approached to collect David’s, she stopped. Stood behind him. Stone quiet and frozen… cold. Then moments later, at the front of the room, she placed the forms into a canvas bag labeled:

NON-COMPLIANT, and left the room without a goodbye. Her shape already fading, as if memory were erasing her in real time.

David looked down. His form was blank. It remained uncollected. His pen was dry. He didn’t know if this was on purpose, or in protest.

Had he stayed the whole time? He wasn’t entirely sure. By the time he looked up from the page, it was empty. The room. He was the only person left.






6.3   THE HOTEL ROOM   |   Buffalo, NY


[|  Revelation  |]

The room had the over-insulated density peculiar to conference hotels, a heat that seemed to accumulate rather than warm. Air moved through the vent in a flat mechanical rhythm. Papers sagged across the desk in loose drifts: frequencies, ratios, fragments of a model that had approached coherence only to recoil from it at the edge of naming.

David closed the door and stood for a moment without taking off his coat. The television flickered on when his bag brushed the remote. A weather anchor in a violent blue tie stood before a radar map warning western New York about lake-effect snow with the solemnity of nuclear war.

“…travel advisory remains in effect through Thurs—”

David muted it. For a second canned laughter burst from some commercial before the silence swallowed it again. The room smelled faintly of industrial detergent and somebody else’s cologne beneath the heat.

The silence in the room was not silence. The hum had followed him up from the lobby, thinned now and distributed through vent-work, lamp ballast, refrigeration, the hidden workings of the building continuing beneath all appearances of rest.

He set the conference duffel on the chair. The plastic conference badge still hung around his neck. He tore it off irritably and missed the trash can by three feet.

When he turned, the cello case was already drawing the eye.

He knelt and opened the latches. The instrument lay inside with its usual gravity, varnish sunk to brown under the room’s yellow light. Familiar, yes. But after the shop, familiarity no longer felt simple. He touched the neck, then the shoulder, then lifted the body toward the lamp. A smell rose from the open case: rosin, old wood, horsehair; and beneath it, something that did not belong to any conference room. Cedar oil. Brass polish. The shop again, impossibly present in the instrument.

He drew the bow from the case and tightened it slowly.

The first open C blossomed into the room,  deeper than he had expected. It passed beneath the vents’ tone, like the two were actively listening for one another. They seemed to approach but then failed to merge, hovering just outside one another. The contact point trembled near coherence strongly enough to raise the hair along his forearms.

When he played the note again, a page on the desk shifted a quarter inch. A water glass on the nightstand drew a minute ring along its inner surface. Somewhere in the wall a cheap screw answered with a sympathetic rattle. The room had not come into tune. It had revealed the degree to which it wanted to.

He shifted to G, then back to C, then took the octave above. The cello answered with something that felt less like projection than recognition. His sternum vibrated. In the afterring he could almost hear some thinner structure pressing toward form and stopping just short of it so that when he stopped playing, the room did not wholly let go.

On the desk, one of his conference pages had shifted enough to expose a penciled ratio:

3:2. holds, but does not open.

He stared at the line with immediate irritation. Christ. Was he becoming one of those people now? Three days around theoretical acousticians and suddenly every legal pad in America looked like the notebook of a desert prophet.

The frequency in the room thickened, or his hearing drifted closer to its center. He raised the bow again and played a stopped fifth against the open string. For one held instant the instrument gathered itself inward. Across the room, atop the desk, a collection of loose pages, notes he had taken on this and that throughout the day, slid subtly into rough alignment, seemingly brushed by a draft too delicate to feel and too precise to dismiss.

Then, with no warning to speak of, the alignment broke. The vent resumed its dull mechanical breathing and the pages settled back against the desk. Whatever had nearly crossed into form withdrew at once, leaving behind only its pressure.

Down the hall an ice machine emptied a fresh tray into its holding bin. The crash startled David, hitting him like a dropped sheet of metal. He flinched so hard that the bow skipped across the string.

“Jesus Christ.”

He lowered the bow and sat it down on the bed, then stood very still, not sure what exactly he was listening to,  the residue perhaps, or maybe nothing at all. Either way, he was content to let the moment fall away into the rest of a moments quiet.

It was within the calm of that quiet that his gaze drifted back to the desk. There, half exposed beneath the shifted pages, lay the business card from the Instrument Repair Shop. He moved to pick it up, turning it over in his fingers as he read and reread the name.

REPAIR SHOP FOR STRINGS. E. Guare, Proprietor.

The card was striking, singular in its strange simplicity. Cream stock. Black ink. No ornament. In fact, no other markings of any kind. The card assumed you either knew where to go or never would.

Then a glint of white caught his attention, only a flash, strange and out of place against the belly of the cello in the corner. He crossed to it, lifted the instrument into the lamplight, and angled the treble f-hole toward its glow. The maker’s label sat where it always had. Behind it, deeper in the cavity, something caught the yellow spill: a narrow repair strip glued to the inner rib. The graphite had faded at the edges.

What he could make out was this:

BFL 11/85. C.G. for C.L.

He stared.

C.L.

Cassandra Lang.

The thought arrived only partially reasoned. What the hell were his mother’s initials doing inside his cello?

He lowered the instrument carefully onto the bed and stood over it.

Call Mom. No. Jesus. She’d be asleep.

Then again, his mother considered midnight a perfectly reasonable time to reorganize closets or become emotionally devastated in the kitchen.

He turned toward the chair and started digging through the conference duffel. There had been a penlight in the swag bag somewhere beneath brochures, branded pens, and a stress ball shaped like a brain.

His hand was still buried in the bag when the phone rang.

He jerked hard enough to nearly knock the cello sideways off the bed. The bow clipped the lamp.

“Shit.”

“David?”

He grabbed the phone. “Hey, Mom.”

“Oh good, I didn’t wake you.”

“You absolutely woke me.”

“No I didn’t.”

“Mom, it’s midnight.”

“Well midnight’s not a real time anyway. Nothing important has ever happened at midnight except murders and babies.”

Despite himself, he smiled and sat down on the edge of the bed. “Mother, you are in the middle of someone else’s conversation again.”

“What? Oh. Sorry, sweetheart.” A pause. “Somebody in this house has to maintain a lively social calendar.”

“You live alone.”

“Thanks for the oh-so-delicate reminder, dear. Besides, that sort of thing has literally never stopped me.”

A slight giggle escaped her. He closed his eyes.

“Well,” she said, “I was trying to decide if I should just call and tell you about this.”

“Funny,” he said. “I was just standing here wondering if I should call you.”

“Well then, I’ve saved you the trouble. So what’s the skinny, darling?”

He looked back toward the open cello, the f-hole turned slightly toward the lamp like an opened mouth. “I’m not sure yet. I just found something weird.”

“With you that could mean literally anything.”

“There’s a repair mark inside the cello. I’ve never noticed it before.”

“Inside?”

“Yeah. Buffalo, nineteen eighty-five. And initials.”

“What initials?”

“C.L. I think. Maybe.”

“My initials?”

“Looks like it.”

A small incredulous laugh burst through the line. “Honey, why on earth would my initials be inside your cello? I don’t even know how to hold the thing correctly. That was your father’s department.”

“Yeah, I know. I just thought maybe…”

“Well if I secretly became a luthier and forgot to mention it, this is very exciting news.”

He laughed softly.

“Anyway,” she said, “I had something too.”

The line quieted.

“Mom?”

Faintly he could hear her murmuring to herself.

“Well now… no, that doesn’t… unless…”

“No,” she said at last, quieter now. “That’s not right either.”

“What isn’t?”

“The papers were your father’s. The scores, the folders, all that endless carrying on. But the cello…” A breath. “No, sweetheart. The cello was your grandfather’s thing.”

David frowned.

“I remember he bought it for you. Worked the whole thing out with your father when you were tiny. Four maybe? Five? Your father wanted something practical and Elliot disappeared for three weeks like he was running guns for the French Resistance.”

David smiled despite himself. “Buffalo.”

“What?”

“The repair slip. Buffalo, eighty-five.”

“Oh, that sounds right. They drove there together at least once.” A pause. “No, twice. Because your grandfather got that speeding ticket.”

“He got a speeding ticket?”

“Oh lord yes. Arthur acted scandalized for weeks. You’d have thought Elliot robbed a bank.”

A chair creaked softly on her end.

“They came home freezing to death and acting very strange afterward.”

“Strange how?”

“Secretive. Whispering in the kitchen. Stopping when I walked in.” She paused. “Honestly I thought they’d lost money gambling somewhere.”

David looked back toward the cello lying open across the hotel bed.

“Once it arrived, Elliot barely let anyone touch it. Not even your father.”

“Why?”

“He kept saying it needed to settle.”

“Settle.”

“I don’t know. Wood acclimating, strings adjusting, moon phases. With those two it was always half science and half witchcraft.”

David’s eyes drifted again to the penciled notation on the desk:

3:2. holds, but does not open.

A pressure gathered slowly behind his ribs.

“Mom,” he said carefully, “did Grandpa ever talk about hearing things?”

“Hearing things?”

“Not voices. Just… tones. Patterns maybe.”

The silence on the line sharpened.

Then:

“I found something too,” she said quietly. “A few days ago.”

His hand tightened around the repair slip. “What?”

“Music. In your father’s Bach.”

She did not need to say which Bach.

“I was looking for a note,” she said. “Something sentimental. You know your father. He hid emotions in books like a Soviet spy.”

David smiled faintly.

“There was a folded sheet tucked into the Prelude. Eight bars. In his handwriting, but very careful. Like he was copying it from somewhere else.” She paused. “And then there’s… there is something here at the bottom: E.L.—heard this once.”

David felt something inside him go very still.

“Your grandfather,” she said.

The room contracted.

“I’ve been trying to play it on the piano,” she continued. “It’s strange.”

“How?”

“It keeps sounding like it’s about to resolve and then it just… refuses.”

“Can you read me the notes?”

She did. One by one. Patiently.

David listened.

And suddenly he was six years old again, half asleep against his grandfather’s chest while Elliot hummed near the window during a thunderstorm. The melody wandering in circles that never quite closed. He had known this music his entire life—not consciously, but the way the body knows a staircase in darkness.

“Mom,” he said softly, “that’s Grandpa’s song.”

A long silence.

“Oh, honey,” she whispered. “You’re right.”

Another pause.

“Arthur used to joke it was the only damn tune Elliot knew.”

Not because he had written it. Because he had heard it once and never escaped it.

“David?”

“I’m here.”

He bent toward the f-hole again, this time with the penlight. Under the harsher beam the letters changed. Not C’s. E’s. The graphite had faded just enough to close the upper arms.

The marking read plainly now:

BFL 11/85. E.G. for E.L.

At the bottom of the case, half trapped beneath the shoulder rest, lay a folded receipt he had barely noticed before. He opened it carefully.

REPAIR SHOP FOR STRINGS, Buffalo, N.Y.
client: E. Lang.
internal adjustment completed / see verbal instructions only.

Not Cassandra.

Elliot.

Not C.G. either. E.G.

E.Guare.

Near the saddle, where the grain tightened toward the endpin, the minute irregularity beneath the varnish returned to him with new force. Around the endpin collar, the ebony looked fractionally newer than the surrounding wear suggested it should. Someone had opened the instrument and closed it so carefully the fact survived only as hesitation in the surface.

“David?” she said again. “What on earth does it mean?”

He almost answered:

Nothing. Probably nothing.

But the sentence would not come. Not because he believed otherwise yet. Only because some older part of him had already started listening.

He looked from the slip to the open cello case, and from the case to the card on the desk.

“I think he heard something,” David said slowly. “And I think music got to it before language did.”

She was quiet a moment.

“Well,” she said at last, “that sounds deeply unhealthy.”

He laughed unexpectedly. “Probably.”

“You’ll tell me what you figure out though, right?”

“I will. Hey mom… you alright?””

A deep-breathed pause lingered across the line.

“Oh, sweetheart, you know me. I’m a total mess, and I’m doing fine. Don’t worry about me. What about you, honey?”

He smiled despite himself. “You know me, Mom. I’m doing fine. And I’m a total mess.”

A soft laugh of recognition moved between them and then fell back into a deeper silence. For a long moment, David just sat listening to the pensive rhythm of his mother’s breathing across the line.

“It’s so very… quiet… without him,” she said at last.

“Oh, Mom.”

David could hear in the silent line, the unmistakable hitched in breath, the rusting of fabric to face, his mother trying to give strong front for her son. He knew only to wait with a soft a quiet as he could find until she recovered.

“I hate the quiet.” Another silence. “Anyway,” she said gently. “It’s late. I should let you get to sleep. I love you, sweetheart.”

“Love you too. Goodnight, mom.”

The line clicked quiet.

David sat motionless on the edge of the bed, the repair slip in one hand, the cooling phone in the other. The melody moved through him again, wandering, unresolved, never arriving where it seemed destined to land. He had fallen asleep to it a thousand times without ever asking where it came from.

The cello lay open at the foot of the bed.

Something had moved from atmosphere into fact.

And fact, however partial, carried its own narcotic authority.

He lay back slowly, the slip resting against his chest. The vent continued breathing overhead. Somewhere beyond the sealed hotel glass a snowplow dragged metal against pavement.

And then:

firelight, ash lifting into wind, a field bright with ruin.
