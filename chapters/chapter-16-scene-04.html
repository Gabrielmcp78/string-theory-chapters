IV. Disappearance | Chorale (senza vibrato)
Largo, quasi immobile

Dawn flexed its muscles over the horizon, peeling shadows from the buildings and laying down a mesh of light that crept, inch by inch, across the apartment floor. For the first time in days, maybe years, David found the silence restorative, a salve rather than a threat. The city’s collective pulse, so frantic in the blue hours, seemed to have forgotten him, or at least agreed to leave him alone for a moment of grace.

He sat back from the desk, vertebrae protesting with a full suite of pops and grinds, and regarded the finished work: two hundred and sixteen measures of score, each bar annotated with sidereal notations and the occasional wry aside in Latin. The message, inked in the lower margin of the final page, glowed in the dawn’s oblique angle. Even now, the fermata at the bottom—an arc with a single dot—struck him as a joke that only he and the universe would ever fully appreciate.

He rolled the stack with care, not wanting to crease the pages, and slid them into the manila envelope he’d scavenged from a neighbor’s recycling bin the night before. The act of sealing the flap with a strip of masking tape felt less like closing a container and more like completing a ritual, a minor sacrament in the new faith of deferred selfhood.

There wasn’t much to pack. A change of clothes, a half-full bottle of prescription anti-inflammatories, the wallet (stripped of ID but heavy with unspent laundry tokens), and the tuning fork. The cello case, emptied of loose mutes and pencils, waited at the center of the floor, a pale stain of wood dust forming a halo beneath its curved back. He circled it, slow and reverent, as if unsure how to begin a goodbye.

He knelt, balanced the instrument on one thigh, and ran the pad of his thumb along the neck, from nut to endpin. The vibrations that answered were dry, spectral, a reminder that the thing could still sing but preferred, for now, to rest. He found himself thinking not of the performances, not even of the viral videos, but of the long hours before the first note, when the world contracted to the simple geometry of hand, string, and the space in between.

He placed the cello in its case, adjusted the velvet supports, and pressed down on the lid until the latches clacked into place. The sound, sharp and final, echoed through the empty room, setting a few loose sheaves of paper trembling on the desk.

He stood, envelope in hand, and hesitated. The rules of physics allowed for infinite points of exit, but tradition demanded ceremony. He propped the envelope against the closed cello case, aligning the seam with the instrument’s scroll, as if the two objects were in conversation.

The walls, dense with notation, radiated a low-grade heat, as if the equations themselves were exothermic. For a moment, he considered wiping them clean—an act of erasure, a retreat into the myth of a tabula rasa. But he left them. The walls would hold the puzzles, the traps, the solutions and decoys, for whoever needed them next. Let the world find its own way through the labyrinth.

He looped the backpack over one shoulder and crossed the room, pausing only at the threshold. Here, in the wedge of light from the open door, he allowed himself a last audit of the space: the empty glass, the ghostly rectangle where the laptop had been, the smudged path his feet had worn into the linoleum. It was all as he’d left it, only lighter, as if the act of composition had untethered a ballast he’d never wanted in the first place.
