I.  The Lounge

[Moderato inquieto]

The faculty lounge, a misnomer if ever there was one, resisted the notion of gathering as ardently as a well-cultivated Petri dish resists sterilization. The battered vinyl sofa offered the tactile experience of a low-wage dentist’s waiting room. The adjacent Formica table—permanently indented with the ringworm pattern of neglected mugs—held the day’s most pressing object: twenty-seven freshly minted copies of David’s manifesto.

They were not professionally bound, nor even collated by the standards of a competent office clerk, but rather stapled at the upper left in a way that forced the reader’s thumb to snap each page over the next, like breaking the seal on a series of illicit confessions. The paper itself was heavy, faintly embossed with the tooth of a specialty stock. On the title page, a dense spray of notation—treble and bass clefs entwined with Dirac brackets—created a visual contradiction that recoiled at the prospect of linear reading. Fermion operators, scrawled across the grand staff lines like parasitic instructions, announced the document’s subtitle:

“A Physical Manifesto of Cosmic Resonance:

Music, Mathematics, and the Harmonic Genesis of Genius.”

Each copy bore its own ecosystem of secondary stains: a burnt-umber thumbprint left by the departmental secretary, who handled the batch with the caution of a hazmat technician; a faint, almost ceremonial dusting of rosin from the morning’s cello practice, embedded in the fiber; a corner already curled by a junior faculty member’s triple-shot Americano. In the margin, a tiny bloodstain—hardly more than a pinprick—attested to David’s longstanding cuticle-nibbling habit.

The first to notice the pile was the thermodynamics chair, a man with a gait engineered for ostentatious suffering and a wardrobe in the improbable hues of burnt peach and suicide blue. He hovered at the coffee station, eyeing the pile with proprietary skepticism. His fingers, stained to the knuckle by dry-erase markers, hovered over the nearest copy before retracting with visible distaste.

He murmured, “Christ,” as if the word itself were a hedge against contamination, and circled the pile, seeking a route to the refrigerator that did not pass directly through the zone of potential responsibility. He executed a flawless avoidance maneuver, retrieved his non-dairy creamer, and departed without once breaking eye contact with the document—or with David, now hovering at the perimeter.

David, for his part, was already rehearsing the impossible. He had not slept since the previous afternoon, barring the micro-naps induced by caffeine withdrawal. His shirt was not just wrinkled but pleated by the mechanics of stress, the left cuff permanently tinged blue by a spill of Pilot G-2 ink. He’d positioned himself at the threshold, not so much a participant as a warden, his body blocking both ingress and egress with the passive menace of a border checkpoint.

He watched as the second reader, a quantum-field theorist notorious for her ability to weaponize silence, approached the table. She did not touch the pile but instead performed a forensic survey, scanning the title page and then, with a minute furrow of the brow, the copyright line at the bottom. Her lips pursed; she released a single, unsourced “Huh,” then made a show of moving her phone exactly two inches further from the document.

The others, alerted by the low hum of mutual dread, entered in staggered pairs: the string theorist (tie adorned with illegible equations), the applied mathematician whose dueling hemispheres warred daily over whether to despise or condescend to physicists, the ancient emeritus who believed all knowledge past 1968 was morally bankrupt. They formed a loose, self-sorting cluster at the end of the table, each finding a reason not to touch the document, their eyes meeting above the pile in silent, communal bafflement.

Only the young graduate student—face still animated by the hope that academia would someday be more than a mechanism for debt accrual and existential scarring—dared to engage. She approached the pile with the hesitant reverence of a person handling the relics of a recently canonized saint. Her name tag read Rebecca. She lifted a copy, weighed it, and thumbed through the first few pages, brow knitting as the logic lapsed from baroque to lunatic within the first two paragraphs.

She read a line aloud, her voice soft but unashamed: “The cosmic background hum—a B-flat just at the threshold of measurable wave energy—is the root resonance from which all structure arises: in physics, in cells, even in cognition. Patterns echo patterns. Harmony recurses.” She stopped, uncertain whether to be amused or alarmed, then set the document down with a care that was almost motherly.

David saw it all: the department chair’s thumb hovering then retreating, the microsecond of fear on the theorist’s face, the way even the grad student’s hope could not withstand the density of his prose. He watched as, one by one, the copies became islands on the Formica surface, each a quarantine zone, untouched and unremarked except for the gradually spreading perimeter of social discomfort they engendered.

He stood at the threshold, right hand twitching with the restless energy of a musician denied his instrument, fingers flexing in the air as if playing an inaudible ostinato. His left arm, pressed hard against his ribcage, cradled a fresh stack of staff paper annotated with the next phase of his project: the translation of resonance from thought to deed.

The silence had not grown quieter, but it had a barometric density, as if the room itself were holding its breath. The air vibrated with all that was left unsaid—that David wasn’t just eccentric, but somehow defective. Contagious.

Rebecca glanced at him. Instead of solidarity, she offered a tight, pitying smile—the smile reserved for the terminally ill.

David returned the smile, or tried to. His lips shaped the gesture, but the rest of his face did not follow, and the effect was more grimace than greeting. He pivoted on his heel, moving with a speed that betrayed the depth of his disappointment, and exited the lounge in three long strides, leaving behind the scent of sweat and failed ambition.

As he moved down the corridor, the muffled voices receded, replaced by the familiar undertones of his inner soundtrack. His fingers, now unoccupied, resumed their phantom playing—drawing invisible arcs through the air, pinky keeping tempo against his trousers. He imagined the music of the spheres as a real, audible thing, vibrating through the bones of the building.

He did not pause to check if anyone followed. He already knew the answer.

Behind him, in the lounge, the manifesto sat like a failed experiment—unobserved, unmeasured, and already decaying toward equilibrium.




II.  The Lecture

[Andante - Poi smarrito]

The lecture hall, though acoustically designed for clarity, favored the propagation of uncertainty. Its sloped floor and parabolic ceiling ensured that even the smallest student-generated anxiety found its way to the front and roosted behind the podium. The chalkboard, an uninterrupted expanse of matte green, bore the layered residue of six consecutive weeks’ worth of advanced quantum mechanics. Each equation, half-erased and half-rewritten, formed the sedimentary record of Lang’s descent.

Of the thirty-six names on the roster, only fourteen students occupied the tiered seats today, most arranged in the defensive pattern of an endangered species. The stragglers—two earnest undergraduates and a sullen TA—occupied the penultimate row, close enough to be noticed but far enough to signal a lack of complicity.

Dr. Lang stood at the chalkboard, a thumbprint of white dust already smeared across his left cheekbone. The opening ten minutes had been routine. But from the moment he uncased the cello and propped it against the edge of the lectern, the proceedings acquired the doomed energy of an open-casket funeral.

He began, as always, with a demonstration: “You must recall that any system, when sufficiently isolated, reveals not just its own structure but the topology of the universe in which it is embedded.” He punctuated the point with a glissando, fingers skidding up the A-string. “Listen—don’t just analyze. Let the overtone tell you what the theory cannot.”

The room was not equipped for performance. The ceiling tiles vibrated in the afterglow of every bow stroke. The stale air seemed to refract the sound, folding it back on itself so that each note acquired a blurred, shattering shadow.

Lang pivoted from the cello to the chalkboard with the disjointed grace of a man whose proprioception had been mapped to a non-Euclidean geometry. He diagrammed a closed curve, annotated it with Hamiltonians, then in a single motion swept the equations aside and replaced them with a musical staff. His handwriting, normally precise, now oscillated wildly: clefs elongated to parody, quarter notes bristling with fermion arrows, bar lines slanted as if fleeing gravity.

“Consider the structure of the Bach Suite No. 5—Sarabande, for instance.” He drew the first two measures with the deliberate slowness of a man bandaging a wound. The sound was raw, the instrument tuned a microtone sharp. “Do you see? The voice leading. It is a quantum walk. Each note, a localized excitation. The rhythm, a measure of temporal uncertainty.”

The students exchanged glances—a Doppler-shift of skepticism and embarrassment—but he pressed forward. “You mustn’t think of time as a scalar. It’s a composite, a superposition of duration and directionality. Look—here—”

He snapped a stick of chalk clean between thumb and index, and drew a pair of coupled wave equations annotated with notations from the Bach fugue. “The resonance pattern here,” he said, voice crescendoing, “mirrors exactly the curvature of spacetime during the 1687 solar maximum! The mathematics are trivial if you just—if you just—” He broke off, breath catching in his throat. He stared at the line of chalk dust accumulating on his sleeve.

Lang resumed, alternating between cello and chalk, each transition more jarring than the last. The musical phrases became fragmentary, the equations denser. At times he abandoned speech altogether, letting the cello’s voice stand in for what language could no longer articulate.

His hair listed in two directions at once. His right hand, powdered with rosin and sweat, left residue on everything it touched. By the end of the hour, the chalkboard was a palimpsest of sound and symbol, the green field cross-hatched with notation that looked, from the back of the hall, less like a proof and more like a frantic plea for rescue.

The breakdown, when it arrived, was almost a relief.

Lang, mid-phrase, stopped. The bow hovered a centimeter above the strings, his left hand frozen in a grotesque parody of first position. For a long moment, nothing happened. Then, with a sound halfway between a cough and a sob, he stared at the center aisle as if expecting someone—or something—to materialize.

He whispered, “It’s so obvious,” though to whom it was not clear. “The coefficient, the universal constant—it’s right here in the harmony, the convergence.” He repeated the phrase, each time quieter, until the words dissolved into breath.

His fingers, slick with chalk and rosin, lost their grip on the bow. It clattered to the floor, rolled to the edge of the dais, and dropped into the well between podium and whiteboard.

The students gathered their things with the decorum of mourners at a closed-casket service. The TA closed her laptop with a soft, respectful click. No one spoke until the last of them had passed into the corridor.

Lang remained where he was, eyes fixed on a point above the centerline, his breathing shallow and arrhythmic.

And then, in a moment begging for solitude and darkness, the opposite arrived. Rune Ishikawa silently slid into the room. He flipped on the second bank of overhead lights, and the 119 Hz. zap of the fluorescent tubes dragged David’s brain into the present.




III.  The Fall

[Grave con precisione]

If the department had a script for academic defenestration, Ishikawa was both author and sole practitioner. He entered the now-empty lecture hall with the authority of a federal agent executing a search warrant, each footfall a reminder that discipline—like entropy—always increased in the universe of higher education.

He said nothing as he took in the aftermath. His gaze swept the chalkboard, the cello, the scattered residue of genius and madness.

Lang sat behind the lectern, one hand resting on the cello’s scroll, the other trailing a white rime of chalk dust across the edge of the desk. His eyes, glassy and slow to focus, tracked Ishikawa’s approach like a man drunken and stooped.

“Dr. Lang,” said Ishikawa, the words as clear and neutral as distilled water. “May I have a moment?”

He did not wait for a reply, but simply set his briefcase precisely at the nearest desk, removed his lambskin gloves, and interlaced his fingers with the solemnity of a surgeon about to pronounce a patient DNR.

“David,” he began, letting the first name ring for a fraction too long, “I won’t waste your time—or mine. The department has received a number of communications regarding your recent… presentations.” A flick of the wrist, and a manila folder materialized. “Student complaints. Attendance irregularities. Administrative queries.” He let the folder rest, unopened.

Lang looked at the folder, then at Ishikawa, as if checking to make sure that both were not the result of a waking dream. “It’s not what you think,” he said at last. “I have documentation. The connections are sound.”

Rune did not blink. “No one is questioning your intelligence, David. But there are processes. We’ve had this conversation before, haven’t we?” His tone was sympathetic, but each word was sanded to a clinical edge.

Lang’s eyes darted to the chalkboard, searching for an anchor, finding only the massacre of notation: an orphaned Bach motif, a phrase circled in blue that read simply PROOF PENDING. “There’s more,” he said. “You need to see the resonance data. I can—”

Rune cut him off with a raised hand. “I’ve seen your draft, David. No one is doubting the boldness of your approach. But this—” and here he gestured at the board, at the cello, at Lang himself—“this is not tenable.”

Lang felt a flush rising, a prickling heat. He became acutely aware of his own posture, the oil slick of sweat, the coarseness of his breath.

Rune’s eyes narrowed—kindly, but without mercy. “David, I’m putting you on administrative leave. Medical, for now. You’ll still be paid. Your classes will be covered.” He set the folder flat. “We’ll need you to surrender your university credentials by the end of the week.”

“Rune, please,” David began. “I understand that you don’t see it, but that doesn’t mean it’s not there. How can you possibly do this to me? I’ve worked my ass off for twenty years.”

David’s voice rose, half weeping, half screaming. It would later be clear that people in the surrounding buildings heard this diatribe as if broadcast directly to them.

“Does that make me certifiable? Or does that make all of you relics? You’re all just priests on the great hill! You don’t want to look. This isn't physics; it’s a closed framework, backed up by the bullshit of accepted norms and the you-pat-my-back field of modern science! I trusted you, Rune! What the hell am I doing here if you don’t even…?”

The tears had taken over. The anger gone, the realization now: “This is all I have. This department is my family. You're taking it. My God… I’m a fool.”

Rune plucked a silk handkerchief from his inner breast pocket, its offering—delicately pinched twixt index and middle finger—his solitary gesture of consolation. Then, with the restraint of butterflied marble, replied, “I know that it feels that way now. But you must understand that everyone cares deeply for you, David. And that, in fact, is exactly why this is what is happening now.”

He picked up the pen, handed it to David, and pushed the folder toward him. “I need your signature.”

“Rune, I’m begging—”

“David, there is nothing more for which you can beg.” Rune’s voice was no longer asking. “Your signature, David. Now.”

His tone dropped an octave for the coup de grâce. “It’s not forever. You can apply for reinstatement at the start of next semester. But for now, David… I really must have your signature here… and here. Let’s not make this any harder than it already is—for both of us.”

The logic of the thing, brutal and inevitable, was already unspooling behind his eyes.

A silence, thick as the air before a summer thunderstorm, filled the space between them.

Lang became aware that students were lingering in the corridor. He imagined their faces: the shock, the pity.

His hand, still powdered with chalk, trembled as he reached for the pen and, with fingers coerced by the inevitable forces of nature and history, affixed his signature there… and there.

Desperately in need of distraction, David moved to the cello case. He tried, with shaking hands, to fit the instrument inside, but his coordination betrayed him. The endpin snagged on the lip, the bow slipped and clattered to the floor. He stooped to retrieve it, but his vision blurred, and for a moment he could not locate the object.

Ishikawa made no move to assist. He merely watched as Lang assembled the last remnants of his dignity.

At the threshold, Lang paused and turned to look back into the room, half-expecting a final word—some offering of an olive branch from Rune, some reassurance that this really would take care of itself given time.

But Rune was already repacking his folder, already donning the gloves, already setting the stage for whatever minor catastrophe awaited the department next. He had moved on, and in so doing had demanded David find ways to do the same.

David walked into the hallway, his gait slightly uneven, the cello case banging against his knee at an interval of every third step. He heard the doors swing shut behind him, the hydraulic hiss of closure, and knew with sudden, perfect certainty that the resonance he’d sought would not be found here.

He turned the corner. The corridor ahead was empty, the only evidence of his passing a faint trail of white chalk dust leading into the wilds of institutional exile.




IV.  The Quartet

[Adagio doloroso]

The rehearsal room, once a crucible of conspiratorial energy and off-color jokes, had been scrubbed to a sterile echo chamber by the time Lang arrived. The fluorescent tubes overhead burned with the impersonal zeal of an autopsy theater, and the grand piano—usually flanked by a clutter of cases and half-drunk water bottles—sat alone at the far end, its lid shut, its voice silenced.

His own chair was gone, leaving a kidney-shaped void in the semicircle where the quartet always convened. The music stand, battered and proud, was folded and propped against the wall like a retired soldier. On the side table, his sheet music had been collected into a tidy packet, the pages squared and bound with a length of red twine more suited to a gift than an eviction notice.

The other musicians stood near the window, conferring in muted tones. Roger Linn noticed Lang’s entrance and nudged the others into a tableau of awkward solidarity. The second violinist, whose habit of biting the inside of her cheek had left a permanent dimple, nodded once and looked away.

The new cellist—a graduate student Lang knew only by the breakneck tempo of her gum chewing—loitered nearby, arms wrapped around her case like it might bolt at any second. Her eyes scanned the room in clipped, jittery bursts, as though rehearsing for a spotlight she hadn’t asked for. She didn’t speak. She didn’t have to. Her presence alone confirmed what the others were too polite to say outright: Lang’s chair was no longer his.

Linn cleared his throat, the sound brittle in the over-lit room.

“David,” he said, gingerly, “good to see you. I, ah, wasn’t sure you’d gotten the message.” He smiled, but it was the smile of a dentist about to broach a nerve.

Lang stood just inside the doorway, music packet in one hand, cello case in the other. He looked at the empty space where his chair had been, then at the cluster of his former colleagues, all of whom seemed to occupy the exact same square foot of linoleum despite their best efforts to suggest a welcoming openness.

“We’ve had some… communication from the department,” Linn continued, eyes darting from Lang’s face to the floor and back again. “Just temporary, until things are sorted out. You understand.”

Lang did not reply. He set the cello case down and rested the music packet on top, fingers lingering on the twine. The knot was tight but elegantly executed, and unmistakably Roger. A double loop with the ends trimmed to matching lengths—something he had done to present prepared sheet music since they were in high school. It was, in its own way, a benediction—a signal that Linn, and by extension, all of them, had wanted to make the severance painless, or at least quick.

The grad student said nothing, only hugged her case tighter and shuffled one shoe behind the other.

Linn continued. “We’ve been asked to perform at the Chancellor’s reception next week,” he said. “We’re doing the Dvořák.”

At that Eleanor tapped Roger gently on the elbow—gesture enough to make clear she would take it for a moment.

“David darling, you must know this isn’t us. It’s politics and, well, goodness… it’s goddamned funding. You know how this works. And I’m sure when you’re ready, we can… we will work you back in. Oh, darling David, I want you to take advantage of this, yes? Recharge. Reset. We all need these moments sometimes.” She glanced back to Roger as if to signal that was all she had.

“Yes,” Roger jumped back in. “Take all the time you need.”

Lang considered the group, the room, the absence of his own chair. He nodded, just once, the gesture so small it might have been a tic. He reached for the cello but stopped. Instead, he untied the music packet, let the sheets fall open on the table, and scanned the top page. His own notations—tiny arrows, circled accidentals, the occasional FIX THIS in capital letters—ran in a red current along the margins. Their transcribbling stood testament to a shared history of practice, of commitment, of joint struggle towards a threshold of excellence and the thousand small accommodations that had once made the group a single organism.

He closed the folder, replaced the twine, and tucked it under his arm. Roger moved towards him and gently placed a solemn hand on David’s shoulder. David turned back to meet his friends eyes—eyes that held decades of words that only silence dare say.

“I know, my friend,” David whispered, touching his hand to Roger’s.

“Good luck with the Dvořák,” he said to them all, and turned for the door.



V.  The Journey

[Largo - Poi tempo primo (memoria)]

David aimed to leave the building—his bow no longer welcome. The adrenaline of the day’s rejections, now complete, began to burn off, and for the first time the emotional weight of what had happened began to toll. A tone so pure and cruel that, without armor, it cut directly to his core.

He tried to stop it—the way one tries not to laugh with a mouth full of drink. He walked in a trance of too much feeling and found himself beneath the grand arc of the atrium that connected the two wings of the music school to the old chapel over the hill.

He could not tell if, when he opened his mouth, words or screams or wordless sorrow would come forth. It scared him. It hurt him. But he no longer had any choice. The blood in his soul had to pour, and he knew it must be heard.

Bracing himself, he felt a tear—then many—rise and spill. The first rolled down his cheek and rested in the fold of his lips, granting permission for his mouth to open and utter the verdict he could no longer hold. What came forth was simple, sad, efficient, and full of pain.

“I am utterly… alone.”

The weight of it pressed into him, buckling him at the knees, and he fell into the dew-lilted evening grass, as if it might take pity on him and grow tall enough to hide him amidst its benevolent blades.

He stayed like that for a long time, palms pressed to the wet ground, the scent of soil and iron in his nose. The atrium roof above him caught the city’s thin light, bending it into a shallow parabola of gold—almost cathedral, almost womb. Through the high windows, a single star blinked against the ribs of the ceiling, and for an instant the atrium became its own kind of chapel: consecrated by the shibboleth of defeat and fatigue.

A breeze passed through the open walkway, carrying the faint smell of candied walnuts from the student café across the quad. It was such a small, human smell that it undid him completely. He closed his eyes, and in that scent—that absurd sweetness—another night came rushing back: the hill behind the music school, Celeste’s laughter ricocheting off the amphitheater’s stone arc, the two of them eating those ridiculous walnuts, sticky and warm from a street vendor, while the stars blinked like half-forgotten notes on a staff too vast to read.

The memories rose unbidden now, spurred onward by the first encroachment.

“Well, I’m right here, big boy,” she said, grinning. “Come and get me, Lang!”

She ran up the stairs, knocking primer cans over. David lunged after her like a lion after its prey. Up they went, two flights of century-old timber. A wild leap sent him shoulder-first into the hallway wall, leaving a splash of celadon green across the plaster. Celeste, halfway up the attic steps, pointed and laughed.

“My, what big and celarium green footprints you have, my conqueror!”

He chased her into the attic—a vast, magical space covering the whole expanse of the house below, eaved windows front and back, twin dormers on either side, each with a built-in bench beneath. They reached the open floor and met in a tangle of laughter and breath and motion—enraptured by the possibility of a life just beginning.

At daybreak they collapsed in the bay window overlooking the street. From there you could see the dome of the observatory glinting beyond campus. They lay there short of breath—finished and beginning all at once.

“Just promise me one thing, darling,” Celeste whispered, panting and smiling from ear to ear. “Just finish this damned house before we’re eighty.”

They fell back into each other, laughing, kissing, breathing as one. He grinned—savage, tender—and in that grin was everything he would later trade for the small, private music of the moment.

It was a big ask, even then. But for years thereafter, he tried to answer it. Weekend after weekend, the tools came out; the work progressed. But the weekends grew fewer. David’s need to produce at the university consumed the rest. Soon, and as sure as night follows day, the promise gave way—making room for the need to compete.

“We’ll get to it,” he promised, year after year. After five. After ten. After fifteen. After nineteen. And then—




VI.  The Attic  Present Day

[Adagio sospeso]

She couldn’t be there when they told him that night. It wouldn’t be fair—to David, or to herself. She understood the others’ decision; when pressed to a vote, she’d even agreed with it. But she would not be there to see his eyes fall, to watch them crash down his cheeks and bury his shoulders beneath the weight of it all.

As rehearsal hour neared, she grabbed a bottle of Pinot and a glass, then found herself climbing the well-boned staircase—from the ground floor, to the second, and onward, up to the attic, untouched since the day she’d arrived.

She moved to the window overlooking East Hill, the glint of moonlight bouncing off the dome of the observatory in the distance. A sip of red wine sat warm on her lips as her right index finger fell onto the wooden framing of the window seat—the only alteration to the house that had ever been finished. Her fingertip traced the etched infinity of a heart-shaped loop—the initials D and C enclosed within its curve.

She had always felt a sixth sense for David. Since the moment Marcus had introduced them—barely three days into graduate school—she had known where he was, even what he was thinking. It never took much; she only needed the courage to look.

She closed her eyes, her finger still tracing, and saw him—felt him—lying under a canopy of some kind. Protected yet exposed, in pain yet clear-eyed. She tried not to force the vision; trying always made it fade. Just breathe, she whispered, and the words began to come.

No—a word. Singular. Familiar.

Alone.

She opened her eyes. The attic was silent but charged, the moonlight catching in the dust like suspended time. She lifted the glass, drank, and whispered his name to no one.




VII.  Home

[Andante - Crescendo poco a poco]

The apartment had always been an artifact of entropy, but now it transcended mere disorder. Lang entered to find the air thick with the fermentation of neglect: sour milk, old coffee, and the high, ammoniac edge of decay. The windows, streaked with the residue of a thousand rainstorms, admitted just enough light to reveal the full spectrum of his madness.

Every available surface was colonized by his project. Sheets of staff paper were taped to the walls in vertical progressions that defied any rational key signature. The staff lines, drawn with mechanical precision, now bent and warped as marginalia consumed them: arrows, cross-references, entire passages blotted out and rewritten at impossible angles. Equations bled into notation; Fourier transforms looped themselves into measures of Ligeti; a single, obstinate E-flat rode at the apex of a pyramid of Schumann lieder and Feynman diagrams.

The kitchen table had become a sacrificial altar to caffeine and computation. The percolator burbled on, its glass carafe stained to the opacity of crude oil, while an adjacent mug overflowed with a slurry of grounds and blue fibers from a chewed ballpoint pen.

The cello—his axis mundi—stood in the center, supported only by a jury-rigged sling of duct tape and twine. It was surrounded by a fortress of open books: Dirac’s Principles of Quantum Mechanics, Hofstadter’s Gödel, Escher, Bach, three different biographies of Newton, and a stack of yellowed journals. Close to the window, a sheaf of napkins—each stained a different shade of coffee—fanned out across the radiator like offerings to an absent god.

Lang moved through the space as if following coordinates only he could perceive. He picked up a nub of charcoal from the windowsill and inscribed a new formula above the couch. The wall, already darkened from a year’s worth of such graffiti, accepted the addition without complaint. He stepped back, considered the alignment, and nodded.

“Temporal resonance pattern,” he muttered, voice hoarse. “It’s not a function of the note—it’s the interval. The ratio. Harmonic constant.”

He set the charcoal down and circled the cello. He plucked the A-string, counted the decay, then played a quick, agitated sequence from the Prelude to Suite No. 1. The sound, amplified by the bare walls, shimmered and died.

He made a notation in the margin of a nearby printout, circled it twice, then returned to the kitchen for more coffee. The act of pouring was an afterthought; he drank it black, grounds and all.

Back at the center, he lowered himself onto the floor, assembling a constellation of books and notepads around him. He read a passage from Einstein’s collected letters, then cross-referenced it with a line from Hildegard of Bingen’s antiphons, tracing the intervallic leaps between the two with a ruler and a bit of red string.

He was, in that moment, neither hungry nor tired, neither confident nor afraid. He was a singularity: all vectors collapsed, all noise absorbed. The rest of the world could not touch him here.

He worked until the only illumination came from the LCD of his ancient desk lamp, which buzzed and flickered at regular intervals like a dying satellite.

Sometime after midnight, he played another passage on the cello—softer now, less a test than a benediction. The note hung in the air, and for a moment the universe seemed to bend toward him, attentive.

He wrote the phrase, “All is one” at the bottom of a fresh sheet of staff paper, then fell asleep where he sat, fingers still curled around the charcoal, forehead pressed to the notation as if by force of gravity alone.




VIII.  Solitude

[Adagio meditativo]

The city was in REM sleep when Lang awoke on the apartment floor. His first instinct was to reach for the cello—to confirm that its wood and strings still held.

He dragged himself upright, using the chair’s leg as a fulcrum. The last time he had eaten was a detail lost to memory. The walls, painted a neutral eggshell, now glowed faintly with the ghostly blue of the equations themselves.

He played without tuning, the instrument’s pegs calibrated to the logic of a new reality. The first note—a low, humming C—vibrated through the walls, bouncing off every equation and fragment of staff paper. With eyes closed, Lang could feel the resonance as an electric current: the A-string buzzing in sympathy with the irrational numbers, the G swelling against the Fibonacci sequence.

He lost himself in the process, the movements automatic, each shift of position corresponding to a concept he had lost the language to describe. The Prelude became a Fourier series, each phrase a component in the infinite sum of himself. Every interval, every overtone, mapped onto the curvature of some distant spacetime, or the granular uncertainty that structured his reality.

The tempo accelerated. He was not performing; he was exorcising—purging each equation from the walls and into the instrument, transforming the theoretical into the audible.

He opened his eyes once, just long enough to see the room ripple at the edges, the equations rearranging themselves into new, fluid forms. His heart thudded in his chest, a polyrhythm against the cello’s drone. The boundaries between sound and number, between body and instrument, began to erode.

He closed his eyes again and pressed harder. The rosin on the bow ignited a friction that left a faint trace of smoke. His left hand began to bleed at the cuticle—a thin line of red decorating the strings. He ignored it.

He heard himself whisper, “The coefficient is in the harmony, the universal constant hidden in the overtones.”

The music crested, then collapsed. Lang let the bow fall, waiting for the universe to signal that it had heard him.

Nothing happened. Or rather, everything did: a slow pulse of blue from the wall, a new arrangement of numbers at the corner of his vision, the faint taste of copper on his tongue.

He played another note, softer this time, and in the instant before sound and silence merged, he understood that the music would not save him—not in any way he could recognize, or share. But it would continue, as would he.

Lang lowered the cello and flexed his aching hands. He looked around the apartment, no longer able to distinguish where the notation ended and the wall began.


The only way to solve for the Universal Constant is through Self.

Through Self… alone.
