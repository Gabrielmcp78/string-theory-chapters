I.1  SUBJECT  |  Buffalo, NY          Present Day

[Andante inquieto]

[|  Travel  |]

The shuttle from the airport had no suspension, or perhaps it simply chose not to use it. Each pothole translated directly into spine, the jolts accumulating like small grievances filed but never addressed. The driver kept his eyes fixed on the road ahead with a solemnity that looked like ritual, but bordered on carelessness, refusing even the briefest courtesies of the rearview mirror.

A laminated sign above the windshield read:


WELCOME TO THE NORTH EASTERN SCIENTIFIC EDUCATORS ANNUAL CONSORTIUM.

NO FOOD. NO DRINK. NO QUESTIONS.


A persistent though fluctuant buzz threaded through the vents, carrying a narrow interval he wanted desperately to ignore. The forty-six-minute ride lasted twelve minutes according to the onboard display, forty-seven according to David’s watch, and a near eternity according to his mind. He found no reason to trust any of them.

Across the aisle, a young postdoc held her laptop open to a half-completed grant proposal. The progress bar had been stuck at fourteen percent for the duration of the ride, though no uploads were in progress.

The shuttle pulled up to the hotel smoothly, as if arriving exactly on time for something important that had already happened without them. No one moved as they disembarked.

“Professor?” The driver called back to David, still staring out the window in the back of the bus. “Professor?” Nothing. Like he was watching something no one else could hear.







I.2   ANSWER   |   MIT - Boston, MA                          April 11, 2005

[Con tensione, andante       21 years ago]

[|  Consensus  |]

“You’re not listening,” David said. “I’m not saying music is mathematics. I’m saying they’re dialects. Same language. Different mouths.”

Marcus sprawled across the single chair he’d commandeered, legs draped over one arm, beer balanced on his chest with the casual entitlement of someone who’d never doubted his place in a room.

“Dialects.” He let the word hang, flat and unimpressed. “And what’s the native tongue, then? What’s the ur-language they’re both bastardizing?”

“Consciousness.”

Marcus snorted. “Jesus Christ.”

“I’m serious, damn it.”

“Oh, I know you’re serious, Dave. That’s the problem.” Marcus swung his legs down, suddenly alert. “You’re always serious about the most dangerous possible thing you could be serious about. It’s like watching someone juggle grenades and congratulate themselves for their hand-eye coordination.”

Celeste sat on the floor opposite David, her back against the radiator that clanked arrhythmically. It was a percussive commentary she’d long since stopped noticing. She held her beer with both hands, the way someone cradles a warm cup in winter. Watching. Always watching first.

“What’s dangerous about it?” she asked.

“What’s dangerous?” Marcus leaned forward. “He’s proposing that the entire edifice of physics, which, by the way, has done pretty well with things like not dying in childbirth and satellites, is just one translation of a consciousness-language we haven’t decoded yet. You know what they call people who think like that?”

“Visionaries?” Celeste offered.

“Unemployed.”

David’s jaw tightened. “You’re reducing it.”

“I’m not reducing anything. You’re inflating. There’s a difference.” Marcus jabbed a finger toward him. “You want to know what kills careers, David? It’s not being wrong. Being right about something nobody’s ready to hear. And you don’t have the patience for that. You want the revelation now. You want to skip the thirty years of proving yourself and jump straight to the Nobel speech.”

“That’s not true.”

“It is, man. It absolutely is. I’ve known you for two years. You’re the smartest person I’ve ever met with absolutely no survival instinct for academia.”

“Maybe academia should work differently.”

“Maybe.” Marcus stood, pacing now, the small room forcing him into a tight loop. “Maybe it should. But it doesn’t. And you know what happens to people who try to change it from the outside? Nothing. They become footnotes. Crackpots. That guy who could have done something if he’d just played the game. Put in the time. Earned the right to break the rules.”

Celeste watched David’s face: the way his eyes went unfocused when he was running internal calculations, the micro-twitch at the corner of his mouth that meant he was suppressing something.

“You think I want to play games,” David said, indignantly.

“Christ, David. You really don’t get it, do you? These aren’t games! They’re gates. You can’t get to the interesting work without passing through them first.”

“And if the gates are designed to filter out exactly the kind of thinking that might actually—”

“Then you beat them.” Marcus was in David’s space now, looming. “You beat them at their own game, and then you flip the table. Last I checked, that’s how revolutions work. Not from some grad student’s dorm room with a half-baked theory about consciousness and music and… what? Harmonics?”

“Resonance.”

“Resonance?” Marcus laughed, but it wasn’t kind. “You want to publish a paper on resonance and expect anyone to take you seriously?”

David was on his feet before Celeste could react. “You don’t get to decide what’s serious.”

“Someone has to! Because you won’t!”

They were chest to chest now, or close enough. Marcus had three inches on him, a fact he’d never once made David feel until this moment. David’s hands were balled. Marcus’s chin was lifted, daring.

“Hey.” Celeste’s voice cut through like a lightning bolt. Sharp. Singular.

They both turned.

She hadn’t moved from the floor, but something in her posture had shifted: a stillness that demanded attention precisely because it refused to compete for it.

“Sit down.” Not a request. “Both of you.”

Marcus opened his mouth.

“Sit.”

He sat. David remained standing for three more seconds, then lowered himself back to the floor, shoulder blades finding the bed frame again.

Celeste let the silence build. Outside, a bus groaned through its gears, Doppler-shifting from minor to major as it passed.

“You’re both right,” she said finally. “That’s the problem.”

Marcus crossed his arms. “Explain.”

“David’s right that the connections are real. There’s something that lines up. The math underneath the music. Call it resonance, or pattern recognition, you can call it pareidolia. But it’s there. And it wants to be understood.”

David exhaled. Celeste held up a hand.

“And Marcus is also right that it doesn’t matter. Not yet. At least, not the way you want it to matter. No one in their right mind storms the gates with a manifesto. You have to earn the right to be taken seriously. It takes time, David… and compromise. And I know, compromise feels like betrayal when you think you can see something nobody else can see.”

“I can see something nobody else—”

“I know.” Her voice was gentle. “I believe you. We both do. But belief isn’t proof. And proof takes time. And you don’t have to solve everything tonight.”

The radiator clanked.

Marcus reached for another beer, cracked it open, and took a long drink. When he spoke again, the fight had drained from his voice.

“I’m not trying to kill your idea, Dave. I’m trying to keep you alive long enough to prove it.”

David stared at the floor. His fingers moved against his thigh, unconscious, compulsive: playing a passage only he could hear.

“What if it takes thirty years,” he said. “What if I prove it and nobody’s left who remembers I was crazy for thinking it?”

“Then you’ll be vindicated.”

“That’s not the same as being heard.”

Celeste reached across the space between them and put her hand on David’s knee.

“We’ll remember,” she said. “Marcus and I. We’ll know what you were trying to say before you could say it.”

Marcus raised his beer in something that was almost a toast, but was undeniably an olive branch.

“To the lunatics.”

David looked at him. Something passed between them; old resentment meeting older affection. And finally… finally, his shoulders dropped.

“To the lunatics.”







I.3   SUBJECT   |    Buffalo, NY         Present Day

[Moderato grottesco]

[|  Conference  |]

The registration table was arranged with military precision, except for the line marked:

LAST NAMES BEGINNING WITH

UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE.

David hovered before deciding he belonged in the H–R line, though the volunteer behind the table glanced at his badge and said, “Oh. You’re one of those,” in a tone suggesting the category was well-known but rarely defined.

The exhibitor hall vibrated with intellectual self-importance. Posters leaned on collapsible frames, each promising incremental advances in fields that seemed to repel one another. A theorist lectured animatedly to a graduate student who nodded while staring into the middle distance. Someone dropped a stack of pamphlets advertising a paid workshop on interdisciplinary synergy; no one offered to help.

A senior physicist explained topological insulators to his winter coat. But the coat seemed unwilling to change its mind.

David kept hearing the same sound from the shuttle whispered through the projector fans, carrying the same unresolved interval. He listened to the opening plenary with the attention one reserves for a fire alarm test: dutiful, doubtful, already bracing for the second round.

His own talk was scheduled for the late afternoon. Location: The Morning Room. He wanted to believe this meant nothing, but he was beginning to find himself less and less convincing.

He couldn’t be the only one to hear it, could he? His head was racing. He could feel the frequency flutter up out of the objects he’d blamed. It had overtone resonance now, enough that he couldn’t tell which frequency was origin and which was harmonic.

At the front desk, he grabbed a ‘Local Tourist Info’ brochure and began to sketch the harmonic relationships on the rear panel.

The moment the concierge handed him a pen, yet another source, ambient and unknown, joined the chorus at precisely 137.28 Hz.

And then he saw him.

Thirty feet away, near the poster session entrance. His beard owned twenty years of grey now, thicker through the middle, reading glasses perched on his nose as he squinted at a conference program.

Dr. Julian Kline, NYU - Astrophysics Chair.

The hum surged in David’s chest with a density that sounded like recognition. As if the frequency had found something to resonate against and was doubling back on itself.

Kline looked up.

Their eyes met.

For exactly 4.47 seconds, neither moved. David watched his own name flood Kline’s face. The cascading effect: first confusion, then clarity, then something that looked remarkably like dread.

Kline turned sharply, unwilling to risk a casual, even knowing second glance. He fumbled his program, flapping at it twice as it escaped his grip and fell to the ground. Then, with a sweep to the floor that flat out looked like distaste, he reared the item of his perturbation and stormed toward the opposite corridor with the stride of a man who had just remembered an urgent appointment that never was.

The phone at the registration desk rang out. Two octaves above the root frequency. David heard it before it happened, or maybe it had been ringing all along and only now achieved audibility.

Either way, exactly as the ring octavated upwards, David swore he saw Dr. Kline jerk forward, as if someone had yanked a wire running through his spine. He caught himself against the doorframe, one hand shooting out, program fluttering to the floor. He froze there for half a breath.

Then slowly, intentionally, desiring exactly no one to have reason to notice, he straightened. Picked up the program without looking at it. And disappeared down the opposing corridor.

David looked down at his own brochure, only to notice that his hands, especially the left one with the flyer in grip, were shaking rather violently.

The concierge stood perfectly placed at the center of it all, a mid-court referee with an unobstructed view, ready to call the match if it came to that. With Kline fully in retreat, he had nowhere left to look but at David.
“Sir… are you alright?”

David flashed a slick smile of assurance at the young man to release him from further responsibility. Then he looked down. The brochure was covered in notation: Frequencies, Ratios, Harmonic intervals spiraling out from a central axis. The work was certainly written in his hand. But he had no memory of executing a single line of it.





















I.4   ANSWER   |   MIT - Boston, MA                                      May 24, 2005

[Presto drammatico       21 years ago]

[|  Phone Call  |]

Celeste’s voice hit the line like a rupture.

“You did what?”

“Exactly what I just told you.” David shot back, barreling through the NYU student union, phone nearly slapped from his hand as he pushed into the chaos of Gramercy.

“I did what had to be done,” he snapped. “I showed him.”

“Showed who?”

“Dr. Kline.”

A pause. Then her breath hitched. A soft, involuntary intake, as if the floor beneath her had shifted.

“David… you didn’t. Not the data?”

“I did. I showed him everything,” David said, voice thinning now. “The plots. The resonance curves. I thought… God, I thought he could take it.”

“You thought?”

In the background, a rustle of footsteps on tile, a muffled exhale. Then Marcus’s voice tore through the line:

“What the fuck, David?”

“Marcus, just hold on now. The moment Kline saw the data, he froze. Went white.”

“David, man. We talked about this. We agreed. No acoustic harmonic data. Not at NYU.”

“You weren’t there. It was a judgment call. I told you, I had to know. Had to see if he… I thought I had him.”

“Fuck.” Marcus’s scream detonated into the phone. Then, to Celeste: confirmation by panic.

“I’m telling you, Marcus, something hit him—hit him deep. He staggered like the numbers had gut-punched him or something.”

“You are unbelievable. Do you have any idea what this means?”

David knew exactly what it meant. The stupidity, so constant, so thick it made him dizzy. He stepped off the curb and almost vanished beneath a blur of screaming cabs slicing through the roundabout.

“Julian Kline? And you gave him resonance?” Marcus was past fury now. His voice curdled into mockery. “Congratulations, genius. It’s not theory anymore. You just took it live. You opened the goddamn door.”

“It’ll hold,” David said. “It’s not—”

“You don’t know that! Jesus, David, you’re so naïve. The whole damn world is going to come flooding in! You’ve fucked us. All of us!”

David could hear him pacing, the voice jerking sideways as Marcus turned on Celeste.

“Your boy just dragged us off a cliff.”

“Marcus, listen to me. It’s going to be okay. You have to trust me.”

“Trust you?” Marcus snapped. “After this? No. No more. I’m not doing this over the phone. Get your ass home. Now. Before you wipe out another wing of science and take our careers with you.”

“Come on, Marcus. You know I would never—”

“No, David. The only thing I know is that you’re an idiot.”

Celeste took the phone before he even felt her move.

“Idiot?” David was spiraling now. “Who’s the idiot here, huh? Me, or—”

She didn’t yell. She never did. Not when her father vanished. Not when David cracked the ceiling. Not even now.

“David,” she said, soft. Precisely.

And that was all it took.  He froze. Exhaled. Silence pressed in like gravity.

“I’m on my way.”

EPISODE I


II.1   THE INSTRUMENT SHOP  |  Buffalo, NY           Present Day

[Adagio religioso]

[|  Detour  |]

He found the shop by accident, though it felt intentional from the start. It was cleverly wedged between a boarded-up travel agency and a florist whose blooms looked more resigned than alive.

A woodcut sign in the window read:


INSTRUMENT REPAIRS. CONSULTATIONS.

QUIET. YOU CAN HEAR IT, YES?


He stared through the window like a child before a toy shop but did not enter. Just stood, mid-gaze, mid-thought, perhaps no thought at all.

The proprietor, an older woman with kind, piercing eyes, saw him lingering and shuffled towards the door.

“You will freeze if you stay like this,” she warned, as she gently ushered him inside with deft, warm hands.

The air in the shop smelled of cedar oil, brass polish, and long-kept vows.

A viola lay open-bodied on the counter, its belly split like a confession. Nearby, a cracked cello rib rested on linen, patient and poised for repair.

She gestured for him to look around, then returned to restringing the viola.

David moved among the shelves, touching nothing. Instruments in varying states of injury lined the walls. Their wounds were small. Honest. Human.

A narrow drawer: BRIDGES—FRAGILE.

Another: SOUND POSTS—DON’T LOSE.

The warnings felt like liturgy.

At the register, he watched as the old woman flipped something between gnarled fingers. An object. He felt drawn to it, as if history had been forged into its surface.

She stopped abruptly and pocketed the object. Her eyes met his. He could not tell whose impulse triggered first. They both felt the flood of recognition. Body having stepped here before. Mind having thought this moment already.

“Do I know you?” David asked.

She paused. “Do you… have a cello in for repair?”

“How did you know I played the… cello?”

She smiled but made no answer. Whatever lay in her pocket, however, answered with a faint, low shimmer, rooting the same thin interval he’d heard since the shuttle. He moved toward the sound, but as he neared, it stilled. Silenced.

Something in him contracted, then softened, like memory returning in a language he no longer spoke.

He laid his palm on the cracked neck of a cello. Its surface was warm to the touch, and carried with it the vague sense of humility.

They stood close, shoulders nearly touching, but did not turn to face one another. Understanding passed between them without words.

Moving to leave, he stopped at the threshold.

“Will I see you again?”

“You already have,” she replied.

He lingered longer than he meant. Saw less than he wanted. He left quieter than he arrived.














II.2   THE WORKSHOP   |   Buffalo, NY

[Largo irreale]

[|  Form A(1)b–Revised  |]

The signage ended at Room 6D.

Room 7B wasn’t listed. David found it by accidental inattention, half a floor past logic, behind a door that stuck before yielding with a sound like paperwork being forcibly approved.

Inside: twelve chairs, arranged in a hesitant oval. No one looked like they had chosen their seat. No one seemed as if they remembered how they’d gotten there.

At the center, a woman in charcoal held a stack of gray forms. The near-match in tone produced a subtle visual failure as she moved among the chairs; at moments, the pages seemed to vanish into her clothing, only to reappear one by one in her hand.

“This is Form A(1)b–Revised,” she instructed. Her voice had the shape of announcement but no clear origin. “Complete it as though it were A(1)a–Original. Ignore pre-filled fields unless they’re blank.”

She moved in slow orbit, placing a copy in each lap.

David glanced at his: checkboxes nested inside contradictions, diagrams that defied perspective. A discreet look at the forms to either side confirmed they were not standardized. He could not tell whether the differences were what mattered, or whether what mattered had itself been altered. He looked to the form for answers. A foolish errand.

A three-dimensional lattice was labeled:

YOUR ROLE

It seemed to shift when viewed head-on.

One section read:

SIGN HERE IF YOU PARTICIPATE

Diagonally overlaid with:

DO NOT SIGN THIS FORM.

Someone raised a hand.

“Do we… participate?”

The facilitator smiled the way statues smile when misunderstood.

“You’ll know.”

No one spoke again.

The fluorescent lights flickered with a pulse less random than rhythmic. The same unresolved interval he’d heard in the hotel vents, in the elevator buttons that lit without input. He’d counted it twice already. Or sixty-eight times. The repetitions blurred so close together that certainty felt like a category error.

The flicker emitted a tone: visual rhythm made audible.

He blinked. Squinted. The facilitator was still speaking, but her lips seemed barely to move.

“…emergent disciplinary spaces…”

The phrase arrived in fragments. Echoes with no origin.

He looked down. The diagram was changing—not morphing, but rotating emphasis. As if being read from another perspective.

He hadn’t moved.

The ink shimmered at certain angles. Notation became structure, became symbol, became nothing.

His limbs felt heavy, over-defined. Present in a way he couldn’t fully accept. The kind of weight you feel in a dream just before you fall through it.

The woman collected the forms without looking at them. When she approached to collect David’s, she stopped. Stood behind him. Stone quiet and frozen… cold. Then moments later, at the front of the room, she placed the forms into a canvas bag labeled:

NON-COMPLIANT, and left the room without a goodbye. Her shape already fading, as if memory were erasing her in real time.

David looked down. His form was blank. It remained uncollected. His pen was dry. He didn’t know if this was on purpose, or in protest.

Had he stayed the whole time? He wasn’t entirely sure. By the time he looked up from the page, it was empty. The room. He was the only person left.







II.3   THE HOTEL ROOM   |   Buffalo, NY

[Andante luminoso]

[|  Revelation  |]

The room had the over-insulated density peculiar to conference hotels, a heat that seemed to accumulate rather than warm. Air moved through the vent in a flat mechanical rhythm. Papers sagged across the desk in loose drifts: frequencies, ratios, fragments of a model that had approached coherence only to recoil from it at the edge of naming.

David closed the door and stood for a moment without taking off his coat. The television flickered on when his bag brushed the remote. A weather anchor in a violent blue tie stood before a radar map warning western New York about lake-effect snow with the solemnity of nuclear war.

“…travel advisory remains in effect through Thurs—”

David muted it. For a second canned laughter burst from some commercial before the silence swallowed it again. The room smelled faintly of industrial detergent and somebody else’s cologne beneath the heat.

The silence in the room was not silence. The hum had followed him up from the lobby, thinned now and distributed through vent-work, lamp ballast, refrigeration, the hidden workings of the building continuing beneath all appearances of rest.

He set the conference duffel on the chair. The plastic conference badge still hung around his neck. He tore it off irritably and missed the trash can by three feet.

When he turned, the cello case was already drawing the eye.

He knelt and opened the latches. The instrument lay inside with its usual gravity, varnish sunk to brown under the room’s yellow light. Familiar, yes. But after the shop, familiarity no longer felt simple. He touched the neck, then the shoulder, then lifted the body toward the lamp. A smell rose from the open case: rosin, old wood, horsehair; and beneath it, something that did not belong to any conference room. Cedar oil. Brass polish. The shop again, impossibly present in the instrument.

He drew the bow from the case and tightened it slowly.

The first open C blossomed into the room,  deeper than he had expected. It passed beneath the vents’ tone, like the two were actively listening for one another. They seemed to approach but then failed to merge, hovering just outside one another. The contact point trembled near coherence strongly enough to raise the hair along his forearms.

When he played the note again, a page on the desk shifted a quarter inch. A water glass on the nightstand drew a minute ring along its inner surface. Somewhere in the wall a cheap screw answered with a sympathetic rattle. The room had not come into tune. It had revealed the degree to which it wanted to.

He shifted to G, then back to C, then took the octave above. The cello answered with something that felt less like projection than recognition. His sternum vibrated. In the afterring he could almost hear some thinner structure pressing toward form and stopping just short of it so that when he stopped playing, the room did not wholly let go.

On the desk, one of his conference pages had shifted enough to expose a penciled ratio:

3:2. holds, but does not open.

He stared at the line with immediate irritation. Christ. Was he becoming one of those people now? Three days around theoretical acousticians and suddenly every legal pad in America looked like the notebook of a desert prophet.

The frequency in the room thickened, or his hearing drifted closer to its center. He raised the bow again and played a stopped fifth against the open string. For one held instant the instrument gathered itself inward. Across the room, atop the desk, a collection of loose pages, notes he had taken on this and that throughout the day, slid subtly into rough alignment, seemingly brushed by a draft too delicate to feel and too precise to dismiss.

Then, with no warning to speak of, the alignment broke. The vent resumed its dull mechanical breathing and the pages settled back against the desk. Whatever had nearly crossed into form withdrew at once, leaving behind only its pressure.

Down the hall an ice machine emptied a fresh tray into its holding bin. The crash startled David, hitting him like a dropped sheet of metal. He flinched so hard that the bow skipped across the string.

“Jesus Christ.”

He lowered the bow and sat it down on the bed, then stood very still, not sure what exactly he was listening to,  the residue perhaps, or maybe nothing at all. Either way, he was content to let the moment fall away into the rest of a moments quiet.

It was within the calm of that quiet that his gaze drifted back to the desk. There, half exposed beneath the shifted pages, lay the business card from the Instrument Repair Shop. He moved to pick it up, turning it over in his fingers as he read and reread the name.

REPAIR SHOP FOR STRINGS. E. Guare, Proprietor.

The card was striking, singular in its strange simplicity. Cream stock. Black ink. No ornament. In fact, no other markings of any kind. The card assumed you either knew where to go or never would.

Then a glint of white caught his attention, only a flash, strange and out of place against the belly of the cello in the corner. He crossed to it, lifted the instrument into the lamplight, and angled the treble f-hole toward its glow. The maker’s label sat where it always had. Behind it, deeper in the cavity, something caught the yellow spill: a narrow repair strip glued to the inner rib. The graphite had faded at the edges.

What he could make out was this:

BFL 11/85. C.G. for C.L.

He stared.

C.L.

Cassandra Lang.

The thought arrived only partially reasoned. What the hell were his mother’s initials doing inside his cello?

He lowered the instrument carefully onto the bed and stood over it.

Call Mom. No. Jesus. She’d be asleep.

Then again, his mother considered midnight a perfectly reasonable time to reorganize closets or become emotionally devastated in the kitchen.

He turned toward the chair and started digging through the conference duffel. There had been a penlight in the swag bag somewhere beneath brochures, branded pens, and a stress ball shaped like a brain.

His hand was still buried in the bag when the phone rang.

He jerked hard enough to nearly knock the cello sideways off the bed. The bow clipped the lamp.

“Shit.”

“David?”

He grabbed the phone. “Hey, Mom.”

“Oh good, I didn’t wake you.”

“You absolutely woke me.”

“No I didn’t.”

“Mom, it’s midnight.”

“Well midnight’s not a real time anyway. Nothing important has ever happened at midnight except murders and babies.”

Despite himself, he smiled and sat down on the edge of the bed. “Mother, you are in the middle of someone else’s conversation again.”

“What? Oh. Sorry, sweetheart.” A pause. “Somebody in this house has to maintain a lively social calendar.”

“You live alone.”

“Thanks for the oh-so-delicate reminder, dear. Besides, that sort of thing has literally never stopped me.”

A slight giggle escaped her. He closed his eyes.

“Well,” she said, “I was trying to decide if I should just call and tell you about this.”

“Funny,” he said. “I was just standing here wondering if I should call you.”

“Well then, I’ve saved you the trouble. So what’s the skinny, darling?”

He looked back toward the open cello, the f-hole turned slightly toward the lamp like an opened mouth. “I’m not sure yet. I just found something weird.”

“With you that could mean literally anything.”

“There’s a repair mark inside the cello. I’ve never noticed it before.”

“Inside?”

“Yeah. Buffalo, nineteen eighty-five. And initials.”

“What initials?”

“C.L. I think. Maybe.”

“My initials?”

“Looks like it.”

A small incredulous laugh burst through the line. “Honey, why on earth would my initials be inside your cello? I don’t even know how to hold the thing correctly. That was your father’s department.”

“Yeah, I know. I just thought maybe…”

“Well if I secretly became a luthier and forgot to mention it, this is very exciting news.”

He laughed softly.

“Anyway,” she said, “I had something too.”

The line quieted.

“Mom?”

Faintly he could hear her murmuring to herself.

“Well now… no, that doesn’t… unless…”

“No,” she said at last, quieter now. “That’s not right either.”

“What isn’t?”

“The papers were your father’s. The scores, the folders, all that endless carrying on. But the cello…” A breath. “No, sweetheart. The cello was your grandfather’s thing.”

David frowned.

“I remember he bought it for you. Worked the whole thing out with your father when you were tiny. Four maybe? Five? Your father wanted something practical and Elliot disappeared for three weeks like he was running guns for the French Resistance.”

David smiled despite himself. “Buffalo.”

“What?”

“The repair slip. Buffalo, eighty-five.”

“Oh, that sounds right. They drove there together at least once.” A pause. “No, twice. Because your grandfather got that speeding ticket.”

“He got a speeding ticket?”

“Oh lord yes. Arthur acted scandalized for weeks. You’d have thought Elliot robbed a bank.”

A chair creaked softly on her end.

“They came home freezing to death and acting very strange afterward.”

“Strange how?”

“Secretive. Whispering in the kitchen. Stopping when I walked in.” She paused. “Honestly I thought they’d lost money gambling somewhere.”

David looked back toward the cello lying open across the hotel bed.

“Once it arrived, Elliot barely let anyone touch it. Not even your father.”

“Why?”

“He kept saying it needed to settle.”

“Settle.”

“I don’t know. Wood acclimating, strings adjusting, moon phases. With those two it was always half science and half witchcraft.”

David’s eyes drifted again to the penciled notation on the desk:

3:2. holds, but does not open.

A pressure gathered slowly behind his ribs.

“Mom,” he said carefully, “did Grandpa ever talk about hearing things?”

“Hearing things?”

“Not voices. Just… tones. Patterns maybe.”

The silence on the line sharpened.

Then:

“I found something too,” she said quietly. “A few days ago.”

His hand tightened around the repair slip. “What?”

“Music. In your father’s Bach.”

She did not need to say which Bach.

“I was looking for a note,” she said. “Something sentimental. You know your father. He hid emotions in books like a Soviet spy.”

David smiled faintly.

“There was a folded sheet tucked into the Prelude. Eight bars. In his handwriting, but very careful. Like he was copying it from somewhere else.” She paused. “And then there’s… there is something here at the bottom: E.L.—heard this once.”

David felt something inside him go very still.

“Your grandfather,” she said.

The room contracted.

“I’ve been trying to play it on the piano,” she continued. “It’s strange.”

“How?”

“It keeps sounding like it’s about to resolve and then it just… refuses.”

“Can you read me the notes?”

She did. One by one. Patiently.

David listened.

And suddenly he was six years old again, half asleep against his grandfather’s chest while Elliot hummed near the window during a thunderstorm. The melody wandering in circles that never quite closed. He had known this music his entire life—not consciously, but the way the body knows a staircase in darkness.

“Mom,” he said softly, “that’s Grandpa’s song.”

A long silence.

“Oh, honey,” she whispered. “You’re right.”

Another pause.

“Arthur used to joke it was the only damn tune Elliot knew.”

Not because he had written it. Because he had heard it once and never escaped it.

“David?”

“I’m here.”

He bent toward the f-hole again, this time with the penlight. Under the harsher beam the letters changed. Not C’s. E’s. The graphite had faded just enough to close the upper arms.

The marking read plainly now:

BFL 11/85. E.G. for E.L.

At the bottom of the case, half trapped beneath the shoulder rest, lay a folded receipt he had barely noticed before. He opened it carefully.

REPAIR SHOP FOR STRINGS, Buffalo, N.Y.
client: E. Lang.
internal adjustment completed / see verbal instructions only.

Not Cassandra.

Elliot.

Not C.G. either. E.G.

E.Guare.

Near the saddle, where the grain tightened toward the endpin, the minute irregularity beneath the varnish returned to him with new force. Around the endpin collar, the ebony looked fractionally newer than the surrounding wear suggested it should. Someone had opened the instrument and closed it so carefully the fact survived only as hesitation in the surface.

“David?” she said again. “What on earth does it mean?”

He almost answered:

Nothing. Probably nothing.

But the sentence would not come. Not because he believed otherwise yet. Only because some older part of him had already started listening.

He looked from the slip to the open cello case, and from the case to the card on the desk.

“I think he heard something,” David said slowly. “And I think music got to it before language did.”

She was quiet a moment.

“Well,” she said at last, “that sounds deeply unhealthy.”

He laughed unexpectedly. “Probably.”

“You’ll tell me what you figure out though, right?”

“I will. Hey mom… you alright?””

A deep-breathed pause lingered across the line.

“Oh, sweetheart, you know me. I’m a total mess, and I’m doing fine. Don’t worry about me. What about you, honey?”

He smiled despite himself. “You know me, Mom. I’m doing fine. And I’m a total mess.”

A soft laugh of recognition moved between them and then fell back into a deeper silence. For a long moment, David just sat listening to the pensive rhythm of his mother’s breathing across the line.

“It’s so very… quiet… without him,” she said at last.

“Oh, Mom.”

David could hear in the silent line, the unmistakable hitched in breath, the rusting of fabric to face, his mother trying to give strong front for her son. He knew only to wait with a soft a quiet as he could find until she recovered.

“I hate the quiet.” Another silence. “Anyway,” she said gently. “It’s late. I should let you get to sleep. I love you, sweetheart.”

“Love you too. Goodnight, mom.”

The line clicked quiet.

David sat motionless on the edge of the bed, the repair slip in one hand, the cooling phone in the other. The melody moved through him again, wandering, unresolved, never arriving where it seemed destined to land. He had fallen asleep to it a thousand times without ever asking where it came from.

The cello lay open at the foot of the bed.

Something had moved from atmosphere into fact.

And fact, however partial, carried its own narcotic authority.

He lay back slowly, the slip resting against his chest. The vent continued breathing overhead. Somewhere beyond the sealed hotel glass a snowplow dragged metal against pavement.

And then:

firelight, ash lifting into wind, a field bright with ruin.





EPISODE II


III.  THE FIREBIRDS   |   MIT - Boston, MA        May 24, 2005

[Furioso tragico      21 years ago]

[|  Suppression  |]

By the time the Wagoner pulled into the alley, the fire was already high enough to send pulsing glimmers of light dancing across the angled fence boards and the back wall of the house. Pages curled as they burned, rising in thin spirals before vanishing into the Boston night like sparked rain falling backwards.

Marcus stood beside the pit with the file box open at his feet, dropping pages in with a steady, practiced motion. Cold, New England air coursed through the yard, but sweat still tracked along his jaw.

David didn’t think. He was out of the car and across the grass before the parking break set.

“Marcus!” He screamed, desperation into the blazing night. “Marcus, no!”

Marcus didn’t look back. He reached into the box and lifted a notebook, already warping in the heat. He held it out, dangling it over the pit as if to force a confession from every line of ink.

David reached for it, but he was far too slow. Pulling it back, with a mocking breath, Marcus held… and then let it fall into the flames. The pages caught instantly. Ink dissolved before David could process what he’d lost.

He switched focus and lunged for the box and what remained of the fever dreams of the endangered treatise. As Marcus stepped in front of him, the box tipped, spilling drafts and notes across the ground. Some caught fire immediately; others lifted and scattered on the wind like birds fleeing the impact of a gunshot.

He dropped to his knees, grabbing whatever came within reach: Marcus’ annotations, Celeste’s harmonic interval notes, pieces of the early proof. Dirt smudged the edges, firelight flickered across the equations, and something in his chest cracked wider than the burn pit.

He looked up at Marcus, papers shaking in his hands.

“This was ours.”

Marcus didn’t flinch. “I know.”

“Then why?”

“Because it’s going to kill us all, man! Unless one of us has the balls to kill it first.”

The words hit harder than any shove. David rose too fast, pages still clutched between his fingers, and collided with Marcus. The shove wasn’t calculated—just the direction the hurt needed to go. Marcus caught the impact, staggered, then came back with the same raw momentum.

They tangled. Not a clean fight, not even fury—just two exhausted men who had pushed the same impossible idea until it pushed them back. Balance tipping without either one choosing the direction.

Celeste’s voice cut across the yard. “Marcus! David! Stop!”

But they didn’t—or they could not.

David’s arm came up in half-defense. Marcus reacted before thought could intervene. His swing wasn’t elegant, but it connected just the same.

The crack against David’s cheekbone snapped the world still. He stumbled back, hand pressed to his face, the taste of blood bright in his mouth.

Celeste ran to him, stumbling as she lurched to catch him, but she was far too late. The two met on crumpled knees in the damp grass of this fragile night.

The fire hissed, as if waiting in suspension for who would make the next move. Pages crackled, burning unabated. Everything within ember-shot held its breath as the fire light reveled in the night airs oxygen.

Then—from out of the silence, at the edge of the yard—a voice intruded.

“Any man who drops his shoulder like that is begging to be hit.”

All three turned.

There was nothing at first—just the dense void where the voice had come from. Then, a form began to emerge, pushing through the dark distance. A man stepped out of the shadows and into the first fringe of firelight. He paused just outside the gate, one hand resting on the latch, the other tucked—palm down—into the front pocket of his silk blazer. He was impeccably dressed. Crisp shirt, tailored trousers, shoes far too polished for trampled grass. He looked like a man arriving at a garden party, not a backyard brawl. Then, releasing the latch, he sauntered into the yard.

“I once fancied myself a boxer,” he said lightly. His eyes drifted across the scene—Marcus still braced, David bleeding, Celeste frozen between shouting and breathing.

“Didn’t take, I’m afraid. Wonderful sport. Terrible left hook.”

No one replied.

His gaze settled on David. He removed the lambskin glove from his right hand—finger by finger.

“David Lang.”

It was not a question. Just a deductive statement of fact.

David swallowed. “That’s… yes. That’s me.”

“Rune Ishikawa.” He extended his ungloved hand. “Pleasure.”

He helped David to his feet, then watched a page catch the wind and rise, glowing at the edges, lifting higher until it winked out over the roofline. Not until it melted into the black night sky did Rune continue.

“Beautiful night for a bonfire,” he noted. “Though perhaps a waste of good research.”

He turned back to David. Gestured forward.

“Walk with me, Mr. Lang. Before someone does something they can’t take back.”

The two moved along the narrow edge of the yard toward the driveway. Behind them, Marcus dropped to his knees, sweeping scattered pages toward himself. Celeste joined him, slapping out small embers with her bare hands.

A loose sheet lifted on the wind and crossed Rune’s path. He caught it without looking. Half an equation, a marked glyph. He held it between two fingers like a dirty rag.

“I had a call this afternoon. From Dr. Kline at NYU.”

Something tightened in David’s ribs. “Yes, we… I had a meeting with him this morning.”

“Indeed. He seemed to think you were somehow… touched. Called your presentation ‘a horror.’ His words.”

They reached the shaded fence line, firelight thinning behind them.

Rune stopped. Turned to face David; the flickering illumination of distant flames cutting both men’s faces—half empty, half a glow—opposing profiles caught in the light.

“Until that call, I intended to pull you out of MIT the moment your degree posted. Hire you on the spot.” He released the page. It drifted down, landing just in front of Rune’s feet. He stepped on it as though dousing a cigarette butt with his shoe. “That was the plan.”

“What changed?”

Rune’s mouth twitched, not quite a smile. “Your meeting this morning.”

He adjusted his glove, smoothing the leather across his knuckles.

“You see, Mr. Lang, I’m not in the habit of driving from Ithaca to Boston on a whim.”

“I wouldn’t think so, sir. That’s… three hundred and thirty‑four miles. Five hours, fifteen minutes—give or take.”

David hadn’t meant to say it. It just came out.

Rune paused.

“Give or take,” he repeated softly.

A beat. Then he continued walking.

“I don’t make house calls. It isn’t part of my work. And yet—here I am. In your delightful company, on this charming New England evening.”

He stopped again.

“Which should tell you something.”

“I think it does, sir.”

“Tell me,” Rune pressed.

“You’re not here out of curiosity.”

“And…?”

“This is no longer a casual conversation.”

“No,” Rune said. “It isn’t. It’s a risk. A serious one.”

David frowned. “Risk? I don’t understand. How am I a risk?”

Rune didn’t answer. He simply turned to look, deeply through David. Another page drifted through the air. He caught it without ever looking at the page as it lilted before him. This time, however, he stopped and began to read.

His stillness had a different quality now.

“‘That boy will be the end of your department.’ That was Kline’s phrase—the phrase that made me get in the car.” He held out the page toward David. “I’m beginning to grasp what he meant.”

David tried: “It’s just math. Just a model. We don’t even know if—”

Rune lifted a hand. The gesture was small and final.

“Don’t.”

He studied the page. Equations. Not just symbols—structure. The laddered spectrum, the ratios, the coupling terms. And underneath, in David’s hand, the compact algorithm that turned a continuous input into that exact stack of modes.

Then Rune stopped dead.

He turned with absolute intent, looking directly into David’s eyes. When he spoke, his voice had dropped to something barely above a whisper.

“Where did you get this?” His perfectly gloved hands shaking under the shock—maybe even terror of what he had just read.

“I wrote it. It’s just—”

“Not what, Lang… Where?”

He tapped a term near the bottom of the page. A correction factor, almost an afterthought: a slow, periodic modulation indexed to solar phase.

David pointed. “We solved for a drive that… I tuned it to the cycle and it persisted.”

Rune’s eyes lifted.

“Did you turn it off?”

David’s mouth opened. Closed. The wind took a few more pages past them like startled birds.

“Well… it… it decays,” he said. “Low amplitude. It just sits in the ground. We monitor it. It’s—”

“You tried to turn it off,” Rune said quietly. “And you could not. Correct?”

David said nothing.

Rune folded the page once and tucked it into his pocket.

“Listen to me.” No change in volume, but the air around the words tightened. “Listen very carefully, Mr. Lang. You mustn’t confuse genius with permission. There are processes. To act otherwise is recklessness.”

He nodded toward the yard—the burning pages, Marcus and Celeste scraping the ground for whatever survived.

“Look at them. Two of your generation’s most promising scientists crawling through mud and embers. One trying to burn down your work on your behalf. The other crying as she tries to save what’s left. You gave a presentation this morning that made one of the driest, most conservative men I know use the word apocalyptic.”

His gaze met David’s.

“And you want to tell me it’s just a model?”

A page flared gold behind them and vanished into the dark.

“You don’t know the history of what you have brushed against,” Rune said. “But I do. And I won’t watch you be eaten alive by it because you happen to understand the sound it makes.”

He straightened.

“You have greatness in you, David. Real, inconvenient greatness. I have no intention of allowing any of you to throw it away. Not on this. Not now.”

Marcus, now understanding which side Rune had fallen on, stepped forward. “Dr. Ishikawa. If I may, this is exactly what I was trying to—”

Rune raised one hand.

“Mr. McGrath. When asking if you may, it’s customary to wait for permission before proceeding.” He paused. “And in this case, no, you may not.”

He gestured for all three to converge before him.

“I will say this once. Your pursuance of this work or anything derived from this work… is over. Now. Furthermore, in order to ensure that my meaning is well and truly understood, you will all three join me at Cornell tomorrow. Dusk. My office.”

David blinked. “Tomorrow?”

“You will bring every piece of paper this work has touched. Drafts. Notes. Star charts. Napkins. Every scrap.”

He stepped past David.

“Follow my instructions to the letter and I can protect you. From your institution. From the consequences of discovery without understanding—of brilliance borne too soon. Let me guide you out of this pit—all of you.”

David forced the question. “And if we don’t?”

Rune stopped and swiveled on a dime to face him once more.

“Then your little adventure ends badly. Not just for you, for all of us. For me. David, you must see this. When you play games with the universe, you risk all that lies within.” He crossed back through the yard, addressing Marcus and Celeste as he passed.

“Mr. McGrath. Ms. Armitage. Tomorrow evening, you dreamers—three. I strongly advise you against keeping me waiting.”

And with a slow turn, The Guardian of East Hill moved like mist through the fence and was gone—melted into the New England night.



STRETTO




IV.1   DISSOLUTION   |   Cornell - Ithaca, NY         Present Day

[Adagio frammentato]

[|  Return  |]

Morning arrived through the blinds in thin white bands that stopped just short of the bed. Heat clung stubbornly to the apartment despite the cracked window above the radiator. Sometime during the night he had kicked half his notes onto the floor. Others remained spread across the blankets beside him, wrinkled, coffee-stained, covered in calculations he only partially remembered writing.

One page contained nothing except: 3:2 repeated down the margin until the numbers collapsed into scratches. He stared at it too long before realizing the shower had already been running several minutes.

The water helped less than expected. Exhaustion had moved past ordinary tiredness sometime yesterday and become something flatter, harder to classify. Heat registered unevenly across his skin. His hands felt delayed by half a second.

Steam gathered across the mirror while he shaved. Clearing part of the glass with his palm revealed a face that looked subtly unfamiliar—not transformed, only older than it should have after two sleepless nights. Arthur’s old Cornell sweater hung loose around his shoulders. He had put it on again without noticing.

Near the sink sat Arthur’s coffee mug with the chipped handle.

Still there.

David had meant to move it weeks ago.

Outside, late-autumn wind drove leaves in frantic circles across the commons. Students crossed campus carrying coffee cups and backpacks, shoulders folded inward against the cold. Somewhere uphill a maintenance truck beeped steadily while reversing near the loading docks behind Goldwin Smith.

Ordinary things still obeyed ordinary rules. The illusion lasted almost to upper campus.

Halfway up the slope toward Feynman Hall, the concrete face of Clark Hall flashed violently green at the edge of his vision. Not tinted green. Green. Saturated enough to hurt.

Morning glare struck the narrow window bands a second later, and by the time he turned fully toward it the building had settled back into its usual brutalist gray.

David stopped walking.

A student brushed past him without noticing.

Further downhill, leaves scraped across the pavement in dry spirals. Their sound arrived oddly flattened, as though distance itself had compressed. His own footsteps echoed back too quickly off the stone path.

Graduate work on signal latency had trained him to notice timing errors almost pathologically. Most people never consciously registered acoustic delay. David heard rooms misbehaving immediately.

He kept moving.

Crossing the Arts Quad just beyond Goldwin Smith, the lawn flashed magenta in his peripheral vision. Not pink. Magenta. The color vanished the moment he looked directly at it, but his body had already reacted. He stepped instinctively off the grass and back onto the walkway before realizing what he was doing.

A few undergraduates glanced at him briefly, then away again. Good. Nobody else appeared to see anything unusual.

By the time Feynman Hall came into view, concentration had narrowed into something procedural. Numbers still behaved correctly. Angles mostly held. Counting breaths helped. So did velocity calculations.

The trick, apparently, was to remain inside systems that could still be measured.

Inside the physics building, overheated air carried burnt coffee, dust, wet wool, and the faint mineral smell old Cornell radiators released every winter once the steam system kicked on. Somewhere down the corridor a copier jammed repeatedly while two faculty members argued about NSF funding outside a seminar room.

Reality steadied slightly. For the moment, it was enough.




IV.2 THE CLASSROOM | Cornell          Ithaca, NY

[Allegro dissonante
| Breakdown |]

The fluorescent lights had started flickering before class even began.

Nobody else seemed especially bothered by it. Students unpacked laptops, dug through backpacks, checked phones. Someone near the back unwrapped a protein bar with excruciating slowness. Two students were still quietly arguing about partial credit on the midterm when David shut the door behind him.

The lock clicked beneath his hand. Too loud.

Thirty-seven students today. He counted them twice without meaning to.

At the front of the room the chalk tray overflowed with broken fragments and pale dust. One piece snapped immediately when he picked it up.

“Consider—”

The sentence disappeared.

For a second he stood facing the board with nothing behind his eyes except fluorescent buzzing and the awareness that his right hand had started shaking hard enough to dust chalk across the floor.

Professional voice. Use the professional voice Lang.

“Consider,” he tried again, “the structural similarities between recursive Feynman mapping and ornamental recursion in late Baroque composition.”

Silence.

Near the back, somebody blinked hard enough to qualify as a philosophical objection.

David nodded once. “Excellent. Good. The healthy skepticism in the room is encouraging.”

A few reluctant laughs surfaced.

“Relax,” he continued, turning toward the board. “You’re physicists. Intellectual despair is part of the accreditation process.”

That got a better response.

Movement helped. Equations still produced comfort through motion. Tensor relationships unfolded cleanly enough once his hand started moving. Harmonic ratios appeared beside them almost unconsciously.

Halfway through the derivation he realized he had unconsciously replaced part of the tensor notation with musical staff lines. He erased it too quickly.

“Unless quantum chromodynamics has recently become much more melodic than I remember,” he said, “ignore the last thirty seconds.”

Actual laughter this time. Good. For a moment the room almost stabilized around it.

The very idea of the standing wave had always bothered him aesthetically. It was nothing more than motion disguised as stillness, energy trapped inside apparent stability.

At the back of the room someone coughed three times in even succession. Another cough answered several rows away. The fluorescent buzz overhead thickened. David stopped writing, perhaps in response, perhaps not, but no one else seemed to react at all.

One student continued typing without looking up. Another stared blankly through him with the dead-eyed endurance unique to second-semester physics majors. Near the windows, somebody had fallen halfway asleep already.

“Rest assured, all of this,” David said, gesturing vaguely at the board, “will absolutely be on the exam.”

Immediate attention returned to the room.

“Good,” he murmured. “Nice to know terror still works pedagogically.”

More laughter. Smaller this time. Then a smartwatch chirped nearby. Minor third interval. Without fully deciding to, David adjusted one of the equations on the board to compensate for the tone. The realization arrived immediately afterward and froze him where he stood.

What the hell are we doing, Lang. Let’s go.

Silence settled across the room again, though not entirely. Ventilation moved through the ceiling ducts. Fabric shifted. Fingers tapped keyboards. Beneath it all the electrical hum widened almost imperceptibly, as though the room were slowly phase-locking around some deeper carrier frequency.

Earlier that semester he had spent two weeks lecturing on synchronization in coupled oscillators: pendulum clocks, electrical grids, neural entrainment. Systems drifting toward coherence simply because proximity made independence energetically expensive.

The thought landed badly.

White dust streaked across his cuff where the chalk had broken in his grip.

“Professor Lang?”

And just like that, concern entered the room in earnest. He looked back at the board. The equations remained mathematically valid. Well, most of them remained… mostly… valid.

Though, as he gave the board further examination, he had to admit that several symbols had drifted sideways into notation he did not remember writing.

A radiator hissed sharply behind him. Then, another cough entered the room in exact time with the fluorescent pulse overhead.

Three-four. Three-four. Three-four. The classroom pulsed in meter.

David stepped backward too fast, stricking the desk behind him hard enough to rattle a stack of exams onto the floor.

“Okay,” he said automatically, too quickly, “that looked significantly more dramatic than it actually was.”

A nervous laugh moved uncertainly through the room.

“Whoever’s watch just made that sound,” he added without turning around, “congratulations. You’re now part of the lecture.”

Another small ripple of laughter. Then, after a second too long:

“Actually, that may have been more true than intended.”

The silence afterward felt wrong immediately. A few students exchanged glances.

“You okay?”

Processing the question took effort.

“Yes…” he said.

The answer sounded suspiciously like a question.




IV.3 THE CORRIDOR

[Adagio sospeso
| Structure |]

By evening the building had mostly emptied. Classroom doors stood open along the corridor like abandoned compartments. Weak amber emergency lighting flattened the hallway into alternating bands of shadow and pale color.

One hand remained against the wall while he walked. He had reached for it initially for surety of balance, but now realized what he had gained was much closer to the assurance of contact.

Somewhere inside his office the cello began resonating again: and open G string. It was barely audible unless he stopped moving entirely. The tone seemed distributed through the building rather than located inside a single room: in pipes, vents, fluorescent housings, hollow spaces behind drywall.

His breathing had shortened gradually over the last hour.

Every few steps the corridor lengthened ahead of him before settling back into proportion. Fatigue through lack of sleep could explained some of it, but certainly not all.

A half-open classroom farther down the hall spilled fluorescent light across the tile in a perfect rectangle. Passing it produced the sudden irrational certainty that someone inside had been waiting quietly for him to cross that exact point in the corridor.

Of course, the room was empty, but his pace quickened afterward, in spite of himself.

The buzz underneath the building had stopped feeling external. Ventilation, blood flow, fluorescent ballast, memory; the distinctions no longer held steady. Somewhere along the way the sound had crossed into him.

Ahead, near the stairwell turn, the ceiling dipped unexpectedly lower. Or was it closer. He could not tell, but it stopped him cold. The sensation vanished almost at once, leaving behind only the unpleasant awareness that his own perception had become negotiable.

Years earlier, while working on signal latency models, David had become briefly obsessed with recursive return behavior in closed acoustic systems. Most of the anomalies were theoretical: feedback structures generated by delayed self-reference, systems reorganizing themselves around echoes of their own output.

The math had bothered him enough that he eventually abandoned the paper halfway through publication review. He never fully articulated why.

Further down the corridor the frequency thickened again. Waiting.

For the first time all day he stopped trying to explain it through exhaustion, grief, or neurological overload. None of those explanations disappeared exactly. They simply no longer covered the full shape of what was happening.

One lecture every spring covered the missing fundamental—the mind’s tendency to reconstruct absent frequencies from harmonic residue alone. Students loved the phenomenon because it sounded mystical.

It wasn’t mystical. Of that he had to be sure. It was predictive compression.

The memory arrived alongside another realization, quieter and far worse: whatever this thing was, some part of him had been hearing it for years. Beneath music. Beneath lecture halls. Beneath his grandfather humming near the window while rain struck the house at night. Not newly discovered. Recognized.

The corridor stretched ahead beneath weak amber light while the building settled softly around him, pipes knocking somewhere below, ventilation dragging through old ducts, fluorescent current humming overhead.

And underneath all of it, patient as breath, the frequency remained.

V.   RECOGNITION

[Largo fatale]

Behind him, twenty years collapsed into twenty seconds. Ahead, the frequency waited patiently, no longer foreign, yet fiendish still.

And it occurred to him that, perhaps, he had always been tuning towards this. He stood still and determined: just listen. And in the listening, a question filled his head.

What if the call never sought response? What if he was not the answer? Might he then become the aperture, the way through, and now, even the vessel?

A moment of involuntary shiver overtook him, though he wasn’t the least bit cold. He looked at his watch and found that it was ninety-two seconds… it was forty-one hours… it was one minute, forty-six seconds before time.
