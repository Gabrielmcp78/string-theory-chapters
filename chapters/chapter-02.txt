--- CHAPTER TEXT BEGINS ---

David’s office sat two buildings and a partial crossing of the arts quad from the main cluster of physics buildings, tucked behind a row of former faculty houses now repurposed as boutique galleries for Cornell history and regional heritage. He could reach it through the unending mid-century administrative hallways, but long ago, in all weather, he had made a habit of taking the scenic route instead, if only to avoid the corridor haunted by the nearly emeritus tenure set and the half-muttered conversational ordeals they imposed.

His suite of offices felt lost to time. It was nothing of his doing or design; he had simply allowed what was there before him to remain. Late afternoon light seeped through warped Venetian blinds, striping stacked Annalen der Physik into a barcode of abandoned inquiries. A cello case leaned against the southern flank of the bookcase, its leather scuffed in the precise pattern of subway-platform collisions from a commute to lessons he now scheduled only in daydreams.

David burst through the door. His coat sailed toward a hook, bag thudding to the floor, papers cascading in a controlled avalanche across the desk. He didn’t even notice Rune Ishikawa, already seated in the corner, attentively stirring a fresh cup of Earl Grey. He was almost indistinguishable from the furniture.

Before David could orient himself, Rune’s voice floated out into the room, unhurried, already mid-thought. If the preceding spectacle had indeed occurred, Rune showed no signs of the offense.

“You know, David,” he took another delicate sip from his cup and with a faint upturn of the right side of his lips, continued. “There simply doesn’t seem to be milk anywhere. I searched and searched, even dared to cross over into the chemistry department. Dreadful world, terrible mistake. Still… nothing.”

“Rune. Come on in.”

“Yes, thank you. So here I am, sitting in your office, drinking an entirely naked cup of Earl Grey. Shameful.”

David dropped into his chair with enough force to test its stress limits. The jolt wiped whatever he’d carried in from the quad clean off his face.

“Rune, about before, I—”

“So David,” Rune continued, unfazed by David’s attempt to speak, “they say Princeton University Press is interested in a revised monograph from you. An expansion on your wonderful decoherence paper.” As Rune continued, his fingers grazed the gilt edge of a volume from 1979’s Encyclopedia Britannica, a set David had inherited from a predecessor who’d abruptly retired to study Tibetan throat singing.

David blinked. “Yes… I’d heard something to that effect.” He struggled to color his tone with an appropriate layer of professional patina. His mind, still half-lit with residual vibrations and snow light, narrowed on Rune’s phrasing. “Sorry Rune, but who’s they exactly?”

“My dear boy, you forget, we share an agent… and a lawyer, for that matter. I had a call from Charles this morning. On other matters, of course.”

“Charles! Right. Of course.” David nodded too many times, buying seconds. “Don’t know where my head is. And he said?”

“He asked after you,” said Rune. “Mentioned you’d been rather difficult to reach of late. Said Princeton Press called him just the other day.”

“I see.” David’s hands began their familiar choreography across the paper-cluttered surface, pulling drawers like organ stops. “Come to think… I did see something from them… sure it’s here somewhere.” The ballet of search and delay. “Ah, here. Princeton U. Press. There we are.”

“Excellent.”

A pause hung between them. Not quite awkward. Not quite comfortable.

“Yes, well… wait and see, right?” David offered.

“Indeed, and… if so.” Rune lifted his hand in a gesture that somehow encapsulated here’s-hoping, pre-congratulations, and a threat disguised as collegial optimism, all at once. It made David’s stomach turn and his toes dig into the squeaking laminate floor.

“Yes. Thanks. Thank you, sir.”

As David lowered his gaze, he drifted, his mind finding distraction in the parallelogram of pale sunlight bisecting his left wrist, where dust motes performed Brownian dances above a stack of Slovakian musicology journals from 1987.

As the drift subsided, David was mildly surprised to find Dr. Ishikawa still in the room. Worse, the man had made himself at home with a decades-old issue of Scientific Educators Quarterly, a publication so barren that forty-seven of its hundred and seventy-one pages had been surrendered to advertisements without any obvious loss to human knowledge. Yet there he stood, absorbed in page sixty-three, then sixty-four, and now sixty-five, as though revelation might yet crawl from the wreckage. David did not remember seeing him rise, cross to the bookshelf, or even take the thing down at all.

Then, with sudden reanimation, Rune made a side-click of his mouth and a corresponding flip-flap closing of the magazine, as if reminded of something he’d left off his check-in-with-Lang list.

“Oh, and I meant to say,” he jolted back in, “that was a remarkably thorough analysis of Pythagorean tuning in Gregorian antiphons last week.”

David exhaled. He only had so many of these polite rejoinders at his fingertips. Taking 1.3 seconds to mentally thumb through his collection, he plucked out, “Oh, did you think so? Well, that’s nice of you to say.”

His eyes followed Ishikawa’s drift toward the precarious ziggurat of unanswered student inquiries and meeting requests stacked beside the monitor.

“Well, I can see you’ve a mountain of paper here to climb. I’ll leave you to it.”

He turned gracefully, judging David’s lack of correspondence management with a move to the door that bordered on indignation. Then, stopping at the threshold, he turned, launching the unsaid into the spoken realm.

“Resonance… equals… survival?”

The air between them compressed towards a sub-zero chill.

“Interesting premise,” Rune allowed, his manicured nails tapping the doorjamb in glissandic 7/8 time. “Resonance as a survival strategy. It has a certain… intellectual elegance. Though one does wonder, David, when you’ll find the breath to move past the curation of the old masters and grace the department with something more… speculative. We do so miss that version of you.”

“Hm? What was that, Rune? Oh… speculative… yes, well…” A weak, ill-conceived semi-chuckle escaped David’s lips, as if to say, I wouldn’t hold your breath.

Dr. Ishikawa mercifully brought the audience to a close. “Well, good night, Professor Lang.”

“Goodnight, Dr. Ishikawa.”

And with the unhurried shuffle of his soft leather shoes, he drifted away.

David rose to see him off but was too late, the gesture mattering not at all. His knuckles brushed the diploma frame as he turned, adjusting its forty-degree tilt to the exact angle Professor Esterházy had set during their last argument about academic legacy. The brass corner left a dent in his proximal phalanx that would throb until evening.


⁂


Was that it? Was that all he had?

David knew the outburst earlier was enough to put him out on his ass. It was a wholly inexcusable breach—no matter the circumstance. Yet the age-old axiom of academic neutrality and avoidance once again played as a superpower.

Rune had always been a nominal presence, barely there until the moment you realized he’d been holding the room together the whole time. It had taken David years to believe the man truly operated on the same neurological and vascular principles as the rest of humanity.

What was he to do now? Should he lock his lectures to keep his boss from discovering that he was losing it? David sat back into his chair with a heavy, unmediated flop, and from the bottom drawer pulled folder LXIII, its tab labeled Theoretical Explorations. And then, below in parentheses, Strike Outs. The lettering appeared written in the geometric hand he’d cultivated during a misguided calligraphy phase. New additions were never inserted without rereading previous rejections. The NSF denial still bore bourbon stains from 2014, the IEEE rebuke citing “excessive poetry in the equations.”

Resigned to relegate yet another nascent rejection, David added page 974. The page was still damp with the ink of a theorem collapsed under the referee’s indifferent gaze. He sighed, took a moment, and shut the drawer.

Surely this was no way to live; of that he was certain. Yet the pattern’s reliability offered a certain comfort. With every rejected treatise or unpublished paper, with every lecture delivered half-baked in the accepted science he knew to be incomplete at best, nonsense at worst, the outcome never changed. The dismissal was predictable. He could rest easier in the knowledge that he had been here before. This unimpressive, even untruthful was at the very least, familiar.

And then there was the rest: requests for advisory meetings, for recommendation letters, the brag sheets of mentees and postdoctoral hopefuls, all of it unanswered and faintly accusatory. Since his father’s death, the weeks had felt like a second conscription into adulthood, brought on less by any single blow than by attrition. The forms kept arriving while his father remained dead. The indecency of that made him flinch.

It was personal. How could it be otherwise? Reality, as ever, had shown no interest in the life David had imagined for himself. And it occurred to him, with the sudden clarity of a cruel joke, that midlife felt a great deal like thirteen: the same vertigo, the same sense of inhabiting a body and a future that refused to explain themselves. And eighty-one, the age his father had been when he died two weeks ago, was disturbingly not a remote continent but a neighboring country: closer to now than to never. Someone ought to do something about that paradox, he thought. Or at least install a better warning system than this blunt annunciation: your father is dead and you are almost forty-seven. Crisis. Midlife. Proceed with extreme caution.

And there, in the far-right corner of the desk, lay the manila envelope from Schaker, Marsheck & Camden, the firm executing his father’s will. David looked at it, looked away, then forced himself to look again, as though staring long enough might let him absorb some portion of its weight. It did not. He could not bring himself to draw the papers from their stiff bureaucratic sleeve, much less sign them: the small, almost laughably ordinary gesture that would make everything final. That single motion would close the long, fraught conversation he and his father had been having since David was nearly fifteen and first understood that the thoughts handed to him need not be the only ones he kept.

They had argued, sometimes mildly, sometimes with real force, about science and music: one obedient to law, the other disciplined only so it could remain human. His father had not wanted to lose him to physics. He had wanted music for David: beauty, honesty, clarity. Music offered such things without embarrassment. Physics, as Arthur had learned from his own father, did not exactly advertise the same rewards in the cheerful pamphlet titled Welcome to Scientific Academia: A Few Things to Expect Before Your Journey Begins.

David had insisted both were possible; that science and music need not remain separate islands. Somewhere in the distant past, he believed, there must have been a land bridge between them; someone merely had to go looking. Now he thought, with a bitter flicker of amusement, that truth was less often buried than eroded. Discovery was never the hardest part. Learning to distinguish fortitude from foolishness was.

Then, with all that and more lingering in his head, he nudged the envelope back into its corner, still unsigned, and turned once more toward the glass, toward the campus trees and darkening grass, towards the shadows of beyond, through the darkness of before.

⁂


Snow thickened into sleet, and sleet turned to ice, laying a fresh sheen of treachery over the quad’s footpaths. Yet the ground around the bench, along with the narrow track by which David had stumbled upon it, remained oddly bare. Untouched by the accumulating glaze, it felt as if the moment itself still radiated some after-heat that refused the world’s new terms.

His mind kept circling back: the bench, the fork, the capsule, the data. A recursion he dreaded. A momentum he could not, for the moment, break.

Somewhere beneath the grass, under layers of dense clay and root-striated soil, lay the capsule he, Celeste, and Marcus had buried: their graduate-school manifestos on revolutionizing acoustic physics, sealed up like a promise and a dare. David’s fingernail traced the rim of his coffee cup, finding the enamel chip where he’d dropped it while trying to convince his father that they were doing the right thing. With that constant voice of challenge in his life now silenced, that long ago foggy evening seemed to have found a voice in him once more. A voice he knew in youth, in new discovery, before they shook hands and agreed it was a song sung before its time: a melody no one was ready, or willing, to hear.

He exhaled, shutting all of it from his mind, for ahead waited the relief of rehearsal, the comfort of trusted, old friends, and an even older cello that had always shielded him from harm.

He looked up at the clock, then down at his own, for the day had proven less than reliable already. Confirmed:

It was one minute, forty-seven seconds from time.
