--- CHAPTER TEXT BEGINS ---

Elegy


For All






















27.1  Resonance


The air over East Hill is bright and new.

The molecules like glass, brittle with the ache of October, as if one deep breath could shatter the sky into measureless, singing shards.

He blinks.

Then continues walking.

Leaves rasp under his shoes like an orchestra tuning in dry reeds.

He looks down to make sure his laces are tied.

They are. He is glad. That was difficult the first time around.

He walks again.

The paths are new, but familiar.

Across the quad, laughter rings, the kind of laughter that bends light.

It reminds him of strings as they warm to pitch, of innocence pretending to be courage.

For a moment he lets it wash over him.

Is this what time is? A sound moving away until it can no longer be heard.

He shrugs at the notion and adjusts his coat.

Then… He steps on.

There is a bench, familiar, old, that waits under a tree flayed half-bare by wind. The bark gleams like old varnish. He sits, slow and deliberate, the wood creaking an octave lower than it once did. In his hands, the paper bundle warms his palms; inside, a sandwich, absurdly simple, fragrant as a memory too fresh to trust. He eats. The bread is soft, the tomato colder than he expected.

He chews, swallows, then looks up. The sun slides between clouds like a metronome changing tempo.

For a heartbeat the hill holds its breath, the buildings, the bronze statues, even the maples arrest mid-flutter, as if waiting for the downbeat of some cosmic baton.

He reaches into his pocket, a sly grin on his face. His fingers pierce the folded wax-paper bag, the faint warmth of its contents seeping through to his palm. A breath of sugar and spice escapes: candied walnuts.

The sweetness, the crunch, the earth and nut of their core open a seascape of another era, a life he’s sure he knew, when the air turned cold and the world still fit inside an afternoon.

Steam curls upward, lazy as the years between then and now.

He is content, no, elated simply to sit here and chew.

Another piece; then two more.

He fears he’s overindulged already.

He wipes a crumb from his sleeve, and farther down, another.

He stands slowly… and begins to walk again.




27.2  Wander and Wonder


So many things look different. New buildings. Taller trees.

Yet so much remains the same.

He is struck most by the students. The way they carry themselves. The way they move through space.

He begins to wander, without destination, for he has long since released the pressures that come with needing to get places.

A squirrel scampers across his path.

He stumbles just slightly, moving out of its way.

Two undergraduates leap up from a bench to help the old man regain his footing.

They are kind and generous of spirit, and he’s grateful, not necessarily for the help.

He is not yet that old.

No, he is glad for the interaction, glad for the hands-on proof that goodness still resides.

One of them has an Introduction to Quantum Harmonics textbook slipping from her knapsack, with a foreword by Dr. Marcus McGrath—President Emeritus of the SolPhi Institute of Universology.

“Quantum Harmonics?” He asks. “How are you enjoying the course?”

The student gleams with enthusiasm. “My absolute favorite class. Im mesmerized!”

“Keep on.” He says. “Theres so very much more.”

His head is calm.

His heart is full.

He’s glad already that he made the trip.

But he has wandered now, in this elation state, long enough to lose his bearings.

A young man approaches. Perhaps he will help.

The student notices, without a word, that the elder seems “loosely directed.”

The old man asks if he might be guided to the music school chapel.

Though it’s just around the corner, the student offers to accompany him, as the hill is slick with rain and the way somewhat treacherous.

He lifts his right leg, showing a freshly grass and mud-stained knee, imparting that he’s just come from there.

They walk together.

The company is a blessing.

Again, the man’s heart is filled with the kindness he finds.

Before even a moment has passed, they are standing in front of the entrance to the chapel on the hill.

The old man’s hands begin to shake.

He is nervous now.

But he tells the young student he’ll be fine, and thanks him.

A deep breath… then another, for good measure.

And the old man moves to enter the now ancient sanctuary.




27.3  Inside


The massive doors stand like sentinels at guard.

It will take all he has to force their opening.

He thinks for a moment that perhaps he’ll need assistance once more.

But a sense of strength embraces him and with a single push, the oaken guardians exhale, the hinges sigh, and the doors give way.

Light spills over the threshold, thick and gold, the smell of beeswax and cedar wrapped around it.

He waits for his eyes to catch up.

The chapel breathes once, cool air sliding down from the rafters as if the building itself were settling.

He steps forward.

Shoes on stone, small, hollow sounds, each one answered by another somewhere in the roof.

Dust drifts through the beam of the rose window, turning the air into water he can almost swim through.

He touches the nearest pew; the polish has worn to silk where generations of hands have rested.

He moves slowly down the aisle.

Here and there a cough of floorboard, a whisper of paper from the lectern.

He pauses, not sure if he’s listening to the place or if the place is listening to him.

The far end glows: a sheet of color thrown by glass, blue, then red, then amber, shifting as a cloud crosses the sun.

He walks through each color as though passing through separate days of his life.

The years blur; only the light remains distinct.

A bench waits in the half–shadow.

He knows it before he thinks.

Fourth pew from the back.

He sits.

The wood groans beneath him, familiar, forgiving.

For a while there is only breathing: the building’s and his own.

Then somewhere below, a single note rises, maybe a student practicing, maybe air moving through pipes.

It hangs, soft as a pulse, and fades.

He looks around, hands folded on the cane across his knees, and lets the stillness fill him.

No numbers now.

No proofs.

Only the quiet weight of having been here before.




27.4  Discovery


He pauses for a moment.

He knows that breath will gateway him towards further, deeper intent.

The clean, sacred air seems to unburden him of all things, and for a moment his brain, like a sinner absolved, is wiped clean.

Why was he here?

The chapel seems to know. The limestone and granite, even the marble whispering clues.

And then it comes to him. He knew it would.

Fourth pew from the back.

He counts softly, as though solving a proof that once belonged to someone else.

Fourth hymnal from the aisle.

The book’s spine yields beneath his fingers. The wood behind it is cool, the varnish worn thin by years of devotion. He slides the hymnal free, slowly, reverently, and the world tilts.

At first, only a faint indentation, a shallow memory in wood. For a heartbeat he thinks it’s a heart; of course he does. That’s how he’s remembered it all these years, the easy symbol, the romantic shorthand.

But when the light shifts, the truth returns.

It isn’t a heart at all. The carving opens upon itself, looping endlessly, two halves meeting without beginning or end. An infinity.

The breath leaves him. Of course, she’d said it once, love was a thing unending.

Within the left curve lies the D, his own mark, cut in the impetuous hand of youth. And in the right, the upper arc of the C, the line he never finished, the cut arrested mid-turn on the night they caught him in the chapel.

The reasoning, the memory, the years, everything rushes back at once, bright as the light cresting through the rose window. He leans closer, the glow gathering along the pew’s edge, and it seems to him that memory, science, and love all describe the same curve when you look closely enough, each trying, in its own language, to name the infinite.

There, within the older incision, gleams a newer wound: the C, complete. The fresh wood is still pale against the darkened grain, a single gesture finishing what he began.

He exhales, the sound half laugh, half prayer.

The loop is whole.

A proof of life and love entwined.

He traces the line once more with the trembling tip of his finger, feeling the junction where one lifetime ends and another continues.

Then the light shifts again, and the chapel fills with silence so dense it seems to hum.




27.5  Reason


He leans back, fingertip resting on the new carving.

How could she have known? He never told her about that night. Never mentioned the unfinished mark or the knife that slipped when the footsteps came.

Yet somehow, she found it. Somehow… she finished it.

Light slides higher across the pew.

He reaches into his coat, surprised by the familiar weight: the old Polaroid camera. He hadn’t planned to bring it. He is relieved now, that he did.

Whrr-click.

A photograph breathes itself into being. Then another. He lays them side by side along the pew, fanning them in the warm air.

The first shows what he expects: the small loop, the initials. The second, something shifts. The grain ripples; the pattern multiplies. By the third, his breath has stopped.

The entire back of the pew is veined with interlocking infinities, the pale cuts shining like wet stone.The dust in the sunlight turns gold, and for a heartbeat he can imagine the moment she must have sat in this same aisle, carving knife trembling, finishing what he began.

He laughs.

Though nothing is necessarily funny.

His laughter, filled with tone infectious, rises from somewhere deeper, older, where thought gives up and sound begins.

The joy is too vast to name; it loops through him like current seeking its own return.

There is no reason to it, no restraint.

Only the certainty that a moment like this, perfect, unbroken, infinite, must, by the pure laws of nature, overflow.

He cannot stop it.

The laughter breaks from him in waves, bright and helpless, until it fills the chapel, echoing through the rafters, up the stone ribs, into light.

“Sir?”

The priest’s voice startles him.

The young man stands halfway down the aisle, concern and politeness divided across his face.

“I’m all right,” the old man says, still looking at the carving. “I didn’t know she’d found it… Didn’t know she’d finished it.”

The priest hesitates. “You knew her?”

He nods.

“You could say that.”

The priest steps closer, a small leather-bound book in his hands.

“She came often,” he says. “For years. Last week she brought this to my office. Said to keep it safe—that there would be someone. It’s the strangest thing… she said I’d know who it was for when they walked in the door and, well… it’s you… isn’t it?”

He shakes his head. An acknowledgment of great wonder and great sadness in one tip of his head.

“Well then, this is yours now.” The priest hands the well-loved, worn tome to the old man. “She said you’d understand.”

“Not sure that I do, but I’m deeply grateful to you, Father.”

“She was a grand lady… a grand, grand lady. I’ll miss her visits very much.”

He takes the book, turns it carefully in his hands. The cover is worn to suede, the spine soft, the pages thick with the faint scent of rosin and paper dust.

Inside: notes in her unmistakable script, curling around psalms, pressed flowers, a lock of hair.

Lines that begin in words and end in melody.

He can’t read for a while. The letters blur. He only feels the shape of her hand in the loops of ink.

He whispers, not to the priest but to the air.




27.6  Completion


Slowly, his vision clears, and he begins to read.

One margined scribble. A paragraph surrounding page number. page. Page after page he turns and reads.

Her voice so heavily imprinted, her heart the chorded muse.

As he continues, the tones of her begin to warble, becoming something less familiar. Older, braver, weaker, wise.

The ink seems also to afford time’s passing a solemn acquiescence; a changing over years—from black to blue, blue to pencil, and then to faint graphite pressed so softly it catches only when the light tilts.

Notes to him. To the music. To the world.

Fifty years of speaking into silence.

The chapel listens. Even the air seems to wait between one breath and the next.

He can’t help himself. He reads aloud a line written in the margin beside Psalm 42:


“Still practicing the same passage. Still thinking of you when the bow wavers. Still believing we’ll find the right note.”


His voice shakes.  She always cuts to his core.

He closes the book to steady it—with hope beyond believing that, in the end, with all those many symphonies between them, the right notes… found their way to fermata.

When he looks up, light from the rose window has spread across the floor, spilling over the pews in ribbons of red and gold.

He feels the warmth on his hands. For a moment, it seems to move with him, as though light and body share one slow rhythm.

The priest, standing nearby, watches.

He has seen grief before—students, widowers, parents—but never anything like this: joy and sorrow bound so tightly that neither can be told, one from the other.

The old man reaches to wipe a tear from his cheek, but finds instead a new bout of laughter bubbling within him, softer now, like someone remembering how to be loved.

“She finished it,” he says again. “All of it.”

The priest nods, not trusting words.

He sets a hand on the back of the pew.

The wood is warm. Vibrating.

A hum too low to be sound.

Somewhere deep below, a string is plucked. Then another.

A viola tuning. The pitch rises, steadies, holds.

The chapel joins it: the glass, the beams, the floor. Every surface begins to sing.

The old man looks around, as if surprised by his own heartbeat.

He sets the book down, places his palm over the carved loop, and closes his eyes.

The priest calls after him. No answer. Then again. A third time—softly. Still, no answer.

Silence.

Then, something stirs, as if the silence itself is answering in his stead.

The priest steps back.

Light thickens in the air; dust motes turn to stars, the pews to silhouettes.

A flare blossoms—white, soundless, absolute.

He throws up an arm to shield his eyes.

As it fades, the priest slowly turns back.

What he sees there before him defies the mind’s reach, but he does not allow the magic of now to negate the truth of

Yet he knows: “Faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen.”

The whispers of this truth, they steady him; giving strength where reason cannot suffice.

Before him, clear as breath, sit two figures where moments ago there sat only one.

They shimmer faintly.

A chord struck just beyond the threshold of hearing—visible only to the part of the eye that still remembers how to dream.

A woman’s hand rests on the man’s sleeve; the light around them trembles, alive and true.

A shared smile caught in amber’s eternal glow.

The grand oak doors of the chapel open of their own accord.

Snow light pours in—white, forgiving, endless.

They stand.

Walk toward it.

And in that moment the priest understands: the music he hears isn’t coming from the practice rooms below.

It’s coming from the walls themselves, from the beams and the glass and the centuries of sound, of love, of life stored within.

The priest does not chase them.

He only watches as the light folds around their shapes—until it’s only light, and then nothing at all.

When he finally walks down the aisle, the chapel has gone still.

Only the faint hum of the pipes remains.

He walks the aisle, slow.

On the pew: a book. Beside it, a Polaroid—sun-faded, motion-blurred, unmistakable.

He picks it up.

Two young lovers, laughing. Caught in motion and flare, as if even the film couldn’t keep up with them.

On the back: a single infinity loop.

And four words in a woman’s hand:

Always with you… Always.

He smiles. Sets the photograph beside the book.

Then turns to close the door.



27.7  For All


As the latch catches, a new sound rises from the catacombs beneath.

A single tuning fork struck by no one is answered by a tone just higher, then another, and another.

The frequencies climb through the harmonic series, each overtone joining the next until the air itself becomes choir.

Low fundamentals murmur beneath bright, glasslike upper voices; a ladder of resonance built from matter and memory.

Then one—the sixth in the series—bends, just shy of true.

A blue note to cradle the whole world inside its imperfection.

The priest doesn’t know the melody, but the words are already on his lips, the way a name returns when you least expect it, tickling the back of your mind until you speak the name aloud.

He whispers them once,

and the question that has followed him since boyhood—

Why?—finally releases its hold.



Outside, the wind carries the harmony across the fingered hills.

Inside, the chapel hums to keep the echo close.

The earth spins. Time sifts.

The Sun, radiant in its cosmic patience,

yearning for a new song to sing,

waits… for whoever dares to hear what comes

next.
