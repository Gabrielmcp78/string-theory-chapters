V. THE WEIGHT OF NAMES    September 1971
Three days after the ARGO incident

A man—young, slight, and moving with the precise care of someone trespassing inside memory—creaked open the oak doors of the newly renovated music chapel on East Hill.

Inside: silence. Dust. Cold breath in the air.

He slipped between pews and climbed behind the towering organ pipes. There, behind a false panel only he knew to unlatch, lay the entrance—an access tunnel few at the university knew existed, and fewer still could navigate.

It had never been meant for routine maintenance. It had been built for ARGO. A silent artery connecting the chapel to the most forbidden space on campus.

He stepped inside. The tunnel closed behind him with a quiet click. The air grew colder as he descended. The route was narrow, ribbed in iron and stone. No light but the one he carried. Symbols carved along the junctions—some chalk, some etched into steel—guided his way. Glyphs his uncle had taught him, back when he was still permitted inside the old language.

Twenty minutes forward, belly of the hill. Past abandoned ducts and purpose-built lies. Until he arrived—through the vent.

He emerged not at the lab, but within it.

The vent grating shifted silently behind him, sealing him inside the most quarantined space Cornell had ever erased.

The lab was humming. Run 28 had never stopped. The terminal glowed green in the dark. A glyph unfurled on-screen every 47 seconds—subtle variations, micro-differences, iterations approaching something unnamable.

He did not look. His uncle had warned him: Some structures only become dangerous when observed.

The thermal printer sat inert, its spool empty. The whiteboard bore Kenji's last thought—cut of, just stopped, mid-equation—as if his mind had simply… stepped out.

The young man photographed it. Then, pulling a perfectly starched, precisely folded handkerchief from his trousers, he wiped it clean.

He moved methodically, knowingly through the space. Every action ticking off one more item on the disciplined and absolute list of must-do’s streaming in his head. Seventeen notebooks went into his canvas bag. He would burn them. Not from fear. From duty.

The equipment breathed around him. The tubes were warm, oscillators idling at rest frequencies. He replaced one of them; a vacuum tube already blown due to the oils of ill-placed and uncovered fingers during installation. He cleared two clogged vents. Reattached a coaxial strand knocked half-loose.

This was not a shutdown procedure he was executing. This was maintenance. This was respect.

And then—before leaving—he moved to the main door. The primary seal. A steel slab the width of a vault, still bolted from the outside stood sentinel before him. No one had entered here since Dr. Tsukino vanished.

From the interior, he slid the iron bar into place. Massive. Groaning. The weight of finality. Now the door was sealed from both sides.

As he made his way back to the to the ventilation shaft, a chill of deep, cavernous air whipped through his chest. He coughed, cleared his throat—the echo of it spiraling out through the passage tunnels for an eternity. Just as the percussive echo died away, he swore he heard footsteps shuffle behind him in the room. He turned slowly, his mind screaming at him that it was nothing, his body, his joints completely disbelieving of such a narrative. Crawled back through the tunnel that joined sound to science.

Structure to pattern. Resonance to intent.

Twenty minutes later, he emerged again behind the chapel organ, dust on his coat, breath shallow but steady.

"Who's there?"

A voice from the nave. A flashlight beam scanned the darkness.

He stepped out.

“Sorry, Father. It's just me…”

The priest lowered the beam. “Mr. Ishikawa? At this hour?”

“I needed a place to think.” He gestured vaguely toward the pews.

The priest studied him. His expression softened. “I am heartily sorry Rune, for the pain and turmoil your family has endured these last weeks.”

“That is most kind of you, Father.”

“I'll leave you to it.”

“Thank you, Father.”

As the priest retreated, a young, relieved Rune Ishikawa stood alone in the chapel.

Beneath him, locked and sealed from within, the lab was still running. Still tuning. Still evolving.

Some experiments do not end. They wait…
