I. Falsifiable Fame
Moderato inquieto

The notifications found him in the shallow waters of sleep, a school of electric minnows nibbling at the edge of consciousness. David surfaced slowly, the ache in his ribs a dull compass orienting him to the reality of his own body in the dim apartment. His phone, face-up on the nightstand, pulsed with a persistent, arrhythmic light that seemed to bleed into the room’s pre-dawn grey.

He reached for it, the movement costing him a sharp intake of breath. The screen was a frantic scroll of subject lines from academic journal servers, a chorus of institutional names he’d long since associated with rejection. Physical Review Letters. Nature Physics. The Journal of Mathematical Physics. The sheer volume was the first impossibility; it was 6:47 a.m. He’d received more emails in the last ten minutes than in the previous month.

A low thrum began in the floorboards, a sensation more than a sound. The 68.48 Hz frequency. It had been a distant presence when he’d fallen asleep, a bass note to his exhaustion. Now it was closer, tuning the very air to its insistence. The phone’s vibrations in his hand seemed to sync with it, the digital pings acting as percussive accents to the sustaining drone.

He opened the first email. The header was from Physical Review Letters, but the formatting was off-kilter, the font a pixelated serif he didn’t recognize. The message was terse, devoid of the usual bureaucratic niceties.

  Professor Lang—

Your manuscript “Harmonic Convergence in Non-Linear Quantum Fields” has been accepted for immediate publication. Congratulations on your pioneering methodology. The peer review commentary is attached.

  —The Editors

Pioneering methodology. The phrase landed with the weight of a stone in his stomach. He’d been told his methodology was “speculative at best, numerically unsound at worst” by the same journal eight weeks ago.

He opened the attached peer review. The text was a labyrinth of near-sense. “…the author demonstrates a profound, if unorthodox, grasp of the vibrational substrate, though the trans-dimensional coupling coefficients require further justification within the prevailing paradigm of…”

It spiraled into jargon that gestured toward profundity but dissolved under scrutiny, like a figure seen through frosted glass. One reviewer’s comment was just a string of numbers:

  137.28…

   432…

  68.48

   5 3

  68.48…

Another email chimed. Nature Physics. Same story. Acceptance. Immediate publication. The review contained a paragraph that began with a coherent sentence about eigenfrequency analysis and ended with the phrase “…the music of the spheres is not a metaphor but a measurable perturbation in the quantum foam.”

Another notification. And another. A cascade of validation from institutions that had built walls to keep his kind of thinking out. He scrolled through the officially published versions online.

The PDFs were there, proper formatting, his name in bold type. But the citations—he checked them—were ghosts. References to papers that didn’t exist in any database, attributed to journals that had never been printed.

A laugh escaped him, sharp and brittle, pulling at the bruised muscles of his torso. It was a hideous joke, a cosmic mockery of everything he’d ever wanted. Yet, beneath the revulsion, a treacherous current of warmth spread through his chest. Validation. The drug he’d been starving for. It didn’t matter that the meal was poisoned; his hunger was too great. To see his work, his equations, laid out in the sacred formats of legitimate science… it short-circuited his higher criticism. The hum in the room seemed to amplify, resonating with this ugly, glorious feeling.

He pushed himself out of bed, the world tilting slightly before righting itself. The ritual of preparation for his morning lecture took on a new, surreal quality. The hot water in the shower beat against his skin in waves that felt syncopated, out of phase with itself. As he dressed, his fingers trembled slightly buttoning his shirt. He chose the one with the least chalk dust.

His gaze fell on the tuning fork, lying on the rumpled sheets where it had fallen from his pocket during the night. The faint cerulean stain on its prongs seemed to glow in the weak light. He picked it up. The metal was cool, but it carried a latent energy, a silent potential. He slipped it into his breast pocket, the weight of it a familiar anchor against his sternum.

The hum deepened, vibrating up through the soles of his feet. It was no longer just a sound; it was a pressure, a presence tuning the apartment, tuning him, for whatever was coming next. He looked at his reflection in the dark screen of his laptop—a pale man with a bruised face, wearing the clothes of a professor who had, against all reason and logic, just become an overnight success.

II. Dream Lecture
Andante sostenuto - Andante luminoso

The walk to campus was conducted under a dome of sound. The 68.48 Hz frequency had woven itself into the fabric of the morning, a pressure that vibrated behind his eyes and made the autumn leaves tremble on their branches with a sympathy that felt unnatural. It was in the grind of the city bus’s engine, the distant whine of construction equipment, the very hum of the power lines overhead. The world had become a resonator for a single, insistent note.

He turned the corner toward Feynman Hall, and the pressure intensified, solidifying into a wall of noise that seemed to emanate from the building itself. The entrance was clogged with bodies. Students stood three deep in the doorway, craning their necks. This was for his 9 a.m. elective, “Advanced Topics in Quantum Foundations,” a class that typically drew eighteen dedicated souls and a handful of the hopelessly lost.

He pushed through the periphery of the crowd, a few muttering “’Scuse me, Professor,” their eyes not quite meeting his. The interior of the hall was a shock. Every seat was taken. Students sat cross-legged in the aisles, leaned against the side walls, perched on the steps between rows. The air was thick with the heat of too many bodies and a tense, electric anticipation.

And in the back row, lined up like skeptical crows on a wire, sat the department’s old guard. Professors who had dismissed his work in faculty meetings, who had written letters questioning his tenure viability, whose own research was a monument to cautious incrementalism. They watched him, faces unreadable.

On the projection screen at the front of the hall was the title page of his newly published—impossibly published—paper from Physical Review Letters. Someone had already put it up. He hadn’t sent it to anyone.

As he moved down the center aisle, the hum focused into a spearpoint of pressure aimed directly at his sternum. His vision greyed at the edges. The weight of all those eyes, the physical press of the sound, the sheer improbability of the scene—it was a dissonance too great to sustain. His hand went to his breast pocket, fingers closing around the cool metal of the tuning fork. He didn’t take it out; he just held it through the fabric of his shirt, pressing it against his pounding heart.

The unbearable pressure of the hum shifted, sliding from a threat towards something that seemed foundational. He was calibrating to it—no, he was being tuned just like everyone in the room—twisted and tamed in this peculiar harmony. The noise in his head burned of, clearing like early cloud cover as he reached the podium. Then taking a moment to place his notes, he took stock: breathe even, limbs calm, no fidget, no concerns, no flop sweat, no errant thoughts. Actually no thoughts at all. He had no idea what was bout lecture on, but in having no idea, he knew every word was ready and waiting for him.

And as he looked out at the sea of faces it was clear, they were waiting for him too. There was none of the usual pre-lecture chatter of phones and whispered conversations. The room was simply filled with that brand of reverent silence that was itself a form of attention.

His voice as he began, emerged clear and measured. He didn’t read from his notes. Each slide on the screen, each concept introduced simply unspooled from him with a fluency that felt both like his and not his. He was a conduit. The traversal and explanation of the complex connections between harmonic oscillation and quantum decoherence, between vibrational modes and information transfer, he managed it all with clarity and rigor. Even some humor was shining through here and there, and this time it was not deployed as cover for some idiotic lapse of his own making. The words were there, perfect and ready, as if he were reading from a script written on the inside of his skull.

He saw understanding dawn on faces that were usually blank with boredom. A sophomore who had struggled with basic wave mechanics was nodding, her pencil poised over her notebook as she captured the flow of ideas. The skeptics in the back row had lost their skeptical slouches. They were leaning forward, one with his glasses pushed up on his forehead, squinting at the screen as if trying to decipher a magic trick.

The scheduled hour ended, and to his delight, no one moved. Hands went up all across the room. The Q&A began, and the questions were not the usual requests for clarification. They were probes, deep and intelligent, coming from undergraduates who yesterday couldn’t solve a simple eigenvalue problem. A physics major asked about the implications for quantum gravity using terminology usually reserved for graduate seminars. A music composition student, whom David had never noticed before, asked a startlingly precise question about the mapping of dissonance onto probability fields.

He answered them all. The answers came from somewhere beyond his conscious mind, articulated with a confidence that was entirely new to him. He was playing the room like his cello, and the room was resonating back at exactly the right frequency. The tuning fork in his pocket was a cool, silent witness to it all.

Finally, after ninety minutes, the spell broke. The department chair in the back row stood, giving a curt nod that seemed to release everyone else from their trance. The room erupted into genuine applause before floating down into the low buzz of conversation. Students gathered their things slowly, as if waking from a deep sleep.

David stood at the podium, his skin buzzing, his mind eerily quiet. The triumph was there, a bright, hot coal in his gut. He had delivered the lecture of his life. But as the hall emptied, the hum returned to its baseline drone, and he was left with the cold, metallic taste of a question: What, exactly, had just happened?

III. Groundbreaking
Allegretto meccanico

The bubble of focused energy from the lecture hall popped the moment he stepped into the corridor. The din of student chatter returned to normal frequencies, but the aftermath of his performance left a wake of awkwardness. Before he could retreat to his office, they descended upon him—the very colleagues whose presence had been so unnerving.

“David. Groundbreaking methodology,” said Professor Evans from condensed matter theory, his hand shooting out for a shake. The grip was firm, lasted a second too long, and his eyes seemed fixed on a point just past David’s left ear.

“Yes, just groundbreaking,” echoed Dr. Chen from astrophysics, emerging from behind Evans with the same vacant smile. She tilted her head at precisely the same angle. “The implications are… revolutionary.”

“Revolutionary implications,” said a third voice. It was Markov, the department’s resident pragmatist, who had once called David’s work “mathematical astrology.” He repeated the phrase with the same cadence, the same slight head tilt. Their congratulations were a round of cannon fire, each shot landing in the same crater. Their eyes held the same glazed quality, their handshakes the same prolonged firmness. It was like being congratulated by a trio of expertly crafted androids whose programming had a minor bug.

David extracted his hand from Markov’s grip, a cold knot tightening in his stomach. “Thank you,” he managed, his voice sounding thin. “I… I should get to office hours.”

He fled down the hall, the echo of their identical praise ringing in his ears. He locked his office door behind him, leaning against it for a moment, breathing in the familiar scent of old books and dust. The framed photo on his desk—a younger him and Celeste at a long-ago conference, smiling—seemed to accuse him of this sudden, unearned acclaim.

He tried to grade papers, but the symbols on the page swam. The hum was a constant presence, a bass note underscoring his anxiety. A soft knock came at the door. He sighed, expecting another robotic colleague.

It was a graduate student, Mia, one of his few remaining advisees. Her face was pale, her fingers nervously tracing the edge of her laptop.

“Professor Lang? Do you have a minute?”

“Of course, Mia. Come in.”

She sat in the worn chair opposite his desk, not meeting his eyes. “It’s about the lecture. And… your paper.” She opened her laptop, the screen flaring to life. It was open to the PDF of his Nature Physics publication. “I was trying to work through the derivation in section three, and…” she trailed off, chewing her lip.

“Uh… yes, of course. Section three. And?” David prompted, a familiar dread rising. The section was the most speculative, the part he’d expected the most criticism on.

“My computer… it’s doing something weird.” She turned the screen toward him.

The equations on the screen were his, but they were alive. They flickered, pixels dancing at the edges. As he watched, a sigma symbol morphed into a treble clef. A partial derivative dissolved into a shimmering staff of musical notation. The text of the peer review comments beside the equations scrolled upward on its own, the words breaking apart and recombining into nonsensical phrases: “…the eigenfrequency of regret…” “…superposition of lament…” “…harmonic decay of institutional memory…”

“It started during your lecture,” Mia whispered, her voice tight with fear. “It’s not just mine.”

As if on cue, a rhythmic clicking started from the laser printer in the corner. It began printing sheet after sheet of paper covered not in text, but in dense, pulsating patterns of black and white that resembled both sound waves and fractal equations. Down the hall, a chorus of notification tones erupted from offices—not the usual random chirps, but a synchronized sequence of notes that perfectly outlined a descending minor scale. The fluorescent light above David’s desk flickered in time with it, 68.48 times per second.

David’s own phone, sitting on the desk, lit up. Notification after notification began cascading down the screen.

He glanced up from the phone with a bewildered grin. “Guess I’m… I’m pretty popular today.”

He had meant it to defuse. It did not defuse. Neither was it funny, that seemed clear. David looked back to the screen, giving way to the general awkwardness that, in truth, defined his entire career of student interaction. He tapped the next notification bubble to appear. The screen went completely black. At first, David thought he had perhaps clicked the damn thing back to sleep. But no. It came roaring back to life. Screaming in bold white text over and over against the black screen:

THE FIRST MARK SECOND ORDER NOT WHERE YOU LEFT IT…… 6

THE FIRST MARK OF THE SECOND ORDER IS NOT WHERE…8———5

THE FIRST MARK OF THE SECOND ORDER………4

THE SECOND MARK OF THE FIRST…8———————3

He instantly flipped the phone, slamming its face down on the desk to his left.

“Just… um… scam email nonsense. Do you get any of that?”

“Um, no. My boyfriend installed this blocker thing that pretty much kills that stuff. If… if you like, I could have him—“

“No. No.” David tried to play it off. Yet another instance of clear and demoralizing student technological superiority on full display. A sensation David feared was increasingly arthritis-inducing, full body, full stop.

“Thank you, Mia, but…”

Mia hugged herself, her eyes wide. She looked from the glitching laptop to the possessed printer to David’s frantic phone. The ambient hum in the room seemed to sharpen, becoming a physical force that vibrated the cup of pens on the corner of his desk. He reached over and slammed a palm over them, stomping the jitter out. A single, well-sharpened piece of no. 2 lead embedded into the center of his palm. He paused there for a moment, grimace-faced and coasting.

“Professor Lang,” she asked, her voice small and terrified, pointing at the impossible equations still dancing on her screen. “Is this… is this supposed to happen?”

The question hung in the air, the most sensible thing he’d heard all day. It shattered the last vestige of his triumphalist delusion. He looked at the terror on his student’s face, at the technology rebelling against reality, and the cool weight of the tuning fork in his pocket felt less like a key and more like a symptom.

“No, Mia,” he said, his voice hollow. “No, it is most definitely not… supposed… to happen.”

Mia, now amongst the very last of his core students still hanging on with the uncanny Professor, had witnessed moments like this before. With Lang, the prevailing wisdom was always to gracefully make your exit and leave him to it—what ever… it was. And so, she… did.

IV. Everything he was not
Andante cantabile

The dissonant symphony of his own day made the harmony of Celeste’s lecture hall feel like stepping into a sacred space. David slipped into the back of the room as she was mid-sentence, her voice a clear, carrying instrument that needed no amplification. She stood before a grand staff projected on the screen, one hand resting on the piano behind her, the other tracing patterns in the air.

“So if we accept that Bach’s Art of Fugue is essentially a study in mathematical permutation,” she was saying, “then the real question isn’t about the beauty of the variation, but about the constraints of the original theme. It’s a cosmological constant. A set of initial conditions from which an entire universe of music unfolds.”

She played a brief, complex phrase on the piano. The notes hung in the air, precise and inevitable.

“Now,” she continued, turning back to the whiteboard where she’d drawn a series of overlapping circles, “map that onto the Many-Worlds Interpretation of quantum mechanics. Each decision point in the composition—a modulation, a rhythmic shift—doesn’t replace the previous path. It branches from it. The fugue doesn’t choose a single reality. It contains all possible realities simultaneously, held in a state of… musical superposition.”

The students were riveted. She made the dizzying connection feel obvious, elegant. She used her hands to illustrate the branching paths, her gestures economical and sure. This was her element: the liminal space between disciplined structure and creative explosion, a space David had always tried to quantify but that she simply inhabited.

He watched, a familiar ache of professional admiration mixing with a deeper, more personal longing. She was everything he was not: grounded where he was untethered, communicatively graceful where he was clumsy, certain in her interdisciplinary leaps where he was plagued by doubt. Her dark hair was pulled back, emphasizing the focused intensity of her expression. She caught a student’s confused frown and rephrased the concept without condescension, using a metaphor about tectonic plates shifting to explain harmonic tension. The student’s face cleared. She was a maestro of clarity.

The lecture ended to a respectful, attentive silence followed by a genuine swell of applause. Celeste smiled, a real, unguarded expression that transformed her face. She gathered her notes, and her eyes scanned the room as the students began to pack up. They found him in the back. Her smile didn’t fade, but it shifted, becoming more complex, tinged with a question.

An hour later, they were at her house. The familiar chaos of her kitchen was a comfort. She poured wine while he sat at the table, the events of his day receding like a fever dream in the face of her calm presence. They slid into the old rhythms of their conversation with an ease that felt like forgiveness.

“So the Kepler group actually showed up?” she asked, shaking her head as she stirred a pot wide egg noodles boiling on the stove. “After that review they gave your tensor network paper?”

“They did. And they all said the same thing.”

“Let me guess,” Celeste leaned over the rising steam, fogging her glasses into blindness. Then she turned to David and with a straight face said, “I just don’t see it, Professor Long.”

David wasn’t laughing. She wiped the fog from her lenses just for visual confirmation. Yup, she bombed.

“Celeste, they all were falling over themselves to shake my hand. I can’t believe Word for word. It was like they’d been programmed.”

“And the student’s computer?”

“Glitching. Turning equations into musical notation. The department printer is probably still outputting sheet music for the apocalypse.”

She listened, her responses sharp and insightful, cutting to the heart of the absurdity. Their talk flowed from the eerie events to the solid ground of theory, to gossip about faculty politics, to a passionate digression about the use of the golden ratio in Bartók’s string quartets. Their hands brushed over the bowl of olives, over the bread basket—small, accidental contacts that sent a current of warmth through him each time.

The wine loosened the remaining knots of tension in his shoulders. The hum was still there, a faint presence at the very edge of hearing, but here, with her, it felt like a distant storm, not an imminent avalanche.

In her bedroom, the light was soft. Her fingers, which had traced equations on a board hours before, now traced the yellowing bruises on his ribs with a violinist’s precise tenderness. He flinched, then relaxed into the touch, the residual pain transforming under her attention from a memory of violence to a marker of present care. They found their way back to each other with the ease of a well-rehearsed piece of music, a familiar cadence in a world that was increasingly out of tune. For a few hours, the relentless hum was just background noise, drowned out by the more immediate frequencies of skin and breath and heartbeat. He fell asleep with the scent of her hair—rosin and lavender—in his lungs, a temporary, merciful silence in his head.

v. Friendly forensics
Moderato secco

The campus bar was a cave of polished dark wood and perpetual twilight. Roger Linn was already there, nestled in a high-backed booth, a half-finished pint of stout on the table. He raised the glass in a slow, deliberate salute as David approached. The gesture lacked enthusiasm.

“To overnight fame,” Linn said, his voice dry. “Or infamy. Hard to tell the difference these days.” His eyes, sharp and assessing, held none of the glazed fervor of the other professors. They were deeply, resolutely skeptical.

David slid into the opposite bench, the leather sighing under his weight. “It’s just… a sudden break, Roger. It happens.”

“Does it?” Linn took a slow drink. “Funny thing. A friend of mine—good physicist, solid work—tried submitting to the Journal of Quantum Resonance last month. Six-month waiting list, they told him. Rigorous peer review. Yet you…” He leaned forward, elbows on the table, lowering his voice. “You got three papers through in what, a day? Two?”

The question hung in the air between them, accompanied by the clink of glasses and the low murmur of other conversations. David’s grip tightened on his own untouched beer. The cold seeped into his palm.

“The work is sound,” David said, the defense sounding hollow even to himself.

“I’m sure it is. Or it was.” Linn’s gaze was unwavering. “But the vehicle, David… There are whispers. Digital paper mills that can fabricate entire citation histories. Phantom peer reviews. And the IT department is logging campus-wide anomalies—network spikes, data corruption… all sorts of rhythmic nonsense that started right around the time your ‘breakthrough’ hit the servers.” He let the implication settle like a sediment in the bottom of a glass. “Just seems like a hell of a coincidence.”

Later, at Celeste’s house, the comfort of the previous night felt distant. She had printed out one of his newly published papers and laid it on her dining table like a coroner preparing for an autopsy. Her finger, which had traced his bruises with such tenderness, now moved with clinical precision down the pages.

“This peer review,” she said, pointing to a comment, “it cites a paper by E. Guare from 1972 on resonant frequencies.”

“So?”

“Eleanor Guare co-founded the ARGO Project with Tsukino. She died in the ’71 incident. She never published anything in ’72. This journal didn’t even exist then.” Her finger moved again. “This citation. The volume number is wrong. This one… the author is a professor at a university that closed in 1985.” She looked up, her face pale in the lamplight. “David,” she said, her voice careful, quiet, each word placed with deliberate weight. “This isn’t real. Something’s happening, and I don’t think it’s good.” She wasn’t accusing him. She was warning him. She was pointing at the cracks in the glorious façade, and he could no longer pretend not to see them.

VI. Ishikawa’s Island
Grave controllato

The only light in Rune Ishikawa’s office came from the green‑shaded lamp on his desk, a small island of order in the encroaching night. As he sat, he removed from his pocket a note from his secretary marked CONFIDENTIAL EYES ONLY ISHIKAWA. He unfolded it and laid it flat upon the desk in front of him. It simply read:

  Contacted. Assessed. Delivered. Done. Yours now. —E.G.

He sat there in a silence that had just been compactified. Deeper… darker. Yet Rune had already contained and compressed the information and it’s implications into the geometry of both room and reality alike. The silence of density confirming isolation. Then more silence… and more. Fifty three seconds after he had first read the confidential note, he reached to answer the phone. It had not rung. His hand hovered, open, ready above the receiver. The phone’s ring sliced through the silence like a scalpel—sharp, present—collapsing his held palm over the receiver. He answered with a single, economical word that was both name and declaration of territory:

“Ishikawa.”

Julian Kline’s voice was barely human, stripped down to signal and static, a raw nerve of fear vibrating along the wire.

“It’s happening… again.”

Rune’s grip on the receiver tightened. The polished bakelite warmed under his fingers, as if the plastic itself remembered previous calls like this.

He said nothing.

“The conference was just the beginning.” Kline’s voice twitched an octave upward, trying to stabilize. “It’s worse now. It’s bleeding outward. Campus‑wide.”

Rune kept his silence. Let him say it all.

Kline’s breathing hitched. “The physics building network hub has been broadcasting a steady 68.48 Hz. signal for over eighteen hours. It’s not just noise—it’s rewriting packet structures. Recursive behavior. The main library’s cooling fans have locked into harmonic resonance. They’re singing, Rune. I mean literally. The harmonic sequence matches the cello note described by—by over a dozen students in Lang’s lecture.”

Rune closed his eyes—not from fatigue, but for focus. His mind began constructing the shape of the breach.

“Manifestations Julian,” rune assured him, “Thats all.”

“Identical auditory hallucinations across multiple subjects. Lissajous curves forming on oscilloscopes in labs running separate iterations of Lang’s equations. Curves that shouldn’t even be possible with their setups. And the people—”

He faltered.

Rune waited.

“Their behavior. Faculty, staff. Repetitive language. Recycled cadence. Vacant expression. Not admiration—resonance. Like they’re… syncing.”

Rune spoke, flat; a diagnostic inquisition. “The acceleration curve?”

“Matches the projections from the classified models,” Kline said. “Almost exactly. Local anomalies shifting toward full-spectrum phase invasion. We’re at the inflection point, Rune. Maybe past it.”

Rune’s voice sharpened. “You’re off‑site.”

“CUNY gateway sniff. Remote tap. I looped in Ithaca’s net admin just to cross-check protocol drift. They bounced me to campus security. Now I’ve got Legal requesting a formal statement on ‘extraordinary data artifacts.’” A dry, bitter laugh. “They want me to confirm or deny with reference to the 1971 event.”

Rune didn’t flinch, but something inside him stilled.

“Only three people on the planet have clearance to speak to that directly,” Kline said. “You’re… the least likely to trip an alarm.”

Rune looked around his office, the lamplight casting old edges into long shadows. “What do you want from me, Julian?”

“I want you to admit you see it. Like last time. Like the signs were there then too. Because this isn’t isolated. It’s the same pattern—same harmonics, same mathematical drift toward phase‑lock. And now it’s not just data. It’s behavior.”

Rune let the silence do its work. Across the line, Kline’s breathing trembled with the weight of what he wasn’t saying.

Rune spoke evenly. “You’ll be asked to join a containment task force. You will accept. No publication. No independent communication. If anyone begins documenting without clearance, you shut it down.”

A pause.

“Do not reference Tsukino’s models by name. Don’t mention Guare at all. Someone is still listening.”

A dangerous beat, then Kline said, quieter, “They said you’ve been tracking the echo ever since.”

Rune exhaled. “In my possession. Secure. Not relevant to the present vector.”

Kline let out a sound—not quite a laugh, not quite a breath.

“You know, when I first sent you his work—twenty years ago—I thought I was being responsible. Undergraduate with an unsettling grasp of harmonics, odd affect, questions that didn’t land right. I figured you’d glance at it, tell me I was being dramatic, and that would be the end of it.”

“You were being dramatic,” Rune said.

“Sure. But you still told me to keep an eye on him.”

A beat passed.

“I was supposed to age out of this,” Kline said. “Become the guy who warns his niece about predatory journals and tells undergrads they’re not as original as they think they are.”

“You do that anyway,” Rune replied.

“It was meant to be that scale of problem, Rune. Footnotes. Archival weirdness. Archaeology.”

“This isn’t archaeology,” Rune said.

“I know. Which is why I’m saying—I’m not Indiana Jones.”

“No.”

“So why does it feel like every time this kid opens his mouth, I’m being chased by a boulder?”

“Because you are.”

Then silence—longer this time. The joking stopped where it always had to.

“If Lang shows up again,” Kline said, the edge back in his voice, “do I engage?”

“Do not,” Rune said. “Not directly. Not until instructed.”

There was no protest. Just the soft hiss of the line.

“Are we safe, Rune?”

Rune didn’t answer immediately.

He thought back—not to the moment in ’71 itself, but to the aftermath: the survivors no one ever talked about, who had exited academia with blank stares and shallow breaths, who never published again, never taught again. Some died quietly. Some vanished. A few… kept watching.

“Stay off the main grid after midnight,” Rune said, and hung up.

He allowed himself thirty seconds—an indulgence by his standards—to stare at the bookshelf.

Then he moved.

His fingers found a particular leather volume—Theoretical Calorimetrics, 1887—and pulled it downward. A soft metallic click. The shelf unlocked, swinging inward a fraction. He wedged the tip of his pen into the seam and pulled.

Behind the hidden panel was a shallow compartment. A single object waited there: a navy-blue metal filing box, chipped and scuffed like something rescued from a forgotten war. A brass key was taped to the lid.

Rune carried it to the desk like hazardous material. Set it in the lamp’s green glow. Turned the key.

The box sighed open.

PROJECT ARGO—TSUKINO / GUARE—INCIDENT 11/28/71—EYES ONLY.

He laid out the contents with the precision of a coroner. Yellowed photos of a lab torn apart from within. Witness statements, half redacted, later retracted. The fear still bled through, as if embedded in the ink itself. And the equations—first in Tsukino’s elegant hand, later overrun with Guare’s frantic, collapsing script. Notes tumbling into corrections, the margins cracking under desperation.

As he removed that last page, he placed his finger into the back right corner of the box and pressed gently. A slight mechanical click sounded, and the bottom flipped up, revealing a slim hidden compartment underneath. Inside, a single envelope, yellowed with age but carefully preserved. He took it out with a reverence that, even for Rune, seemed uncommon. The letter was addressed to Kenji Tsukino. Its return address—St. John, U.S. Virgin Islands. Posted March of 1965.

Rune exuded the letter, unfolded the stationery, and took a deep, solemn breath.

Dear Dr. Tsukino,  March 1, 1965

I write to on behalf of myself, Elliot Lang, and the brave scientists who we both had the privilege of working with at L.A. Dr. Lang has informed me that he relayed to you certain data packs and research touching on the Frequency problem, and that you intend to pick up the work. I very much regret that anyone has been further burdened with this subject.

If not for my health, I would be standing in front of you now to deliver this admonishment in person. I fear you do not understand the weight of what you are brushing up against. No one can, until it has touched them and played them for fools.

Kenji, you are a brilliant man of science, but you and I both know that brilliance does not, should not equal permission.

There. Enough from me. My love to your dear wife. Please be careful in this, old friend.

  Yours,

      J.R.O.

P.S. I’ve enclosed a small reminder of that which I speak. 0.7 grams. That’s all we did. Tuned 0.7 grams.

As he folded the letter and picked up the envelope, a small object rustled inside. Rune tipped it up and into his palm slid a small, rusted-over paperclip.

He held it in the palm of his hand and turned to gaze out the window onto the quad beyond.

  Less than a gram… tuned to calamity.

Then, restoring the object and the letter into the envelope and, once housed, all three into the compartment, he withdrew from the shallow desk drawer to his left. Rune pulled Lang’s most recent preprint and placed it beside the old files.

They were not similar. They were not inspired by. They were… identical.

Recursive harmonic functions. Coupling coefficients linking acoustic resonance to quantum collapse. The same beautiful, impossible idea: that the substrate of reality could be tuned.

In the final page of Tsukino’s notes, a line circled so violently the paper had torn:

68.48 Hz—The Frequency—Initiates phase‑lock—DO NOT allow sustained resonance.

Lang hadn’t plagiarized. He hadn’t theorized. He’d rediscovered.

The system was converging around him, folding reality into a new harmonic configuration. Peer review, instant publication, algorithmic favor—they weren’t coincidence. They were symptoms.

He picked up the phone again, this time to his secretary.

“Jessica, would you get me on Professor Armitage’s schedule—I need the next 20 minutes she’s got, please.”

“Certainly, sir.”

He held the line as she linked calendars.

“Dr. Ishikawa, Celeste has office hours posted this afternoon. She’s available in just over an hour.”

“Not anymore, she’s not. I’ll take it. Thank you, Jessica.”

“Certainly, sir.”

He hung the receiver back in its cradle, then slid open the center drawer of his desk and withdrew an elegant silver cigar lighter. He looked to the note from Guare, considering it one last time.

  Yours now.

Then, lifting the confidential page from the desktop between his index and middle fingers—as if it were something contaminated—he held it over the wastebasket at his feet and struck the flint.

The paper caught quickly, curling inward as flame and smoke consumed its edges. Just before the heat reached his fingers, he released it into the empty bin below. A faint hiss rose from the flames as trapped moisture fled into vapor under the growing heat. Brief crackles followed, rigid fibers giving way, leaving behind a fragile lattice of ash that finally let slip a ribbon of smoke, thinning as it rose, until it dissolved into the stillness of the room.

Rune sat back, letting the chair creak beneath him like a ship under pressure. He had tried, years ago, to divert Lang’s trajectory. Subtly. Quietly. But the boy hadn’t just veered off course.

He’d built a ramp.

And the world was already mid‑air.

VII. The Thickening

The air in David's apartment had thickened into something he moved through rather than breathed. The 68.48 Hz frequency was no longer a sound but a medium—resistant, filling, present in his lungs with every breath.

He stood in the kitchen watching a glass of water. Its surface quivered with impossible precision: standing waves formed a perfect grid, peaks and nodes holding steady. When he touched it, the pattern shattered, then reasserted itself stronger than before.

A low groan came from the wall. The plaster had beaded with moisture that shivered in time with the hum. He pressed his palm flat against it. The vibration traveled up his arm, into his jaw.

He found the tuning fork in his pocket—cool, certain. He struck it against the counter.

The tone cut through. For three seconds, the room settled. The water stilled. The wall stopped trembling. The pressure in his head abated.

Then the note decayed. The hum returned, amplified now, almost angry. Water splashed over the glass rim. The wall pulsed.

He stumbled to the cello case and pressed the vibrating fork against its side. The instrument responded, its hollow body weaving the tone into a richer chord. The room held its breath again. Another few seconds of peace. Another decay.

The hum rushed back louder than before, and now it carried the ghost of the pure tone he'd introduced into something dissonant. The cello's strings began to vibrate in sympathy.

He sank to the floor, back against the instrument, the silent fork in his hand. The hum was in the floorboards, in the building, in his bones. His thoughts began to align to its rhythm. Panic gave way to something else—something altogether quieter.
