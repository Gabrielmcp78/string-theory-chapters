I. 
Lento misterioso, senza misura

In the thinning veil where light bent toward shadow, David’s senses recoiled from the insubstantial, reassembling around familiar edges—the worn cello case leaning like a sentinel, the math‑riddled walls breathing softly in the ambient dusk.

David drew a slow breath, spine unfolding beneath a weight he had not yet dared to bear. The air thickened, then settled, draping the expanse in a heavy velvet that made every detail feel newly intimate.

And then the only sound the man needed to hear in this moment floated through him—every ache and pain and pore of him—in one familial voice: the voice of a father, and of his love:

Some things are not meant for an audience…

  Like this, then that. Closer. Don’t grip. Just…

  Three steps: breathe… Three steps: breathe.

He felt his own edges go soft as the hand resting gently on his shoulder—the hand he hadn’t noticed was there all along—softly lifted away. Warmth. Then the specific absence of warmth. Cold air. The kind that carries the particular intelligence of October on a hill that has known many such Octobers.

Then something hard and familiar beneath him. He looked down at his hands. They were in his lap. They were his. The blood on the palm—faint now, days‑old it seemed, already becoming history.

He knew exactly where he was. He did not know how he had arrived, but he understood, without needing to examine it further, that this was the correct order of things.

East Hill flared out before him in the dense blur of first light. The chapel steeple, the cresting tops of bare‑branched trees above, catching the first gray‑gold suggestion of morning’s barely‑light. The bench beneath him creaked once—the sound of old iron remembering weight—and then was still.

Six feet below, something hummed. 68.48 Hz. And then, slowly, gently, it slid down its long‑held density, shedding layer by layer—relinquishing tone for tonality first, and then… the hum slid past 53.67 Hz, down to 33.15 Hz, 20.52 Hz… It passed through the ears of humanity’s grasp—12.67 Hz, 7.83 Hz… and then was gone.

David sat with it for a moment—sat and listened. He sat and looked out into the end of darkness as light began to press forth from the deep and reach for him. He could hear it again—this new light.

The dawn that would come had not yet breached the horizon, but he felt it gathering. When he felt sure that light was the only sound he heard, he stood.

The first fissure lay far beyond the low-slung roofs and skeletal trees that flared out over lower East Hill and to the city beyond. A pale incision along the rim of the horizon as though the earth itself had been quietly opened during the night and was now revealing the faintest interior glow. The air possessed that hourless stillness that belongs neither to night nor to morning but lingers between them like a hummingbird hovering, its wings fluttering with such speed as to make movement in either direction seem to lose the appearance of action.

He rolled his shoulders once, then again, listening inwardly for complaint, and found only the easy mechanics of a body unburdened. He bent at the waist, touched his palms flat against the cold planks of the bench, and straightened again without effort, as though some unseen clerk had cleared his ledger of aches during the interval between… then and now was as specific as his mind would in this moment allow.

  So this is what normal feels like.
