IV. THE APARTMENT
Adagio, con malinconia

As he left the music school, David felt the city return in sharp lines and pale silence. The snow-lit quad stretched before him, a frozen ledger of the passing of souls. Two sets of footprints: hers and his, running parallel until they split. At that point, the path they’d shared for years broke quietly apart—hers turning toward the bridge, his descending into the dark fold of lower East Hill.

He didn’t look back. He couldn’t. He knew what he’d see: his own solitude, pressed into the snow like a sentence without end. Winter, unblinking, recorded everything.

The walk home took ten minutes. Lately, it felt like less. The body moved before thought arrived. Eighteen months of repetition had erased the in-between—those few blocks where once there was conversation, or silence that held meaning. Now, he left rehearsal and found himself at the apartment door, unsure what had passed in the time between. Grief had become a function. A habit. A reflex.

The door to the apartment, with its tall frosted-green glass, shut behind him with a hollow cough—the closing of a vault. The narrow entry hall funneled him forward until the apartment opened into its two-story cavity. Brick rose the full height of the far wall, the fireplace centered like a sentinel. In the far left corner, a slender vertical window cut upward through jagged brickwork; glass banded in uneven waves. On the right wall, a long horizontal casement stretched twelve feet across the kitchen wall, its lower sill just shy of the island’s steel lip.

The spiral staircase wound at the room’s center like a coil of wire, its glass treads catching stray city light. Above, the loft—wire-railed, half office, half catwalk—looked down into the main chamber. On the wall by the entry, the chalkboard installation waited: three massive counterweighted panels, disguised when shut as a plain white surface but ready to unfurl floor to ceiling like a trifold altar. Even dormant, it radiated the threat of dust.

Loneliness had crystallized here, a structure wholly unsympathetic. Neglect hung from crown-molding cracks. Chalk dust caked at the baseboards. A stranger with even minimal powers of observation could pin David to this place and vice versa in two minutes flat.

It was not a warm space, but neither was it clinical. Its design hovered in a state of unfinished intent—at once composed and disjointed. Though it had not been wholly thought through, David found it curiously suited to thinking. Those few who had spent time within its walls all remarked on the same thing: the apartment detested company. And yet, with David, it behaved differently, as if it had chosen him. It enfolded him with a quiet, maternal sense of care, a belonging that asked for nothing in return.

Sheet music colonized every surface, allowing for a massive B-minor scale to sprawl out across the coffee table. Clef symbols and poly-quivers curling out of time—the turgid product of overwritten tensor equations allowed to run roughshod over the terrain. The apartment was losing itself to resonance. Everything had new permission to ring.

In the kitchen, David found himself uncertain whether the hollowness was hunger or something else entirely. He resolved the question the only way he knew—through precision. The julienne demanded it. Each stroke of the blade against the board became staccato percussion, syncopating with the blue hiss of the stovetop flame. Combustion harmonics emerged among the carrots and pilot lights. The ordinary thermodynamics of his kitchen began to pulse with an unfamiliar rhythm, and he had no choice but to move with it.

Rallentando

As he sat down to eat, his notebook slipped from the arm of the couch, cozying up beside him like a supplicant. Soon its pages were fat with the whispers of midnight epiphanies: diagrams of harmonic oscillator eigenstates and the jagged calculus of vibrato dynamics. His handwriting slanted forward, letters like fermions fleeing confinement. He slashed through a line, tearing the paper—the sound of the rupture echoing the cello string’s snap.

The same adrenaline-boil scalded him. It was a disconnected synesthesia he could no longer govern. It felt as though his own electromagnetic field was slipping frequency, dragging his body chemistry with it.

To establish a baseline—to confirm that cause still produced effect—he reached into his pocket. The folded note from his TA was still there. He didn’t look at it right away. He held the paper between his fingers as if it could anchor him.

He knew what it contained. The scrawled reprimand. The catalog of errors. Proof of the afternoon’s collapse.

He unfolded it.

What he saw ruptured something cleanly and at once. The page was covered in doodles. Nothing else. No notes. No corrections. Just idle shapes looping across the paper.

David dropped it as if it had turned venomous. The page drifted to the floor—harmless, obscene. He stepped on it hard, grinding it into the oak bellow, as though pressure alone might suffocate whatever truth it threatened to assert.

David reached for his phone, and with no concession to present reality, pressed the impossible-to-execute button on his favorites list. A photograph of his father materialized on the screen as the call routed to the now obscene recording of his father’s ghost:

“Hello, this is Arthur Lang. I can’t come to the phone at present. Please leave a message after the tone, and I’ll return your call at the earliest interval.”

He hung up, as though he had reached into the afterlife and felt his hand singed by the attempt.

“God damn it, Dad,” he murmured, his voice carrying the timbre of a child left alone in a storm—cold, abandoned, and furious at being stranded.

He shifted his gaze to the mirror above the mantel, studying a face assembled from borrowed time. More than ever before, he could see his father's eyes tearing through his own. The features—passed down with genetic precision—now clamoring for prominence in the man's eternal absence, an overlay so vivid it felt spectral. He had never permitted himself to fully see how much of his father's face resided in his own. But now, the truth of it was so blindingly obvious as to render his prior denials an act of embarrassing, almost willful ignorance—a deception sustained not despite the evidence, but precisely because acknowledging it meant confronting the inevitability that now stood before him.

David raised his hand and with two fingers touched his own face in the glass. For one flickering moment, he felt he could see himself and his father separated in the mirror before him, could feel the touch on his actual cheek. But then… gone.

His right iris contracted, catching some shift of light, and with it came a sudden photonic epiphany: his best work lived only in the liminal shimmer between tremolos and theorems, forever fleeing the act of observation. To document it was to lose it; to share it was to distort it. Perhaps that was the cruelty of his inheritance—not the burden of unfinished conversation, but the knowledge that some things are not meant for an audience.

By 2:47 a.m. the room had become a triangle of potentials. He stood equidistant from stove, chalkboard wall, and cello case—each a vertex, none resolved.

The refrigerator compressor droned a perfect fourth above the bathroom faucet’s drip. Chalk dust shimmered in the spill from the vertical window, suspended like cosmic background radiation.

Outside the brick, beyond the city’s pall of light, the universe hummed its eternal B-flat—waiting for a listener in tuned enough to decode its musings.
