III. Scherzo: The Purge | Chair Clearing  
Presto distruttivo

The aftertaste of the vision lingered in David's occipital lobe. He expected hallucination, or at least the unreality of an all-nighter. Instead he got clarity, as if everything not tied to integer ratios had been erased.

Everything except his office.

He stood in the doorway and saw it all at once: the steel desk with its faux-industrial pretensions, the mismatched chairs, the unvacuumed rug, the sagging bookshelf, binders fat with equations he no longer believed. None of it could survive what he had become.

He purged with annihilating focus. One chair shrieked as he hurled it into the hallway.

Then the second, which had concealed a fossilized sandwich beneath its cushion.

When the rug resisted—glued by decades of neglect—he ripped it up, releasing a cloud of dust into the morning light.

Papers followed. Books, cables, a structurally impossible lamp, loose chalk, paperweights, a sea lion-shaped eraser. His intellectual history became a corridor landfill.

Inside, however: clean surfaces. Blank board. Emptiness. Order.

Almost.

One chair remained. One he could stomach.

It needed a counterpart.

He grabbed it by the backrest and stormed into the hallway, the chair dragging behind him in a continuous shriek of metal on tile. His feet were bare. His pace was manic. Each step struck the floor with the damp, flat percussion of a man whose relationship with standard procedure had collapsed.

Room 317.

He knew exactly where its twin resided. He had lifted the first chair from Professor Garrin's classroom nine months earlier—just the one. At the time it had felt like parity. Gentleman's larceny. Academic détente. But the stakes had shifted. Important work waited. The chair was essential infrastructure. Institutional decorum could metabolize its own obsolescence.

David opened the rear door mid-lecture.

The class turned as one organism—thirty faces executing synchronized acknowledgment. At the board stood Professor Garrin, marker suspended mid-diagram, caught in the collapse between thought and notation.

David stepped inside. Scanned the back wall. Located the chair.

He moved forward without acknowledgment, the first chair behind him carving a trajectory between desks like agricultural equipment through fragile soil. Students leaned back, less from fear than from the pressure of witnessing such a violation in real time.

“Professor Lang,” Garrin's voice emerged, then thinned. “Can I… help you with something?”

No response.

David bent, lifted the second chair, tested its weight, then turned for the door. On his way out he delivered a single nod—gratitude and dismissal in equal measure.

He walked back into the hall, both chairs trailing behind him in overlapping frequencies of protest.

The door settled into its frame with a soft click.

Silence.

Then the room detonated into whispers. Heads pivoted. Notebooks tilted backward for urgent conference. Someone produced a phone but did not raise it, holding it instead like an object whose use might make the event irreversible.

At the front, Professor Garrin remained motionless, his gaze fixed on the rear door as if staring might reverse what had occurred. Students watched his face with fascinated attention, his processing visible in stages.

Finally he reached toward the wall-mounted phone. Realizing he was nowhere near it, he began moving sideways while his face remained locked forward. His outstretched hand served as both navigation and brake. It found the handset. He stopped. Lifted the receiver. Pressed the Dean's direct line by procedural memory alone.

Then he stepped into the hallway in time to see David disappearing into the corner office suite at the far end, two chairs dragging behind him like protesting animals.

The call rang once. Twice. Then: “Good afternoon, Dean's office. How may I connect you?”

David burst back into his office like a man returning from a war nobody else had heard of. The chairs clanged through the doorframe in overlapping protest. He set them down with ferocious precision at forty-five-degree angles across from his desk, as though their placement satisfied an equation he had not written.

He dropped his bag into the nearer chair. Chalk dust rose in delayed response.

Then he set about reconstructing the previous night's work.

He extracted a fresh stick of chalk with a sharp crack and hesitated at the board. Begin with recapitulation, or pursue the new interval still resonating in his bloodstream?

His tinnitus, usually a polite hiss at the periphery, now buzzed with nervous anticipation.

The office registered the change immediately.

Sound traveled farther than the dimensions allowed. Light struck the board and cast no shadows where shadows should have gathered.

David took the second chair and sat.

The quiet settled, revealing its internal architecture. Beats and intervals resisted conventional alignment. The environment had been simplified, not resolved. The structure beneath remained active, newly exposed, like bone through cleared tissue.

Somewhere in the building, metal contracted. A pipe adjusted pressure without stimulus. The larger pattern held itself in suspension.

A knock came at the door.

Perfectly timed, as if whoever stood outside had been waiting for this specific interval—the exact moment his hand touched chalk without yet putting it to the board.

The knock came again.

Lang turned too quickly and nearly dropped the sliver of paper.

Dr. Ishikawa stood in the entrance, purveyor of polite antagonisms. His hair was, as always, slicked to an impossible smoothness; his suit jacket carried the snow of his own vanities. He leaned in, eyes flicking first to the chalkboard, then to the expression David had failed to smooth from his face.

"I like what you've done with the place, David. Especially the outer hallway."

David pocketed the paper. "It's the only way to get things done, Rune. You know how it is."

Ishikawa entered, hands clasped behind his back. He studied the board, then the desk, then David himself. "You look like hell," he said, not unkindly.

"Oh well, you should have seen the other guy."

Rune looked briefly surprised to find David still capable of humor. "Honestly, though. Have you slept?"

"Not really," said Lang, and for once it was not a lie.

Ishikawa gestured at the equation. "Interesting sequence you have there. Didn't expect to see this transition." He pointed to the line—Lang's line, though not in his handwriting—where the frequency ratios bled seamlessly into quantum numbers. "You might be onto something." His eye moved farther down. "Yes. And this here. Fascinating. Elegant… Wait a minute. What have you done with David Lang?"

The two men looked at one another with matching, unwilling grins.

"If you can get this to publish," Rune said, nudging the desk with his hip, "you might actually be cited by someone outside your usual fan club."

Lang felt a brief warmth rise in his chest before the next wave of self-doubt buried it. "Thanks," he said, and meant it as much as he ever meant anything.

Rune turned toward the door, then stopped with exaggerated realization. "I almost forgot."

He slid the folded front page of the Ithaca Times onto the corner of the desk. With a marker from Lang's pen cup, he circled a line in the top story, pressing thick black ink into the paper.

"November already. Shocking. Truly. You know Carl was born in November," Rune said lightly. "Strange, the things we remember. Past numbers playing with future ones. All of them poised to inspire—or ruin. Right, Lang?"

"What's that, Rune? Oh, yes. Sagan. Never had the chance to meet him, I'm afraid. Saw him speak once when I was in high school, I think."

"Marvelous man. Masterful scientist." Rune paused. "Don't know what made me think of that. Must be the winds of November or some other such thing."

"Winds… yes," David said.

Ishikawa grinned. David returned a thin smile.

"Well, I'll leave you to it," Rune said, stepping back toward the hall. "Good work, David. Good." He paused once more. "And try to get some rest. You're beginning to look positively eighteenth-century."

Lang watched him go.

Then he turned back to the desk.

Rune's black circle cut through the column like a target. The text inside it seemed to lift from the newsprint.

AS EXTREME SOLAR EVENT APPROACHES EARTH, NASA SCIENTISTS PINPOINT DATE: NOVEMBER 28, 2026

David thought back to the first time he had met Rune Ishikawa. Years of working with him, despising him, needing him, fearing him, admiring him. Two decades of knowing the angle of his nudges and the softness with which he delivered them.

An x-class solar event was only days away. The resonance data alone could fuel a decade of work.

Then the boards began to call to him again.

Gypsum and slate retook the field of his focus. The equation remained. So did the sliver of foolscap in his pocket. So did the sense that the air in the office had been charged overnight.

He picked up the chalk and began to write.

The proof came easily, as if he were recalling it rather than producing it. That was the unfamiliar sensation. Not invention. Retrieval.

His hand did not shake. The ratios resolved. The tinnitus receded into a calm, even tone.

He looked at the two matching chairs and had the brief, humiliating thought that he owed Garrin both fruit and apology. Embarrassing did not begin to cover it.

On the walk home that evening, he attempted something like a personal pep talk. At least he could still recognize the fault line when it gave way, even if he had seen it ten minutes too late. He had not, in any conventional sense, lost his mind.

What had become clear to him instead was harder to admit: the strictures governing polite days and polite men had very little use for clarity once it began making demands.

The visions had shown him something true. That no one else could see it, or should have to, no longer seemed to matter.

An owl screeched from somewhere across the East Hill dark. David stopped for a moment in the cold and looked out over the ravine.

He had seen, in the architecture of numbers and notes, the armature of the world. And David found it… beautifully terrifying.
