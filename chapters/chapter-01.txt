I


THE IVY LEAGUE

[Andante sostenuto—Presto agitato]

It was, perhaps, an unfortunate hour to be speaking of coherence.

David Lang had not slept. Three medicinally inadvisable energy drinks and four hundred miles of Massachusetts interstate had pushed his nervous system into tomorrow, while the rest of him remained behind to object. He had compensated with a sensible breakfast: mini donuts and coffee. He had organized himself precisely. He had achieved ten minutes early.

Then he remembered the apology gift. For what, he rarely knew, only that it would soon be required. A book for Celeste, something on tuning methods in eighteenth-century Europe, now lay in his still-packed suitcase with all the other gestures too delicate to survive travel.

For one professionally indefensible moment, David considered remembering it to be enough.

Then standards returned. He climbed the spiral staircase to the bedroom loft. The suitcase sat where he had left it, unattended from the moment he had returned.

He pulled the book from beneath a heap of dirty dress socks. A glance at the opening pages began the fatal arithmetic: still early enough to close his eyes for one minute. The rest of him agreed immediately, and David fell into an unintended morning nap.

When he woke fifteen minutes later, he found all plans for the morning already failing at the test vector. For several seconds he remained beside the bed, listening to the apartment arrange itself around the fact. The coffee had gone cold. The clock had advanced with indecent calm.

Fifteen minutes.

Which meant, allowing for the hill, the crossing, and the pedestrian uselessness of human legs, that he was already eight minutes and forty-seven seconds past his time.

David leapt up, scrambling across the grated bridge and out onto the balcony. As he looked down over the burgeoning morning, the short distance between him and the southern entrance of Cornell's Ithaca campus spread out before him. He could just make out what looked like the familiar patterns of the gang already gathered below, already drawing themselves into their usual migratory clot, already moving toward the first rise without him.

David Lang was never late. Never. That fact had always been sufficient to guarantee the rest of him. But today was clearly different from yesterday. The fear of surrendering forever to this new reality, one of substandard timing and forgotten gestures, caused him to take more than the ordinary measures in hand. He took the stairs two at a time, even leapt from the third-from-last step down to the oak floors below, and neglected to grab the essential armor of his sunglasses on the way out the door.

The moment he hit the street, something was wrong with his feet. They advanced in small, unauthorized surges, quickening him into brief, ugly bursts that broke the measured dignity of his usual walk to campus. It was almost running, though running at least would have possessed the decency of confession. This was haste pretending to be judgment.

He crossed the narrow streets south of campus with his coat tugging at his shoulders and his breath clipping in the cold. At the corner, a woman appeared with a yippy little nut of a dog. David slowed at once, arranging his face into reflective morning purpose. He pretended to be interested in the animal so as to avoid direct engagement with the owner attached to it. They passed. David counted three steps for dignity, then let his feet resume their treason.

Cold had sharpened the morning. The town's final porches sat slick and accusatory as Ithaca's last residential blocks began to acquiesce to the grand gates of the Ivy League.

By the time Cornell’s lower East Hill rose before him, David had restored his pace to something that could still, under generous interpretation, be called walking.

The gaggle was already assembled below the first rise. Faculty, staff, graduate students: the usual cohort moving up the path. And there, near the center, Sweeney was talking. Of course he was.

Four days of David’s absence had left a vacuum, and Sweeney, like all soft men with tenure, had expanded to fill it.

David felt something in him sharpen. Territory. A morning ritual improperly occupied. An imbalance awaiting correction.

He looked down and found sabotage in full bloom: both laces dangling across his shoes. The initial looping and knotting had not survived the scramble toward the first rise. Begrudgingly, he knelt to repair the debacle, one eye on the task at foot and one on the task ahead.

Finally done, he sprang upright and broke into the short, undignified spurts of speed required to catch them.

He cut left through the trees that flanked the south side, taking the slope at an angle. Dead leaves broke underfoot. He kept low without meaning to, ridiculous and predatory, pacing them from cover until he drew parallel.

Sweeney was mid-anecdote. Useless as ever, charming as dirt.

"—I'm telling you, he said cross-disciplinary intimacy to my face," Sweeney was saying. "In a budget meeting. To my face. Can you imagine?"

David stepped out from the trees and into the migration.

"My God, Sweeney, intimacy and face in the same sentence? And in mixed company? You must have some good lawyers on retainer."

Sweeney turned, barked a laugh, then almost ruined it by becoming kind.

"Lang. Welcome back."

"Don't."

"Don't what?"

"Lower the room temperature with your humanity. It's undignified."

Naomi Feld, walking just behind Sweeney, hid a smile behind her coffee. Bardon from History gave David a cautious nod, the kind people gave at funerals and department votes. Two graduate students drifted in their wake.

Sweeney recovered first. "You look almost refreshed."

"Yes," David said. "It's true what they say. Burying a parent can be remarkably restorative. Takes ten years off the face, and five off your life."

Silence. He let it hold just long enough to punish them.

"Oh, come on. I'm back. No more kid gloves. Surely that was better than cross-disciplinary intimacy."

Naomi lost the fight first. Sweeney followed, slower, appalled at himself. The graduate students smiled dutifully at the pavement.

"And for the record," David said, "there are only two ways disciplinary intimacy lands. Title IX, or tenure."

That broke them properly.

Bardon leaned forward. "Speaking from experience, are we, Professor Lang?"

David glanced at him. "I don't know, Bardon. Shouldn't you wait to see which side wins, then dodge conclusions for the rest of your career and let the next man make the call? History: always two steps behind."

"Good to have you back, Lang," Sweeney said, roughening the line so it would not have to count as sincerity.

The group adjusted around him. Half the gossip had been about him. David caught the little nods, the softened faces, the quick professional rearrangements of sympathy into banter. He ignored all of it. He had returned, and the world would resume its proper cruelties.

They reached the lower crossing, where the older paving stones gave way to the darker run laid nearer the quad.

David stopped. His body simply refused the next step.

The effect was immediate. Behind him, the line collapsed into itself: shoes scuffing, brief apologies, Sweeney's hand coming up to steady Naomi's coffee before it betrayed everyone.

"Lang?" Sweeney said.

At first the sensation was almost ordinary. Indigestion, perhaps. A pressure under the sternum. A sour inward lurch.

Then the sound arrived. Grating under pressure, pitched low enough to feel structural, as if one of the buried utilities beneath campus had mistaken his rib cage for pipe. It moved through his chest before it reached his ears. Deep, menacing, unforgiving.

David took one hard breath, or tried to. The air would not quite enter. For one impossible second, he assumed they had all heard it.

Then his stomach turned with the sickening sensation of a body briefly released from time. For a flashing instant he swore the skin of his face had drawn tight; then, just as quickly, it sank the other way, into some greyed and brittle age not yet his.

Then the present snapped shut around him.

"You heard that," David said.

Sweeney glanced around. "Heard what?"

"That tone just now."

Naomi looked from one to the other. "Oh, we heard your tone, Professor."

A small nervous laugh ran through the group and died young.

David took a half step back. The pressure eased. His gaze dropped to the seam in the paving. The darker stones began there, running uphill toward the quad as though the campus had drawn a line through itself and left it waiting.

Sweeney circled back, now genuinely concerned and therefore harder to mock.

"Hey," he said, lowering his voice. "Really. Glad you're back, David. He was a great man, your dad. Be proud of a big life, yeah?"

"Yeah, thanks, Hank. He was. Appreciate it."

Sweeney studied him. "You okay? You're sweating like it's eighty degrees out here." A pause. "More like eight, yeah?"

Naomi took one step closer.

"I'm fine," David said. "Long few days is all."

"Of course. Take it easy. Don't let those quantum undergrads take you down."

"Please. They'll need a quorum first."

Sweeney allowed himself to be absorbed back into the advancing clot.

Only David and Naomi remained.

"You're not fine," she said.

"No. But I am late."

She followed his gaze to the stones. "What happened?"

"I don't know."

That was too honest. He regretted it immediately.

"Do you want me to call someone?"

"No."

"Walk in with you, then."

He adjusted the satchel. His hands were shaking, which offended him more than the nausea.

"I think I'd rather preserve the illusion of dignity, if nothing else."

"David."

He stood there breathing once, then again, and forced the air to settle him back into a shape the world would accept.

"I know," he said. Then, more quietly: "I know."

Naomi nodded toward campus. "I'll be nearby."

"How ominous."

"Good."

She moved off toward the hall.

David waited until she had gone fully around the corner and out of sight. Then, affording the seam a wide berth, he slowly continued toward the main entrance to Feynman Hall.

At the door he paused with one hand on the handle. Under his watch, he could feel the skin of his wrist begin to stretch and pulse. A glance down: eight minutes and forty-seven seconds past his time.

He drew one breath. Then another. Stand up straight. Smile on. Pull.



⁂

Deep in Sub-level Two, in a concrete corridor omitted from every floor plan since the Nixon administration, a copper bypass valve surrendered at last to the pressure of its own history. It did exactly what any dying thing must: it exhaled.

A thin grey trickle of 1971-era oxygen leaked into the ventilation shaft—air trapped for fifty-five years, stagnant and mildewed, carrying the ghost of Kenji Tsukino's last cigarette. The same atmosphere that had been present for Sequence 47 now threaded through the ducts and conduits beneath East Hill, an air that had once vibrated at a frequency the universe had been reluctant to reveal.

The vapor rose, a silent passenger in the building's circulatory system, climbing the dark verticality of the service shafts past the biology labs, past the bursar's recycled breath, until it found the primary intake for Feynman Hall.

David Lang was thirty feet from the classroom door when the first tendril of it reached him.

The hallway cooled around his ankles. Behind him, sunlight lay in white bands across the striated glass, but the chill rose from below, precise as a hand. Something brushed his cheek: dry, light, intimate. He wiped at it. Nothing. Only sweat, chalk dust, and the sudden scent of radiator steam; a smell that began to dissolve as he neared the hall into something that felt less like odor than memory: a past-tense being catalogued before he had agreed to name its source.

He stood there clutching his lecture notes too tightly, listening to nothing at all. And then one thing became brilliantly clear: he was being watched, actively seen from a very short and punishing distance.

Behind the doors, thirteen students waited to be disappointed. The outcome was all but preordained, but David, as always, entered with the intention to win them over entirely. All he required was a provisional level of trust, temporary as it might be, sufficient to allow for a moment or two of genuine, suspended belief.


⁂

"Good morning."

The lecture hall was tall. Very tall. One part classroom, two parts bakehouse, its narrow window bank running floor to ceiling along the rear wall, overlooking the glacier-carved canyons of East Hill. The panes sliced the daylight into fractured shards, casting them across the hall with a suspicion of intent.

A brushed-metal plaque by the door marked his assignment to the post. The inscription praised the room's "unique ornamentation," claiming it had been designed to "soothe the psyche and elicit heightened states of mental acuity."

They said the same things about heroin for a hundred years.

Sixty-three names on the roster. Twenty-seven bodies in the seats. Of those, perhaps thirteen were present in any sense that mattered.

"My," he said. "A full house, I see."

He set his satchel on the desk and glanced down at the roster with a small show of uncertainty.

"Let me make sure you're all in the right place. This is…" He traced a line with one finger and, with a false search, found, "Introduction to Quantum Dynamics. Excellent. Always reassuring when the room and the paperwork agree."

"And I am—" He checked once more, letting the front row in on the insincerity. "David Lang. The Feynman Chair of Quantum Dynamics. A grand title, I know. And aren't you lucky? My speciality and the class focus are uncommonly aligned."

"If this is not your intended destination, or if my tenured and faintly immortalized presence strikes you as too intimidating, then by all means go to lunch." He tipped the chalk toward the door. "Otherwise, remain with us and starve on science and superposition."

The room offered a mild pulse of amusement. David felt the old register find him. Dry, amused, even occasionally lethal. The ease of it irritated him. Even his gifts had begun lying on his behalf.

"Let's jump right back in today, yes? Try this again. Causality: the old narcotic."

He turned to the board and wrote:

|Ψ⟩ = Σ cᵢ |ϕᵢ⟩

"This," he said, tapping the Ψ, "is the whole wave function. Everything that can be, gathered into one expression. A probability cloud, if you like metaphors." A pause. "I like metaphors. They're all we have."

"Here's the part that matters," he said, and something in his voice shifted. Less performance. More confession. "Before you get involved, endless possibilities. After you engage, no choices left. That's the grace. And the trap."

He let it land.

The room loosened. A small laugh, genuine enough. He could still do this. The muscle memory was there, buried under years of bureaucratic sediment and the slow erosion of giving a damn.

He stepped to the desk for a fresh stick of chalk. When his fingers closed around the box, pain shot through them. Sudden. Familiar. He did not wince. Wincing was for people who had not made peace with the body's slow betrayal.

Dust hung in the slant of light, each mote briefly suspended in its own small uncertainty.

"Now," he said, turning back to the board. "Look here."

He wrote beneath the first expression:

K(b,a) = Σ exp(iS/ℏ)

"This is the sum over histories. Every possible way of getting from the beginning of a thing to the end of it. The paths contribute amplitudes. Some reinforce. Some cancel. The ones we call reality are, more or less, what survives the argument."

His chalk hovered a fraction too long at the end of the line.

Then, without deciding to, he added:

+ η(t)

David stared at it. He had written noise into the equation. The room waited.

He heard himself say, "And if the system admits noise—" but the rest of the thought melted into nothing as the buzz came roaring, grinding back. And now, for the first time, he knew its number.

68.48 Hz.

The figure arrived in his mind with impossible authority. Fully formed. Calibrated elsewhere and inserted behind his eyes.

68.48 Hz.

A frequency beneath speech. Beneath thought. Too low to belong to the room and too precise to be imagined.

It scraped through him, deep and remembered. It passed under the sternum, found the hinge of his jaw, touched the small bones inside his ear with the cold familiarity of a key entering a lock.

His hand hovered over the board. The chalk trembled between his fingers. A white fleck broke loose and landed on the cuff of his sleeve.

He forced his hand down.

"All right," he said, and his voice came out steadier than he deserved. "Let's shift the frame."

He turned back to the board. The hum burrowed deeper, scratching at the base of his skull. He needed something solid.

He wrote in block letters:

HOW DO YOU MEASURE PROXIMITY?

Then underneath:

d = √(Δx² + Δy²)

He stepped back. The hum pressed against his eardrums. The windows were doing strange things to the light.

"When the world resonates," he said, his voice gone softer now, stranger, "you feel less alone. Like there's structure underneath the noise. Drift just a little…” He snapped his fingers. "And that structure collapses."

He scanned the room.

"There is a fundamental frequency beneath everything. Beneath language. Beneath light."

Then something tapped on the shoulder of his mind.

He opened his eyes to find Dr. Rune Ishikawa standing, no placed, in the doorway. The chair of the department. The Guardian of East Hill. Arranged like a symbol.

David had no idea how long Rune had been standing there.

He drew breath for an introduction, but Rune stepped in first and took it from him.

"Are you not, in fact, describing musical harmony, Professor Lang?"

David froze. Something inside him pulled taut—a string tuned too high for too long.

"No," he said. "No, I'm not, Rune. Christ. What an idiotic question. Harmony is passive. It's a fucking consequence. Resonance is physical. It builds."

The room went still.

"Oh, I see." Rune smiled a thin, elegant blade of a smile. "Vulgarity notwithstanding, you're saying harmony is a result of the intrinsic resonant forces underpinning the intervals?"

"Well, yes. I mean—"

"But resonant structures can alter without corrupting the base harmonics, isn't that right?"

"That is a conveniently—"

"You know," Rune continued, turning to address the students as if David had ceased to exist, "it's been a long time since my marching band days. I once fancied myself a bassoonist. Fabulous instrument. Terrible embouchure, I'm afraid."

"Dr. Ishikawa, was there something you needed?”

“Oh, it’s a trifle David, and correct me if I'm wrong, but a minor third atop a major third creates a major triadic harmony, yes?”

“Well, yes.” David had to concede that.

“Yet that same harmonic structure, when inverted, can be created by a major third and—oh, help me David, won't you? It's a…"

"A perfect fourth," David said through his teeth.

"Of course." Rune snapped his gloved fingers. "A perfect fourth." He placed both hands in the front pockets of his suit jacket and offered the room a slight, elegant shrug. "I must be missing something. Or perhaps the inquiry is, as you say, idiotic."

David was seething. A cornered animal in a room full of witnesses.

"Harmony happens when everything is already aligned," he said, voice low and dangerous. "Resonance creates that alignment."

He turned toward the doorway. A few students turned with him, but most stared at David, as there was nothing else to look at.

The doorway was empty. Rune had walked away mid-battle. A preemptive victory.

David caught his breath. Let the fury settle like ash. Then he turned back to the board and wrote:

RESONANCE = SURVIVAL

This time, he let it stay.

Behind him, the soft shuffle of leather-soled shoes retreated down the hallway—unhurried, administrative, the sound of a man who had already won.

He snapped on a false grin and gestured toward the empty doorway.

"Dr. Ishikawa, ladies and gentlemen. Always an honor to have his unique perspective." The smile died before it reached his eyes. "What a legend."

He lunged toward the projector and flipped the power. The machine whirred to life. The first slide flickered on-screen.

"Now. This is a section from the Lang-Feldman Diagram. It maps the overlap between psychoacoustics and neural patterning in recursive feedback loops. Or at least it did, before someone rewrote the grant proposal as a sound art initiative."

He clicked forward. A waveform graph.

Click. An anatomical rendering of the cochlea, the inner ear.

Click. Wait —

An unexpected photograph cut through the light.

A Polaroid.

Within its thick white frame: evening fog, a field of winter-green grass. At the center, a single tuning fork, half-buried in wet soil. The resonance prong pointed to the sky.

David's blood stopped moving.

He knew this image. He had been there when it was taken. Celeste. The tuning fork. The year of the agreement. The capsule.

His mind braced as the ancient moment unfurled, unbidden and unstoppable. The smell of dug-up earth. The weight of shared knowledge pressing down on the shovel. The click of the Polaroid. Celeste's voice, cold and clear in the mist:

If it's real, it'll claw its way out. It will come back to us.

She had meant it as encouragement. It returned to him now as pure threat.

The hum burrowed deeper into his chest. His lungs faltered.

Then, suddenly, the sound pulled back. The pressure eased. Slowly, his voice returned.

"My apologies," he said. "Wrong… wrong file."

He shut off the projector. The image was gone. The memory was not.

"Professor?"

The voice rose from the front row. It did not register.

"Professor Lang?"

The class sat poised on the edge of reaction. Teaching and breaking had become, for the moment, difficult to distinguish.

A biology major auditing the course found she had less fear or reverence for the subject than the others. She got up, walked to the aisle, and marched straight down to meet Lang's frozen face. With a bravery the remaining students found grotesquely admirable, she pressed her cheek against the blackboard, mirroring his sideways stare, and shouted:

"Will… this… be… on… the… midterm… Professor?"

David's eyelid twitched.

His arm fell to his side. Without moving the rest of his body, he locked his eyes with hers, cracked an absurdly exaggerated grin, and replied in a ghoulish voice:

"All concepts presented are… potentially examinable."

The tension shattered. Students howled in laughter and relief.

"I had you all so good just now."

More laughter. He let it wash over him, already calculating how many minutes remained.

"I think I've put you through enough for one day. Let's end there, shall we?" Then, louder, as the rustle of the end of things filled the air: "You'll read Kozlowski's Sympathetic Structures for next time. Chapters two through six. Pay attention to the footnotes. The math is wrong, but the metaphors are sound."

Students packed their notes and slipped out one by one.


The TA was the last to leave. She handed him her doodle-covered lecture notes. They were a veritable travel log of geometric non-sequiturs, concluding with a thinly scribbled block of text:

Clever save, David. Everything alright?

Above it, something only partially scribbled out:

WHAT THE FUCK?

David blinked, then swallowed, finally remembering to inhale. She had earned her stipend.

As the door closed, his gaze drifted toward the back of the hall. Through the windows, the quad stretched out in cold silence. He could hear it now. Flowing under the benches. Creeping up the tree trunks. Moving through the falls and ice beyond.

David dashed back to the desk, grabbed a fresh eraser, and began obliterating the afternoon's wreckage. Chalk dust snowed over his oxfords. In the instinct to erase what he believed but could not defend lay everything: not just the salvage of a lecture, but the life he had once thought his birthright, now buried with the theories he had abandoned twenty years ago.

The clean boards bore witness to an institutional truth: if it wasn't declared in chalk, it hadn't happened.

Yet David stopped short of total erasure.

RESONANCE = SURVIVAL

He stepped back, staring at the declaration. The backs of his knees struck the front bench, and his body yielded at once. Within moments, he had slipped into the late-afternoon sleep of the overworked and the defeated.


Hours later he awoke slumped in the front row, neck cramped against the bench. A shadow stood in the doorway.

"Do my eyes deceive, or is that Professor David Lang, still here on a Thursday… and at mopping hour?"

"Your eyes are true, Ralph. Evening."

"I believe I predicted this one, Professor."

Ralph stepped gently into the room, dragging his cart. David held out a twenty, pinched between two fingers.

"Got me pegged. This belongs to you."

The old man waved it off, then rested a worn, calloused hand on David's shoulder.

"Some of us sleep for free. Folks like you gotta pay for it."

Ralph's attention shifted to the single equation on the slate.

"Now that's something I've not heard before, Professor."

"How does it strike you?"

"True, I think." Ralph squinted. "But you're the professor. I just clean up at the end of the night."

"Come on, Ralph. Out with it."

"Well, as I understand it, resonance builds up. Makes things more than what they are. Makes 'em powerful. Dangerous." He shrugged. "So I guess if I'm resonance, then yeah—I equal survival. What do you think?"

David stared at the old man for a long moment.

"Honestly, I've no earthly idea anymore, my friend. Except that maybe you should be the professor."

Ralph wandered back to his cart. As he turned, David slipped the twenty into his shirt pocket.

"Goodnight, Professor Lang. Watch those side roads tonight. Slick as thieves."

As he collected his things, the hall exhaled the last of the day's light. He walked into the corridor.

Yet in the space between the lecture hall and the building's double doors, something shifted.

The Polaroid. Where the hell did it come from?

His pace quickened. The files were locked. Password-protected. There was no pathway for that slide to end up in the deck. None.

And Rune. Forty-eight hours' advance notice. University policy. The man had waited. Planned. Stood in the doorway like he was collecting evidence.

The anger clarified with each step. By the time David hit the building's double doors and burst through into the quad, he was boiling.

Snow attacked him immediately. Fat flakes drove sideways in the wind, but could not land. The moment they touched his coat, his face, his hair, they vaporized. Steam rose from his shoulders. The heat radiating off him turned the falling snow into a halo of negative space—flakes approaching and then repelling as though he were magnetized wrong.

He barely noticed. His mind was angling toward the office and the files. And that noise—the frequency—how the hell could he know it, name it so precisely? 68.48 Hz. It wasn't even a dead-on pitch. Almost C#2, sure. But not exactly. Almost half the fine-structure —

Jesus, Lang. Don't do that.

Then, as if naming the thing aloud was enough to reawaken the beast, the buzz returned.

It came roaring, detonating in his inner ear like a depth charge, so sudden and violent that he staggered mid-step, one shoe skidding on ice.

"No." He said it loud, though he had not meant to. "Get out. I'm done with you."

The sound grew. Doubled. With each breath, the vibration sank deeper into his lungs. His vision blurred at the edges. Through it he could feel the hum pulling—directional now, coming from ahead. Somewhere in the quad.

David's rage shifted. Turned predatory.

"Fine," he said into the frigid night. "You want me to find you? I'll fucking find you."

He pushed out toward it, following the resonance like a blood trail. The hum intensified with each step. Denser. His molars ached. His sternum vibrated. The frequency climbed into his sinuses, his eye sockets, the hollow spaces behind his ears.

Ten feet. Twenty. Thirty into the quad.

Until—his shin struck iron.

The bench materialized out of fury and snow. Green paint weathered to gray-green, armrests pocked with rust, east-facing, twenty-eight feet off the main path.

The hum was coming from beneath. Below. Six feet down. Where the capsule had been sealed, held in the deep by dirt and clay and the collective certainty that some knowledge should remain unknown.

David stood there, chest heaving, snow evaporating off his coat, staring down at the iron armrest, the weathered slats, the frozen ground beneath.

Twenty years in the earth. And it was still vibrating.

She'd said it would come back to them. He'd wanted to believe her then. He needed to believe her, but the wanting had curdled into forgetting, and the forgetting had hardened into routine; a routine that had carried him past this bench four thousand times without incident.

Until today.

Now the hum was pushing at him. Compressing its wave deep into his skull. David slammed his palms to his ears, the pressure building unchecked within him.

He would not allow it. It would not take him. Not after all this time.

And then it happened. A sound tore out of him so completely it stripped the world clean.

The windows shuddered. The stone walls answered. Even the air flinched.

And immediately, the awful, relentless buzz slid quickly down and out of his bones. The world went still. Silence blanketed the quad. Then a whisper of wind, a cackling crow, a hint of distant laughter. He stood there in the snow, breath catching, throat scraped bare, not knowing what part of him had remembered how to make a sound like that.

When he felt steady enough to dare it, David turned from the site, rejecting its call, its pull on him. He forced his feet to turn. Forced himself forward into the snow.

But behind him, beneath the bench and frozen earth, it remained. Waiting.

Celeste had been right. It had come back. And time would wait no longer.



























II


THE Office

[Andante sostenuto—Presto agitato]

David’s office sat two buildings and a partial crossing of the arts quad from the main cluster of physics buildings, tucked behind a row of former faculty houses now repurposed as boutique galleries for Cornell history and regional heritage. He could reach it through the unending mid-century administrative hallways, but long ago, in all weather, he had made a habit of taking the scenic route instead, if only to avoid the corridor haunted by the nearly emeritus tenure set and the half-muttered conversational ordeals they imposed.

His suite of offices felt lost to time. It was nothing of his doing or design; he had simply allowed what was there before him to remain. Late afternoon light seeped through warped Venetian blinds, striping stacked Annalen der Physik into a barcode of abandoned inquiries. A cello case leaned against the southern flank of the bookcase, its leather scuffed in the precise pattern of subway-platform collisions from a commute to lessons he now scheduled only in daydreams.

David burst through the door. His coat sailed toward a hook, bag thudding to the floor, papers cascading in a controlled avalanche across the desk. He didn’t even notice Rune Ishikawa, already seated in the corner, attentively stirring a fresh cup of Earl Grey. He was almost indistinguishable from the furniture.

Before David could orient himself, Rune’s voice floated out into the room—unhurried, already mid-thought—as if none of the preceding spectacle had occurred.

“You know, David,” he took another delicate sip from his cup and with a faint upturn of the right side of his lips, continued. “There simply doesn’t seem to be milk anywhere. I searched and searched, even dared to cross over into the chemistry department. Dreadful world, terrible mistake. Still… nothing.

“Rune. Come on in.”

“Yes, thank you. So here I am, sitting in your office, drinking an entirely naked cup of Earl Grey. Shameful.”

David dropped into his chair with enough force to test its stress limits. The jolt wiped whatever he’d carried in from the quad clean off his face.

“Rune, about before, I—”

“So David,” Rune continued, unfazed by David’s attempt to speak, “they say Princeton University Press is interested in a revised monograph from you. An expansion on your wonderful decoherence paper.” As Rune continued, his fingers grazed the gilt edge of a volume from 1979’s Encyclopedia Britannica, a set David had inherited from a predecessor who’d abruptly retired to study Tibetan throat singing.

David blinked. “Yes… I’d heard something to that effect.” He struggled to color his tone with an appropriate layer of professional patina. His mind, still half-lit with residual vibrations and snow light, narrowed on Rune’s phrasing. “Sorry Rune, but who’s they exactly?”

“My dear boy, you forget, we share an agent… and a lawyer, for that matter. I had a call from Charles this morning. On other matters, of course.”

“Charles! Right. Of course.” David nodded too many times, buying seconds. “Don’t know where my head is. And he said?”

“He asked after you,” said Rune. “Mentioned you’d been rather difficult to reach of late. Said Princeton Press called him just the other day.”

“I see.” David’s hands began their familiar choreography across the paper-cluttered surface, pulling drawers like organ stops. “Come to think… I did see something from them… sure it’s here somewhere.” The ballet of search and delay. “Ah, here. Princeton U. Press. There we are.”

“Excellent.”

A pause hung between them. Not quite awkward. Not quite comfortable.

“Yes, well… wait and see, right?” David offered.

“Indeed, and… if so.” Rune lifted his hand in a gesture that somehow encapsulated here’s-hoping, pre-congratulations, and a threat disguised as collegial optimism, all at once. It made David’s stomach turn and his toes dig into the squeaking laminate floor.

“Yes. Thanks. Thank you, sir.”

The surface nicety was about to spin his mind into primordial goo. David searched for immediate deflective focus material… found.

The espresso had grown a skin reminiscent of volcanic lakes. A leathery membrane quivering whenever a delivery truck passed the building. He found further distraction in the parallelogram of pale sunlight bisecting his left wrist, where dust motes performed Brownian dances above a stack of Slovakian musicology journals from 1987.

As the spin subsided, David was mildly surprised to find Dr. Ishikawa still in the room. Worse, the man had made himself at home with a decades-old issue of Scientific Educators Quarterly, a publication so barren that forty-seven of its hundred and seventy-one pages had been surrendered to advertisements without any obvious loss to human knowledge. Yet there he stood, absorbed in page sixty-three, then sixty-four, and now sixty-five, as though revelation might yet crawl from the wreckage. David did not remember seeing him rise, cross to the bookshelf, or even take the thing down at all.

Then, with sudden reanimation, Rune made a side-click of his mouth and a corresponding flip-flap closing of the magazine, as if reminded of something he’d left off his check-in-with-Lang list.

“Oh, and I meant to say,” he jolted back in, “that was a remarkably thorough analysis of Pythagorean tuning in Gregorian antiphons last week.”

David exhaled. He only had so many of these polite rejoinders at his fingertips. Taking 1.3 seconds to mentally thumb through his collection, he plucked out, “Oh, did you think so? Well, that’s nice of you to say.”

His eyes followed Ishikawa’s drift toward the precarious ziggurat of unanswered student inquiries and meeting requests stacked beside the monitor.

“Well, I can see you’ve a mountain of paper here to climb. I’ll leave you to it.”

He turned gracefully, judging David’s lack of correspondence management with a move to the door that bordered on indignation. Then, stopping at the threshold, he turned, launching the unsaid into the spoken realm.

“Resonance… equals… survival?”

The air between them compressed towards a sub-zero chill.

“Interesting premise,” Rune allowed, his manicured nails tapping the doorjamb in glissandic 7/8 time. “Resonance as a survival strategy. It has a certain… intellectual elegance. Though one does wonder, David, when you’ll find the breath to move past the curation of the old masters and grace the department with something more… speculative. We do so miss that version of you.”

“Hm? What was that, Rune? Oh… speculative… yes, well…” A weak, ill-conceived semi-chuckle escaped David’s lips, as if to say, I wouldn’t hold your breath.

Dr. Ishikawa mercifully brought the audience to a close. “Well, good night, Professor Lang.”

“Goodnight, Dr. Ishikawa.”

And with the unhurried shuffle of his soft leather shoes, he drifted away.

David rose to see him off but was too late, the gesture mattering not at all. His knuckles brushed the diploma frame as he turned, adjusting its forty-degree tilt to the exact angle Professor Esterházy had set during their last argument about academic legacy. The brass corner left a dent in his proximal phalanx that would throb until evening.


⁂


Was that it? Was that all he had?

David knew the outburst earlier was enough to put him out on his ass. It was a wholly inexcusable breach—no matter the circumstance. Yet the age-old axiom of academic neutrality and avoidance once again played as a superpower.

Rune had always been a nominal presence, barely there until the moment you realized he’d been holding the room together the whole time. It had taken David years to believe the man truly operated on the same neurological and vascular principles as the rest of humanity.

What was he to do now? Should he lock his lectures to keep his boss from discovering that he was losing it? David sat back into his chair with a heavy, unmediated flop, and from the bottom drawer pulled folder LXIII, its tab labeled Theoretical Explorations. And then, below in parentheses, Strike Outs. The lettering appeared written in the geometric hand he’d cultivated during a misguided calligraphy phase. New additions were never inserted without rereading previous rejections. The NSF denial still bore bourbon stains from 2014, the IEEE rebuke citing “excessive poetry in the equations.”

Resigned to relegate yet another nascent rejection, David added page 974. The page was still damp with the ink of a theorem collapsed under the referee’s indifferent gaze. He sighed, took a moment, and shut the drawer.

Surely this was no way to live; of that he was certain. Yet the pattern’s reliability offered a certain comfort. With every rejected treatise or unpublished paper, with every lecture delivered half-baked in the accepted science he knew to be incomplete at best, nonsense at worst, the outcome never changed. The dismissal was predictable. He could rest easier in the knowledge that he had been here before. This unimpressive, even untruthful was at the very least, familiar.

And then there was the rest: requests for advisory meetings, for recommendation letters, the brag sheets of mentees and postdoctoral hopefuls, all of it unanswered and faintly accusatory. Since his father’s death, the weeks had felt like a second conscription into adulthood, brought on less by any single blow than by attrition. The forms kept arriving while his father remained dead. The indecency of that made him flinch.

It was personal. How could it be otherwise? Reality, as ever, had shown no interest in the life David had imagined for himself. And it occurred to him, with the sudden clarity of a cruel joke, that midlife felt a great deal like thirteen: the same vertigo, the same sense of inhabiting a body and a future that refused to explain themselves. And eighty-one, the age his father had been when he died two weeks ago, was disturbingly not a remote continent but a neighboring country: closer to now than to never. Someone ought to do something about that paradox, he thought. Or at least install a better warning system than this blunt annunciation: your father is dead and you are almost forty-seven. Crisis. Midlife. Proceed with extreme caution.

And there, in the far-right corner of the desk, lay the manila envelope from Schaker, Marsheck & Camden, the firm executing his father’s will. David looked at it, looked away, then forced himself to look again, as though staring long enough might let him absorb some portion of its weight. It did not. He could not bring himself to draw the papers from their stiff bureaucratic sleeve, much less sign them: the small, almost laughably ordinary gesture that would make everything final. That single motion would close the long, fraught conversation he and his father had been having since David was nearly fifteen and first understood that the thoughts handed to him need not be the only ones he kept.

They had argued, sometimes mildly, sometimes with real force, about science and music: one obedient to law, the other disciplined only so it could remain human. His father had not wanted to lose him to physics. He had wanted music for David: beauty, honesty, clarity. Music offered such things without embarrassment. Physics, as Arthur had learned from his own father, did not exactly advertise the same rewards in the cheerful pamphlet titled Welcome to Scientific Academia: A Few Things to Expect Before Your Journey Begins.

David had insisted both were possible; that science and music need not remain separate islands. Somewhere in the distant past, he believed, there must have been a land bridge between them; someone merely had to go looking. Now he thought, with a bitter flicker of amusement, that truth was less often buried than eroded. Discovery was never the hardest part. Learning to distinguish fortitude from foolishness was.

Then, with all that and more lingering in his head, he nudged the envelope back into its corner, still unsigned, and turned once more toward the glass, toward the campus trees and the darkening grass.

“I will, Dad,” he muttered. “Goddamn it. I… I will.”


⁂


Beyond the window, snow thickened into sleet, and sleet turned to ice, laying a fresh sheen of treachery over the quad’s footpaths. Yet the ground around the bench, along with the narrow track by which David had stumbled upon it, remained oddly bare. Untouched by the accumulating glaze, it felt as if the moment itself still radiated some after-heat that refused the world’s new terms.

His mind kept circling back: the bench, the fork, the capsule, the data. A recursion he dreaded. A momentum he could not, for the moment, break.

Somewhere beneath the grass, under layers of dense clay and root-striated soil, lay the capsule he, Celeste, and Marcus had buried: their graduate-school manifestos on revolutionizing acoustic physics, sealed up like a promise and a dare. David’s fingernail traced the rim of his coffee cup, finding the enamel chip where he’d dropped it while trying to convince his father that they were doing the right thing. With that constant voice of challenge in his life now silenced, that long ago foggy evening seemed to have found a voice in him once more. A voice he knew in youth, in new discovery, before they shook hands and agreed it was a song sung before its time: a melody no one was ready, or willing, to hear.

He exhaled, shutting all of it from his mind, for ahead waited the relief of rehearsal, the comfort of trusted, old friends, and an even older cello that had always shielded him from harm.

He looked up at the clock, then down at his own, for the day had proven less than reliable already. Confirmed:

It was one minute, forty-seven seconds from time.







III


THE REHEARSAL HALL

[Largo maestoso]

The Music School’s residence on East Hill had never resolved into a single, settled idea. It was ever more of a slow-growing organism that had learned its shape by necessity, by habit, by the pressure of young lives needing rooms in which to fail and begin again.

The original 1907 Elizabethan chapel sat at its center, a masonry heart, cool and enduring, around which the future had gathered and thickened over decades, addition by addition, corridor by corridor, until the whole became less a plan than a history you could walk through.

By the seventies, the place had been reborn, rebranded as “living architecture,” and the phrase, for once, did not feel like a lie. Engineers carved deep into the glacial hillside to find something solid enough to trust, then lifted the newer structures up and out, rising just over sixty-eight feet above the slope, sheltering an arched amphitheater beneath a spanning canopy of old-forest timber that seemed, even now, to remember life before being pressed into the service of sound.

Overhead, a lattice of loft offices and narrow bridges looked down on the rehearsal spaces like catwalks, spare and watchful. Above the musicians hung the acoustic clouds, those pale, suspended planes, stitched with seams that never fully closed, so that sound did not merely bounce and die but wandered upward, slipping through the gaps into rafters, into the chapel attic, where it gathered and lingered: a stray oboe line, a cello’s open string, the soft bruise of timpani, a flute’s thin bright thread—things that should have dissipated but did not, hanging in the dark like prayers that had spoken once and refused now to be unspoken.

Below, fluorescent corridors lined with practice rooms tunneled under the hill, binding the old stone house to the defiant 1974 annex, a towering, unclassifiable mass that fused acoustics to geology with a kind of stubborn confidence. Concrete, here, did not simply contain music; it held it. It caught vibrato in the hidden lattice of rebar and returned it, faintly, even when no one was present to deserve it—so that in the silences between terms, the walls seemed to keep a low, borrowed life, as if the building could not quite bring itself to forget.

It was a place designed to receive, to reverberate, and to remember.

The main rehearsal hall smelled of rosin and old wool, with the faint tang of brass polish and the salted trace of decades of nervous sweat. Acoustic clouds hung from the ceiling like sleeping bats, and the parquet floor—honeyed once, long ago—now cratered by the recurring scrape of chair legs and music stands, a topography of discipline and desire made permanent by repetition, by ambition rehearsed until it left marks you could not stamp out without erasing the story.


⁂


Roger Linn, the 1st Violinist, and Professor of String Performance, was already there, spreading music across the stands when David shouldered through the door, and for a moment the room held him the way it held every arrival—without comment, without mercy, simply making space.

Prokofiev’s Second sat atop the pile, corners weighted flat, the familiar pages worn soft at the edges. Months ago they’d penciled bowings into the margins—thick, insistent annotations that looked less like instructions than an ongoing argument with the composer himself, a private negotiation between living bodies and dead certainty.

David set the cello case down with a heavy, unmusical thud. His coat still held the cold.

Linn looked up, and the grin came easily—an old reflex—his arms opening for a brief second as if to embrace and then thinking better of it, bow dangling from one hand like a thought he hadn’t finished forming.

David gave a low laugh and dragged his sleeve across his face, as though he could wipe away whatever clung there.

Linn lifted the bow in a small, fluent gesture that read: how was your day? Or perhaps, more truthfully: do we survive this week?

David shrugged, and the slump of his shoulders answered plainly enough.

They had been speaking this grammar since Boston—exhaustion as syntax, the old recursive loop of who’s-worse-off-this-week rendered wordless by long practice, the sort of intimacy that doesn’t require confession because it has already seen everything confession would contain.

Eleanor Rieger, the quartet’s second violin, entered next in a flurry of wool and efficiency, moving as if she were late to something important even when she wasn’t, dispensing a cheek kiss to Linn, and the same to David, each deployed with the semi-sincere warmth of a diplomat working a reception line. Her overcoat swung off mid-stride, a practiced flourish, as if the body beneath it had no time for weather.

And then she stopped.

She had not seen David since the funeral, and the recognition arrived in her face with a small hitch, sincere even deeply rendered.

“Oh, David, darling. How are you, my dear? Your mother… oh, I’m just so sorry. For you both. How is she doing?”

“Oh, you know my mother,” David said. “She’s a mess and she’s doing great. As always.”

“Bless her heart. You’ll send her my love, won’t you.” Eleanor took his forearm in that official, pressurized manner whose unspoken grammar was universal: You hang in there now.

“You know,” Linn offered, “Eleanor and I were trying to figure out which of us caught more performances of the BSO back when your father was second chair. What did we decide, Eleanor—between us, maybe one-hundred-twenty? One-twenty-five?”

“Oh, easily,” Eleanor said, allowing him the larger share with an elegant little concession. “Though you had me by a mile, Roger.”

“Sound like you both had me by about… a hundred and twenty-five,” David said, the quip only slightly exaggerating the numbers.

Eleanor smiled, sloughing it off as she drifted away toward her chair, already breaking open the small private rites that preceded her playing, as if there were a door inside her she had to unlock before sound could enter.

By the time she sat, she was humming some interior pitch only she could hear, her body tuning itself with the slow seriousness of an instrument brought too quickly from cold air into warmth.

Roger caught David’s eye. They’d surrendered the attempt to understand Eleanor’s preparation years ago, accepting it the way one accepts a relative’s harmless superstition: with affection, with fatigue, with quiet relief that not everything needed translating.

“It was a lovely service,” Linn said softly as David began unpacking his things, the words placed gently, as though setting down a fragile object.

“It was,” David agreed, quietly. “Thanks for all your help last week.”

“Wouldn’t have been anywhere else. Was honored to be with you, my friend.”

What followed were the usual, small contacts. Pats to the back, a grip to the shoulder. They were the gestures of attempts, bearing thirty years of friendship and family, and all the things people mean but never quite manage to say. The sort of things you swear, at eighty-one, you wish you’d begun saying freely at thirteen, only to realize too late how little time is left to make up for the silence… the assumptions.

And then Celeste Armitage appeared in the doorway.

She did not enter as Eleanor entered, in motion first; she arrived as if the room had been waiting for her and she had known it. She paused there, scanning the space with a calm, unhurried assurance, viola snug against her back, and then stepped forward. As she passed David, still turned toward the coat pegs, she reached out and touched his elbow, firm and warm, a real hello: not tentative, not ceremonial or managed.

David glanced back, caught her eye, and gave the smallest nod, half greeting, half something quieter that did not yet have words. He hung his coat. When he turned again, she was already at her chair, settled as if she had always been there.

No one asked where the music was. Linn tapped the corner of his stand with a knuckle, glanced around once, letting the room collect itself.

Then he raised his bow and sounded a clear A that hung in the air like a plumb line. One by one, the others followed: Eleanor’s second violin, Celeste’s viola, finally David’s cello, each coaxing their open strings into alignment with that pure, unwavering pitch. For a few moments, the air thickened with a dissonant tapestry of fifths, a purposeful cacophony of micro-adjustments as fine tuners were tweaked and pegs nudged.

Then, as if a single breath were released, the sounds coalesced into a luminous, resonant chord. A moment of silence fell, the tension of preparation giving way to the collective focus of now.

“The Kabardinian,” Roger said, smiling now, the name spoken like a private signal. “Shall we?”

Chairs creaked into readiness. Bows lifted in a loose, coordinated arc. A shared breath, gathered more than taken, moved through the group as if it belonged to them all.

And then, at last, Prokofiev arrived.



⁂





SERGEI PROKOFIEV

  String Quartet No. 2

  “Kabardinian,” Op. 92


[I.   Allegro sostenuto]

Resonance preceded sound. David felt the cello’s body thrum against his sternum before bow touched string. Then came the opening chord: a striking bitonal clash, F bass under B-flat major, a poly-chordal figure that wanted blood, full of mountain air and mythic keening, the visceral strain of folk dance and night terror.

By measure twelve, David's attention fractured. The cello's G-string resonated at 196 Hz. It was close enough to the room's natural mode at 161.8 Hz that the interference pattern became visible.



Not metaphorically. Actually visible: dust motes tessellating in the standing wave, golden ratio spirals unfolding in three dimensions.

David’s fingers maintained their accuracy. Every position landed clean; every shift glided into place with practiced inevitability. His intonation was flawless. So precise it felt less like playing and more like gravity. The bow arm moved as if unspooled from a long preordained phrase, its weight and articulation embedded into muscle fiber through years of trench warfare with this godless composition.

But his mind had slipped its tether.

It rose past the fingerboard and f-holes, past the curvature of the quartet's intimate enclosure, past even Celeste’s rising breath, chasing the harmonic series as it bloomed and rose in spectral cascades over the group. He was mapping nodes, tracing overtones, measuring the room in frequencies instead of feet. His inner ear parsed the world in ratios and resonances, every phrase reduced to its sine wave skeleton.

The others noticed almost immediately. Roger glanced at Celeste, and between them, an acknowledgement that a drifting had begun.

He was still playing. Clean. Perfect. Untouchable. But he wasn’t with them anymore. His eyes weren’t on the score or anyone else. They fixed upward, reading patterns no one else could see: reflections, cancellations, the ghostly interference of vibrating wood and air.

His presence felt altered. Robotic, or worse. As if the soul had stepped sideways, leaving the body behind to execute its task. The bow still drew sound from the string, but each note arrived emptied of breath, risk, or will. A performance without a performer.



[II.  Adagio]

Celeste tried everything. She bent phrases, breathed warmth into her tone, leaned into rubato like an outstretched arm reaching for his, waiting for David’s cello to answer with the expected resolution.

He played the notes. Exactly as written. Flawless. Unmoved. Absent.

She leaned toward him again, instinctively, drawn by the shape of a void. Her breath caught on the edge of a phrase—nearly late.

And then, the memory struck. Mid-bow. No warning, just the sharp, silent incision of knowing. It arrived like dissonance unprepared: twenty years compressed into a single note that would not resolve.

This, in the end, is what undid them. This other absence. He had simply evaporated from their shared life molecule by molecule. Weekends disappeared behind the closed door of his study. While he pursued theoretical elegance, everything outside it, her, their home, the rituals of ordinary life, was reduced to background noise. The equations on his blackboard garnered ten times the affection that she could ever hope to inspire in him. There remained no escape, no earthly choice but to recognize that she had lost him to the purity of his own abstraction. And in the end, the most heartbreaking part was this:

He had never… even noticed… she was gone.

Her bow trembled. She whispered beneath her line, barely audible:

“David… stay with us.” Then, softer still, like a prayer, “Stay with me.”

By the midpoint of the Adagio, the violinists were visibly discouraged. Roger had seen this in him before. But not like this, not for years. The concern was obvious across all their faces. But Celeste, she felt a much deeper wound: that his body sat beside her while his mind flew through unreachable spaces, brushing constants and thresholds as though he were only notes away from discovering a new melody of quantum order.

Her plea lingered. Her pain trembled through each soaring buttress of the lofted annex until she could endure it no longer.

At the end of the second movement, she called the break herself. “Can we just—” She didn’t finish. She set down her viola and, with one miraculous surge of grace and courage, reached for him.

Her hand found his forearm, shaking him gently. “David. David, darling. Come back. Hey. You in there?”

His eyes twitched beneath closed lids before opening—bleary, confused, then sharpening on her face. As always, the razor’s edge of what he had just left, what he had allowed again, pierced him like a blade until Celeste stopped the blade from existing with the pure understanding of her eyes, and he was back.

He saw what he’d done, saw what she’d been carrying while he explored his numeric wonder-verse. The knowledge would keep him awake all night, turning over what he’d learned and what it had cost, again.

[III.  Allegro]

They resolved to regroup. Finding the violinists graciously willing, they launched into the third and final movement. The allegro opened with brittle, percussive throng. David was present, engaged; he and Roger even shared a startled moment of collective terror and exhilaration, cello to violin, as they climbed the wall of a fierce modulation.

Then, thirteen seconds less than two minutes in, rehearsal marker thirty-seven, came the col legno hammers: bows flipped, wood striking string with violent force. And then:


SNAP!



This was not the gentle pop of a worn string, but a violent detonation. The cello’s C string exploded under tension, a silver whip loosed at dangerous speed. It cracked like a gunshot, slicing the air at eye level.

All four froze, predator-still. Every muscle locked. For an endless moment they checked faces, eyes, necks. The wire could have blinded, cut, even severed.

They watched, unmoving, as the coil spat and fell harmless to the floor. Silence, for a full measure, maybe two. Then, a collective exhale. Then glances, one to each. And finally, uncontrollably, laughter. All four at once, wild with relief and adrenaline. The laughter shook them. Unmusical, uncontrolled, purely human.

All four of them let the moment unfurl as it saw fit; each ensuring private moments of camaraderie and reassurance with each of the other members. Roger and Eleanor had been around this block between the four musicians before, and were smart enough to begin laying down the structural requirements necessary  to ensure that they would not be caught in the gravity of Lang and Armitage if it could at all be avoided.

So it was with an unspoken swiftness, a singular wink and nose twitch from Eleanor to Roger, and an even more stealthy, behind-the-back hand tilt from Roger to Celeste, that the two veteran violinists quickly gathered their things and, with polite goodnights administered, fled toward the comfort and quiet of home.

But Celeste and David lingered, longer than the laughter required. Twenty years of shared rehearsal rooms carried them further: near-misses, small catastrophes, the lived history of making music together.

“Well, you were in rare—” Celeste began, then dissolved again. “Sorry. Rare… probably best left rare form this evening.”

“Really? I could've sworn I nailed every note. Haydn never had

such a cellist.”

She stopped mid-laugh. “Haydn?”

“Opus 76, right?” He kept his face perfectly straight.

“David, for God's sake, it's Prokofiev!”

“Oh.” He paused, mock-thoughtful. “The Kabardinian thing? Huh. Well that explains the col legno.”

She turned to the empty doorway. “Hey, you guys, David was playing Haydn all evening!”

They collapsed again, laughter spilling into glances, folding into blinks and nods, the gestures that live so close to regret they can borrow milk from each other without having to knock.

Later, and often, David would call himself an idiot—that he could keep her through the chaotic early years only to lose her once he was grown and supposed to be wise. Twenty years together, never married, and somehow that difference hurt more. He had lost her to the purity of his numeric mania, and he knew it.

At the double doors, she paused, scarf coiled, hand on the push bar. Something in the corridor behind her had changed. A shift in the air.

She turned back slowly. “David…?”

He was already looking at her. Something behind his eyes held, then shuttered.

A beat. Then he shook his head, just once. “It’s nothing.”

She waited, reading him. He was hiding something. Afraid. Her breath caught as the darkness bled in. She almost said something more. But it was no longer her place.

“Get some sleep, David,” she whispered. “Goodnight.”

“Goodnight, Cele.”

Only he could call her that, and only in the solemnity of their private world.

She lingered a moment longer, a slight smile cracking her defenses. The moment lasted exactly 2.47 seconds before she slid into the snow-shimmered quad and out of reach once more.


⁂


Deep in the tunnel beneath, or perhaps in the back pew of the old chapel, someone took a deep, laden breath, praying through the resonance of music and memory. The annex still lived, still served, still listened.

David melted back into the rehearsal hall, pulled on his overcoat, and sealed the cello away with the care of a man handling something both precious and dangerous. In the corridor, a distant vibraphone aligned perfectly with his tinnitus, a pure sustained note that would ring in his ears until well past two a.m.

David stopped to join the structure in its end-of-day rituals. He could tell only a handful of people remained in the building. A palpable sense of compression flowed heavily through the halls, burdening the air as the walls and ceiling catalogued the memory of Prokofiev’s wild chords and the mercy of a snapped string, folding them into its timbered rafters.

David glanced up at the clock above the double exit doors, then down to his watch. It was three minutes, forty-seven seconds past time.












IV


THE APARTMENT

[Adagio, con malinconia]

As he left the music school, David felt the city return in sharp lines and pale silence. The snow-lit quad stretched before him, a frozen ledger of the passing of souls. Two sets of footprints: hers and his, running parallel until they split. At that point, the path they’d shared for years broke quietly apart: hers turning toward the bridge, his descending into the dark fold of Lower East Hill.

He didn’t look back. He couldn’t, for he knew what he’d see: his own solitude, pressed into the snow like a sentence without end. Winter, unblinking, recorded everything.

The walk home took ten minutes. Lately, it felt like less. The body moved before thought arrived. Eighteen months of repetition had erased the in-between: those few blocks where once there was conversation, or silence that held meaning. Now, he left rehearsal and found himself at the apartment door, unsure what had passed in the time between. Grief had become a function. A habit. A reflex.

The door to the apartment, with its tall frosted-green glass, shut behind him with a hollow cough: the closing of a vault. The narrow entry hall funneled him forward until the apartment opened into its two-story cavity. Brick rose the full height of the far wall, the fireplace centered like a sentinel. In the far left corner, a slender vertical window cut upward through jagged brickwork; glass banded in uneven waves. On the right wall, a long horizontal casement stretched twelve feet across the kitchen wall, its lower sill just shy of the island’s steel lip.

The spiral staircase wound at the room’s center like a coil of wire, its glass treads catching stray city light. Above, the loft, wire-railed, half office, half catwalk, looked down into the main chamber. On the wall by the entry, the chalkboard installation waited: three massive counterweighted panels, disguised when shut as a plain white surface but ready to unfurl floor to ceiling like a trifold altar. Even dormant, it radiated the threat of dust.

Loneliness had crystallized here, a structure wholly unsympathetic. Neglect hung from crown-molding cracks. Chalk dust caked along the baseboards. A stranger with even minimal powers of observation could pin David to this place and vice versa in two minutes flat.

It was not a warm space, but neither was it clinical. Its design hovered in a state of unfinished intent, at once composed and disjointed. Though it had not been wholly thought through, David found it curiously suited to thinking. Those few who had spent time within its walls all remarked on the same thing: the apartment detested company. And yet, with David, it behaved differently, as if it had chosen him. It enfolded him with a quiet, maternal sense of care, a belonging that asked for nothing in return.

Sheet music colonized every surface, allowing for a massive B-minor scale to sprawl out across the coffee table. Clef symbols and poly-quivers curling out of time: the turgid product of overwritten tensor equations allowed to run roughshod over the terrain.

In the kitchen, David found himself uncertain whether the hollowness was hunger or something else entirely. He resolved the question the only way he knew: he grabbed the grease-glossed pizza box from 2 days prior, found 3 slices of dried out pepperoni inside, and gave them to the microwave to work its magic.

[Rallentando]

As he sat down to eat, his notebook slipped from the arm of the couch, cozying up beside him like a supplicant. Soon its pages were fat with the whispers of midnight epiphanies: diagrams of harmonic oscillator eigenstates and the jagged calculus of vibrato dynamics. His handwriting slanted forward, letters like fermions fleeing confinement. He slashed through a line, tearing the paper. The sound of the rupture echoing the cello string’s snap.

The same adrenaline-boil scalded him. It was a disconnected synesthesia he could no longer govern. It felt as though his own electromagnetic field was slipping frequency, dragging his body chemistry with it.

To establish a baseline, to confirm that cause still produced effect, he reached into his pocket. The folded note from his TA was still there. He didn’t look at it right away, only held the paper between his fingers as if it could anchor him.

He knew what it contained. The scrawled reprimand. The catalog of errors. Proof of the afternoon’s collapse.

He unfolded it.

What he saw ruptured something cleanly and at once. The page was covered in doodles. Nothing else. No notes. No corrections. Just idle shapes looping across the paper.

David dropped it as if it had turned venomous. The page drifted to the floor. Harmless. Obscene. He stepped on it hard, grinding it into the oak below, as though pressure alone might suffocate whatever truth it threatened to assert.

David reached for his phone, and with no concession to present reality, pressed the impossible-to-execute button on his favorites list. A photograph of his father materialized on the screen as the call routed to the now obscene recording of his father’s ghost:

“Hello, this is Arthur Lang. I can’t come to the phone at present. Please leave a message after the tone, and I’ll return your call at the earliest interval.”

He hung up, as though he had reached into the afterlife and felt his hand singed by the attempt.

“God damn it, Dad,” he murmured, his voice carrying the timbre of a child left alone in a storm: cold, abandoned, and furious at being stranded.

He shifted his gaze to the mirror above the mantel, studying a face assembled from borrowed time. More than ever before, he could see his father's eyes tearing through his own. The features passed down with genetic precision now clamored for prominence in the man's eternal absence, an overlay so vivid it felt spectral. He had never permitted himself to fully see how much of his father's face resided in his own. But now, the truth of it was so blindingly obvious as to render his prior denials an act of embarrassing, almost willful ignorance; a deception sustained not despite the evidence, but precisely because acknowledging it meant confronting the inevitability that now stood before him.

David raised his hand and with two fingers touched his own face in the glass. For one flickering moment, he felt he could see himself and his father separated in the mirror before him, could feel the touch on his actual cheek. But then… gone.

His right iris contracted, catching some shift of light, and with it came a sudden photonic epiphany: his best work lived only in the liminal shimmer between tremolos and theorems, forever fleeing the act of observation. To document it was to lose it; to share it was to distort it. Perhaps that was the cruelty of his inheritance, not the burden of unfinished conversation, but the knowledge that some things are not meant for an audience.

By 2:47 a.m. the room had become a triangle of potentials. He stood equidistant from stove, chalkboard wall, and cello case. Each a vertex, none resolved.




The refrigerator compressor droned a perfect fourth above the bathroom faucet’s drip. Chalk dust shimmered in the spill from the vertical window, suspended like cosmic background radiation.

Outside the brick, beyond the city’s pall of light, earth hummed its eternal B-flat, waiting for a listener in-tune enough to decode its musings.
