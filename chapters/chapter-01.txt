THE IVY LEAGUE

[Andante sostenuto—Presto agitato]

It was, perhaps, an unfortunate hour to be speaking of coherence.

David Lang had not slept. Three medically inadvisable energy drinks and four hundred miles of Massachusetts interstate had pushed his nervous system into tomorrow, while the rest of him lagged behind in protest.

He compensated by administering a sensible breakfast consisting of mini donuts and black coffee, then arranged himself, as ever, into the appearance of competence.

He had managed to ready himself, against the morning's odds, a full ten minutes and thirteen seconds before his time.

Then he remembered: the book. A groveling gift for Celeste, though the offense it answered rarely became clear until the gift became necessary. He'd found it languishing in the front parlor of his parents' house, crammed between a collection of Wagner scores and a Bartók-Meets-the-BSO LP: something about tuning methods in eighteenth-century Europe. If she loved it, and she would, such a gift might keep him in her better graces. But damn, it was buried upstairs in the suitcase with all the other gestures too delicate to survive travel.

For one professionally indefensible moment, David considered whether just remembering it might be enough.

Then standards returned. With a small, resentful grunt, he climbed the spiral staircase to the bedroom loft, moved with direct haste to bed and suitcase, and pulled the book from beneath a heap of dirty dress socks.

As he reached to crack open the cover, his left arm went slack. It dropped to his side, heavy and useless for a single bewildering second before sensation rushed back all at once.

David frowned and flexed his fingers. No pain. No numbness. Just the sickening misstep of an extra stair in the dark.

Exhaustion certainly.

As he rolled the stiffness from his wrist and glanced down at his watch, the world tilted subtly out of sequence.

Somewhere, in what David was sure could not have been more than forty-six… maybe forty-seven seconds, just over fifteen minutes had disappeared. The loss left him five minutes late.

The watch face stared back at him with perfect indifference.

David blinked, pivoting his focus toward the loft windows, hoping the angle of the light itself might contradict the numbers. The apartment remained perfectly ordinary. Heat clicking through the radiators. Morning glared across the railing. Traffic somewhere downhill, beyond the glass, pressed onward.

But the missing minutes remained missing.

A moment later he was scrambling across the grated bridge and out onto the balcony. The short distance between the apartment and the southern entrance of Cornell’s Ithaca campus stretched out before him. He could just make out the familiar pattern of the gang already gathered, already drawing itself into its usual formation, already moving toward the first rise without him.

David Lang was never late. Never. That fact had always been sufficient to guarantee the rest of him. But today had begun its slide toward the unreliable, and the fear of surrendering forever to a life of substandard timing and forgotten gestures sent him to more drastic measures. He took the stairs two at a time, even leapt from the third step to the floor below, neglecting to grab the essential armor of his sunglasses before heading out.

He crossed the narrow streets south of campus with his coat tugging at his shoulders and his breath clipping in the cold. At the corner, a woman appeared with a yapping little nut of a dog. David slowed at once, arranging his face into reflective morning purpose. As they passed, he leaned down, pretending to be interested in the animal so as to avoid direct engagement with its owner. David counted three steps for dignity, then straightened himself, allowing his pace to betray him again.

As the town thinned toward campus, its porches slick with cold, its modesty already surrendering to Cornell’s gates, a voice came over him, remembered and thin.

I don’t know how you stomach those conversations.

It came the way Arthur came now, just pieces surfacing where the man used to be. By the time East Hill rose before him, David had restored his pace to something that could still, under generous interpretation, be called walking, but his mind now began doing the skipping.

Always one up on the next man. Always something, son.

The something was building in him as he climbed. A readiness. The old reflex of having the room before he entered it.

For a moment, he managed to shake himself free. The apparently new phenomenon of his very dead father’s very loud voice could wait for another time, if such a time existed.

He looked out over the lower quad and caught the bunch of them, already assembled and on the move: faculty, staff, graduate students, the usual cohort already congealing into ritual. And there, near the center, was Sweeney, talking, talking, talking. Of course he was.

Four days of David's absence had left a vacuum, and Sweeney, like all soft men with tenure, had expanded to fill it.

David felt something in him sharpen. Territory being threatened. He moved to quicken his pace, then almost tumbled straight over himself.

A glance to his shoes found sabotage in full bloom: both laces crisscrossing the other with plain devil's work engaged. Begrudgingly, he knelt to right the mess, one eye on the task at foot and one on the task ahead. Finally done, he sprang upright and broke back into the short, undignified spurts of speed required to catch them, cutting through the trees and rejoining the group halfway up the slope.

Sweeney was mid-anecdote.

"—I'm telling you, he said cross-disciplinary intimacy to my face," Sweeney was saying. "In a budget meeting. To my face. Can you imagine?"

David stepped into the migration. "My God, Sweeney, intimacy and face in the same sentence? And in mixed company, no less? You must have an excellent harem of lawyers on retainer."

Sweeney turned, barked a laugh, then almost ruined it by becoming kind. "Lang. Welcome back."

"Don't."

"Don't what?"

"Lower the room temperature with your humanity. It's undignified."

Naomi Feld, walking just behind Sweeney, hid a smile behind her coffee. Bardon from History gave David a cautious nod, the kind people gave at funerals and department votes. Two graduate students drifted in their wake.

Sweeney recovered first. "You know, you look almost refreshed."

"Yes," David said. "Well, it's true what they say. Burying a parent can be remarkably restorative."

The line landed hard. Too hard. He felt it immediately: the microscopic withdrawal in posture around him, the uncertainty over whether the joke had granted permission to laugh. So he smiled wider than necessary.

"Takes ten years off the face," he added, "and approximately five off your lifespan."

Naomi lost the fight first. Sweeney followed, slower, appalled at himself.

"And for the record," David said, "there are only two ways disciplinary intimacy lands. Title IX, or tenure."

That broke them properly. But the chilled wind had caught David's eyes, and they were watering now. Just the wrong moment to be open to a flanking attack.

Bardon leaned forward to take his shot. "Speaking from experience, are we, Professor Lang?"

David glanced at him, like his family honor was at stake. "I don't know, Bardon. Shouldn't you wait to see which side wins, then dodge conclusions for the next thirty years and let the next generation of younger, better-looking scholars retrofit morality onto your cowardice? History, after all, is just procrastination with archives and a bad hairpiece, right?"

"Good to have you back, Lang," Sweeney said, roughening the line so it would not have to count as sincerity. David suddenly felt like a schoolyard bully. He was only half convinced that was a bad thing.

The group adjusted around his return. Half the gossip prepared for the morning had clearly been about him. David caught the little nods, the softened faces, the quick rearrangements of sympathy into banter. He ignored all of it. He had returned, and the world would resume its proper cruelties.

As they reached the lower crossing, where the older paving stones gave way to the darker run laid nearer the quad, David stopped.

Not hesitated. Stopped.

The effect was immediate. Behind him, the line collapsed into itself: shoes scuffing, brief apologies, Sweeney's hand rising automatically to steady Naomi's coffee before it betrayed everyone.

"Lang?"

At first the sensation was almost ordinary. A pressure under the sternum. A sour inward lurch. Then came the sound: grating under pressure, pitched low enough to feel structural, as though one of the buried utilities beneath campus had mistaken his rib cage for pipe. It moved through his chest before it reached his ears. Deep. Menacing. He felt suddenly certain he was about to vomit.

David took one hard breath, or tried to. The air would not quite enter.

Then his stomach turned again, this time with the sickening sensation of a body briefly released from time itself. For one flashing instant he swore the skin of his face had drawn tight; then, just as quickly, it sagged the other way into some greyed and brittle age not yet his.

Then the present snapped shut around him.

"You heard that," David said.

Sweeney glanced around. "Heard what?"

"That tone just now."

Naomi looked from one to the other. "Oh, we heard your tone, Professor."

A nervous laugh passed briefly through the group and died young.

David took a half step back. The pressure eased, and his gaze dropped to the seam in the paving stones.

Sweeney circled back, now genuinely concerned and therefore much harder to tolerate. "You okay, Lang? You're sweating like it's eighty degrees out here."

"I'm fine," David said. "Long few days is all."

"Of course." Sweeney lowered his voice slightly. "Really, though. Glad you're back, David. He was a great man, your dad. Be proud of a big life, yeah?"

"Yeah," David said. "Thanks, Hank. Appreciate that."

"And don't let those quantum undergrads take you down."

"Please. They'd need a quorum first."

Sweeney, accepting David's continuity of humor as sufficient proof of stability, allowed himself to be absorbed back into the advancing clot.

Only Naomi remained.

"You're not fine," she said.

"No," David admitted. He looked past her toward the quad, now thinning into silence as students vanished into classrooms. "But I am late."

She followed his gaze to the seam in the stones. "What happened?"

"I don't know."

The honesty escaped him before he could revise it into something more survivable.

"Do you want me to call someone? Celeste, maybe?"

"No. God, no."

"Walk in with you, then."

He adjusted the satchel. His hands were shaking, which offended him more than the nausea.

"I think I'd rather preserve the illusion of dignity, if nothing else."

"David."

He stood there breathing once, then again, until the tremor in his chest settled into something manageable.

"I know," he said quietly.

Naomi nodded toward campus. "I'll be nearby."

"How ominous."

"Good."

She turned and headed toward the hall.

David waited until she disappeared fully around the corner before continuing toward the entrance to Feynman Hall, keeping well clear of the seam.

He navigated the main entrance with his usual nonchalance, even appeared to sauntered through a gaggle of administrative secretaries before landing in his hallway. But the cover cost him something he felt rip at his stomach like acid and water combined. At exactly thirty feet from the classroom doors, he stopped again.

So many proofs, son.

David forced a deep, forged breath.

So much proving for approval. There's something to be said for grace notes, don’t you think, Davey?

It was a bullshit, cheap-shot line his father used way too much, and he knew it. The skin beneath his watch began to pulse. He glanced down.

Five minutes and forty-seven seconds behind his time.

Shit.


⁂


Deep in Sub-level Two, in a concrete corridor omitted from university floor plans sometime during the Nixon administration, a copper bypass valve finally failed. A thin breath of trapped air slipped into the ventilation system.

The shaft carried it upward through the buried architecture of East Hill: past biology labs, steam conduits, abandoned storage rooms, electrical chases sealed decades earlier behind renovations no one fully remembered.

By the time it reached Feynman Hall, the air no longer smelled stale. It smelled of radiator steam, machine oil, and the hot mineral dust of old laboratory equipment, the same dry metallic scent that used to cling to his grandfather’s sweaters after long nights in the physics building.

The hallway cooled around his ankles. Behind him, sunlight lay in white bands across the striated glass, but the chill rose from below, precise as a hand. Something brushed his cheek. He wiped at it, but nothing was there.

He stood there clutching his lecture notes too tightly, listening to nothing at all while becoming aware, with terrible and immediate certainty, that he was being observed from a very short distance by something unwilling to announce itself further.

Behind the doors, twenty-seven students waited to be disappointed. The outcome was all but preordained, but David, as always, entered with the intention to win them over entirely. All he required was a provisional level of trust, temporary as it might be, sufficient to allow for a moment or two of genuine, suspended belief.

“Good morning.”


⁂


The lecture hall was tall. Very tall. One part classroom, two parts bakehouse, its narrow window bank running floor to ceiling along the rear wall overlooked the glacier-carved canyons of East Hill, their angled panes slicing daylight into shards. The light never arrived cleanly here, only fractured. False.

A brushed-metal plaque by the door marked the advent of the halls construction. The inscription praised the room's "unique ornamentation," claiming it had been designed to "soothe the psyche and elicit heightened states of mental acuity."

They said the same things about heroin for a hundred years.

Sixty-three names on the roster. Twenty-seven bodies in the seats. Of those, perhaps thirteen were present in any sense that mattered.

"My," he said. "A full house, I see."

He set his satchel on the desk and glanced down at the roster with a small show of uncertainty.

"Let me make sure we’re all in the right place. This is…" He traced a line with one finger and, with a false search, found, "Introduction to Quantum Dynamics. Excellent. Always reassuring when the room and the paperwork agree."

"And I am—" He checked once more, letting his TA Rebecca, along with the rest of the front row in on the insincerity. "David Lang. The Feynman Chair of Quantum Dynamics. A grand title, I know. And aren't you lucky? My speciality and the class focus are uncommonly aligned."

A rustling of bags and a few hints of general amusement

"If this is not your intended destination, or if my tenured and faintly immortalized presence strikes you as too intimidating, then by all means go to lunch." He tipped the chalk toward the door. "Otherwise, remain with us and starve on science and superposition."

The room offered a mild pulse of amusement. David felt the old register find him. Dry, amused, even occasionally lethal. The ease of it irritated him.Even his had begun lying on his behalf.

"Let's jump right back in today, yes? Try this again. Causality: the old narcotic."

He turned to the board and wrote:

|Ψ⟩ = Σ cᵢ |ϕᵢ⟩

This," he said, tapping the Ψ, "is the whole wave function. Everything that can be, gathered into one expression. A probability cloud, if you like metaphors." A pause. "I like metaphors. They're all we have."

"Here's the part that matters," he said, and something in his voice shifted. Less performance. More confession. "Before you get involved, endless possibilities. After you engage, no choices left. That's the grace. And the trap of superposition."

He let it land.

The room loosened. A small laugh, genuine enough. He could still do this. The muscle memory was there, buried under years of bureaucratic sediment and the slow erosion of giving a damn.

He stepped to the desk for a fresh stick of chalk. When his fingers closed around the box, pain shot through them. Sudden. Familiar, a moments glancing had David expecting to once again loose control of the arm as earlier, but then… nothing. He looked just beyond his focus into the space between himself and the benches. Dust hung in the slant of light, each mote briefly suspended in its own small uncertainty.

"Now," he said, turning back to the board. "Look here."

He wrote beneath the first expression:

K(b,a) = Σ exp(iS/ℏ)

"This is the sum over histories. Every possible way of getting from the beginning of a thing to the end of it. The paths contribute amplitudes. Some reinforce. Some cancel. The ones we call reality are, more or less, what survives the argument."

His chalk hovered a fraction too long at the end of the line.

Then, without deciding to, he added:

+ η(t)


David stared at it. He had written noise into the equation. The room waited.

He heard himself say, "And if the system admits noise—" but the rest of the thought melted into nothing as the buzz came roaring, grinding back. And now, for the first time, he knew its number.

68.48 Hz.

The figure arrived in his mind with impossible authority. Fully formed. Calibrated elsewhere and inserted behind his eyes.

68.48 Hz.

A frequency beneath speech. Beneath thought. Too low to belong to the room and too precise to be imagined. It scraped through him, deep and remembered, passing under the sternum, towards the hinge of his jaw, then touched the small bones inside his ear with the cold familiarity of a key entering a lock.

His hand hovered over the board. The chalk trembled between his fingers. A white fleck broke loose and landed on the cuff of his sleeve.

He forced his hand down.

"All right," he said, and his voice came out steadier than he deserved. "Let's shift the frame."

As he turned back to the board, the hum burrowed deeper, scratching at the base of his skull. He needed something solid. Perhaps a pivot to Einstein, something familiar would do. He had half a mind to ask if anyone had an apple he could use, but he decided to leave Newton out of this.

The wave of frequency thrumming though his body moved now in a manor that felt like encouragement or even assistance. So much so that, as the block letters materialized under his hand, David began to understand that he had not yet consciously decided to write anything at all. Only upon stepping back to read it aloud, was David truly aware of what is said.

HOW DO YOU MEASURE PROXIMITY?

Then underneath:

d = √(Δx² + Δy²)

He stepped back. The hum pressed against his eardrums. In the back of the hall, the bank of windows began to work their scattered magic. David usually had no trouble blocking out their signature glare, but at this moment, they seemed to have the better of him.

"When the world resonates," he said, his voice gone softer now, stranger, "you feel less alone. Like there's structure underneath the noise. Drift just a little…” He snapped his fingers. "And that structure collapses."

He scanned the room.

"There is a fundamental frequency beneath everything. Beneath language. Beneath light."

Then something tapped on the shoulder of his mind.

He opened his eyes to find Dr. Rune Ishikawa standing, no placed, in the doorway. The chair of the department. The Guardian of East Hill. Arranged like a symbol.

David had no idea how long Rune had been standing there.

He drew breath for an introduction, but Rune stepped in first and took it from him.

"Are you not, in fact, describing musical harmony, Professor Lang?"

David froze. Something inside him pulled taut—a string tuned too high for too long.

"No," he said. "No, I'm not, Rune. Christ. What an idiotic question. Harmony is passive. It's a fucking consequence. Resonance is physical. It builds."

The room went still.

"Oh, I see." Rune smiled a thin, elegant blade of a smile. "Vulgarity notwithstanding, you're saying harmony is a result of the intrinsic resonant forces underpinning the intervals?"

"Well, yes. I mean—"

"But resonant structures can alter without corrupting the base harmonics, isn't that right?"

"That is a conveniently—"

"You know," Rune continued, turning to address the students as if David had ceased to exist, "it's been a long time since my marching band days. I once fancied myself a bassoonist. Fabulous instrument. Terrible embouchure, I'm afraid."

“Dr. Ishikawa, was there something you needed?”

“Oh, it’s a trifle David, and correct me if I'm wrong, but a minor third atop a major third creates a major triadic harmony, yes?”

“Well, yes.” David had to concede that.

“Yet that same harmonic structure, when inverted, can be created by a major third and a… oh, help me David, won't you? It's a…”

“A perfect fourth,” David said through his teeth.

“Of course.” Rune snapped his gloved fingers. “A perfect fourth.” He placed both hands in the front pockets of his suit jacket and offered the room a slight, elegant shrug. "I must be missing something. Or perhaps the inquiry is, as you say, idiotic."

David was seething. A cornered animal in a room full of witnesses.

"Harmony happens when everything is already aligned," he said, voice low and dangerous. "Resonance creates that alignment. And you, you of all people, Rune… you know that.”

He turned toward the doorway. A few students turned with him, but most stared at David, as there was nothing else to look at.

The doorway was empty. Rune had walked away mid-battle. A preemptive victory.

David caught his breath. Let the fury settle like ash. Then he turned back to the board and wrote:

RESONANCE = SURVIVAL

This time, he let it stay.

Behind him, the soft shuffle of leather-soled shoes retreated down the hallway, unhurried, administrative, the sound of a man who had already won.


He snapped on a false grin and gestured toward the empty doorway.

"Dr. Ishikawa, ladies and gentlemen. Always an honor to have his unique perspective." The smile died before it reached his eyes. "What a legend."

This was it. He was out of time and grace. David knew he had to save this now or it would fall out of reach. He lunged toward the projector and flipped the power. The machine whirred to life as the first slide flickered on-screen.

"Now. This is a section from the Lang-Feldman Diagram. It maps the overlap between psychoacoustics and neural patterning in recursive feedback loops. Or at least it did, before someone rewrote the grant proposal as a sound art initiative."

He clicked forward. A waveform graph.

Click. An anatomical rendering of the cochlea, the inner ear.

Click. Wait.

An unexpected photograph cut through the light. A Polaroid. Within its thick white frame: evening fog, a field of winter-green grass. At the center, a single tuning fork, half-buried in wet soil. The resonance prong pointed to the sky.

David's blood stopped moving. He knew this image. He had been there when it was taken. Celeste. The tuning fork. The year of the agreement. The capsule.

His mind braced as the ancient moment unfurled, unbidden and unstoppable. The smell of dug-up earth. The weight of shared knowledge pressing down on the shovel. The click of the Polaroid. Celeste's voice, cold and clear in the mist:

If it's real, it'll claw its way out. It will come back to us.

She had meant it as encouragement. It returned to him now as pure threat.

The hum burrowed deeper into his chest. His lungs faltered.

Then, suddenly, the sound pulled back. The pressure eased and  slowly, his voice returned.

"My apologies," he said. "Wrong… wrong file."

He shut off the projector. The image was gone. The memory was not.

Then, quietly, with hazed intention, he reached out and touched the blackboard; sweeping over its surface, just a fingertip. The chalk dust met his skin. For one brief moment, there was nothing. No hum. No room. Only the strange impression, impossible and immediate, of a string answering somewhere beyond him.

"Professor?"

The voice rose from the front row, but it did not register.

"Professor Lang?"

Silence persisted, total and unyielding, as the room held for a while on the edge of an unanswered question. Faces remained turned toward the blackboard, toward Lang, toward the symbols that had spread across three panels and climbed into the margins where the lecture should have ended.

A biology major auditing the course watched him for several moments, then gathered her notebook, slipped a pen behind her ear, and made her way down the aisle. When she opened the door, a draft of autumn air moved through the room, carrying the smell of wet leaves from the quad.

After that, the silence began to loosen. A student near the back glanced at his phone. Another closed a laptop. Bags were zipped. Chairs folded upward. Conversations started in whispers and drifted toward the hallway. Even the students who had spent years defending Lang's eccentricities left with an unusual gravity, as though they had seen something give way in a structure they had always trusted.

By the time David opened his eyes, the lecture hall had emptied.

For several seconds, he failed to understand what he was seeing. The chalk remained between his fingers. The blackboard stood before him, crowded and pale. Afternoon light slanted through the windows. His mind reached for the thread of whatever argument he had been making and found only the faint impression of having once held it. He turned toward the seats expecting a raised hand, a shifting body, some patient expression waiting for him to finish, and found row after row of vacant desks.

A folded sheet of paper rested on the lectern. He unfolded it.

"What the fuck?"

Rebecca's handwriting.

His gaze moved to the clock. The hands seemed at first merely wrong, then impossible, and finally undeniable. Nearly three hours had passed. Outside, the quad had emptied. The paths crossing the grass lay deserted beneath a pale sky, and the trees stood motionless in the cooling light.

Then another sensation entered his awareness. It came so quietly that he first mistook it for memory. Beneath the ordinary hum of the building, beneath the ventilation and the distant groan of old pipes, something was moving. The impression traveled under the floorboards and beyond the walls, out beneath the roots of the elms. David remained still, listening, while a recognition he had spent two decades suppressing rose in him and took hold.

He crossed the room and seized an eraser from the tray beneath the board. Chalk dust burst into the air as equations vanished beneath hurried strokes. Diagrams disappeared. Arrows dissolved. Whole chains of reasoning collapsed into gray smears. Dust settled on his sleeves and gathered along the leather of his shoes while he worked his way across the board, driven by an urgency that had little to do with professional embarrassment.

The clean boards bore witness to an institutional truth: if it wasn't declared in chalk, it hadn't happened.

Yet David stopped short of total erasure.

RESONANCE = SURVIVAL

He could almost swear he’d ran the eraser over the mock equation. Yes of course he had, but there it remained. And the sight of its beaconed typeface, defiant and foolish at once, began to settled his electrified nerves. He stepped back, staring at the declaration, an almost smile sneaking out from beneath the dread.


⁂


Whatever peace had settled ever so slightly over him, David knew it had no power to stay for long. For as he collected his things in silence, that strange silence began collecting him. The hall of glass exhaled its long-stretched shards of afternoon, casting him gently towards the slow-blackening hallway, and into the spinning world beyond.

Yet in the space between his lecture hall and the building's double doors, something shifted. Probabilities began to whirl and cry with a kind of havoc he could not contain or ignore.

The Polaroid. Where the hell did it come from?

His pace quickened. The files were locked. Password-protected. There was no pathway for that slide to end up in the deck. None.

And Rune. Forty-eight hours' advance notice. University policy. The man had waited. Planned. Stood in the doorway like he was collecting evidence.

The anger clarified with each step. By the time David hit the building's double doors and burst through into the quad, he was boiling.

Snow attacked him immediately. Fat flakes drove sideways in the wind, but could not land. The moment they touched his coat, his face, his hair, they vaporized. Steam rose from his shoulders. The heat radiating off him turned the falling snow into a halo of negative space, flakes approaching and then repelling as though he were magnetized wrong.

He barely noticed. His mind was angling toward the office and the files. And that noise, the frequency. How the hell could he know it, name it so precisely? 68.48 Hz. It wasn't even a dead-on pitch. Almost C#2, sure. But not exactly. Almost half the fine-structure.

Jesus, Lang. Don't do that.

Then, as if naming the thing aloud was enough to reawaken the beast, the buzz returned.

It came roaring, detonating in his inner ear like a depth charge, so sudden and violent that he staggered mid-step, one shoe skidding on ice.

"No." He said it loud, though he had not meant to. "Get out. I'm done with you."

The sound grew. Doubled in defiance. With each breath, the vibration sank deeper into his lungs. His vision blurred at the edges. Through it he could feel the hum pulling. It was directional now, coming from ahead. Somewhere in the quad.

David's rage shifted. Turned predatory.

"Fine," he said into the frigid night. "You want me to find you? I'll fucking find you."

He pushed out toward it, following the resonance like a blood trail. The hum intensified with each step. Denser. His molars ached. His sternum vibrated. The frequency climbed into his sinuses, his eye sockets, the hollow spaces behind his ears.

Ten feet. Twenty. Thirty into the quad. Until his shin struck iron. The bench materialized out of fury and snow. Green paint weathered to gray-green, armrests pocked with rust, east-facing, twenty-eight feet off the main path.

The hum was coming from beneath. Below. Six feet down. Where the capsule had been sealed, held in the deep by dirt and clay and the collective certainty that some knowledge should remain unknown.

David stood there, chest heaving, snow evaporating off his coat, staring down at the iron armrest, the weathered slats, the frozen ground beneath.

Twenty years in the earth. And it was still vibrating.


⁂


She'd said it would come back to them. He'd wanted to believe her then. He needed to believe her, but the wanting had curdled into forgetting, and the forgetting had hardened into routine; a routine that had carried him past this bench four thousand times without incident.

Until today.

Now the hum was pushing at him. Compressing its wave deep into his skull. David slammed his palms to his ears, the pressure building unchecked within him.

He would not allow it. It would not take him. Not after all this time.

And then it happened. A sound tore out of him so completely it stripped the world clean.

The windows shuddered. The stone walls answered. Even the air flinched.

And immediately, the awful, relentless buzz slid quickly down and out of his bones. The world went still. Silence blanketed the quad. Then a whisper of wind, a cackling crow, a hint of distant laughter. He stood there in the snow, breath catching, throat scraped bare, not knowing what part of him had remembered how to make a sound like that.

When he felt steady enough to dare it, David turned from the site, rejecting its call, its pull on him. He forced his feet to turn. Forced himself forward into the snow.

But behind him, beneath the bench and frozen earth, it remained. Waiting.

Celeste had been right. It had come back. And time would wait no longer.
