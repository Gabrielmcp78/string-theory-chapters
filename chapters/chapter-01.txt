I.  THE CLASSROOM

[Andante sostenuto—Presto agitato]

It was, perhaps, an unfortunate hour to be speaking of coherence.

Professor David Lang stood hunched before a blackboard that had grown suspicious of his intentions, inscribing symbols not with confidence but with the tragic diligence of a man increasingly convinced—by mounting evidence—of his own elite academic failure to rise.

The lecture hall, always five degrees too warm, was a quiet expanse of yellows and browns. Two back walls of impossibly thin, improbably tall windows overlooked the glacier-carved canyons of East Hill, their right-angled panes slicing daylight into shards. The light never arrived cleanly here—only fractured. False.

Now, the room was simply upholstery for the chair. And a very well-endowed one at that. David was the Feynman Chair of Quantum Dynamics at Cornell University—had been for nearly nineteen years. Though lately he could sense—in brittle emails, in the deflective eyes of students, in the way a chalk screech pierced from board to bone—that the title, once a reward, had become its own diagnosis: a public credential for a man in private decline.

It wounded him from within—this institutionalized impulse to guard truth, teach safely, hedge science. He’d played the role so long, even he couldn’t say where the performance boundaries were. A brushed-metal plaque by the door marked David’s assignment to the post. The inscription highlighted the room’s design, claiming its “unique ornamentation” aimed to “soothe the psyche and elicit heightened states of mental acuity.”

They said the same things about heroin for a hundred years.

Though sixty-three students were on the roster, roll call revealed that, on this crystalline afternoon, only twenty-seven had deemed Professor Lang’s insights of greater value than a midday nap. Of these, David identified a mere thirteen who appeared present in any meaningful sense. A young woman in the first row drew elaborate mollusk shells in her notebook’s margin that, in their recursive curvature, seemed to mirror the lecture’s content through cryptic isomorphism. Her glazed-over eyes, however, told David everything he needed to know about the depth of that correspondence.

David’s voice—once confident and of dry wit—had lately taken on a more resigned, ingratiating timbre as he explained superposition to the drowsy air.

“Imagine,” he began, “a coin—already in your pocket—its presence unknown, and therefore its value is…. Anyone?” Nothing. Not a sound from the peanut gallery. He continued, “Time’s up. Thank you for playing. Indeterminate is your answer for those keeping score.”

He looked about the room, dead eyes everywhere. “Also… indeterminate, I see. Okay. Only when your hand closes around it does the system collapse and a definite worth emerge. That’s when you learn whether you can afford a proper meal, or will have to settle for yet another pack of ramen noodles.”

A mild chuckle caught throughout the room.

They’re alive. A miracle. Use it.

“You think I’m joking? I’m not. Until observed, that coin lives in a cloud of probability. It could be a dime, a quarter, even some rare mis-strike worth more than your tuition. Though who are we kidding? No coin is worth that much. But, hey, take a look around the room. See if you can spot the person with the Ivy League tuition penny in their pocket.”

Students began pointing at each other. A few, emboldened, even pointed at David.

“Me?” He laughed. “Not a chance. I’m not that lucky.”

The room loosened. For a moment, they were with him.

“That’s the tyranny and the grace of superposition. Before you get involved: possibilities are endless. After you choose to engage: no choices left.”

They settled. The silence that followed was attentive, almost reverent. David let himself enjoy it, the faint rise of pride he did not bother to hide from himself. He had missed this. It used to come easier.

He stepped to the desk for a fresh stick of chalk, one hand still half-raised toward the students, as if to hold the thought in place until he turned back. When his fingers closed around the box, pain moved through them. Sudden. Familiar. His joints now remembering something they knew too well.

Dust hung in the slant of afternoon light, each mote briefly suspended in its own small uncertainty.

He turned back to the board before the moment could thin, his arm swinging wide like a conductor drawing in some distant section. The clean edge of the chalk struck sharp against the board.

“Now, look—here—” he said, scrawling the causal expansion across the slate:



“This,” he tapped the Ψ, “is the whole wave function—the system. Everything that can be, all at once. A probability cloud, if you like metaphors, which I do.”

He underlined the summation. “This sum—this Σ stacks up all the possible states the system could collapse into. Each|ϕᵢ⟩ is a state, a version of reality. The little cᵢ out front? That’s how likely each one is. Their weights. Their vote count, if you like democracy, which I do.”

He paused just a hair too long.

“But this,” he circled the final term, “this… is—”

Then—nothing.

Now… he wasn’t sure if he’d actually said the word noise—or if, at that exact moment, he’d only heard it.

But something… something answered.

A hum—no, a buzz. Deep, menacing, unforgiving. 68.48 Hz. Not a guess. The number came to him without warning, instantly, as if calculated somewhere else and then inserted into his head.

It flooded his mind like seawater into a breached hull. The sound scraped deep into the soul of his belly—visceral, remembered, yet horribly present. His hand hovered. He couldn’t move, could barely breathe, as the room melted out of cohesion.

They’d seen it. How could it be otherwise? And with that, the superposition of this lecture collapsed.

He had to pivot. He couldn’t think—the low droning in his ears, a blatant, involuntary confession disguised as formalism, mocking him from all sides. Still, David knew he could salvage this. He’d done it before. But he would have to abandon this tack.

“All right, you know what… let’s… let’s shift the frame. Honestly, I’m not even sure I understand what I‘m saying there, so...”

He turned back to the board, heartbeat—staccato. Maybe a switch to Einstein would do—something familiar. He had half a mind to ask if anyone had an apple, but decided to leave Newton out of this.

What came to him instead wasn’t strictly physics, but it was something, and David—unsettled and unsure—decided to give it a chance.

  How do you measure proximity?

Then underneath:




He stepped back. Squinted. Erased it—Rewrote:




Better. Maybe. He just wasn’t sure. He couldn’t hear himself—wasn’t thinking in tune—the buzz of memory cutting off the link of lecture.

“When the world resonates,” he said, voice gone soft and strange, “you feel less alone. Even when no one’s listening—you feel connected. Like there’s… structure underneath the noise. But drift just a little half a harmonic even, and that structure collapses. The space between intention and reception grows.”

He turned. Looked over the room. “It’s not theoretical. It’s measurable. There’s a fundamental frequency under everything. Beneath language. Beneath light.”

Silence. The kind that signals judgement.

And then, he saw him.

Dr. Rune Ishikawa.

Standing—no, placed—in the doorway. The chair of the department. The Guardian of East Hill. Arranged like a symbol.

Shit! How long had he been standing there? How much of that dreck had he…

Then Rune did the unthinkable. He piped in. “Are you not, in fact, describing… musical harmony, Professor Lang?”

David froze. Something inside him snapped taut. A tension string pulled too far, too fast.

“No, no I’m not, Rune. Christ! What an idiotic question.” He began careening back and forth. “Harmony is passive. It’s a fucking consequence. Resonance is physical. It builds.”

“Oh, I see, so—” and he smiled slyly, “vulgarity notwithstanding, you’re saying harmony is a result of the intrinsic resonant forces underpinning the intervals?”

“Well, yes. I mean…” Lang had lost the thread.

Ishikawa had most certainly not. “Resonant structure can alter without altering the base harmonics, isn’t that right?”

“That is a conveniently—“

“You know,” Rune continued, as if David was not even there, “it’s been a long time since my marching band days, so correct me if I’m wrong, but a minor third atop a major third creates a major triadic harmony. However, inverted, that same harmonic structure can be created by a major third and… oh, shoot—what is it… help me, won’t you, David? It’s a—”

“A perfect fourth,” David replied, begrudgingly.

“Of course,” Rune snapped his gloved fingers, “that’s right, a perfect fourth. Well… I must be missing something, yes?” Rune placed both his hands palms down into the front pockets of his suit jacket, and with a slight, elegant shrug finished the line, “or perhaps the inquiry is, as you say… idiotic.”

David was seething now. Not lecturing. Not reasoning. Just raw.

“Harmony happens when everything is already aligned. Resonance creates that alignment.”

He swiveled back and pointed toward the doorway.

“And you—you of all people, Rune—you know that—”

Silence. Nothing. There was no one in the doorway. Rune was gone. No expression. No comment. Just gone.

David caught his breath. Let the fury settle like ash. Then, he turned back to the board—confused, embarrassed even. He simply wrote:


Resonance = Survival


This time, he let it stay.

Behind him, the sound of shoes shuffling—soft leather, unhurried—the pace of administrative authority.

David stood there, frozen, staring into the void. The raw, reckless edge of what he’d said tore through him line by line. There was no taking it back; no apologizing for it either. Any absolution would only dissolve him in his students’ eyes. He needed a way out. But he’d have to own it. He lunged toward the projector, flipped the power as if the switch alone could crack open the cage he’d built.

The machine whirred to life—light cutting through the fog of before—casting a silver glow across the dust-veiled room. The first slide flickered onscreen. And then, to complete this brute-force recovery, he shouted out his next command:

“Right then. Moving on! Now… this is a section from the Lang–Feldman Diagram. It maps the overlap between psychoacoustics and neural patterning in recursive feedback loops. Or at least it did, until someone rewrote the grant proposal as a ‘sound art initiative.’”

Mild laughter. Or maybe it was only static.

He clicked forward. A waveform graph.

Click.

An anatomical rendering of the cochlea—the inner ear.

Click—

Wait…

A photograph sliced through the photon mist and bled onto the screen. A Polaroid.

Within its thick white frame—evening fog, a vast field of frost-kissed northeastern green. At the center of the frame, a single tuning fork, half-buried in wet soil. The resonance prong pointed to the sky as if a question had been answered that no one was supposed to hear.

David nearly choked. He knew this image. He had taken this image. Or maybe…no—Celeste had. Yes.

His mind began to tease him, the ancient moment unfurling again: the tuning fork, Celeste striking it against the bench.

The year of the agreement. The capsule. The year before everything…

The humming buzzsaw suddenly slammed back into his skull—deeply resonant, violent, modulant. It pulsed with an insistence that refused to be ignored. This was more than memory. This was menace!

His hands hovered over the remote, but he was unable to grab it. The mounting tremble in his forearm now bordering on quake. Menace or no, that picture was staring at him—unflinching. Immutable. He could not help but stare right back. The image swirled—an unencumbered recursive.

Fog.

Field.

Fork.

He remembered the unholy stillness of that long-ago night. The smell of dug-up earth, of guilty evening dew. The added weight that held the distance between what they knew and what anyone else would believe—it made each shovelful land like a hundred pounds of darkness falling, pressing down, blotting out the light of knowledge they'd all agreed must go to ground that night.

He could hear the click of the Polaroid afterwards… how long it took to develop. Celeste’s voice floating on air; freezing over the crystalline mist.

If it's real, it'll claw its way out. It will come back to us.

She had meant it to encourage, but it returned to David now as pure threat.

Now the hum burrowed deeper into David’s chest, the air turning dense, resistant. His lungs faltered. It felt like a hand had closed around his throat—tight, invisible, lifting him into stillness. Then, suddenly, the sound pulled back. The pressure eased. The air thinned, as if exhaling for him. And slowly, uncertainly, his voice returned.

It was softer than before. Not a lie exactly, but close enough—like telling someone the fall will only last a second, when you both know time stretches differently on the way down.

“My apologies,” he said. “Wrong… wrong file.”

He returned, tried to finish the line of inquiry as if nothing had happened, but clearly something had. Every thought suddenly arrived exsanguinate, dulled, useless. Something inside him had shifted and his ability to share knowledge was turning from him.

He shut off the projector. The image was gone, but the memory had not finished developing.

Then—quietly, and with unconscious intent—he reached out and touched the blackboard. Sweeping over its surface—just a few fingertips, no more. The chalk dust greeted him like an old friend, and for one brief, unknowable moment there was nothing. No hum. No room. Only the strange impression—impossible, immediate—of a string answering somewhere beyond him.

“Professor?” The voice rose from the front row, but it did not register to David. “Professor Lang?”

Still nothing. His fingers—his entire body—now presented as inanimate, stuck, mired in the act of reaching back and wondering through time.

The class sat poised on the edge of reaction—glassy-eyed—all crunching the same calculation. Was this a prank? Some kind of retribution for a class-wide lack of vigor? Or perhaps all was well—pedagogy in suspended motion—the point too clever by half. No one wanted to be the first to decide which.

A biology major auditing the course to fill a requirement found she had less fear or reverence for the subject of the frozen professor. She got up, walked around the edge of the bench, into the aisle, and marched right down to meet Lang’s frozen face. With a bravery that the remaining students found grotesquely admirable, she pressed her cheek against the blackboard, mirroring his sideways stare. Then with a tone suited to someone trying to speak through a windstorm shouted, “Will… this… be… on… the… mid…term… Professor?”

David’s eyelid twitched. The stunt had worked—jolting him back to the present. His arm and hand released their frozen hold and fell to his side. And without moving his body further, he locked his eyes with hers, cracked an absurdly exaggerated grin, and with a ghoulish voice replied, “All concepts presented are… potentially examinable.”

The tension shattered, students howled in laughter and relief. David dared not dwell on what he had just managed to wiggle out of.

With this impossible recovery firmly in hand, he exclaimed, “Oh, I had you guys so good just now!”

More laughter, though some of it felt less than genuine. David checked the clock. Exactly five minutes, twenty-eight seconds remained.

“I think I’ve put you all through enough for one day. Let’s end there, shall we?” he said. Then louder as the rustle of the end of things filled the air, “You’ll read Kozlowski’s ‘Sympathetic Structures’ for next time. Chapters two through six. Pay attention to the footnotes. The math is wrong, but the metaphors are sound.”

Students, at once relieved and confused, packed their notes and slipped out one by one, shuffling and whispering as they passed. Some smiled with admiration. Others avoided his gaze completely. Lang felt the reluctance; he always had. That faint dread had long been part of his professorial mystique. Early in his career, this knowledge had stung, when the world was young and full of promise. But now it had hardened into a badge of purity. He found it, if not admirable, at the very least, useful.

The TA, a graduate student, was the last to leave. She handed him her doodle-covered lecture notes, a veritable travel log of geometric non sequiturs that concluded with a thinly scribbled block of text that read, “Clever save, David. Everything alright?”

Above the note of concern, something else—a crude declarative, only partially scribbled out—as if half intending to redact the words beneath, half to highlight them. It simply read, “WHAT THE FUCK?”

David blinked, then swallowed, finally remembering to inhale. It was the perfect transcription of the debacle that had been the last hour and a half, and he knew it.

As the last student exited and the door closed, he allowed his haunted gaze to drift towards the back of the hall. Winter light thinned to silver, stretching time in its wake as he looked out through the striated windows to the fall-frosted quad beyond.

He could hear it… flowing under the benches, creeping and climbing up the tree trunks beyond, flowing through the rapid falls of water and ice. It was out there… out there pacing… waiting for him… always patient, but now in punishment. The towering glass would not be protection enough—not remotely so. Whatever the source, whatever the reason, the message was clear. This chase was over. David knew it—felt it—heard it deep within. He could run no longer.

He dashed back to the desk, grabbed a fresh eraser, and began obliterating the afternoon's wreckage. Chalk dust snowed over his oxfords like sand through an hourglass possessed of endless patience. In the haze of what he'd disappeared—in the instinct to distance himself from what he believed but couldn't defend—lay everything. Not just to salvage a lecture, but to reclaim the life that was promised him—that he was so certain was his birthright; a life now buried with the theories he'd abandoned twenty years ago.

The clean boards now bore witness to an institutional truth: if it wasn’t declared in chalk, it hadn’t happened. Yet David stopped short of total erasure. Whether the choice came from bravery or foolishness, he could not say.

He stepped back, staring at the declaration. The backs of his knees struck the front bench, and his body yielded at once to the prompt. He sat, let his weight surrender the effort of holding itself upright, and within moments slipped into the kind of late-afternoon sleep that overtakes the overworked and the defeated.

Hours later he awoke slumped over in the front row, his neck cramped against the bench. The words remained on the board, gleaming white against the dull black surface.

David sat there, gazing, lingering, wondering… when a shadow in the doorway caught his eye.

“Do my eyes deceive, or is that Professor David Lang, still here on a Tuesday…and at moping hour?”

“Your eyes are true, Ralph. Evening.”

“I believe I predicted this one, Professor.”

Ralph stepped gently into the room, dragging his cart in tow.

“Yes sir. Even ran the math for you. Remember? What was it?”

“Yes, eighty-six percent of the time. You were right, of course.”

“That’s it, yeah. Eighty-six percent of the time, Tuesday evening rounds—Professor Lang’ll be fast asleep in his big, important Feynman chair, dreaming of chalkboards.”

David chuckled, exhausted and amused. As Ralph approached, David held out a twenty, pinched between two fingers.

“Got me pegged on this one, Ralph. I believe this belongs to you, then.”

The octogenarian waved it off with a scoff, then rested a worn, calloused hand on David’s shoulder.

“Some of us sleep for free. Folks like you gotta pay for it.” Ralph’s attention shifted to the single equation on the slate.

Resonance = Survival.

“Now that’s something I’ve not heard before, Professor.”

“How does it strike you?” David asked, a half-smirk teetering on his lips.

“True… I think. But of course… well, you’re the Professor, David. I just clean your knowledge up at the end of the night, but… I mean…”

“Come on, Ralph. Out with it.”

“Resonance builds up. Makes things more than the sum of their parts, right? Makes ‘em dangerous. So, I guess if I’m resonance, then, yeah… I equal survival. I don’t know. What do you think? You’re the professor.”

“Honestly, I’ve no earthly idea anymore, my friend. Except that… maybe you should be the professor.”

“You wanna trade this mop for that chalk?” He waved the idea off without even a second thought.b “Not on your life.”

“You’re a wise man, Ralph.”

“I know,” Ralph nodded, then lingered a moment before wandering back to his cart and mop with a quiet grin tickling his lips. As he turned to go, David gently slipped the twenty-dollar bill into Ralph’s shirt pocket.

“Goodnight, Professor Lang. Watch those side roads tonight. Slick as thieves.”

David flicked his fingers in acknowledgment—his reply curling away like cigarette smoke through a row of abandoned slot machines.

As he collected his things in silence, that silence began collecting him. The hall of glass exhaled its last shards of daylight, casting him gently towards the slow-blackened hallway, and into the spinning world once more.

Yet in the space between his lecture hall and the building's double doors—that long corridor of polished tile and flickering fluorescence—something shifted. Probabilities began to whirl and cry havoc.

The resignation and docility of acceptable scholastic procedure began crumbling all around him.

The Polaroid. Where the hell did it come from?

His pace quickened. The files were locked away! Password-protected. There was no pathway for that slide to end up in the deck. None.

And Rune.

Forty-eight hours advance notice. University policy—evaluation requirements—Section 4.7 of the faculty handbook.

The anger clarified with each step. Sharpened. By the time David hit the building's double doors and burst through into the quad, he was boiling.

Snow attacked him immediately—fat flakes driving sideways in the wind—but couldn’t land. The moment they touched his coat, his face, his hair, they vaporized. Steam rose from his shoulders. The heat radiating off him turned the falling snow into a halo of negative space, flakes approaching and then repelling like he was magnetized wrong.

He barely noticed. No, his mind was angling towards the office and files. And that noise… the frequency… was this some sort of cruel joke? 68.48 Hz… That’s precisely the—

Then, as if simply naming the thing out loud was enough to re-awaken the beast, the buzz returned.

It was not gradual. It did not creep its way back. It came roaring.

It detonated in his inner ear like a depth charge—so sudden and violent he staggered mid-step, one oxford skidding on ice.

“No. Not now!” He said it loud, though he had not intended to. “I'm done with you!” He screamed it into the night air.

But the sound was clearly not done with him.

It grew. Doubled. The air thickened with it—each breath pulling the vibration deeper into his lungs. His vision blurred at the edges. The snow, still falling, still repelled, created an impossible halo about his head, and through it he could feel the hum pulling.

Directional now. Coming from—

Ahead. Somewhere in the quad.

David's rage shifted. Turned predatory.

“Fine,” he yelled into the frigid night. “You want me to find you? I'll fucking find you!”

He dredged out toward it—heading now away from his office—following the resonance like a blood trail. The hum intensified with each step. Not louder. Denser. His molars ached. His sternum vibrated. The frequency climbed into his sinuses, his eye sockets, the hollow spaces behind his ears.

Ten feet. Twenty. Thirty into the quad.

The snow and lamplight conspired against him—refracting, diffusing, turning distance into abstraction. He could barely see three feet ahead. But it was not sight he needed now.

He could feel it. Closer. Closer. Until…

His shin struck iron.

The bench materialized out of fury and snow—green paint weathered to gray-green, armrests pocked with rust, east-facing, twenty-eight feet off the main path.

The hum wasn't coming from around it. It was coming from beneath it. Six feet down. Where the capsule had been sealed—buried with dirt and the collective certainty that some knowledge should stay buried.

David stood there—chest heaving, snow evaporating off his coat—staring down at the iron armrest, the weathered slats, the frozen ground beneath.

Twenty years in the earth, and it was still vibrating.

She'd said it would come back to them. He'd wanted to believe her then—needed to believe her—but the wanting had curdled into forgetting, and the forgetting had hardened into routine; a routine that had carried him past this bench four thousand times without incident.

Until today.

Now the hum was pushing at him. Compressing its wave deep within his skull. The oscillating dread-knot now hard-pressed against the very threshold of his sanity. David slammed his palms to his ears—the pressure building unchecked within him.

He would not allow it. It would not take him. Not after all this time.

And then… it happened.

Now… he didn’t mean to scream. He didn’t choose it. It wasn’t fear, or reason, or anything the mind could own. It rose from somewhere older—from a depth inside him that had no name.

Not voice. Not thought. Just pressure. Memory without memory.

And then: release.

A sound tore out of him so completely it stripped the world clean.

The windows shuddered. The stone walls answered. Even the air flinched.

And immediately, the hum—the awful, relentless hum—stopped.

A silent moment passed over the quad. A whispering of wind. A cackling crow. The distant laughter of students in situ. He stood there in the snow, breath catching, throat scraped bare, not knowing what part of him had remembered how to make a sound like that.

David turned from the site. Rejected its call—its pull on him. Forced his feet, his body to turn and walk… just start walking forward—away. Just… walk… away.

Behind him, the bench, and all that lay beneath, would be waiting now. Truth still had fire, it seemed—with fuel to burn. It had all returned. Clawed its way back just as Celeste said it would. It was time. And time, it seemed, would wait no longer.




II.  THE OFFICE

[Moderato, ma non troppo]

David’s office resisted occupancy like a poorly tuned instrument resisted pitch. Light bled through warped Venetian blinds, striping the stacked Annalen der Physik journals into a barcode of abandoned inquiries. A cello case leaned against the southern flank of the bookshelf, its leather scuffed in the precise pattern of subway-platform collisions from a commute to lessons he now scheduled only in daydreams.

David burst through the door—coat sailing toward a hook, bag thudding to the floor, papers cascading in a controlled avalanche across the desk. He didn’t even notice Rune Ishikawa, already seated in the corner, sipping on a cup of Earl Grey, almost indistinguishable from the furniture.

Before David could orient himself, Rune’s voice floated out into the room—unhurried, already mid-thought—as if none of the preceding spectacle had occurred.

“You know, David,” he took another delicate sip from his cup and with a faint upturn of the right side of his lips, continued. “There simply doesn’t seem to be milk anywhere. I searched and searched, even dared to cross over into the chemistry department. Dreadful world, terrible mistake. Still… nothing.

“Rune… come on in.”

“Yes, thank you. So here I am, sitting in your office, drinking an entirely naked cup of Earl Grey. Shameful.”

David dropped into his chair with enough force to test its stress limits. The jolt wiped whatever he’d carried in from the quad clean off his face.

“Rune, about before, I—”

“So David,” Rune continued, unfazed by David’s attempt to speak, “they say Princeton University Press is interested in a revised monograph from you—expanding on the decoherence paper.” As Rune continued, his fingers grazed the gilt edge of a volume from 1979’s Encyclopedia Britannica, a set David had inherited from a predecessor who’d abruptly retired to study Tibetan throat singing.

David blinked. “Yes… I’d heard something to that effect.” He struggled to color his tone with an appropriate layer of professional patina. His mind, still half-lit with residual vibrations and snow light, narrowed on Rune’s phrasing. “Sorry Rune, but who’s they exactly?”

“My dear boy, you forget—we share an agent. And a lawyer, for that matter. I had a call from Charles this morning. On other matters, of course.”

“Charles! Right. Of course.” David nodded too many times, buying seconds. “Don’t know where my head is. And he said?”

“He asked after you,” said Rune. “Mentioned you’d been rather difficult to reach of late. Said Princeton Press called him just the other day.”

“I see.” David’s hands began their familiar choreography across the paper-cluttered surface, pulling drawers like organ stops. “Come to think… I did see something from them… sure it’s here somewhere.” The ballet of search and delay. “Ah—Princeton U. Press. There we are.”

“Excellent.”

A pause hung between them. Not quite awkward. Not quite comfortable.

“Yes, well… wait and see, right?” David offered.

“Indeed, and… if so.” Rune lifted his hand in a gesture that somehow encapsulated here’s-hoping, pre-congratulations, and a threat disguised as collegial optimism, all at once. It made David’s stomach turn and his toes dig into the squeaking laminate floor.

“Yeah—Thanks. Thank you, sir.”

The surface nicety was about to spin his mind into primordial goo. David searched for immediate deflective focus material… found.

The espresso had grown a skin reminiscent of volcanic lakes—a leathery membrane quivering whenever a delivery truck passed the building. He found further distraction in the parallelogram of pale sunlight bisecting his left wrist, where dust motes performed Brownian dances above a stack of Slovakian musicology journals from 1987.

As the spin subsided, David was madly surprised to find Dr. Ishikawa still in the room. Worse, the man had made himself at home with a decades-old issue of Scientific Educators Quarterly, a publication so barren that forty-seven of its hundred and seventy-one pages had been surrendered to advertisements without any obvious loss to human knowledge. Yet there he stood, absorbed in page sixty-three, then sixty-four, and now sixty-five, as though revelation might yet crawl from the wreckage. David did not remember seeing him rise, cross to the bookshelf, or take the thing down at all.

Then, with sudden reanimation, Rune made a side-click of his mouth and a corresponding flip-flap closing of the magazine, as if reminded of something he’d left off his check-in-with-Lang list.

“Oh—and I meant to say,” he jolted back in, “that was a remarkably… thorough analysis of Pythagorean tuning in Gregorian antiphons last week.”

David only had so many of these polite rejoinders at his fingertips. Taking 1.3 seconds to mentally thumb through his collection, he plucked out, “Oh, did you think so? Well, that’s nice of you to say.”

His eyes followed Ishikawa’s drift toward the precarious ziggurat of unanswered student inquiries and meeting requests stacked beside the monitor.

“Well, I can see you’ve a mountain of paper here to climb. I’ll leave you to it.”

He turned gracefully, judging David’s lack of correspondence management with a move to the door that bordered on indignation. Then, stopping at the threshold, he turned, launching the unsaid into the spoken realm.

“Resonance… equals… survival?”

The air between them compressed towards a sub-zero chill.

“Interesting premise,” Rune allowed, his manicured nails tapping the doorjamb in glissandic 7/8 time. “Resonance as a survival strategy. It has a certain… intellectual elegance. Though one does wonder, David, when you’ll find the breath to move past the curation of the old masters and grace the department with something more… speculative? We miss that version of you.”

“Hm? What was that, Rune? Oh… speculative… yes, well…” A weak, ill-conceived semi-chuckle escaped David’s lips, as if to say, I wouldn’t hold your breath.

Dr. Ishikawa mercifully brought the audience to a close. “Well, Good night, Professor Lang.”

“Goodnight, Dr. Ishikawa.”

And with the unhurried shuffle of his soft leather shoes, he drifted away.

David rose to see him off but was too late, the gesture mattering not at all. His knuckle brushed the diploma frame as he turned, adjusting its forty-degree tilt to the exact angle Professor Esterházy had set during their last argument about academic legacy. The brass corner left a dent in his proximal phalanx that would throb until evening.

Was that it? Was that all he had?

David knew the outburst earlier was enough to put him out on his ass. It was a wholly inexcusable breach—no matter the circumstance. Yet the age-old axiom of academic neutrality and avoidance—once again—played as a superpower.

Rune had always been a nominal presence, barely there until the moment you realized he’d been holding the room together the whole time. It had taken David years to believe the man truly operated on the same neurological and vascular principles as the rest of humanity.

What was he to do now? Should he lock his lectures so as to keep his boss from discovering that he was losing it? David sat back into his chair with a heavy, unmediated flop, and, from the bottom drawer, pulled folder LXIII, its tab labeled Theoretical Explorations. And then, below in parentheses, Strike Outs. The typeface was written in the geometric hand he’d cultivated during a misguided calligraphy phase. New additions were never inserted without rereading previous rejections—the NSF denial still bearing bourbon stains from 2014, the IEEE rebuke citing “excessive poetry in the equations.”

Resigned to relegate yet another nascent rejector, David added page 974—still damp with the ink of a theorem collapsed under the referee’s indifferent gaze—and shut the drawer.

Surely this was no way to live; of that he was certain. Yet the pattern’s reliability offered a certain comfort. With every rejected treatise or unpublished paper, with every lecture delivered half-baked in the accepted science he knew to be incomplete at best, nonsense at worst, the outcome never changed. The dismissal was predictable. He could rest easier in the knowledge that he had been here before. This unimpressive, even untruthful was at the very least, familiar.

And then there was the rest: requests for advisory meetings, for recommendation letters, the brag sheets of mentees and postdoctoral hopefuls, all of it unanswered and faintly accusatory. Since his father’s death, the weeks had felt like a second conscription into adulthood, brought on less by any single blow than by attrition. The forms kept arriving while his father remained dead. The indecency of that made him flinch.

It was personal. How could it be otherwise? Reality, as ever, had shown no interest in the life David had imagined for himself. And it occurred to him, with the sudden clarity of a cruel joke, that midlife felt a great deal like thirteen: the same vertigo, the same sense of inhabiting a body and a future that refused to explain themselves. And eighty-one—the age his father had been when he died two weeks ago—was, disturbingly, not a remote continent but a neighboring country: closer to now than to never. Someone ought to do something about that paradox, he thought. Or at least install a better warning system than this blunt annunciation: oops—your father is dead and you are almost forty-seven… crisis—midlife, go.

And there, in the far-right corner of the desk, lay the manila envelope from Schaker, Marsheck & Camden, the firm executing his father’s will. David looked at it, looked away, then forced himself to look again, as though staring long enough might let him absorb some portion of its weight. It did not. He could not bring himself to draw the papers from their stiff bureaucratic sleeve, much less sign them—the small, almost laughably ordinary gesture that would make everything final. That single motion would close the long, fraught conversation he and his father had been having since David was nearly fifteen and first understood that the thoughts handed to him need not be the only ones he kept.

They had argued—sometimes mildly, sometimes with real force—about science and music: one obedient to law, the other disciplined only so it could remain human. His father had not wanted to lose him to physics. He had wanted music for David: beauty, honesty, clarity. Music offered such things without embarrassment. Physics, as Arthur had learned from his own father, did not exactly advertise the same rewards in the cheerful pamphlet titled Welcome to Scientific Academia: A Few Things to Expect Before Your Journey Begins.

David had insisted both were possible—that science and music need not remain separate islands. Somewhere in the distant past, he believed, there must have been a land bridge between them; someone merely had to go looking. Now he thought, with a bitter flicker of amusement, that truth was less often buried than eroded. Discovery was never the hardest part. Learning to distinguish fortitude from foolishness was.

Then, with all that and more lingering in his head, he nudged the envelope back into its corner—touched, considered, still unsigned—and turned once more toward the glass, toward the campus trees and the darkening grass.

“I will, Dad,” he muttered. “Goddamn it. I… I will.”

Beyond the window, snow thickened into sleet, and sleet turned to ice, laying a fresh sheen of treachery over the quad’s footpaths. Yet the ground around the bench—along with the narrow track by which David had stumbled upon it—remained oddly bare, untouched by the accumulating glaze, as if the moment itself still radiated some after-heat that refused the world’s new terms.

His mind kept circling back: the bench, the fork, the capsule, the data. A recursion he dreaded. A momentum he could not, for the moment, break.

Somewhere beneath the grass—under layers of dense clay and root-striated soil—lay the capsule he, Celeste, and Marcus had buried: their graduate-school manifestos on revolutionizing acoustic physics, sealed up like a promise and a dare. David’s fingernail traced the rim of his coffee cup, finding the enamel chip where he’d dropped it while trying to convince his father that they were doing the right thing. With that constant voice of challenge in his life now silenced, that long ago foggy evening seemed to have found a voice in him once more. A voice he knew in youth, in new discovery, before they shook hands and agreed it was a song sung before its time—a melody no one was ready, or willing, to hear.

He exhaled, shutting all of it from his mind, for ahead waited the relief of rehearsal, the comfort of trusted, true friends of care, and a cello that had always shielded him from true harm.

He looked up at the clock, then down at his own—for the day had proven less than reliable already. Confirmed:

It was one minute, forty-seven seconds from time.




III.  THE REHEARSAL HALL

[Largo maestoso]

The Music School’s residence on East Hill had never resolved into a single, settled idea—more of a slow-growing organism that had learned its shape by necessity, by habit, by the pressure of young lives needing rooms in which to fail and begin again. The original 1907 Elizabethan chapel sat at its center, a masonry heart, cool and enduring, around which the future had gathered and thickened over decades, addition by addition, corridor by corridor, until the whole became less a plan than a history you could walk through.

By the seventies, the place had been reborn—rebranded, really—as “living architecture,” and the phrase, for once, did not feel like a lie. Engineers carved deep into the glacial hillside to find something solid enough to trust, then lifted the newer structures up and out, rising just over sixty-eight feet above the slope, sheltering an arched amphitheater beneath a spanning canopy of old-forest timber that seemed, even now, to remember life before being pressed into the service of sound.

Overhead, a lattice of loft offices and narrow bridges looked down on the rehearsal spaces like catwalks, spare and watchful. Above the musicians hung the acoustic clouds—those pale, suspended planes—stitched with seams that never fully closed, so that sound did not merely bounce and die but wandered upward, slipping through the gaps into rafters, into the chapel attic, where it gathered and lingered: a stray oboe line, a cello’s open string, the soft bruise of timpani, a flute’s thin bright thread—things that should have dissipated but did not, hanging in the dark like prayers that had spoken once and refused now to be unspoken.

Below, fluorescent corridors lined with practice rooms tunneled under the hill, binding the old stone house to the defiant 1974 annex, a towering, unclassifiable mass that fused acoustics to geology with a kind of stubborn confidence. Concrete, here, did not simply contain music; it held it. It caught vibrato in the hidden lattice of rebar and returned it, faintly, even when no one was present to deserve it—so that in the silences between terms, the walls seemed to keep a low, borrowed life, as if the building could not quite bring itself to forget.

It was a place designed to receive, to reverberate, and to remember.

The main rehearsal hall smelled of rosin and old wool, with the faint tang of brass polish and the salted trace of decades of nervous sweat. Acoustic clouds hung from the ceiling like sleeping bats, and the parquet floor—honeyed once, long ago—now cratered by the recurring scrape of chair legs and music stands, a topography of discipline and desire made permanent by repetition, by ambition rehearsed until it left marks you could not stamp out without erasing the story.

Roger Linn, the 1st Violinist, and Professor of String Performance, was already there, spreading music across the stands when David shouldered through the door, and for a moment the room held him the way it held every arrival—without comment, without mercy, simply making space.

Prokofiev’s Second sat atop the pile, corners weighted flat, the familiar pages worn soft at the edges. Months ago they’d penciled bowings into the margins—thick, insistent annotations that looked less like instructions than an ongoing argument with the composer himself, a private negotiation between living bodies and dead certainty.

David set the cello case down with a heavy, unmusical thud. His coat still held the cold.

Linn looked up, and the grin came easily—an old reflex—his arms opening for a brief second as if to embrace and then thinking better of it, bow dangling from one hand like a thought he hadn’t finished forming.

David gave a low laugh and dragged his sleeve across his face, as though he could wipe away whatever clung there.

Linn lifted the bow in a small, fluent gesture—How was your day?—or perhaps, more truthfully, Do we survive this week?

David shrugged, and the slump of his shoulders answered plainly enough.

They had been speaking this grammar since Boston—exhaustion as syntax, the old recursive loop of who’s-worse-off-this-week rendered wordless by long practice, the sort of intimacy that doesn’t require confession because it has already seen everything confession would contain.

Eleanor Rieger, the quartet’s second violin, entered next in a flurry of wool and efficiency, moving as if she were late to something important even when she wasn’t, dispensing a cheek kiss to Linn, and the same to David, each deployed with the semi-sincere warmth of a diplomat working a reception line. Her overcoat swung off mid-stride, a practiced flourish, as if the body beneath it had no time for weather.

And then she stopped.

She had not seen David since the funeral, and the recognition arrived in her face with a small, genuine hitch—sincere and deeply rendered.

“Oh, David, darling. How are you, my dear? Your mother… oh, I’m just so sorry. For you both. How is she doing?”

“Oh, you know my mother,” David said. “She’s a mess and she’s doing great. As always.”

“Bless her heart. You’ll send her my love, won’t you.” Eleanor took his forearm in that official, pressurized manner whose unspoken grammar was universal: You hang in there now.

“You know,” Linn offered, “Eleanor and I were trying to figure out which of us caught more performances of the BSO back when your father was second chair. What did we decide, Eleanor—between us, maybe one-hundred-twenty? One-twenty-five?”

“Oh, easily,” Eleanor said, allowing him the larger share with an elegant little concession. “Though you had me by a mile, Roger.” She smiled and drifted away toward her chair, already breaking open the small private rites that preceded her playing, as if there were a door inside her she had to unlock before sound could enter.

By the time she sat, she was humming—some interior pitch only she could hear—her body tuning itself with the slow seriousness of an instrument brought too quickly from cold air into warmth.

Roger caught David’s eye. They’d surrendered the attempt to understand Eleanor’s preparation years ago, accepting it the way one accepts a relative’s harmless superstition: with affection, with fatigue, with quiet relief that not everything needed translating.

“It was a lovely service,” Linn said softly as David began unpacking his things, the words placed gently, as though setting down a fragile object.

“It was,” David agreed, quietly. “Thanks for all your help last week.”

“Wouldn’t have been anywhere else. Was honored to be with you, my friend.”

And then, the small contacts. Pats to the back, a grip to the shoulder. They were the gestures of attempts, bearing thirty years of friendship and family, and all the things people mean but never quite manage to say. The sort of things you swear, at eighty-one, you wish you’d begun saying freely at thirteen—only to realize, too late, how little time is left to make up for the silence… the assumptions.

And then Celeste Armitage appeared in the doorway.

She did not enter as Eleanor entered, in motion first; she arrived as if the room had been waiting for her and she had known it. She paused there, scanning the space with a calm, unhurried assurance, viola snug against her back, and then stepped forward. As she passed David—still turned toward the coat pegs—she reached out and touched his elbow, firm and warm, a real hello: not tentative, not ceremonial or managed.

David glanced back, caught her eye, and gave the smallest nod—half greeting, half something quieter that did not yet have words. He hung his coat. When he turned again, she was already at her chair, settled as if she had always been there.

No one asked where the music was. Linn tapped the corner of his stand with a knuckle, glanced around once, letting the room collect itself.

Then he raised his bow and sounded a clear A that hung in the air like a plumb line. One by one, the others followed. Eleanor’s second violin, Celeste’s viola, finally David’s cello—each coaxing their open strings into alignment with that pure, unwavering pitch. For a few moments, the air thickened with a dissonant tapestry of fifths, a purposeful cacophony of micro-adjustments as fine tuners were tweaked and pegs nudged. Then, as if a single breath were released, the sounds coalesced into a luminous, resonant chord. A moment of silence fell, the tension of preparation giving way to the collective focus of now.

“The Kabardinian,” Roger said, smiling now, the name spoken like a private signal. “Shall we?”

Chairs creaked into readiness. Bows lifted in a loose, coordinated arc. A shared breath—gathered more than taken—moved through the group as if it belonged to them all.

And then, at last, Prokofiev arrived.

SERGEI PROKOFIEV

  String Quartet No. 2

  “Kabardinian,” Op. 92


[I.   Allegro sostenuto]

Resonance preceded sound. David felt the cello’s body thrum against his sternum before bow touched string. Then came the opening chord—a striking bitonal clash, F bass under B-flat major, a poly-chordal figure that wanted blood, full of mountain air and mythic keening, the visceral strain of folk dance and night terror.

By measure twelve, David's attention fractured. The cello's G-string

resonated at 196 Hz—close enough to the room's natural mode at 161.8 Hz that the interference pattern became visible.




Not metaphorically. Actually visible: dust motes tessellating in the standing wave, golden ratio spirals unfolding in three dimensions.

David’s fingers maintained their accuracy. Every position landed clean; every shift glided into place with practiced inevitability. His intonation was flawless—so precise it felt less like playing and more like gravity. The bow arm moved as if unspooled from a long preordained phrase, its weight and articulation embedded into muscle fiber through years of trench warfare with this godless composition.

But his mind had slipped its tether.

It rose past the fingerboard and f-holes, past the curvature of the quartet's intimate enclosure—past even Celeste’s rising breath, chasing the harmonic series as it bloomed and rose in spectral cascades over the group. He was mapping nodes, tracing overtones, measuring the room in frequencies instead of feet. His inner ear parsed the world in ratios and resonances, every phrase reduced to its sine wave skeleton.

The others noticed almost immediately. Roger glanced at Celeste, and between them, an acknowledgement that a drifting had begun.

He was still playing—clean, perfect, untouchable—but he wasn’t with them anymore. His eyes weren’t on the score or anyone else. They were fixed upward, reading patterns no one else could see: reflections, cancellations, the ghostly interference of vibrating wood and air.

His presence felt altered. Robotic, or worse. As if the soul had stepped sideways, leaving the body behind to execute its task. The bow still drew sound from the string, but each note arrived emptied of breath, risk, or will. A performance without a performer.



[II.  Adagio]

Celeste tried everything. She bent phrases, breathed warmth into her tone, leaned into rubato like an outstretched arm—reaching for his, waiting for David’s cello to answer with the expected resolution.

He played the notes. Exactly as written. Flawless. Unmoved. Absent.

She leaned toward him again, instinctively, drawn by the shape of a void. Her breath caught on the edge of a phrase—nearly late.

And then, the memory struck. Mid-bow. No warning—just the sharp, silent incision of knowing. It arrived like dissonance unprepared: twenty years compressed into a single note that would not resolve.

This, in the end, is what undid them. This other absence. He had simply evaporated from their shared life—molecule by molecule. Weekends disappeared behind the closed door of his study. While he pursued theoretical elegance, everything outside it—her, their home, the rituals of ordinary love—was reduced to background noise. The equations on his blackboard garnered ten times the affection that she could ever hope to inspire in him. There remained no escape, no earthly choice but to recognize that she had lost him to the purity of his own abstraction. And in the end, the most heartbreaking part was this:

He had never… even noticed… she was gone.

Her bow trembled. She whispered beneath her line, barely audible:

“David… stay with us.” Then, softer still—a prayer, “Stay with me.”

By the midpoint of the Adagio, the violinists were visibly discouraged. Roger had seen this in him before. But not like this—not for years. The concern was obvious across all their faces. But Celeste… she felt a much deeper wound—that his body sat beside her while his mind flew through unreachable spaces, brushing constants and thresholds as though he were only notes away from discovering a new melody of quantum order.

Her plea lingered. Her pain trembled through each soaring buttress of the lofted annex until she could endure it no longer.

At the end of the second movement, she called the break herself. “Can we just—” She didn’t finish. She set down her viola and, with one miraculous surge of grace and courage, reached for him.

Her hand found his forearm, shaking him gently. “David. David, darling. Come back. Hey. You in there?”

His eyes twitched beneath closed lids before opening—bleary, confused, then sharpening on her face. As always, the razor’s edge of what he had just left, what he had allowed again, pierced him like a blade until Celeste stopped the blade from existing with the pure understanding of her eyes, and he was back.

He saw what he’d done, saw what she’d been carrying while he explored his numeric wonder-verse. The knowledge would keep him awake all night, turning over what he’d learned and what it had cost—again.


[III.  Allegro]

They resolved to regroup. Finding the violinists graciously willing, they launched into the third and final movement. The allegro opened with brittle, percussive throng. David was present, engaged; he and Roger even shared a startled moment of collective terror—cello to violin—terror and exhilaration as they climbed the wall of a fierce modulation.

Then, thirteen seconds less than two minutes in, rehearsal marker thirty-seven, came the col legno hammers: bows flipped, wood striking string with violent force. And then—


SNAP!


Not the gentle pop of a worn string, but a violent detonation. The cello’s C string exploded under tension, a silver whip loosed at dangerous speed. It cracked like a gunshot, slicing the air at eye level.

All four froze, predator-still. Every muscle locked. For an endless moment they checked faces, eyes, necks. The wire could have blinded, cut, even severed.

They watched, unmoving, as the coil spat and fell harmless to the floor. Silence, for a full measure, maybe two. Then a collective exhale. Then glances, one to each. And finally, uncontrollably, laughter. All four at once, wild with relief and adrenaline. The laughter shook them. Unmusical, uncontrolled, purely human.

All four of them let the moment unfurl as it saw fit; each ensuring private moments of camaraderie and reassurance with each of the other members. Roger and Eleanor had been around this block between the four musicians before, and were smart enough to begin laying down the structural requirements necessary  to ensure that they would not be caught in the gravity of Lang and Armitage if it could at all be avoided.

So it was with an unspoken swiftness, a singular wink and nose twitch from Eleanor to Roger, and an even more stealthy, behind-the-back hand tilt from Roger to Celeste, that the two veteran violinists quickly gathered their things and, with polite goodnights administered, fled toward the comfort and quiet of home.

But Celeste and David lingered, longer than the laughter required. Twenty years of shared rehearsal rooms carried them further: near-misses, small catastrophes, the lived history of making music together.

“Well, you were in rare—” Celeste began, then dissolved again. “Sorry. Rare… probably best left rare form this evening.”

“Really? I could've sworn I nailed every note. Haydn never had

such a cellist.”

She stopped mid-laugh. “Haydn?”

“Opus 76, right?” He kept his face perfectly straight.

“David, for God's sake, it's Prokofiev!”

“Oh.” He paused, mock-thoughtful. “The Kabardinian thing? Huh. Well that explains the col legno.”

She turned to the empty doorway. “Hey, you guys, David was playing Haydn all evening!”

They collapsed again, laughter spilling into glances, folding into blinks and nods, the gestures that live so close to regret they can borrow milk from each other without having to knock.

Later, and often, David would call himself an idiot—that he could keep her through the chaotic early years only to lose her once he was grown and supposed to be wise. Twenty years together, never married, and somehow that difference hurt more. He had lost her to the purity of his numeric mania, and he knew it.

At the double doors, she paused, scarf coiled, hand on the push bar. Something in the corridor behind her had changed—a shift in the air.

She turned back, slowly. “David…?”

He was already looking at her. Something behind his eyes—held, then shuttered.

A beat. Then he shook his head, just once. “It’s nothing.”

She waited, reading him. He was hiding something. Afraid. Her breath caught as the darkness bled in—almost said something more. But it was no longer her place.

“Get some sleep, David,” she whispered. “Goodnight.”

“Goodnight, Cele.”

Only he could call her that—and only in the solemnity of their private world.

She lingered a moment longer, a slight smile cracking her defenses. The moment lasted exactly 2.47 seconds before she slid into the snow-shimmered quad and out of reach once more.

Deep in the tunnel beneath—or perhaps in the back pew of the old chapel—someone took a deep, laden breath, praying through the resonance of music and memory. The annex still lived, still served, still listened.

David melted back into the rehearsal hall, pulled on his overcoat, and sealed the cello away with the care of a man handling something both precious and dangerous. In the corridor, a distant vibraphone aligned perfectly with his tinnitus—a pure sustained note that would ring in his ears until well past two a.m.

David stopped to join the structure in its end-of-day rituals. He could tell only a handful of people remained in the building. A palpable sense of compression flowed heavily through the halls, burdening the air as the walls and ceiling catalogued the memory of Prokofiev’s wild chords and the mercy of a snapped string, folding them into its timbered rafters.

David glanced up at the clock above the double exit doors, then down to his watch—it was three minutes, forty-seven seconds past time.


IV.  THE APARTMENT

[Adagio, con malinconia]

As he left the music school, David felt the city return in sharp lines and pale silence. The snow-lit quad stretched before him, a frozen ledger of the passing of souls. Two sets of footprints: hers and his, running parallel until they split. At that point, the path they’d shared for years broke quietly apart—hers turning toward the bridge, his descending into the dark fold of lower East Hill.

He didn’t look back. He couldn’t. He knew what he’d see: his own solitude, pressed into the snow like a sentence without end. Winter, unblinking, recorded everything.

The walk home took ten minutes. Lately, it felt like less. The body moved before thought arrived. Eighteen months of repetition had erased the in-between—those few blocks where once there was conversation, or silence that held meaning. Now, he left rehearsal and found himself at the apartment door, unsure what had passed in the time between. Grief had become a function. A habit. A reflex.

The door to the apartment, with its tall frosted-green glass, shut behind him with a hollow cough—the closing of a vault. The narrow entry hall funneled him forward until the apartment opened into its two-story cavity. Brick rose the full height of the far wall, the fireplace centered like a sentinel. In the far left corner, a slender vertical window cut upward through jagged brickwork; glass banded in uneven waves. On the right wall,  a long horizontal casement stretched twelve feet across the kitchen wall, its lower sill just shy of the island’s steel lip.

The spiral staircase wound at the room’s center like a coil of wire, its glass treads catching stray city light. Above, the loft—wire-railed, half office, half catwalk—looked down into the main chamber. On the wall by the entry, the chalkboard installation waited: three massive counterweighted panels, disguised when shut as a plain white surface but ready to unfurl floor to ceiling like a trifold altar. Even dormant, it radiated the threat of dust.

Loneliness had crystallized here, a structure wholly unsympathetic. Neglect hung from crown-molding cracks. Chalk dust caked at the baseboards. A stranger with even minimal powers of observation could pin David to this place and vice versa in two minutes flat.

It was not a warm space, but neither was it clinical. Its design hovered in a state of unfinished intent—at once composed and disjointed. Though it had not been wholly thought through, David found it curiously suited to thinking. Those few who had spent time within its walls all remarked on the same thing: the apartment detested company. And yet, with David, it behaved differently, as if it had chosen him. It enfolded him with a quiet, maternal sense of care, a belonging that asked for nothing in return.

Sheet music colonized every surface, allowing for a massive B-minor scale to sprawl out across the coffee table. Clef symbols and poly-quivers curling out of time—the turgid product of overwritten tensor equations allowed to run roughshod over the terrain. The apartment was losing itself to resonance. Everything had new permission to ring.

In the kitchen, David found himself uncertain whether the hollowness was hunger or something else entirely. He resolved the question the only way he knew—through precision. The julienne demanded it. Each stroke of the blade against the board became staccato percussion, syncopating with the blue hiss of the stovetop flame. Combustion harmonics emerged among the carrots and pilot lights. The ordinary thermodynamics of his kitchen began to pulse with an unfamiliar rhythm, and he had no choice but to move with it.


[Rallentando]

As he sat down to eat, his notebook slipped from the arm of the couch, cozying up beside him like a supplicant. Soon its pages were fat with the whispers of midnight epiphanies: diagrams of harmonic oscillator eigenstates and the jagged calculus of vibrato dynamics. His handwriting slanted forward, letters like fermions fleeing confinement. He slashed through a line, tearing the paper—the sound of the rupture echoing the cello string’s snap.

The same adrenaline-boil scalded him. It was a disconnected synesthesia he could no longer govern. It felt as though his own electromagnetic field was slipping frequency, dragging his body chemistry with it.

To establish a baseline—to confirm that cause still produced effect—he reached into his pocket. The folded note from his TA was still there. He didn’t look at it right away. He held the paper between his fingers as if it could anchor him.

He knew what it contained. The scrawled reprimand. The catalog of errors. Proof of the afternoon’s collapse.

He unfolded it.

What he saw ruptured something cleanly and at once. The page was covered in doodles. Nothing else. No notes. No corrections. Just idle shapes looping across the paper.

David dropped it as if it had turned venomous. The page drifted to the floor—harmless, obscene. He stepped on it hard, grinding it into the oak bellow, as though pressure alone might suffocate whatever truth it threatened to assert.

David reached for his phone, and with no concession to present reality, pressed the impossible-to-execute button on his favorites list. A photograph of his father materialized on the screen as the call routed to the now obscene recording of his father’s ghost:

“Hello, this is Arthur Lang. I can’t come to the phone at present. Please leave a message after the tone, and I’ll return your call at the earliest interval.”

He hung up, as though he had reached into the afterlife and felt his hand singed by the attempt.

“God damn it, Dad,” he murmured, his voice carrying the timbre of a child left alone in a storm—cold, abandoned, and furious at being stranded.

He shifted his gaze to the mirror above the mantel, studying a face assembled from borrowed time. More than ever before, he could see his father's eyes tearing through his own. The features—passed down with genetic precision—now clamoring for prominence in the man's eternal absence, an overlay so vivid it felt spectral. He had never permitted himself to fully see how much of his father's face resided in his own. But now, the truth of it was so blindingly obvious as to render his prior denials an act of embarrassing, almost willful ignorance—a deception sustained not despite the evidence, but precisely because acknowledging it meant confronting the inevitability that now stood before him.

David raised his hand and with two fingers touched his own face in the glass. For one flickering moment, he felt he could see himself and his father separated in the mirror before him, could feel the touch on his actual cheek. But then… gone.

His right iris contracted, catching some shift of light, and with it came a sudden photonic epiphany: his best work lived only in the liminal shimmer between tremolos and theorems, forever fleeing the act of observation. To document it was to lose it; to share it was to distort it. Perhaps that was the cruelty of his inheritance—not the burden of unfinished conversation, but the knowledge that some things are not meant for an audience.

By 2:47 a.m. the room had become a triangle of potentials. He stood equidistant from stove, chalkboard wall, and cello case—each a vertex, none resolved.




The refrigerator compressor droned a perfect fourth above the bathroom faucet’s drip. Chalk dust shimmered in the spill from the vertical window, suspended like cosmic background radiation.

Outside the brick, beyond the city’s pall of light, the universe hummed its eternal B-flat—waiting for a listener in tuned enough to decode its musings.
