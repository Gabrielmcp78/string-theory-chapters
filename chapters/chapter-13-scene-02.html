II. THE CALL
Cornell Physics Department | 11:47 A.M. Eastern

Dr. Rune Ishikawa found himself in an unfamiliar—and potentially compromising—state of affairs as the most inexplicable morning of his career bled into afternoon. He had spent the preceding hours monitoring solar-flare data that defied every model he’d developed over thirty years of astrophysics and cosmological research. The electromagnetic storm should have destroyed Cornell’s detection equipment, but somehow their arrays were not only functional—they were operating beyond theoretical specifications.

The final hour of the morning was slipping away as the venerable doctor floated in a sort of befuddled daze of mental inquiry. Only as he began the dance of the doze-off, head-drop, jerk-awake routine did he realize he hadn’t eaten in fourteen hours. Abandoning his monitors, he set out on a mission for sustenance. Three mini-fridges into reconnaissance, all he’d found was mold and a catalogue of unsanctioned “experiments” he mentally earmarked for inclusion in the next faculty newsletter.

Just when despair had begun to settle in, he unearthed a jar of crunchy peanut butter lurking behind a curry disaster—and, in a cupboard above, a single sleeve of saltines made an offering of itself to the weary professor. He was attempting the impossible—scraping peanut butter with a saltine—when he heard it.

Ring-ring.

The faint, unmistakable double cadence of the old rotary phone in the lab.

Ring-ring.

Installed in 1964, it had stubbornly survived the digital revolutions of the eighties and nineties. Amused it still worked, Rune chuckled, and reengaged the battle of his make shift lunch—the peanut butter clearly had the upper hand.

By the third messy bite, clarity struck.

  Shit. That might be the only phone within fifty miles that still works.

He bolted for the lab, the peanut butter jar ricocheting from his grip and bouncing down the hallway in a bizarre kick-the-can relay. The absurdity only spurred him faster.

The phone was still ringing when he burst into the control center. He tossed aside binders, a cork board of faded index cards, and a regulations posting from 1991, finally uncovering the mid-brown relic.

He snatched it up and shouted, “I’m here! Hello?”

Dead line.

“Damn it.”

Frustrated, he crammed crackers and peanut butter into his mouth in a frantic avalanche, only to glue the roof of his mouth shut with paste. He scrambled for water, even stale coffee dregs—

Ring-ring.

The battle axe rang again.

“Hello—” Rune tried to articulate through the peanut spread.

“Dr. Ishikawa?” squawked a voice on the other end.

“Speaking.” Rune mercifully found a bottle of water under the desk, swigging until his mouth was clear enough for educated discourse.

“Sir, this is the International Emergency Satellite Service. I have a secure priority call for you from UCLA Astrophysics. Please hold for Dr. Marcus McGrath.”

“Marcus?! Yes—yes, put him through. Marcus, are you there? Can you—”

“Rune! I’ve got you. You sound like a transistor radio. Can you hear me?”

“I hear you. I’m here.”

“Well, well, Dr. Ishikawa—the great Guardian of East Hill. Seen anything interesting lately, old friend?”

“Dr. McGrath! I didn’t think celebrities of your stature were allowed satellite calls before 2 P.M.! Marcus, what the hell is going on? What do you know?”

“Rune, it’s beyond. The presidents put me at point for coordinating global scientific response to this morning’s electromagnetic event. I was glad to see Cornell on our list of surviving facilities. How did your equipment hold up?”

“I honestly don’t know,” Ishikawa admitted, staring at data streams that shouldn’t exist. “I was collecting solar-flare measurements when the pulse hit. Expected total failure. Instead, our arrays began detecting cosmic background patterns I’ve never seen before.”

“Are you processing a holographic recording? Anonymous cellist—about forty-seven minutes of footage?”

“Yes. It arrived through the emergency network. Strangest file I’ve ever seen. Our computers are exhibiting behaviors that…” Rune hesitated, studying equations blooming across his screens.

“Rune? Did I lose you?”

“I’m here, Marcus. Sorry. These interlaced frameworks… the glyphs… they look…”

“Familiar?” Marcus cut in. “Yeah, I had the same thought. Like twenty-years-ago familiar.”

“My dear boy, I know what you’re implying, but this… this is not that. What I’m seeing… this is operating at a scale we’ve never touched. This is a completely different universe of complexity.”

“You’re right,” Marcus said quietly. “I just can’t believe it.”

“No, look at these segments. The algorithmic landmarks… they’re… David’s.” The implications now began to flood and codify Rune’s mind simultaneously. “Marcus, there’s something else. It’s about David. Before we go any further, you need to know. A few months ago, he began pushing these theories; obsessing about some kind of consciousness-integrated mathematics, an eleven-dimensional cognition model. It stopped being harmless speculation. I was genuinely worried for his sanity.” Rune lowered his voice. “Lang… what have you done?”

“What do you mean? Rune, what’s wrong with David? Is he there? Can I talk to him?”

“Oh God, Marcus. No. He’s… not himself. I suspended him two weeks ago—for his own safety.”

“Wait, wait.” McGrath’s voice spiked with urgency. “Consciousness mathematics? Eleven dimensions? Rune, don’t you see? He was onto something! Everything he was ‘mumbling about’—this is it!”

Understanding crashed over Ishikawa. “Marcus, I’m looking at the footage now. Those equations—Jesus Christ, they’re covering David’s chalkboards. His office, his lecture hall— they’re both plastered with it.”

“His boards?” Marcus’s voice sharpened. “They’re still there?”

“Yes. I left everything exactly as he—” Rune stopped, blood running cold. “Marcus, write this down.” He lunged for an extension sheet on the adjacent wall, the tug of the short phone cord pulling the handset away from his mouth as he yelled, “607-836-9939. That’s David’s classroom extension. I’m on my way now. Call me there in two minutes.”

He slammed the phone down and sprinted for the door.
