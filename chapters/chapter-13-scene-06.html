VI. TEMPORALity
Present Day

The speaker grid crackled. The last harmonics of Celeste’s declaration still hovered in the line, a shimmering after-tone no compression algorithm could erase.

Then a smaller sound intruded—ticks, like an old wall clock bleeding through the feed.

“What is that? Who’s patching analog noise?” McGrath barked.

No one answered. The ticks accelerated, outpacing any human rhythm. Screens in the Keck control room drifted out of sync; one showed 09:56 A.M., another 10:04, another a timestamp from two minutes in the future.

“Tolliver,” Marcus said softly, “verify system time.”

“Already did, sir. It keeps… sliding. The NTP server says we’re negative thirty-four seconds from standard.”

“Negative—?” McGrath stopped. “That’s not lag. That’s prediction.”

At CERN, Kovács leaned into her terminal. “Marcus, are you getting duplicate packets? I’m receiving your questions before you speak them.”

“What?”

“I just logged your last sentence—word for word—thirteen seconds ago.”

Rune Ishikawa watched the chalk glow intensify, symbols advancing across the boards like text from an unseen typist. “It’s echoing us,” he murmured. “The system’s anticipating dialogue. It’s building harmonic expectation—the way a melody knows its own resolution.”

Celeste stared at the blackboard equation that had begun to pulse:

Each pulse a heartbeat. Each heartbeat slightly ahead of her own.

She whispered, “The future’s bleeding backward.”

Monitors across the network replied. A new data stream overlaid the existing chatter—frames of video appearing in reverse order, showing their conversation rewinding, then branching. Every few seconds the feed corrected itself.

“This is quantum phase slippage,” McGrath said, voice tight. “Temporal coherence collapse across the entire mesh. We’re inside the waveform.”

The realization rippled through the call: they were no longer observing David Lang’s resonance—they were participating in it. Every response folded back into the signal and returned ahead of its source. The experiment had annexed causality.

For thirty-two seconds, global science stood inside a chord that included its own future.

Then the harmonics converged, compressing to silence.

McGrath exhaled, half-laughing. “Hey, I bet I can tell you all what’s gonna happen in about twenty-eight seconds.”

Laughter trickled in, forced at first, but needed—everyone knew that much. Until the twenty-eight seconds passed and with the clockwork of just-before-this, the sound mist of temporal static flanged back at them—current. It was zero minutes : zero second in time.
