II. To Ground
Largo funebre - Ma con speranza

By the time they stepped outside, the fog had begun to gather—heavy and insistent. It clung low across the grass in slow spirals, coiling around the bases of the trees and slipping like water between the red stones of the quad. The air had changed. Quieter now. Weighted in some unspoken way.

They walked without direction, but there was only one place left to go.

The bench waited at the edge of the eastern rise, under the wide-bent limbs of the oldest tree on campus—an elm or something older, no one could say. Behind it, the hillside sloped up and away, a long stretch of impossible green, empty but watchful in the dark.

Rune was already there. He didn’t speak. He didn’t need to.

A shovel rested against the bench. Polished handle. Clean blade. Marcus picked it up and began to dig.

The soil came loose too easily, like something had passed through here before them. David cleared the sides as the hole widened. Celeste moved quietly beside them, unrolling the final copy of their notes and placing the capsule on the grass with the kind of care reserved for old instruments and unlit matches.

When the hole was deep enough, David lowered the capsule into the earth. It didn’t clatter or shift. It simply landed.

Celeste stepped forward.

From her coat, she pulled a tuning fork—slender, slightly dulled at the ends, still marked from the last calibration tests they’d run in the studio. She struck it gently against the leg of the bench, and the sound it made was too large for the air it entered.

She knelt and pressed it into the soil just above the pit, tines upright. It stood humming, faintly, its base half-sunk in fog.

Then she took out the camera. Clicking the Polaroid twice in quick succession.

The first shot slid out into her hand. She lifted it, watched the image develop—grass, fork, mist, the dark shape of the tree in the background. Then she quietly placed the camera back into her jacket as the second shot followed with a soft mechanical click.

Rune stepped forward and took the photograph from her fingers. He didn’t ask permission. She didn’t offer it. The gesture passed between them like a breath someone else had exhaled.

He tossed the photograph into the capsule.

Then he paused.

His hand hovered above the tuning fork. The sound was still there—full in a way that didn’t match the shape of it. Like a wire pulled just tight enough to hum.

He picked it up. Held it. Waited. Then, with no change in expression, he dropped it in.

The moment it struck the bottom of the capsule, the sound swelled—deep, resonant, unnatural. Something heavy and old. Like the earth beneath them had responded.

The note hung in the air, then went on hanging.

Marcus closed the lid. The hum remained.

They filled the hole in silence. Packed the dirt. Smoothed the edges. Covered the mound with leaves and scattered gravel. When they finished, there was no mark left. No sign anything had been there at all.

Rune extended his hand. One by one, they shook. No words were exchanged. No promises made. Just contact.

They watched Rune Ishikawa walk back over the crest of the hill and into the fog beyond, then Marcus and Celeste turned to leave, but David remained. Stalwart. Resigned.

He stood motionless at the edge of the covered pit, eyes still fixed on the soil, like the shape of it hadn’t changed.

He tilted his head slightly, listening. Listening.

Celeste noticed first. She turned to loop back, casually, as if she had other reasons to return to the hill. She stopped just short and tried to sidestep her way back in at his side like she hadn’t left. After a brief assessment of David’s need for human contact, she said, “If it's real, it'll claw its way out. It will come back to us. I know it will.”

He didn’t answer, though he swore for years thereafter that he had. She didn’t repeat herself, for she felt in that moment like once was already tempting something that acted like fate.

They turned slowly away and, holding each other at the sides, walked off the hill and down to the faculty parking lot together.

The Wagoner waited, engine idling low. Marcus crawled over the front bench and into the back as David and Celeste flanked both sides of the wood-paneled old wagon. The car crawled, engine gurgling, across the empty lot, stopping at the guardhouse that secured the reserve lot. An officer stepped out, clipboard in hand.

“Good evening. Name, please?”

“David Lang.”

The officer scanned the sheet, and pulled the license that had been checked in upon their arrival.

“And can you confirm your date of birth, Mr. Lang?”

“Of course,” David said, “November 28, 1979.”

The guard nodded, took one final look from photo to face and back, and handed David the ID. “Thank you folks. Drive safely this evening.”

The gate arm lifted, and they pulled away from campus, slowly making their way down and off the slopes of East Hill. As they pulled away, the red brake lights caught the pressing fog, painting the hillside in hidden crimson. The three drove for 70 miles in a silence so thick it hurt to look the other way. When at last the lashing valley released them towards open road and home, it was Marcus who braved a well dropped hammer into the deep.

“Hey Dave, man. You’re just gonna love what I get you for your birthday in 20 years!”
